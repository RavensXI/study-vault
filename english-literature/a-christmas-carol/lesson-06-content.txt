# Stave 5: Transformation & Redemption

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Scrooge Wakes: A New Beginning</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Stave 5 opens with one of the most joyful moments in English literature. Scrooge wakes up and the bedpost is his own. Dickens writes: &ldquo;Best and happiest of all, the Time before him was his own, to make amends in!&rdquo; The emphasis on &ldquo;his own&rdquo; &mdash; repeated three times in the opening lines &mdash; shows Scrooge reclaiming his life. After three staves of being a passive observer of his past, present, and future, Scrooge is now in control.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Scrooge is overwhelmed with emotion. Dickens describes him &ldquo;laughing and crying in the same breath&rdquo; and making &ldquo;a perfect <dfn class="term" data-def="A reference to the ancient Greek sculpture of a man and his sons entangled with serpents, used by Dickens to describe Scrooge comically tangled in his stockings.">Laoco&ouml;n</dfn> of himself with his stockings.&rdquo; This mix of comedy and emotion is deliberate. After the darkness of Stave 4, Dickens uses humour to signal that genuine transformation has occurred. Scrooge is no longer the cold, calculating miser &mdash; he is joyfully, messily human.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Scrooge declares: &ldquo;I will live in the Past, the Present, and the Future. The Spirits of all Three shall strive within me.&rdquo; This shows he has absorbed the lessons of all three ghosts and commits to sustained change, not just a fleeting moment of goodness.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Scrooge&rsquo;s Similes: Language of Transformation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Dickens uses a series of <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech comparing two things using &lsquo;as&rsquo; or &lsquo;like&rsquo; to create a vivid image.">similes</dfn> to describe the transformed Scrooge: &ldquo;I am as light as a feather, I am as happy as an angel, I am as merry as a schoolboy. I am as giddy as a drunken man.&rdquo; Each simile connects Scrooge to ideas of innocence, joy, and freedom &mdash; the exact opposite of his Stave 1 description as &ldquo;hard and sharp as flint&rdquo; and &ldquo;solitary as an oyster.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The contrast is deliberate and powerful. In Stave 1, the similes describe cold, hard, isolated objects. In Stave 5, they describe lightness, happiness, and connection to others. Dickens is using the same literary technique across the novella&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="A narrative structure where the ending mirrors the beginning, creating a sense of completion and reinforcing key themes.">cyclical structure</dfn> to measure Scrooge&rsquo;s moral transformation. The reader can see exactly how far he has come.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Scrooge&rsquo;s Actions: Proving the Change</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Dickens does not simply tell us Scrooge has changed &mdash; he <em>shows</em> it through a series of generous actions that directly mirror and reverse his behaviour in Stave 1.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>The boy and the turkey:</strong> Scrooge sends a boy to buy the prize turkey and has it delivered to the Cratchit family anonymously. This contrasts with Stave 1, where Scrooge threatened a carol singer with a ruler. Now, instead of attacking a child, he rewards one. Instead of hoarding, he gives freely.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>The charity collector:</strong> Scrooge approaches the same gentleman he dismissed in Stave 1, whispering a large donation in his ear. The man is so shocked he can barely speak: &ldquo;Lord bless me!&rdquo; Scrooge adds: &ldquo;A great many back-payments are included in it.&rdquo; Dickens uses the phrase &ldquo;back-payments&rdquo; to suggest Scrooge is making amends for years of neglect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Fred&rsquo;s dinner:</strong> Scrooge goes to his nephew Fred&rsquo;s house for Christmas dinner &mdash; the invitation he cruelly refused in Stave 1. This reconciliation symbolises the restoration of family bonds that Scrooge had rejected.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Bob Cratchit:</strong> Scrooge raises Bob&rsquo;s salary and pledges to &ldquo;endeavour to assist your struggling family.&rdquo; He becomes a &ldquo;second father&rdquo; to Tiny Tim, who &ldquo;did NOT die.&rdquo; Dickens capitalises &ldquo;NOT&rdquo; for emphasis &mdash; this is the emotional payoff of the entire novella.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Dickens writes that Scrooge &ldquo;was better than his word. He did it all, and infinitely more.&rdquo; The phrase &ldquo;infinitely more&rdquo; shows that true <dfn class="term" data-def="The process of being saved from sin, error, or wrongdoing through genuine change and moral improvement.">redemption</dfn> is not a single act but a lifelong commitment to generosity and compassion.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">The Cyclical Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Stave 5 deliberately mirrors Stave 1. Dickens revisits the same characters, settings, and situations &mdash; but with transformed outcomes. The weather changes from &ldquo;cold, bleak, biting&rdquo; fog to &ldquo;clear, bright, jovial, stirring cold; cold, piping for the blood to dance to; Golden sunlight; Heavenly sky.&rdquo; Even the weather reflects Scrooge&rsquo;s inner transformation.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">This <dfn class="term" data-def="When something non-human is given human emotions or characteristics, or when weather and setting reflect a character&rsquo;s mood.">pathetic fallacy</dfn> reinforces Dickens&rsquo;s message: when people change for the better, the world around them changes too. The cyclical structure creates a deeply satisfying conclusion &mdash; every wrong from Stave 1 is put right in Stave 5.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n21">The novella ends with Dickens&rsquo;s direct address to the reader: &ldquo;May that be truly said of us, and all of us!&rdquo; followed by Tiny Tim&rsquo;s famous words: &ldquo;God bless Us, Every One!&rdquo; Dickens breaks the fourth wall to speak directly to his Victorian audience. His message is not just about Scrooge &mdash; it is about <em>all</em> of us. He argues that anyone can change, that generosity creates community, and that the spirit of Christmas should extend &ldquo;all the year.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22">The novella was published in December 1843 and sold out within five days. It transformed the way many Victorians thought about Christmas and charity. Dickens achieved exactly what he set out to do: he used storytelling to change hearts and minds.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n23">For the 30-mark essay on Stave 5, compare similes across staves to show structural awareness. Always link Scrooge&rsquo;s actions in Stave 5 back to specific moments in Stave 1 to demonstrate understanding of the cyclical structure. Include Dickens&rsquo;s social purpose &mdash; the examiner rewards answers that explain <em>why</em> Dickens wrote the novella, not just what happens in it. Use phrases like &ldquo;Dickens intends the reader to&hellip;&rdquo; or &ldquo;Dickens uses this to convey his message that&hellip;&rdquo;</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n24">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n25">Scrooge&rsquo;s Stave 5 similes (&ldquo;light as a feather,&rdquo; &ldquo;happy as an angel&rdquo;) directly contrast his Stave 1 descriptions (&ldquo;hard as flint,&rdquo; &ldquo;solitary as an oyster&rdquo;), measuring his moral transformation.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n26">Every action in Stave 5 reverses a wrong from Stave 1: the carol singer, the charity collector, Fred&rsquo;s invitation, and Bob&rsquo;s salary are all put right.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n27">Dickens&rsquo;s final message is that redemption requires sustained effort, not a single gesture &mdash; Scrooge &ldquo;was better than his word&rdquo; and kept Christmas &ldquo;all the year.&rdquo;</li></ul>
