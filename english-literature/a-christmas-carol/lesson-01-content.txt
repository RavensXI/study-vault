# Victorian Context & Dickens' Purpose

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Did Dickens Write A Christmas Carol?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Charles Dickens published <em>A Christmas Carol</em> in December 1843, during a period of extreme inequality in Victorian Britain. The gap between rich and poor was enormous. While wealthy Victorians lived in luxury with servants and grand houses, millions of working-class families struggled to survive. Dickens had personal experience of poverty &mdash; when he was twelve years old, his father was imprisoned for debt, and young Charles was sent to work in a <dfn class="term" data-def="A factory where workers pasted labels onto pots of boot blacking, a type of shoe polish.">blacking factory</dfn>. This childhood trauma shaped his lifelong passion for social justice.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">In 1843, Dickens read a Parliamentary Commission report on the employment of women and children. He was horrified by the conditions described &mdash; children as young as four were forced to work in mines and factories for long hours with little pay. He originally planned to write a political pamphlet, but while walking the streets of Manchester, he decided a story would be far more powerful. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A short fictional narrative, longer than a short story but shorter than a novel, typically under 100 pages.">novella</dfn> could reach people&rsquo;s hearts in a way that statistics never could.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Dickens was not born into poverty, but he had direct experience of it. His three key biographical facts for the exam: (1) his father was imprisoned for debt, (2) he worked in a factory as a child, and (3) he wanted to change conditions for the poor, especially children.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Victorian Poverty &amp; the Poor Law</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Victorian society was deeply divided. Those who were poor lived in squalor, deprivation, and misery. They barely had enough money or food to survive. Child mortality was extremely high, and there was no free healthcare &mdash; if you fell ill and could not work, your entire family was at risk of starvation. The only safety net was the <dfn class="term" data-def="An institution established under the Poor Law where destitute people were housed and made to work in harsh conditions in exchange for basic food and shelter.">workhouse</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The Poor Law Amendment Act of 1834 created workhouses as the main form of relief for the destitute. However, conditions inside were deliberately made harsh to discourage people from seeking help. Families were separated, food was minimal, and the work was gruelling. The Victorian upper classes believed that poverty was caused by laziness or moral failure, not by an unjust system. This attitude meant that many wealthy people saw no reason to help those less fortunate than themselves.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">The Industrial Revolution &amp; Child Labour</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">From the mid-1700s onwards, Britain&rsquo;s economy shifted from agriculture to factory-based industrial production. This required a huge workforce, including children who were forced into horrific working conditions for pitiful wages. Children worked in coal mines, textile mills, and factories, often suffering injuries or death. In 1800, only 60% of men and 40% of women were literate &mdash; without education, poor people had almost no chance of escaping poverty.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Dickens hated how children were treated in the workplace. His own experience in the blacking factory left him with a deep empathy for child workers. Through <em>A Christmas Carol</em>, he wanted wealthy readers to see the human cost of their indifference and to change their behaviour.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Dickens chose to set his story at Christmas because it was a time when people were already thinking about generosity, family, and goodwill. The Victorian era was when Christmas &ldquo;went mainstream&rdquo; &mdash; Christmas cards, crackers, and Christmas trees were all introduced during this period. Dickens used the festive season to amplify his message about social responsibility.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Dickens&rsquo;s Purpose &amp; the Gothic Tradition</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Dickens wrote <em>A Christmas Carol</em> to highlight the shocking contrast between rich and poor and to persuade his readers to change their attitudes. He presented wealthy society as &ldquo;pleasant, ordered and extravagant&rdquo; and contrasted it with poor society shown as &ldquo;disordered, barbaric and unhappy.&rdquo; Through his iconic characters and ghostly plot, he manipulated his readers&rsquo; emotions to make his argument hit harder than any political essay could.</p>

Dickens uses Gothic conventions &mdash; Marley&rsquo;s ghost, dark foggy streets, and supernatural visitations &mdash; to create tension and fear. A common Gothic theme is the idea that sins have the power to curse and affect you in the future, which is exactly what happens to Scrooge through Marley&rsquo;s chain and the ghostly visits.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Structure: Staves, Not Chapters</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Dickens divides his novella into five &ldquo;staves&rdquo; rather than chapters. A <dfn class="term" data-def="The set of five horizontal lines on which musical notes are written; Dickens uses the term instead of 'chapters' to connect his novella to the idea of a Christmas carol (song).">stave</dfn> is a set of five lines in music on which notes are written. By calling his sections &ldquo;staves,&rdquo; Dickens connects the structure of the novella to the title &mdash; the whole book is presented as a song of redemption. Just as a carol has verses that build towards a joyful conclusion, the five staves chart Scrooge&rsquo;s journey from cold-hearted miser to generous, redeemed man.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Dickens also makes a <dfn class="term" data-def="A reference to another literary work within a text, used to draw parallels or add layers of meaning.">literary allusion</dfn> to Shakespeare&rsquo;s <em>Hamlet</em> at the opening of Stave 1. Just as Shakespeare needs his audience to believe Hamlet&rsquo;s father is truly dead before his ghost appears, Dickens insists that &ldquo;Marley was dead: to begin with&rdquo; so that the reader will be shocked when his ghost visits Scrooge.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n19">When writing about context in the exam, never just list historical facts. Always link context directly to Dickens&rsquo;s purpose. For example, instead of writing &ldquo;Workhouses existed in Victorian Britain,&rdquo; write &ldquo;Dickens uses Scrooge&rsquo;s dismissal of the poor to workhouses to criticise Victorian society&rsquo;s cruel attitude towards poverty.&rdquo; Context should support your analysis of the writer&rsquo;s methods and message.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n20">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n21">Dickens published <em>A Christmas Carol</em> in 1843 to criticise the huge gap between rich and poor in Victorian society and to persuade wealthy readers to take social responsibility seriously.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n22">Victorian attitudes blamed poverty on laziness (influenced by Malthus). Workhouses were deliberately harsh. Dickens used Scrooge to represent &mdash; and challenge &mdash; these cruel attitudes.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n23">The novella blends Christmas tale, ghost story, and Gothic fiction. The five &ldquo;staves&rdquo; link to the musical structure of a carol, charting Scrooge&rsquo;s journey towards redemption.</li></ul>
