# London & Checking Out Me History

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">London &mdash; William Blake</h2>
<h2 data-narration-id="n2">Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n3">William Blake (1757&ndash;1827) was a poet, painter and printmaker who lived through the <dfn class="term" data-def="A period of rapid social and industrial change in Britain from the late 18th century, bringing urbanisation, factory work and widespread poverty.">Industrial Revolution</dfn>. He witnessed poverty, child labour and the growing divide between rich and poor in London. Blake was deeply critical of institutions he saw as corrupt &mdash; the Church, the monarchy and the government &mdash; which he believed oppressed ordinary people instead of helping them. &ldquo;London&rdquo; was published in his 1794 collection <em>Songs of Experience</em>.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">The Poem &mdash; What Happens</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">The speaker walks through the streets of London and describes what he sees and hears. Every face shows &ldquo;marks of weakness, marks of woe.&rdquo; He hears cries from men and infants, and the &ldquo;mind-forg&rsquo;d manacles&rdquo; of mental oppression. He observes the suffering of chimney sweepers (child labourers), soldiers whose blood runs down &ldquo;Palace walls,&rdquo; and young prostitutes whose curses &ldquo;blast&rdquo; the innocence of newborn children. The poem ends with the striking <dfn class="term" data-def="Placing two contrasting ideas, images or words together for effect.">oxymoron</dfn> &ldquo;Marriage hearse&rdquo; &mdash; even love and happiness are tainted by death and disease.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">&ldquo;<strong>I wander through each charter&rsquo;d street, / Near where the charter&rsquo;d Thames does flow</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The repetition of &ldquo;charter&rsquo;d&rdquo; suggests everything in London has been mapped out, owned and controlled. Even the river &mdash; a natural feature &mdash; has been confined by human authority. Blake implies that freedom has been stripped away by those in power.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">&ldquo;<strong>Marks of weakness, marks of woe</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The repetition of &ldquo;marks&rdquo; works on two levels: &ldquo;mark&rdquo; as a verb (he notices) and as a noun (visible signs of suffering). The abstract nouns &ldquo;weakness&rdquo; and &ldquo;woe&rdquo; emphasise that the suffering is both physical and emotional. Everyone in London is affected.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n9">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;The mind-forg&rsquo;d manacles I hear&rdquo; &mdash; <dfn class="term" data-def="Metal shackles or handcuffs, symbolising imprisonment and lack of freedom.">Manacles</dfn> are handcuffs or chains. Blake says these are &ldquo;mind-forg&rsquo;d&rdquo; &mdash; forged in the mind &mdash; suggesting people are trapped not just physically but psychologically by the rules and systems of those in power.</p>
</div>

    <p data-narration-id="n10">&ldquo;<strong>Every black&rsquo;ning Church appalls</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The verb &ldquo;black&rsquo;ning&rdquo; suggests the Church is being morally darkened &mdash; it is failing to help the poor and is therefore complicit in their suffering. &ldquo;Appalls&rdquo; literally means &ldquo;to make pale&rdquo; (from the white funeral cloth), suggesting death and horror.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11">&ldquo;<strong>The hapless Soldier&rsquo;s sigh / Runs in blood down Palace walls</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; This powerful metaphor suggests soldiers who fought and died for king and country are ignored by those in the palace. The blood running down the walls is a visceral image of guilt &mdash; Blake blames the monarchy directly for the suffering of soldiers.</p>
  </div></div>

<p data-narration-id="n12">&ldquo;<strong>Blasts the new-born Infant&rsquo;s tear / And blights with plagues the Marriage hearse</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The verb &ldquo;blasts&rdquo; is violent and destructive, implying that even newborn innocence is destroyed by the city&rsquo;s corruption. The oxymoron &ldquo;Marriage hearse&rdquo; fuses a symbol of love (marriage) with a symbol of death (hearse), suggesting that even the most hopeful aspects of life are poisoned by the city&rsquo;s problems.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Structure &amp; Form of London</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">The poem has four quatrains (four-line stanzas) with an ABAB rhyme scheme, creating a steady, relentless rhythm &mdash; like the speaker&rsquo;s footsteps as he walks through London. This regularity mirrors the repetitive suffering Blake witnesses. Each stanza focuses on a different sense: sight (stanza 1), sound (stanza 2), then the specific suffering of institutions (stanza 3) and innocence destroyed (stanza 4).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Checking Out Me History &mdash; John Agard</h2>
<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">John Agard (born 1949) grew up in Guyana, which was a British colony until 1966. He received a <dfn class="term" data-def="Centred on European culture, history and values, often at the expense of other cultures.">Eurocentric</dfn> education that taught British and European history while ignoring the achievements of Black and Caribbean historical figures. After moving to Britain, Agard became one of the most celebrated performance poets in the country. &ldquo;Checking Out Me History&rdquo; was published in the collection <em>Half Caste and Other Poems</em> after Agard had lived in Britain for approximately 30 years.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">The Poem &mdash; What Happens</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">The speaker angrily lists the European historical figures he was taught about in school (Dick Whittington, Lord Nelson, Florence Nightingale) and contrasts them with Black historical figures he was never taught about: Toussaint L&rsquo;Ouverture (leader of the Haitian Revolution), Nanny de Maroon (Jamaican freedom fighter), Shaka the great Zulu (military leader), and Mary Seacole (nurse in the Crimean War). The poem ends with the speaker declaring he is now &ldquo;checking out me own history&rdquo; and &ldquo;carving out me identity.&rdquo;</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Agard uses a <dfn class="term" data-def="A structure with two distinct, contrasting parts running in parallel.">dual structure</dfn> throughout the poem: standard-text stanzas about European history alternate with italic stanzas about Black history. This visually separates the two histories on the page, mirroring the way the speaker&rsquo;s education kept them apart.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">&ldquo;<strong>Dem tell me / Dem tell me / Wha dem want to tell me</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The <dfn class="term" data-def="Non-standard spelling that represents how words are actually pronounced, reflecting accent or dialect.">phonetic spelling</dfn> of &ldquo;Dem&rdquo; (them) and &ldquo;me&rdquo; reflects Agard&rsquo;s Creole dialect, asserting his Caribbean identity and resisting the dominance of Standard English. The angry repetition creates an accusatory, frustrated tone &mdash; &ldquo;they&rdquo; (the education system) only taught him what they wanted him to know.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n23">Agard uses a motif of light when describing Black historical figures: Toussaint is &ldquo;de beacon of de Haitian Revolution,&rdquo; Nanny de Maroon is a &ldquo;fire-woman,&rdquo; and Mary Seacole is &ldquo;a healing star among the wounded&rdquo; and &ldquo;a yellow sunrise to the dying.&rdquo; This light imagery suggests these figures are sources of guidance, hope and inspiration. It contrasts sharply with the simple, almost childish descriptions of European history (&ldquo;de dish ran away with de spoon&rdquo;), implying that the speaker&rsquo;s formal education was shallow compared to the rich, powerful stories of Black history.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n24">&ldquo;<strong>Bandage up me eye with me own history / Blind me to me own identity</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The violent metaphors of &ldquo;bandage&rdquo; and &ldquo;blind&rdquo; suggest that the colonial education system deliberately injured the speaker by hiding his cultural identity. The verb &ldquo;blind&rdquo; implies he has been robbed of the ability to see himself clearly.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">&ldquo;<strong>But now I checking out me own history / I carving out me identity</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The volta (&ldquo;But now&rdquo;) marks a turning point. The active verb &ldquo;carving&rdquo; has connotations of hard, physical work, suggesting that reclaiming identity is a painful but empowering process. The shift from &ldquo;Dem&rdquo; to &ldquo;I&rdquo; shows the speaker taking ownership of his own narrative.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Comparing Both Poems</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Shared theme &mdash; power of institutions:</strong> Both Blake and Agard criticise systems that oppress ordinary people. Blake targets the Church, monarchy and state; Agard targets the colonial education system. Both poets suggest that the real damage is psychological &mdash; Blake&rsquo;s &ldquo;mind-forg&rsquo;d manacles&rdquo; and Agard&rsquo;s being &ldquo;blind&rdquo; to his own identity.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Key difference:</strong> Blake&rsquo;s poem ends without hope &mdash; the &ldquo;Marriage hearse&rdquo; suggests a cycle of destruction that cannot be broken. Agard, by contrast, ends on a note of defiance and empowerment: &ldquo;I carving out me identity.&rdquo; Blake is an observer of suffering; Agard is a participant fighting back.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n29">When comparing two poems in one lesson, the examiner wants you to show you can analyse <strong>both poems in equal depth</strong>. A common mistake is spending too long on one poem and running out of time for the other. Plan your comparison by identifying 2&ndash;3 points of comparison (e.g. theme, method, tone) and discuss both poems under each point. Always use comparative connectives: &ldquo;similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;contrastingly,&rdquo; &ldquo;whereas,&rdquo; &ldquo;likewise.&rdquo;</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n30">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n31">Blake uses &ldquo;London&rdquo; to protest against institutional oppression &mdash; the Church, monarchy and state are all blamed for the suffering of ordinary people.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Agard uses dialect, dual structure and light imagery to reclaim the Black historical figures erased by a Eurocentric education system.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">Both poems criticise systems of power that damage people psychologically, but Agard&rsquo;s poem ends with hope while Blake&rsquo;s does not.</li></ul>
