# Ozymandias — Power & Its Decay

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Percy Bysshe Shelley &mdash; Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792&ndash;1822) was one of the leading <dfn class="term" data-def="A literary movement emphasising emotion, nature and individualism over reason and order.">Romantic</dfn> poets. He was politically radical, opposed tyranny, and believed passionately in individual liberty. He wrote &ldquo;Ozymandias&rdquo; in 1818 after hearing that archaeologists had discovered pieces of a funeral statue of the Egyptian pharaoh Ramesses II and planned to send them to the British Museum.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The poem&rsquo;s title comes from the Greek name for Ramesses II. &ldquo;Ozy&rdquo; derives from the Greek <em>ozium</em> meaning &ldquo;to breathe&rdquo; or &ldquo;air,&rdquo; and &ldquo;mandias&rdquo; from <em>mandate</em> meaning &ldquo;to rule.&rdquo; Ramesses II was one of the most powerful pharaohs in Egyptian history &mdash; he built more temples, took more wives (eight) and had more children (over 100) than any other pharaoh. He used slave labour to construct vast monuments he imagined would last forever.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Shelley wrote this sonnet as a political message: no matter how powerful a ruler may be, time and nature will always destroy their legacy. This directly challenged the idea of absolute, permanent power.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Poem &mdash; What Happens</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The narrator meets a traveller from &ldquo;an antique land&rdquo; who describes a ruined statue standing in the desert. Only two &ldquo;vast and trunkless legs of stone&rdquo; remain, with a &ldquo;shattered visage&rdquo; (face) half-sunk in the sand nearby. The sculptor captured the king&rsquo;s arrogant expression &mdash; a &ldquo;frown,&rdquo; &ldquo;wrinkled lip&rdquo; and &ldquo;sneer of cold command.&rdquo; On the pedestal, Ozymandias boasts: &ldquo;Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!&rdquo; But nothing remains around the statue except &ldquo;lone and level sands&rdquo; stretching endlessly.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">&ldquo;<strong>Two vast and trunkless legs of stone</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The adjective &ldquo;vast&rdquo; emphasises the enormous scale of the statue, suggesting the immense power Ozymandias once held. Yet &ldquo;trunkless&rdquo; shows the body is missing &mdash; his power is literally incomplete and broken. The opposing images create a sense of destruction: something once mighty is now fragmented.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9">&ldquo;<strong>Half sunk a shattered visage lies, whose frown, / And wrinkled lip, and sneer of cold command</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The <dfn class="term" data-def="The use of a series of words, phrases or clauses listed one after another for emphasis.">listing</dfn> of &ldquo;frown,&rdquo; &ldquo;wrinkled lip&rdquo; and &ldquo;sneer&rdquo; builds a picture of a cruel, arrogant leader. The adjective &ldquo;cold&rdquo; suggests he ruled without compassion or empathy. The verb &ldquo;shattered&rdquo; implies that even the physical record of his cruelty has been destroyed by time.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n11">&ldquo;<strong>Nothing beside remains. Round the decay / Of that colossal Wreck, boundless and bare / The lone and level sands stretch far away</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The short sentence &ldquo;Nothing beside remains&rdquo; is a powerful <dfn class="term" data-def="A pause near the middle of a line of poetry, often created by punctuation.">caesura</dfn> that halts the poem and emphasises the total absence of Ozymandias&rsquo;s civilisation. The oxymoron &ldquo;colossal Wreck&rdquo; juxtaposes size with ruin &mdash; the statue was once huge and impressive but is now just wreckage. The sibilance in &ldquo;lone and level sands stretch&rdquo; mimics the whispering wind and the slow erosion of sand covering the remains, reinforcing nature&rsquo;s quiet, unstoppable power.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Structure &amp; Form</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Ozymandias is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A 14-line poem, traditionally associated with love, with a specific rhyme scheme and metre.">sonnet</dfn> &mdash; traditionally associated with love poetry. Shelley subverts this by writing about the decay of power instead. The poem has 14 lines with roughly 10 syllables per line (iambic pentameter), but the rhyme scheme (ABAB ACDC EDE FEF) is irregular and doesn&rsquo;t follow a traditional Petrarchan or Shakespearean pattern. This irregular structure mirrors the fragmented statue itself &mdash; nothing about Ozymandias&rsquo;s legacy remains orderly or intact.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n14">Shelley uses enjambment throughout &mdash; lines flow into each other without pauses, reflecting the endless sands and the unstoppable passing of time. The caesura in &ldquo;Nothing beside remains.&rdquo; creates a dramatic pause, drawing the reader&rsquo;s attention to the finality of decay. Combined, these devices create an uneven, broken rhythm that mirrors the crumbling statue.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n15">The poem uses a framing narrative &mdash; the speaker is retelling a story told by a &ldquo;traveller.&rdquo; This distances the reader from Ozymandias, making the pharaoh feel remote and forgotten. The sculptor&rsquo;s art outlasts the ruler, but even that art is decaying. Shelley seems to suggest that while art preserves some element of human existence, ultimately nothing is permanent.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>One line has 11 syllables instead of the standard 10: &ldquo;My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings.&rdquo; This extra syllable could reflect how Ozymandias saw himself as above the rules &mdash; too important to be contained by ordinary structures.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Themes &amp; Comparison Suggestions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Power of nature vs human power:</strong> Compare with <em>Storm on the Island</em> (Heaney) or <em>Exposure</em> (Owen), where nature also dominates humanity. In Ozymandias, nature quietly erodes; in Storm on the Island, it attacks violently.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Pride and arrogance of leaders:</strong> Compare with <em>My Last Duchess</em> (Browning), where the Duke also displays arrogant, controlling power. Both speakers reveal their cruelty without fully intending to. However, Ozymandias focuses on power&rsquo;s impermanence, while My Last Duchess shows power being actively exercised.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>The transience of power:</strong> Compare with <em>London</em> (Blake), where institutional power oppresses people. Blake suggests power corrupts the present; Shelley shows that even the most powerful leaders are eventually forgotten.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n21">In a 30-mark comparison question, always structure your answer around <strong>similarities and differences</strong> in how the poets present the same theme. For Ozymandias, focus on the irony between Ozymandias&rsquo;s boast and the reality of his ruined statue. Use the phrase &ldquo;the poet suggests&rdquo; or &ldquo;Shelley implies&rdquo; to show you are analysing the writer&rsquo;s intentions, not just describing the poem. Remember: AO1 (ideas), AO2 (methods/language analysis) and AO3 (context) are all assessed.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n22">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n23">Shelley uses irony to show that Ozymandias&rsquo;s boast &mdash; &ldquo;Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!&rdquo; &mdash; is meaningless because nothing of his civilisation survives.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n24">The irregular sonnet form mirrors the fragmented, broken statue and reinforces the theme of decay.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25">Sibilance, caesura and enjambment work together to convey the quiet, unstoppable power of nature over human ambition.</li></ul>
