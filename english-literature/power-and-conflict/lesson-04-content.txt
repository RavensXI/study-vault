# My Last Duchess — Power & Control

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Robert Browning &mdash; Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Robert Browning (1812&ndash;1889) was a Victorian poet best known for his use of the <dfn class="term" data-def="A poem in which a single character speaks to a silent listener, revealing their personality and attitudes through what they say.">dramatic monologue</dfn>. He was fascinated by history, European culture and the psychology of power. &ldquo;My Last Duchess&rdquo; (1842) is based on the life of Alfonso II, Duke of Ferrara, who ruled a region in northern Italy from 1559 to 1597.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The Duke&rsquo;s first wife was Lucrezia de&rsquo; Medici, who died in 1561 aged just 17, only two years after their marriage. She died under suspicious circumstances and may have been poisoned. The poem is set three years after her death: an emissary (a messenger) has been sent by the Count of Tyrol to arrange the Duke&rsquo;s next marriage. The Duke shows the emissary a portrait of his late wife and, in doing so, inadvertently reveals his possessiveness, jealousy and cruelty.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">The Poem &mdash; What Happens</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">The Duke draws back a curtain to reveal a painting of his &ldquo;last Duchess&rdquo; and begins describing her. He complains that she was too easily pleased &mdash; she smiled at everyone, not just him. She treated his &ldquo;gift of a nine-hundred-years-old name&rdquo; with the same gratitude as any common kindness. The Duke felt this diminished his status, but he refuses to &ldquo;stoop&rdquo; to telling her how to behave. Instead, he ominously states: &ldquo;I gave commands; / Then all smiles stopped together.&rdquo; The poem ends with the Duke and the emissary walking downstairs to discuss the new marriage, pausing to admire a bronze statue of Neptune &ldquo;taming a sea-horse&rdquo; &mdash; a final symbol of the Duke&rsquo;s desire to control.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">&ldquo;<strong>That&rsquo;s my last Duchess painted on the wall, / Looking as if she were alive</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The possessive pronoun &ldquo;my&rdquo; immediately establishes the Duke&rsquo;s sense of ownership. The phrase &ldquo;as if she were alive&rdquo; has a chilling double meaning: it literally describes the lifelike quality of the painting, but also hints that she is dead &mdash; and possibly killed by his &ldquo;commands.&rdquo; The word &ldquo;last&rdquo; implies she is one in a series, reducing her to an item in a collection.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n8">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Since none puts by / The curtain I have drawn for you, but I&rdquo; &mdash; The Duke controls who sees the portrait. In life, he could not control the Duchess&rsquo;s smiles; in death, he has total control over her image. The curtain is a symbol of his power &mdash; only he decides who gets access to her.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n9">&ldquo;<strong>She had / A heart &mdash; how shall I say? &mdash; too soon made glad, / Too easily impressed</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The parenthetical &ldquo;how shall I say?&rdquo; mimics natural, unrehearsed speech, but the Duke is clearly choosing his words carefully to manipulate the emissary&rsquo;s impression. The repetition of &ldquo;too&rdquo; reveals his complaint: the Duchess was too kind, too happy, too generous with her affection. What sounds like a compliment (&ldquo;glad,&rdquo; &ldquo;impressed&rdquo;) is actually a criticism &mdash; he resented her warmth because it was not reserved exclusively for him.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">&ldquo;<strong>She thanked men &mdash; good! but thanked / Somehow &mdash; I know not how &mdash; as if she ranked / My gift of a nine-hundred-years-old name / With anybody&rsquo;s gift</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The Duke is outraged that the Duchess treated his ancient family name as equal to any ordinary kindness. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The use of dashes, pauses and interruptions in a character's speech to mimic natural thought patterns and reveal personality.">caesurae</dfn> (dashes) throughout these lines create a halting, agitated rhythm that reveals the Duke&rsquo;s barely controlled anger.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n12">&ldquo;<strong>Notice Neptune, though, / Taming a sea-horse, thought a rarity, / Which Claus of Innsbruck cast in bronze for me!</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The Duke ends by drawing attention to another artwork: the Roman god Neptune &ldquo;taming&rdquo; a sea-horse. This is a powerful <dfn class="term" data-def="Something that represents a bigger idea or concept beyond its literal meaning.">symbol</dfn> &mdash; Neptune (the Duke) dominates the sea-horse (the Duchess). The exclamation &ldquo;for me!&rdquo; reminds the listener that the Duke is a collector of beautiful objects, and the Duchess was just another possession. The name-dropping of &ldquo;Claus of Innsbruck&rdquo; shows his desire to impress through status and wealth.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Structure &amp; Form</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">The poem is a single, continuous <dfn class="term" data-def="A poem in which a single character speaks to a silent listener, revealing their personality and attitudes through what they say.">dramatic monologue</dfn> of 56 lines written in <dfn class="term" data-def="A metrical pattern of five pairs of unstressed and stressed syllables per line, creating a natural speech-like rhythm.">iambic pentameter</dfn> &mdash; 10 syllables per line with a natural speech rhythm. The use of rhyming couplets (AABB) gives the poem a sense of control and order, reflecting the Duke&rsquo;s desire to dominate everything around him. However, the heavy use of <dfn class="term" data-def="When a sentence or clause runs on past the end of a line of poetry into the next line without a pause.">enjambment</dfn> works against this regularity &mdash; the Duke&rsquo;s speech spills over line endings, suggesting his emotions are harder to contain than he lets on.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n15">We only hear the Duke&rsquo;s voice &mdash; the emissary is silent, and the Duchess cannot speak for herself. This reflects the power imbalance: the Duke dominates the conversation just as he dominated his wife. The listener (and the reader) must piece together the truth from what the Duke inadvertently reveals. He intends to present himself as a cultured, reasonable man, but his jealousy, possessiveness and potential violence leak through his words. Browning uses the dramatic monologue to create dramatic irony &mdash; we understand the Duke better than he understands himself.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The poem is cyclical &mdash; it starts and ends with a discussion of art (the painting, then the Neptune statue). This suggests the Duke sees people and art as interchangeable possessions. The Duchess has been reduced from a living person to an object on a wall.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Themes &amp; Comparison Suggestions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Power and control:</strong> Compare with <em>Ozymandias</em> &mdash; both feature arrogant men who use their power to dominate others. However, Ozymandias&rsquo;s power is destroyed by time, while the Duke&rsquo;s power over the Duchess continues even after her death (he controls the curtain). The Duke&rsquo;s power is intimate and personal; Ozymandias&rsquo;s was political and public.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Control of women / objectification:</strong> Compare with <em>The Emigr&eacute;e</em> or <em>Poppies</em> for contrasting female perspectives. In My Last Duchess, the woman has no voice &mdash; her story is told entirely by the man who controlled her.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Pride and jealousy:</strong> Compare with <em>Checking Out Me History</em> &mdash; both poems deal with controlling what someone can see or know. The Duke controls the Duchess&rsquo;s image; the colonial system controls Agard&rsquo;s access to his own history. However, Agard fights back; the Duchess cannot.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n21">For My Last Duchess, always remember that the Duke is an <strong>unreliable narrator</strong>. He presents himself as reasonable, but his words reveal jealousy and possessiveness. In the exam, show the examiner you can spot the gap between what the Duke <em>says</em> and what Browning <em>reveals</em>. This is dramatic irony, and it is essential for a top-level response. Use phrases like &ldquo;the Duke intends to present himself as... but Browning reveals...&rdquo;</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n22">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n23">Browning uses the dramatic monologue form to let the Duke inadvertently reveal his possessiveness, jealousy and cruelty &mdash; creating dramatic irony throughout.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n24">The poem explores how power and control can destroy human relationships &mdash; the Duke treated the Duchess as a possession, not a person.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25">The Neptune statue at the end symbolises the Duke&rsquo;s desire to &ldquo;tame&rdquo; and dominate, suggesting he will treat his next wife the same way.</li></ul>
