# Tissue & Poppies

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Tissue &mdash; Imtiaz Dharker</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A poet born in Pakistan, raised in Glasgow, who lives in Britain and India, often exploring themes of identity, freedom, and the role of women.">Imtiaz Dharker</dfn> wrote <em>Tissue</em> as part of her 2006 collection <em>The Terrorist at My Table</em>. The poem uses paper as an <dfn class="term" data-def="A metaphor that runs throughout an entire poem, comparing one thing to another across multiple stanzas.">extended metaphor</dfn> for the fragility of human life, power, and the structures we build to control the world around us.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The poem opens with a clear statement: &ldquo;Paper that lets the light shine through, this is what could alter things.&rdquo; Light is a symbol of truth, hope, and transparency throughout the poem. Dharker suggests that if we let the light in &mdash; if we were more open and less obsessed with rigid structures &mdash; we could change the world.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The poem references the <dfn class="term" data-def="The holy book of Islam, treated with great respect and reverence.">Koran</dfn>, where families write &ldquo;names and histories, who was born to whom, the height and weight, who died where and how, on which sepia date.&rdquo; Paper records the most important moments of human life &mdash; births, deaths, family connections &mdash; yet it is thin and fragile. This paradox is at the heart of the poem.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Paper, Buildings, and Borders</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Dharker imagines what would happen &ldquo;if buildings were paper&rdquo; &mdash; we would &ldquo;feel their drift, see how easily they fall away on a sigh, a shift in the direction of the wind.&rdquo; Buildings represent human power and permanence, but Dharker suggests they are less important than we think. The rhyme of &lsquo;drift&rsquo; and &lsquo;shift&rsquo; plays on the idea of movement and impermanence &mdash; even the most solid structures could be blown away.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">&ldquo;Maps too. The sun shines through their borderlines.&rdquo; The short sentence &ldquo;Maps too&rdquo; is blunt and decisive. Borders and national boundaries are drawn on paper &mdash; they are human inventions, not natural laws. The sun shining through the &ldquo;borderlines&rdquo; suggests these divisions are arbitrary and could be dissolved by something as natural as light.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">&ldquo;Fine slips from grocery shops&rdquo; &mdash; even receipts control our lives. The simile &ldquo;might fly our lives like paper kites&rdquo; suggests that money and capitalism have the power to lift us up or carry us away, and that our reliance on material wealth is as fragile and temporary as a kite in the wind.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Form and Structure of Tissue</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The poem is built from short stanzas of mostly four lines, with extensive <dfn class="term" data-def="When a line of poetry continues into the next line without punctuation, creating a flowing, continuous effect.">enjambment</dfn>. The stanzas are like thin layers of paper themselves &mdash; the form mirrors the content. The final stanza is just one line (&ldquo;turned into your skin&rdquo;), isolating it for impact. The lack of regular rhyme reflects the freedom and fluidity that Dharker advocates &mdash; she resists the rigid structures she criticises in the poem.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Poppies &mdash; Jane Weir</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="A Manchester-based poet known for exploring themes of loss, motherhood, and domestic life.">Jane Weir</dfn> wrote <em>Poppies</em> from the perspective of a mother whose son has left to join the army. The poem is deeply personal and emotional, tracing her grief through domestic imagery and sensory details. It is set around <dfn class="term" data-def="The Sunday nearest to 11 November, when the UK remembers those who died in wars.">Armistice Sunday</dfn>, connecting private grief to the nation&rsquo;s collective remembrance.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">The poem opens: &ldquo;Three days before Armistice Sunday and poppies had already been placed on individual war graves.&rdquo; The word &lsquo;individual&rsquo; is significant &mdash; each poppy represents a single life, a single family devastated by loss. The mother then describes preparing her son to leave: she smooths down his collar, resisting the urge to hold him back.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Weir uses textile and sewing metaphors throughout: &ldquo;my stomach busy making tucks, darts, pleats.&rdquo; These are dressmaking terms that describe her anxiety in physical terms &mdash; her stomach is knotting itself like fabric being folded. As a textile designer herself, Weir draws on her own experience to make the mother&rsquo;s grief tangible and physical.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Key Techniques in Poppies</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">The poem traces a chronological journey from the son&rsquo;s departure to the mother&rsquo;s solitary walk to a war memorial. At the memorial, she leans against it and listens, hoping to hear her son&rsquo;s voice: &ldquo;I listened, hoping to hear your playground voice catching on the wind.&rdquo; The word &lsquo;playground&rsquo; brings us back to childhood innocence, contrasting sharply with the reality of war.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n21">&ldquo;I was brave&rdquo; &mdash; the mother uses the same word (&lsquo;brave&rsquo;) typically associated with soldiers. She is fighting her own battle: the battle to let her son go without breaking down.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22">&ldquo;Released a song bird from its cage&rdquo; &mdash; the metaphor compares her son to a bird she has set free. There is beauty in the image, but also loss &mdash; once released, the bird cannot be called back. The &lsquo;cage&rsquo; represents the safety of home and childhood.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23">&ldquo;Sellotape bandaged around my hand&rdquo; &mdash; the verb &lsquo;bandaged&rsquo; links a domestic object to injury and war. Even small, everyday actions carry the weight of her grief. The poem blurs the boundary between the domestic world and the world of conflict.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Comparing Tissue and Poppies</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Both poems deal with fragility and loss, but from very different angles. <em>Tissue</em> is philosophical and abstract &mdash; Dharker meditates on the fragility of all human structures, power, and life itself. <em>Poppies</em> is deeply personal and emotional &mdash; Weir focuses on one mother&rsquo;s specific, tangible grief.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">In <em>Tissue</em>, fragility is presented positively: Dharker suggests we should embrace impermanence rather than cling to rigid power structures. In <em>Poppies</em>, fragility is painful: the mother&rsquo;s world is fragile because her son&rsquo;s life is at risk. Both poets use tactile imagery &mdash; Dharker&rsquo;s pages &ldquo;smoothed and stroked&rdquo; and Weir&rsquo;s sewing metaphors &mdash; but for opposite purposes: Dharker celebrates the delicacy of paper, while Weir uses domestic textures to express barely contained grief.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n27">When writing about <em>Tissue</em>, examiners reward students who can explain the extended metaphor clearly. Don&rsquo;t just say &lsquo;paper represents life&rsquo; &mdash; explain how different types of paper (Koran pages, maps, receipts, tissue/skin) represent different aspects of human experience. For <em>Poppies</em>, always discuss the mother&rsquo;s perspective and her <strong>specific</strong> domestic imagery &mdash; this is what makes the poem distinctive.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n28">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n29"><em>Tissue</em> uses paper as an extended metaphor for the fragility of human life and power &mdash; Dharker argues that impermanence should be embraced, not feared, and that human life is more &lsquo;grand&rsquo; than any building.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30"><em>Poppies</em> presents a mother&rsquo;s grief through domestic imagery, sewing metaphors, and a chronological journey from her son&rsquo;s departure to a war memorial &mdash; the poem shows how conflict devastates families, not just soldiers.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">Both poems explore fragility, but Dharker sees it as liberating while Weir presents it as a source of profound pain and loss.</li></ul>
