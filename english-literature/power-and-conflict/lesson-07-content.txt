# Kamikaze & The Emigrée

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Kamikaze &mdash; Beatrice Garland</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A London-based poet and NHS clinician whose first poetry collection was published in 2013.">Beatrice Garland</dfn> wrote <em>Kamikaze</em> about a Japanese pilot during World War Two who was sent on a suicide mission but turned back. <dfn class="term" data-def="Japanese pilots in World War Two who deliberately crashed their planes into enemy ships as a suicide attack, considered a great honour.">Kamikaze</dfn> pilots were trained to fly their aircraft into enemy ships &mdash; it was portrayed as the highest patriotic honour. By the end of the war, nearly 4,000 kamikaze pilots had died.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The poem is narrated by the pilot&rsquo;s daughter, recounting the story &ldquo;later to her children.&rdquo; The pilot himself has no voice in the poem &mdash; his silence mirrors the way his family silenced him after his return. Garland uses this <dfn class="term" data-def="The perspective or voice from which a poem or story is told.">narrative voice</dfn> to explore what happens when a soldier chooses life over duty.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The pilot &ldquo;embarked at sunrise with a flask of water, a samurai sword in the cockpit, a shaven head full of powerful incantations and enough fuel for a one-way journey into history.&rdquo; The details &mdash; the sword, the shaven head, the &ldquo;one-way journey&rdquo; &mdash; all emphasise that he was prepared to die. The word &ldquo;incantations&rdquo; suggests he has been influenced by patriotic propaganda, almost as if he is under a spell.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Pilot&rsquo;s Decision</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The daughter imagines that &ldquo;half way there&rdquo; her father looked down and saw &ldquo;the little fishing boats strung out like bunting on a green-blue translucent sea.&rdquo; The <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech comparing two things using &lsquo;like&rsquo; or &lsquo;as&rsquo;.">simile</dfn> comparing fishing boats to bunting is ironic &mdash; bunting suggests celebration, but there will be no victorious return for this pilot. The &ldquo;green-blue translucent sea&rdquo; is beautiful and peaceful, contrasting sharply with the violent mission he was on.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Beneath the boats, the pilot sees fish &ldquo;arcing in swathes like a huge flag waved first one way then the other.&rdquo; A flag is a powerful symbol of national identity &mdash; the very thing the pilot was supposed to die for. But here, nature provides its own &lsquo;flag&rsquo; that seems to call him back to life. The <dfn class="term" data-def="Repetition of the &lsquo;s&rsquo; sound, often creating a hushed, sinister, or soothing effect.">sibilance</dfn> in &ldquo;dark shoals of fishes flashing silver as their bellies swivelled towards the sun&rdquo; creates a smooth, flowing sound that reflects the gentle movements of the fish and the beauty of nature.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">The pilot then &ldquo;remembered&rdquo; his childhood &mdash; building cairns of &ldquo;pearl-grey pebbles&rdquo; on the shore with his brothers, watching his grandfather&rsquo;s fishing boat come safe to land. These innocent memories of family and nature contrast with the violence of his mission. The colours &ldquo;silver&rdquo; and &ldquo;pearl-grey&rdquo; make nature sound precious and valuable &mdash; worth living for.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">The Emigr&eacute;e &mdash; Carol Rumens</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><dfn class="term" data-def="An English poet, lecturer, and translator whose 1993 collection included The Emigr&eacute;e.">Carol Rumens</dfn> wrote <em>The Emigr&eacute;e</em> about a speaker who left a city as a child and clings to an idealised memory of it. The title uses the feminine French form, telling us the speaker is female. The city may represent a real place, or it could symbolise a person, a time, or an emotion that the speaker has been forced to leave behind.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">The poem opens: &ldquo;There once was a country&hellip; I left it as a child but my memory of it is sunlight-clear.&rdquo; The fairy-tale opening (&ldquo;There once was&rdquo;) suggests loss, while &ldquo;sunlight-clear&rdquo; establishes the poem&rsquo;s central motif &mdash; light. Throughout the poem, the speaker&rsquo;s memories are always associated with brightness and warmth, even as reality darkens.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;It may be at war, it may be sick with tyrants, but I am branded by an impression of sunlight.&rdquo; The verb &lsquo;branded&rsquo; is violent &mdash; it suggests the positive memory has been burned into her permanently, like a mark on skin. Nothing can change her childhood view, not even war or oppression.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Key Techniques in The Emigr&eacute;e</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Rumens uses <dfn class="term" data-def="Giving human qualities or characteristics to something non-human.">personification</dfn> to bring the city to life: &ldquo;my city comes to me in its own white plane. It lies down in front of me, docile as paper.&rdquo; The city becomes a living companion that the speaker can love and protect. The adjective &lsquo;docile&rsquo; (gentle, easily led) and the simile &lsquo;as paper&rsquo; suggest the memory is both fragile and controllable &mdash; unlike the real city, which has been changed by conflict.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">&ldquo;Time rolls its tanks&rdquo; is a powerful metaphor that personifies time as an invading military force. The &ldquo;frontiers&rdquo; that &ldquo;rise between us, close like waves&rdquo; combine military and natural imagery to show how the speaker is being cut off from her homeland &mdash; by politics, by distance, and by the passage of time.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n19">The final stanza introduces a threatening &lsquo;they&rsquo; who &ldquo;accuse me of absence&rdquo; and &ldquo;circle me.&rdquo; The repetition of &lsquo;they&rsquo; creates a menacing, oppressive atmosphere. &ldquo;They accuse me of being dark in their free city&rdquo; suggests racism or xenophobia &mdash; the speaker is treated as an outsider in her new home.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20">Yet the poem ends defiantly: &ldquo;My city hides behind me. They mutter death, and my shadow falls as evidence of sunlight.&rdquo; Even her shadow &mdash; caused by sunlight &mdash; is proof that light exists. The speaker refuses to let threats destroy her positive memory. The final word, &ldquo;sunlight,&rdquo; echoes throughout the poem, symbolising hope, identity, and the enduring power of memory.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Comparing Kamikaze and The Emigr&eacute;e</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Both poems explore how memory and identity are shaped by forces beyond the individual&rsquo;s control. In <em>Kamikaze</em>, the pilot&rsquo;s beautiful childhood memories pull him away from his mission &mdash; but society punishes him for choosing life. In <em>The Emigr&eacute;e</em>, the speaker&rsquo;s memories protect and sustain her against hostility in her new home.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">The poems present contrasting views of silence. The pilot in <em>Kamikaze</em> is silenced by shame &mdash; he has no voice in the poem and his family learns &ldquo;to be silent.&rdquo; The speaker in <em>The Emigr&eacute;e</em> refuses to be silenced &mdash; she holds onto her language (&ldquo;I can&rsquo;t get it off my tongue. It tastes of sunlight&rdquo;) and her memory as acts of resistance against those who would erase her identity.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n24">For the comparison question (30 marks), always compare <strong>methods</strong>, not just themes. For example: Garland uses a third-person narrative voice to show the pilot has been silenced, while Rumens uses first person to show the speaker&rsquo;s defiant ownership of her memory. Structure your essay with clear comparative connectives: &lsquo;Similarly,&rsquo; &lsquo;In contrast,&rsquo; &lsquo;While both poets&hellip;&rsquo;</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n25">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n26"><em>Kamikaze</em> explores how a pilot&rsquo;s decision to reject his suicide mission leads to family shame and social death &mdash; Garland uses nature imagery, narrative voice, and silence to convey the devastating consequences of defying duty.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n27"><em>The Emigr&eacute;e</em> presents memory as a source of power and resistance &mdash; Rumens uses light imagery, personification of the city, and a defiant first-person voice to show that identity cannot be erased by threat or distance.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n28">Both poems explore the tension between individual identity and external forces (patriotism, political oppression), but the pilot is destroyed by society&rsquo;s expectations while the emigr&eacute;e resists them.</li></ul>
