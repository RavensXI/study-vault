# Comparing Poems & Exam Skills

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Paper 2 Section B Question</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In the AQA English Literature exam, Paper 2 Section B gives you <strong>one poem</strong> printed on the page and asks you to compare it with <strong>one other poem</strong> of your choice from the Power and Conflict anthology. The question is worth <strong>30 marks</strong> and you should spend approximately <strong>45 minutes</strong> on it, including planning time.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The question will always focus on a theme or idea &mdash; for example: &ldquo;How does the poet present the effects of conflict?&rdquo; or &ldquo;How is the power of nature presented?&rdquo; You need to discuss <strong>both poems throughout</strong> your answer, comparing their methods and effects on the reader. This is not two separate essays &mdash; it is one <dfn class="term" data-def="An essay that discusses the similarities and differences between two texts throughout, rather than writing about each text separately.">comparative essay</dfn>.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The 30 marks are split across four <dfn class="term" data-def="The skills and knowledge areas that examiners use to assess your work.">Assessment Objectives</dfn>: <strong>AO1</strong> (ideas and understanding), <strong>AO2</strong> (analysis of methods &mdash; language, form, structure), <strong>AO3</strong> (context &mdash; when, why, and how the poem was written), and <strong>AO4</strong> (comparison &mdash; similarities and differences between the two poems).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Choosing Your Comparison Poem</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The poem on the paper is chosen for you. Your job is to pick the best poem to compare it with. Choose a poem that shares the same <strong>theme</strong> but presents it <strong>differently</strong>. The strongest essays compare poems that have both similarities and differences &mdash; this gives you more to write about.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">For example, if the printed poem is <em>Remains</em> (the psychological effects of war), strong comparison choices include: <em>War Photographer</em> (both show trauma, but soldier vs. witness), <em>Bayonet Charge</em> (both show the reality of combat, but present vs. remembered experience), or <em>Kamikaze</em> (both show lasting consequences of war, but PTSD vs. social shame).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Planning Under Time Pressure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Spend <strong>5 minutes planning</strong> before you start writing. A good plan saves time because you know exactly what each paragraph will cover. Use a simple grid:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Planning grid:</strong> Write the question theme at the top. Below, create two columns (Poem 1 and Poem 2). For each poem, note 3&ndash;4 short quotations with the method used (e.g. &ldquo;metaphor,&rdquo; &ldquo;personification&rdquo;). Then draw arrows between quotes that connect &mdash; these become your comparison points. Each arrow is one paragraph.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">The SEAS Paragraph Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Each paragraph should follow the <dfn class="term" data-def="A paragraph structure for poetry comparison: Statement, Example, Analysis, Speculation/comparison.">SEAS</dfn> method to hit all four assessment objectives:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>S &mdash; Statement:</strong> Make a clear point about how one poem presents the theme. Link this directly to the question. For example: &ldquo;Armitage presents the effects of conflict as a haunting, inescapable memory that replays in the speaker&rsquo;s mind.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>E &mdash; Example:</strong> Support your statement with a short, relevant quotation. Keep quotes brief &mdash; 3&ndash;6 words is ideal. For example: &ldquo;The speaker sees &lsquo;every round as it rips through his life.&rsquo;&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>A &mdash; Analysis:</strong> Identify the method (metaphor, verb, personification) and explain its effect on the reader. Use analytical language: &lsquo;suggests,&rsquo; &lsquo;connotes,&rsquo; &lsquo;implies,&rsquo; &lsquo;reflects.&rsquo; For example: &ldquo;The violent verb &lsquo;rips&rsquo; has connotations of destruction and conveys the fragility of human life.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>S &mdash; Speculation/Comparison:</strong> Compare with the second poem. Use comparative connectives and explain how the second poet presents the same idea similarly or differently. For example: &ldquo;Similarly, Duffy presents the photographer as haunted by &lsquo;a half-formed ghost&rsquo; &mdash; the metaphor suggests the memories are incomplete yet inescapable, much like the soldier&rsquo;s flashbacks in Remains.&rdquo;</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Worked Example: Comparing Remains and War Photographer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Question:</strong> &ldquo;How does the poet present the effects of conflict in Remains? Compare this to one other poem from Power and Conflict.&rdquo;</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Model Paragraph</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n27">Armitage presents the effect of conflict as a haunting and troubling memory that becomes inescapable for the speaker. When describing the moment of violence, the speaker states he sees &ldquo;every round as it rips through his life.&rdquo; The violent verb &lsquo;rips&rsquo; has connotations of destruction and fragility, suggesting the man&rsquo;s life was torn apart as easily as paper. This violent image stays with the soldier even when he is &ldquo;home on leave&rdquo; &mdash; the memory is so powerful that &ldquo;the drink and the drugs won&rsquo;t flush him out,&rdquo; showing that no substance can remove the trauma embedded in his brain. The metaphor of the dead man being &ldquo;dug in behind enemy lines&rdquo; turns the speaker&rsquo;s own mind into a battlefield, suggesting his mental health is under siege.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28">Similarly, Duffy presents the war photographer as deeply traumatised by what he has witnessed, even when he is safely back in &ldquo;Rural England.&rdquo; His hands &ldquo;did not tremble then though seem to now&rdquo; &mdash; the verb &lsquo;tremble&rsquo; connotes fear and fragility, showing the delayed effect of trauma. Like the soldier in <em>Remains</em>, the photographer was able to function in the war zone but falls apart at home. However, while the soldier in <em>Remains</em> is a direct participant who pulled the trigger, the photographer is a witness whose guilt comes from documenting suffering rather than preventing it. Duffy&rsquo;s use of the accusatory pronoun &lsquo;they&rsquo; in &ldquo;they do not care&rdquo; adds anger to his isolation &mdash; he is alienated not just by his memories but by a public that refuses to engage with the reality of conflict.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n29">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>1. Writing two separate essays:</strong> The biggest mistake is writing about Poem 1 for half the essay, then Poem 2 for the other half. You must compare <em>throughout</em> &mdash; every paragraph should reference both poems.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>2. Feature-spotting without analysis:</strong> Don&rsquo;t just say &ldquo;the poet uses a metaphor.&rdquo; Explain <em>what</em> the metaphor means, <em>why</em> the poet chose it, and <em>how</em> it affects the reader.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>3. Ignoring context:</strong> AO3 marks require you to link the poem to when and why it was written. For example, Armitage based <em>Remains</em> on real soldier testimonies; Duffy was friends with war photographers.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>4. Long quotations:</strong> Keep quotes short (3&ndash;6 words). Embed them in your sentences rather than writing out whole lines. Short quotes show you can identify the most important words.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">In the exam, <strong>plan for 5 minutes, write for 35 minutes, check for 5 minutes.</strong> Aim for 3&ndash;4 SEAS paragraphs, each comparing both poems. Start each paragraph with a clear statement linked to the question. Use the poet&rsquo;s surname (not first name): &lsquo;Armitage presents&rsquo; not &lsquo;Simon presents.&rsquo; Always &ldquo;parrot the question&rdquo; &mdash; repeat its key words in your answer.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">Paper 2 Section B (30 marks, 45 minutes) requires a comparative essay on a printed poem plus one of your choice &mdash; compare methods and effects throughout, never write two separate essays.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Use the SEAS structure (Statement, Example, Analysis, Speculation/Comparison) in every paragraph to hit AO1, AO2, AO3, and AO4.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Know the key themes (Power of Nature, Effects of Conflict, Abuse of Power, Identity, Loss) and which poems pair well together so you can make a confident choice under exam pressure.</li></ul>
