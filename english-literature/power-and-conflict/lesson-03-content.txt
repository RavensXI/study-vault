# The Charge of the Light Brigade & Bayonet Charge

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Charge of the Light Brigade &mdash; Alfred, Lord Tennyson</h2>
<h2 data-narration-id="n2">Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1809&ndash;1892) was Poet Laureate during the Victorian era. He wrote &ldquo;The Charge of the Light Brigade&rdquo; in December 1854, shortly after reading a newspaper report about the Battle of Balaclava during the <dfn class="term" data-def="A war fought between Russia and an alliance including Britain, France and the Ottoman Empire from 1853 to 1856, largely in the Crimean Peninsula (modern-day Ukraine).">Crimean War</dfn>. On 25 October 1854, a miscommunicated order sent 673 cavalrymen of the Light Brigade charging into a valley surrounded by Russian cannon on three sides. Armed only with swords against guns, between 100 and 200 soldiers were killed in a charge lasting barely 20 minutes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4">The poem is not about the military blunder that caused the charge &mdash; it is a tribute to the soldiers&rsquo; <dfn class="term" data-def="The courage and willingness to sacrifice for one's country or a cause.">patriotic</dfn> courage and obedience in the face of certain death.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">&ldquo;<strong>Half a league, half a league, / Half a league onward</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The repetition creates a galloping rhythm that mimics the horses&rsquo; hooves thundering forward. A league is about three miles, so half a league is roughly 1.7 miles &mdash; a short but deadly distance. The relentless repetition conveys the unstoppable momentum of the charge; once begun, there is no turning back.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">&ldquo;<strong>Into the valley of Death / Rode the six hundred</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech in which one thing is said to be another, suggesting a comparison without using 'like' or 'as'.">metaphor</dfn> &ldquo;valley of Death&rdquo; is a biblical reference (Psalm 23), elevating the soldiers&rsquo; sacrifice to something sacred and heroic. The refrain &ldquo;Rode the six hundred&rdquo; is repeated at the end of almost every stanza, constantly reminding the reader of the large number of men involved &mdash; and how many were lost.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n8">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Theirs not to make reply, / Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die&rdquo; &mdash; These three rhyming lines sum up military obedience. The soldiers knew &ldquo;someone had blundered&rdquo; (the order was a mistake) but followed it anyway because that was their duty. Tennyson admires this unquestioning loyalty.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n9">&ldquo;<strong>Cannon to right of them, / Cannon to left of them, / Cannon in front of them</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The repetition and listing of &ldquo;cannon&rdquo; surrounds the soldiers textually, just as the Russian guns surrounded them physically. The reader feels the claustrophobic, inescapable danger from all directions.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">&ldquo;<strong>Into the jaws of Death, / Into the mouth of Hell</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The <dfn class="term" data-def="Giving human or living qualities to something non-human.">personification</dfn> of Death and Hell as monsters with jaws and mouths creates a terrifying image of the battlefield as something that devours soldiers. The capitalisation of &ldquo;Death&rdquo; and &ldquo;Hell&rdquo; gives them the status of proper nouns &mdash; real, powerful entities.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Structure &amp; Form of Charge</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The poem has six stanzas of varying length, creating an irregular structure that mirrors the chaos of battle. The dactylic metre (one stressed syllable followed by two unstressed: DUM-da-da) mimics the rhythm of galloping horses. The stanzas follow the chronology of the charge: riding in (stanzas 1&ndash;3), fighting (stanza 4), retreating (stanza 5), and the tribute (stanza 6). The change from &ldquo;Rode the six hundred&rdquo; to &ldquo;Not the six hundred&rdquo; and then &ldquo;Left of six hundred&rdquo; tracks the diminishing number of survivors.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Bayonet Charge &mdash; Ted Hughes</h2>
<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Ted Hughes (1930&ndash;1998) wrote &ldquo;Bayonet Charge&rdquo; in 1957, drawing on the experiences of his father, who fought in World War I. Unlike Tennyson, Hughes was not writing to honour soldiers but to expose the raw, terrifying reality of combat. The poem focuses on a single, unnamed soldier running across no-man&rsquo;s land, experiencing fear, confusion and the complete breakdown of <dfn class="term" data-def="Devotion to and love of one's country; loyalty to national ideals.">patriotism</dfn> under fire.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">&ldquo;<strong>Suddenly he awoke and was running &mdash; raw</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The poem begins <em>in medias res</em> (in the middle of the action). The adverb &ldquo;Suddenly&rdquo; jolts the reader into the chaos, and the adjective &ldquo;raw&rdquo; at the end of the line suggests the soldier is exposed, vulnerable and unprepared &mdash; like an open wound.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">&ldquo;<strong>Bullets smacking the belly out of the air</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The violent verb &ldquo;smacking&rdquo; has connotations of physical pain and aggression. The personification of the air having a &ldquo;belly&rdquo; makes the bullets feel even more destructive &mdash; they are ripping through the very atmosphere around the soldier.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;In bewilderment then he almost stopped&rdquo; &mdash; The powerful noun &ldquo;bewilderment&rdquo; shows the soldier&rsquo;s total confusion. &ldquo;Almost stopped&rdquo; is terrifying because stopping in no-man&rsquo;s land means becoming a sitting target. Hughes shows that the reality of battle is not noble or glorious but confusing and paralysing.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n21">&ldquo;<strong>In what cold clockwork of the stars and the nations / Was he the hand pointing that second?</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The metaphor &ldquo;cold clockwork&rdquo; presents war as a mechanical, unfeeling machine in which individual soldiers are just cogs. The rhetorical question shows the soldier questioning why he is there &mdash; patriotic ideals have been replaced by existential doubt.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n22">&ldquo;<strong>A yellow hare that rolled like a flame / And crawled in a threshing circle</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The hare is an innocent animal caught in the violence of the battlefield, symbolising the destruction of innocence in war. The simile &ldquo;like a flame&rdquo; suggests its fur is burning, while &ldquo;threshing circle&rdquo; implies mechanical violence &mdash; threshing is the agricultural process of beating grain, but here it describes the hare&rsquo;s agonised, repetitive death throes. The hare shocks the soldier back into survival mode.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n23">&ldquo;<strong>King, honour, human dignity, etcetera / Dropped like luxuries</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; Hughes lists all the patriotic ideals that are supposed to motivate soldiers, then dismisses them with the contemptuous word &ldquo;etcetera.&rdquo; The simile &ldquo;dropped like luxuries&rdquo; suggests that in the terror of combat, noble ideas are the first things to go &mdash; they are unnecessary extras when survival is all that matters.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Comparing Both Poems</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Attitude to war:</strong> Tennyson glorifies war and admires the soldiers&rsquo; obedience and sacrifice. Hughes strips away all glory, presenting war as terrifying, confusing and dehumanising. Tennyson focuses on the group (&ldquo;the six hundred&rdquo;); Hughes focuses on one unnamed, isolated soldier.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Methods:</strong> Tennyson uses galloping rhythm, repetition and personification of Death to create an exciting, dramatic tone. Hughes uses <em>in medias res</em>, violent imagery and enjambment to create a chaotic, breathless pace that mirrors the soldier&rsquo;s panic. Both poems show the physical danger of battle, but only Hughes shows the psychological collapse of the individual.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Context:</strong> Tennyson wrote as Poet Laureate shortly after the event &mdash; his role was partly to honour national heroes. Hughes wrote a century later, influenced by his father&rsquo;s traumatic WWI experiences and a society increasingly sceptical of glorifying war after two world wars.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n28">When comparing these two poems, focus on the <strong>contrast in attitudes to war</strong>. Tennyson celebrates duty and sacrifice; Hughes questions whether war is worth the suffering. A top-level response will analyse how the poets&rsquo; <strong>methods</strong> (rhythm, imagery, structure) reinforce these different attitudes. Always link your points to the poets&rsquo; contexts &mdash; Tennyson&rsquo;s role as Poet Laureate vs Hughes&rsquo;s anti-war perspective shaped by WWI&rsquo;s legacy.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n29">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n30">Tennyson uses galloping rhythm, repetition and biblical imagery to honour the bravery of the Light Brigade soldiers who obeyed a doomed order.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">Hughes uses violent imagery, <em>in medias res</em> and a single soldier&rsquo;s perspective to expose the terror and confusion of combat, stripping away patriotic ideals.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Together, the poems show how attitudes to war shifted from Victorian patriotic honour to modern anti-war realism.</li></ul>
