# Remains & War Photographer

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Remains &mdash; Simon Armitage</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A poet from Huddersfield who often writes about ordinary people and modern life, including the effects of war on soldiers.">Simon Armitage</dfn> wrote <em>Remains</em> based on the real testimony of a soldier named Guardsman Tromans, who served in Iraq. The poem describes a moment when three soldiers shoot a looter &mdash; and the guilt that haunts the speaker long after.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The poem opens with a casual, almost dismissive tone: &ldquo;On another occasion, we get sent out to tackle looters raiding a bank.&rdquo; The conversational voice makes it sound like a soldier retelling a story to a mate. Armitage uses this <dfn class="term" data-def="Informal, everyday language that imitates natural speech patterns.">colloquial</dfn> register deliberately &mdash; it mirrors how real soldiers speak and makes the horror feel more authentic.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The phrase &ldquo;probably armed, possibly not&rdquo; is repeated later in the poem. This <dfn class="term" data-def="When a word or phrase is used more than once for emphasis or to highlight a key idea.">repetition</dfn> emphasises the speaker&rsquo;s doubt and guilt &mdash; he can never be sure whether the man he killed was actually dangerous.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Key Techniques in Remains</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Armitage uses violent imagery to show the horror of the shooting: &ldquo;I see every round as it rips through his life.&rdquo; The verb &lsquo;rips&rsquo; has connotations of destruction and suggests the fragility of human life. The metaphor &ldquo;rips through his life&rdquo; implies not just physical death but the total erasure of a person.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">After the shooting, a fellow soldier &ldquo;tosses his guts back into his body&rdquo; before the looter is &ldquo;carted off in the back of a lorry.&rdquo; The cold, unfeeling verbs &lsquo;tosses&rsquo; and &lsquo;carted&rsquo; show how the other soldiers treat the dead man as inconsequential &mdash; but the speaker cannot detach himself in the same way.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">The &ldquo;blood-shadow&rdquo; that &ldquo;stays on the street&rdquo; acts as a visual metaphor. The compound noun fuses blood with shadow, suggesting the memory has become inseparable from the speaker &mdash; like a shadow that follows him everywhere, it cannot be washed away.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">War Photographer &mdash; Carol Ann Duffy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><dfn class="term" data-def="A Scottish poet who served as the UK Poet Laureate from 2009 to 2019, known for giving voices to overlooked perspectives.">Carol Ann Duffy</dfn> wrote <em>War Photographer</em> based on her friendship with real war photographers. The poem follows a photographer developing his pictures in a darkroom, remembering the suffering he witnessed abroad.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The opening stanza establishes a sombre, ritualistic mood: &ldquo;In his darkroom he is finally alone with spools of suffering set out in ordered rows.&rdquo; The <dfn class="term" data-def="Repetition of the &lsquo;s&rsquo; sound, often creating a hushed, sinister, or soothing effect.">sibilance</dfn> in &lsquo;spools of suffering set&rsquo; creates a hushed, almost reverential atmosphere. Duffy compares the photographer to &ldquo;a priest preparing to intone a Mass&rdquo; &mdash; suggesting his work is sacred and solemn.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Belfast. Beirut. Phnom Penh. All flesh is grass.&rdquo; The three place names are separated by full stops (<dfn class="term" data-def="The use of full stops to separate individual words or short phrases within a line, creating pauses and emphasis.">caesura</dfn>), forcing the reader to pause on each war zone. The biblical allusion &ldquo;All flesh is grass&rdquo; (Isaiah 40:6) reminds us that human life is brief and fragile.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Key Techniques in War Photographer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Duffy structures the poem in four equal stanzas of six lines with a regular ABBCDD <dfn class="term" data-def="The pattern of rhyming words at the end of lines in a poem.">rhyme scheme</dfn>. This rigid, controlled form contrasts with the chaos of conflict &mdash; it reflects both the &ldquo;ordered rows&rdquo; of photographs and the photographer&rsquo;s attempt to impose calm on traumatic memories.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The contrast between home and war zone is central. &ldquo;Rural England&rdquo; represents &ldquo;ordinary pain which simple weather can dispel&rdquo; &mdash; problems in England are trivial compared to &ldquo;fields which don&rsquo;t explode beneath the feet of running children in a nightmare heat.&rdquo; This juxtaposition highlights the photographer&rsquo;s alienation: he exists between two worlds but belongs to neither.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n18">Duffy criticises the British public&rsquo;s shallow response to suffering. The editor will &ldquo;pick out five or six&rdquo; images from &ldquo;a hundred agonies in black-and-white&rdquo; &mdash; reducing vast human suffering to a handful of pictures for a &ldquo;Sunday&rsquo;s supplement.&rdquo; Readers&rsquo; &ldquo;eyeballs prick with tears between the bath and pre-lunch beers&rdquo; &mdash; any sadness is fleeting and squeezed between comfortable routines.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19">The poem ends with the photographer on an aeroplane, staring &ldquo;impassively at where he earns his living and they do not care.&rdquo; The pronoun &lsquo;they&rsquo; is accusatory &mdash; it separates the photographer from the public and suggests anger and isolation. The cyclical structure (returning to the war zone) implies nothing changes and the cycle of suffering continues.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Comparing Remains and War Photographer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Both poems explore how conflict traumatises those who witness it, even after they return home. In <em>Remains</em>, the soldier tries &ldquo;drink and drugs&rdquo; to forget but fails. In <em>War Photographer</em>, the photographer&rsquo;s hands &ldquo;did not tremble then though seem to now&rdquo; &mdash; the trauma surfaces when he is safe, not when he is in danger.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">However, the speakers have different relationships to the violence. The soldier in <em>Remains</em> was a direct participant: he pulled the trigger and feels overwhelming guilt (&ldquo;his bloody life in my bloody hands&rdquo;). The photographer was a witness and documenter &mdash; his moral conflict comes from photographing suffering rather than helping. Both are powerless against the psychological scars that follow them home.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n23">When comparing these poems, focus on <strong>how</strong> the poets present psychological damage differently. Armitage uses colloquial, conversational language to make PTSD feel raw and real. Duffy uses formal, controlled stanzas to mirror the photographer&rsquo;s attempt to impose order on chaos. Always link your quotes to the question &mdash; don&rsquo;t just describe techniques, explain their effect on the reader.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n24">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n25"><em>Remains</em> uses colloquial voice, repetition, and caesura to convey a soldier&rsquo;s guilt and PTSD &mdash; the memory of violence becomes inescapable.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n26"><em>War Photographer</em> uses a rigid rhyme scheme, religious imagery, and contrast between home and war zone to show a photographer&rsquo;s isolation and the public&rsquo;s indifference to suffering.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n27">Both poems show that the psychological effects of conflict last long after the violence ends, but the soldier&rsquo;s guilt as a participant differs from the photographer&rsquo;s moral conflict as a witness.</li></ul>
