# Exposure & Storm on the Island

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Exposure &mdash; Wilfred Owen</h2>
<h2 data-narration-id="n2">Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Wilfred Owen (1893&ndash;1918) is one of the most celebrated war poets. He served as an officer in World War I and was killed in action just one week before the war ended. &ldquo;Exposure&rdquo; was written in 1917&ndash;18, based on his experience of the brutal winter conditions in the trenches of northern France. Owen&rsquo;s poetry aimed to expose the true horror of war to a public that had been fed <dfn class="term" data-def="Government-produced information designed to promote a particular political viewpoint, often misleading or biased.">propaganda</dfn> about heroism and glory.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4">In this poem, the soldiers&rsquo; greatest enemy is not the German army but the weather itself. They wait in freezing trenches for an attack that never comes. The repeated refrain &ldquo;But nothing happens&rdquo; captures the agonising futility of their situation: they are slowly dying from cold and exhaustion, not enemy fire.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">&ldquo;<strong>Our brains ache, in the merciless iced east winds that knive us</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The opening line immediately establishes suffering. The <dfn class="term" data-def="Giving human or living qualities to something non-human.">personification</dfn> of the winds as &ldquo;merciless&rdquo; and the verb &ldquo;knive&rdquo; (meaning &ldquo;to stab&rdquo;) presents nature as a deliberate, violent attacker. The alliterative &ldquo;iced east&rdquo; creates a sharp, harsh sound that reinforces the biting cold. Nature is not passive background &mdash; it is an active, brutal enemy.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;But nothing happens&rdquo; &mdash; This refrain appears at the end of several stanzas and is the poem&rsquo;s most important line. The soldiers are waiting for an enemy attack, but none comes. Instead, they slowly succumb to the cold. The repetition creates a sense of futility, monotony and hopelessness &mdash; there is no dramatic climax, just a slow, agonising death by exposure.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n8">&ldquo;<strong>Pale flakes with fingering stealth come feeling for our faces</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The <dfn class="term" data-def="The repetition of consonant sounds at the beginning of nearby words, creating a particular effect.">alliteration</dfn> of &ldquo;f&rdquo; sounds creates a soft, sinister whisper &mdash; the snow approaches like an assassin, quietly and stealthily. The personification of snowflakes &ldquo;feeling for our faces&rdquo; makes the snow seem predatory, searching for victims. Owen presents nature not as grand or dramatic but as a silent killer.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9">&ldquo;<strong>Slowly our ghosts drag home</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The soldiers imagine returning home, but Owen calls them &ldquo;ghosts&rdquo; &mdash; a metaphor suggesting they are already dead, or feel dead inside. The verb &ldquo;drag&rdquo; implies exhaustion and reluctance, as if even the thought of home requires enormous effort. This also foreshadows that many will not survive to go home.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n10">When the soldiers imagine home, they picture &ldquo;shutters and doors, all closed.&rdquo; The repetition of &ldquo;closed&rdquo; creates a devastating image of rejection &mdash; they feel shut out by those at home, forgotten by the very society they are fighting to protect. &ldquo;All their eyes are ice&rdquo; uses a metaphor to suggest the people at home are cold, indifferent, perhaps even dead to the soldiers&rsquo; suffering. <p data-narration-id="n11">&ldquo;<strong>Tonight, this frost will fasten on this mud and us, / Shrivelling many hands, and puckering foreheads crisp</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The violent verbs &ldquo;fasten,&rdquo; &ldquo;shrivelling&rdquo; and &ldquo;puckering&rdquo; describe the frost literally killing the soldiers, freezing their bodies until they shrivel. The adjective &ldquo;crisp&rdquo; is disturbing because it is usually associated with food, dehumanising the soldiers&rsquo; frozen corpses.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Structure &amp; Form of Exposure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The poem has eight stanzas, each with five lines. Owen uses half-rhyme throughout (e.g. &ldquo;knive us&rdquo; / &ldquo;nervous,&rdquo; &ldquo;wire&rdquo; / &ldquo;war&rdquo;). Half-rhyme creates a persistent sense of unease and dissatisfaction &mdash; nothing feels complete or resolved, reflecting the soldiers&rsquo; endless, unresolved waiting. The refrain &ldquo;But nothing happens&rdquo; appears as a shortened final line in multiple stanzas, its brevity emphasising the anti-climax and futility.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Storm on the Island &mdash; Seamus Heaney</h2>
<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Seamus Heaney (1939&ndash;2013) was a Northern Irish poet and Nobel Prize winner. &ldquo;Storm on the Island&rdquo; was published in 1966 in his first collection, <em>Death of a Naturalist</em>. On the surface, it describes a community on a remote island preparing for and enduring a violent storm. However, the poem can also be read as an <dfn class="term" data-def="A story, poem or image that can be interpreted to reveal a hidden meaning, usually a moral or political one.">allegory</dfn> for the political conflict in Northern Ireland (the Troubles), where communities lived under constant threat of violence. The first eight letters of &ldquo;Stormont&rdquo; (the seat of the Northern Irish government) are hidden in the title: <strong>STORM</strong>on<strong>T</strong>he<strong>I</strong>sland.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Language Analysis &mdash; Key Quotes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">&ldquo;<strong>We are prepared: we build our houses squat</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The poem opens with confidence. The first-person plural &ldquo;We&rdquo; suggests a collective community, and &ldquo;prepared&rdquo; implies they feel ready to face whatever comes. The colon introduces the evidence: practical, solid houses built &ldquo;squat&rdquo; (low and sturdy). Yet this confidence will be undermined as the storm arrives.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">&ldquo;<strong>Nor are there trees which might prove company</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The absence of trees is both practical (no windbreaks) and emotional. Trees would provide &ldquo;company&rdquo; &mdash; personified as friends or comforters. Their absence emphasises the islanders&rsquo; isolation and vulnerability. The land itself has been stripped bare by the very winds they now face.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Space is a salvo. We are bombarded by the empty air&rdquo; &mdash; Heaney uses military language (&ldquo;salvo,&rdquo; &ldquo;bombarded&rdquo;) to describe the storm. A salvo is a volley of gunfire, and &ldquo;bombarded&rdquo; means attacked with bombs. This military <dfn class="term" data-def="A group of words related to a particular theme or subject.">semantic field</dfn> transforms the storm from a weather event into a violent assault, linking it to the conflict in Northern Ireland.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n21">&ldquo;<strong>The flung spray hits / The very windows, spits like a tame cat / Turned savage</strong>&rdquo; &mdash; The simile compares the storm to a &ldquo;tame cat turned savage&rdquo; &mdash; something familiar and trusted that suddenly becomes dangerous. This could represent how communities in Northern Ireland saw neighbours turn violent during the Troubles. The violent verbs &ldquo;hits&rdquo; and &ldquo;spits&rdquo; create a sense of aggression and unpredictability.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n22">The poem&rsquo;s final line is paradoxical &mdash; the oxymoron &ldquo;huge nothing&rdquo; describes the wind, which is invisible yet powerful. The islanders cannot see or fight their enemy; they can only endure it. On an allegorical level, this reflects the fear of living through the Troubles &mdash; the constant, invisible threat of violence that could erupt at any moment. Compare this with Owen&rsquo;s &ldquo;But nothing happens&rdquo; &mdash; both poems end with &ldquo;nothing,&rdquo; but where Owen&rsquo;s nothing is passive futility, Heaney&rsquo;s nothing is an active, terrifying force.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Structure &amp; Form of Storm on the Island</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">The poem is a single 19-line stanza written in <dfn class="term" data-def="Unrhymed poetry that follows a regular metre (usually iambic pentameter), giving it a natural, speech-like quality.">blank verse</dfn> (unrhymed iambic pentameter). The continuous single stanza mirrors the relentless, unbroken force of the storm &mdash; there is no shelter or pause. The conversational tone (&ldquo;you know what I mean&rdquo;) draws the reader in, but gradually gives way to fear and uncertainty as the storm&rsquo;s power becomes apparent.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Comparing Both Poems</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Shared theme &mdash; nature&rsquo;s power over humanity:</strong> Both Owen and Heaney present nature as an overwhelming force that humans cannot defeat. Owen&rsquo;s soldiers are slowly killed by frost and wind; Heaney&rsquo;s islanders are &ldquo;bombarded by the empty air.&rdquo; In both poems, nature is personified as an attacker &mdash; Owen&rsquo;s winds &ldquo;knive&rdquo; the soldiers, Heaney&rsquo;s spray &ldquo;spits.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Key difference:</strong> Owen&rsquo;s poem is explicitly about war &mdash; the soldiers are real, the suffering is physical and fatal. Heaney&rsquo;s poem uses the storm as a metaphor for political conflict. Owen&rsquo;s ending (&ldquo;But nothing happens&rdquo;) expresses futility and hopelessness; Heaney&rsquo;s ending (&ldquo;It is a huge nothing that we fear&rdquo;) expresses fear of the unknown.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Compare with Ozymandias:</strong> All three poems show nature as more powerful than humanity. In Ozymandias, nature quietly erodes over centuries; in Exposure, it kills in hours; in Storm on the Island, it attacks in violent bursts.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n29">When writing about Exposure, always comment on the <strong>half-rhyme</strong> &mdash; it is one of Owen&rsquo;s most distinctive techniques and shows the examiner you understand form and structure. Explain that half-rhyme creates unease and dissatisfaction, mirroring the soldiers&rsquo; unresolved suffering. For Storm on the Island, don&rsquo;t forget the allegorical reading &mdash; examiners reward students who can discuss the poem on <strong>both a literal and metaphorical level</strong> (the storm and the Troubles).</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n30">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n31">Owen uses personification, half-rhyme and the refrain &ldquo;But nothing happens&rdquo; to present the trenches as a place of futile, agonising suffering where nature is the real enemy.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Heaney uses military imagery and the oxymoron &ldquo;huge nothing&rdquo; to transform a storm into an allegory for the political fear of the Northern Irish Troubles.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">Both poems show nature dominating humanity, but Owen&rsquo;s focus is physical suffering and death, while Heaney&rsquo;s is psychological fear of an invisible, unpredictable threat.</li></ul>
