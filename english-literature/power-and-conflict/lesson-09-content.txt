# The Prelude — Power of Nature

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Context: William Wordsworth and the Romantic Movement</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="An English Romantic poet (1770&ndash;1850) who celebrated nature, emotion, and personal experience in his poetry.">William Wordsworth</dfn> was one of the most important poets of the <dfn class="term" data-def="A literary movement (late 18th&ndash;early 19th century) that valued emotion, nature, individualism, and the power of the imagination over reason and industry.">Romantic</dfn> period. He grew up in the Lake District and believed that nature was a powerful, almost spiritual force that could teach, humble, and transform human beings. <em>The Prelude</em> is Wordsworth&rsquo;s autobiographical poem &mdash; it traces his intellectual and emotional development from childhood to adulthood.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The extract studied for AQA Power and Conflict comes from Book One of <em>The Prelude</em> (1799 version) and describes a night when the young Wordsworth stole a rowing boat on a lake, only to be terrified by the looming presence of a mountain. The experience permanently changed his relationship with nature.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">The Beautiful: Confidence on the Lake</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">The extract begins on a summer evening. The young Wordsworth finds a boat &ldquo;tied to a willow tree&rdquo; and takes it out on the lake. The language is calm and beautiful: he describes rowing in &ldquo;an act of stealth and troubled pleasure.&rdquo; The oxymoron &lsquo;troubled pleasure&rsquo; hints at guilt &mdash; he knows stealing the boat is wrong, but the excitement of the adventure overpowers his conscience.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">As he rows, the boat moves &ldquo;like a swan&rdquo; &mdash; a graceful <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech comparing two things using &lsquo;like&rsquo; or &lsquo;as&rsquo;.">simile</dfn> that reflects the beauty and tranquillity of the scene. The rhythm is smooth and controlled. The boy is confident, at ease with nature, enjoying his power over the water. He fixes his gaze on a ridge &mdash; &ldquo;the horizon&rsquo;s utmost boundary&rdquo; &mdash; and rows steadily towards it.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Wordsworth uses first-person narration throughout. This makes the experience intensely personal and draws the reader into his psychological journey. The poem is not just about nature &mdash; it is about how nature changes the way we think and feel.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">The Sublime: The Mountain Appears</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A concept from Romantic philosophy describing an experience of awe, terror, and wonder in the face of something vast and powerful, especially in nature.">sublime</dfn> was a key concept for Romantic poets. It described an experience that was simultaneously beautiful and terrifying &mdash; the overwhelming awe a person feels when confronted with nature&rsquo;s immense power. This is exactly what happens to Wordsworth on the lake.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">As the boy rows further, a mountain suddenly rises into view: &ldquo;a huge peak, black and huge, as if with voluntary purpose.&rdquo; The repetition of &lsquo;huge&rsquo; emphasises the mountain&rsquo;s overwhelming size. The <dfn class="term" data-def="Giving human qualities or characteristics to something non-human.">personification</dfn> &mdash; &ldquo;as if with voluntary purpose&rdquo; &mdash; makes the mountain seem alive and intentional, as though it has chosen to confront the boy.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n12">The boy&rsquo;s confidence collapses. He turns the boat around &ldquo;with trembling oars&rdquo; and rows back to shore. <h2 data-narration-id="n13">The Volta: A Changed Perspective</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A turning point or shift in a poem where the tone, mood, or argument changes direction.">volta</dfn> (turning point) in this extract is the moment the mountain appears. Before it, the language is calm, beautiful, and controlled. After it, the language becomes dark and disturbed.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">The impact on Wordsworth is profound and lasting. For &ldquo;many days&rdquo; after, his brain &ldquo;worked with a dim and undetermined sense of unknown modes of being.&rdquo; The abstract language &mdash; &lsquo;dim,&rsquo; &lsquo;undetermined,&rsquo; &lsquo;unknown&rsquo; &mdash; shows that the boy cannot fully understand or articulate what he has experienced. Nature has revealed something beyond human comprehension.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">&ldquo;No familiar shapes remained, no pleasant images of trees, of sea, or sky, no colours of green, or blue, or silver.&rdquo; The repetition of &lsquo;no&rsquo; is devastating. The beautiful natural world he once enjoyed has been replaced by &ldquo;huge and mighty forms, that do not live like living men.&rdquo; Nature is no longer a source of pleasure &mdash; it is a terrifying, incomprehensible power.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Huge and mighty forms, that do not live like living men, moved slowly through the mind by day, and were a trouble to my dreams.&rdquo; The mountain haunts the boy even in sleep. This echoes the PTSD-like trauma seen in <em>Remains</em> &mdash; both speakers are psychologically changed by their encounters with overwhelming power. The difference is that Wordsworth sees this as a necessary lesson from nature.</p>
</div>

    <p data-narration-id="n18">The extract is written in <dfn class="term" data-def="Unrhymed lines of iambic pentameter (ten syllables per line, with alternating unstressed and stressed beats).">blank verse</dfn> &mdash; unrhymed iambic pentameter. This was the traditional form for epic and philosophical poetry, giving the extract a grand, serious tone. The lack of rhyme also reflects the natural, conversational quality of Wordsworth&rsquo;s voice &mdash; a hallmark of Romantic poetry.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19">Enjambment is used extensively, creating long, flowing sentences that build momentum as the boy rows and then accelerate as the mountain appears. The absence of stanza breaks mirrors the continuous, overwhelming nature of the experience.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Connections to the Anthology</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><em>The Prelude</em> connects powerfully to other poems in the anthology. It shares the theme of nature&rsquo;s power with <em>Storm on the Island</em> (Heaney) and <em>Exposure</em> (Owen) &mdash; in all three, humans are humbled or overwhelmed by natural forces. It also connects to <em>Ozymandias</em> (Shelley), where nature outlasts human power and pride. The psychological aftermath &mdash; &ldquo;a trouble to my dreams&rdquo; &mdash; echoes the haunting memories in <em>Remains</em> (Armitage) and <em>War Photographer</em> (Duffy), though Wordsworth sees his encounter as transformative rather than purely destructive.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n22">When writing about <em>The Prelude</em>, always discuss the <strong>shift in tone</strong> (the volta). The examiner wants to see that you understand how the poem moves from beauty to terror. Use the term &lsquo;sublime&rsquo; &mdash; it shows you understand the Romantic context. If comparing with another poem, focus on how both poets present nature as a force that overwhelms or changes human beings.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n23">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n24">The extract from <em>The Prelude</em> describes Wordsworth stealing a boat and being terrified by a mountain &mdash; the experience transforms his understanding of nature from beautiful to sublime.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25">The volta marks a dramatic shift: calm, beautiful language is replaced by dark, disturbing imagery as the mountain appears personified and threatening.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n26">Wordsworth uses blank verse, enjambment, and personification to convey the mountain&rsquo;s power and the lasting psychological impact (&ldquo;a trouble to my dreams&rdquo;) of encountering the sublime.</li></ul>
