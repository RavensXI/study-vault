# Comparing Two Unseen Poems

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Understanding Question 2</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">After the 24-mark single-poem analysis, the exam gives you a second unseen poem and asks you to <dfn class="term" data-def="Identifying and analysing the similarities and differences between two poems in terms of their methods, themes, and effects on the reader.">compare</dfn> the two poems. Question 2 is worth 8 marks and should take about 15 minutes: 5 minutes reading and annotating the new poem, and 10 minutes writing one detailed comparison paragraph.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Many students lose marks on this question by writing about each poem separately. The examiner wants a <strong>genuinely comparative</strong> response &mdash; one where you move back and forth between the poems, drawing connections and contrasts in the same sentence or paragraph.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 2 is marked out of 8 on three levels. Level 3 (7&ndash;8 marks) requires you to &ldquo;identify, explore and compare how the poets use form, language and/or structure&rdquo; and &ldquo;compare how the poets use these techniques to affect, manipulate or influence the reader.&rdquo; The key word is <strong>compare</strong> &mdash; not analyse separately.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The PEECPEED Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">For the 8-mark comparison, use the <dfn class="term" data-def="A comparative paragraph structure: Point, Evidence, Explore (Poem 1), Connective, Point, Evidence, Explore (Poem 2), Develop &mdash; designed for linking two texts.">PEECPEED</dfn> framework. This structure keeps you focused on comparison throughout:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>P</strong> &mdash; Make a point about Poem 1 that answers the question. <strong>E</strong> &mdash; Provide evidence (an embedded quotation from Poem 1). <strong>E</strong> &mdash; Explore the technique and its effect. <strong>C</strong> &mdash; Use a <dfn class="term" data-def="Words or phrases that signal comparison between texts &mdash; showing similarities (similarly, likewise, correspondingly) or differences (however, conversely, in contrast, whereas).">comparison connective</dfn> to link to Poem 2. <strong>P</strong> &mdash; Make a connected point about Poem 2. <strong>E</strong> &mdash; Provide evidence from Poem 2. <strong>E</strong> &mdash; Explore the technique and its effect. <strong>D</strong> &mdash; Develop by reflecting on what the comparison reveals about the poets&rsquo; different attitudes or purposes.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">What to Compare</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">The question will usually tell you the <dfn class="term" data-def="The central ideas, subjects, or messages explored in a poem &mdash; such as love, death, nature, conflict, identity, power, or loss.">theme</dfn> or focus for comparison (e.g. &ldquo;feelings towards loss and death&rdquo;). Within that theme, you should compare three things:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>1. Methods:</strong> How do the poets use language and/or structure differently? One poet may use personification while the other uses an extended metaphor. One may use regular rhyme while the other uses free verse. <strong>2. Attitudes:</strong> Do the poets feel the same way about the theme, or differently? One may accept death peacefully while the other resists it. <strong>3. Effects on the reader:</strong> Do the poems make you feel the same emotion, or contrasting ones? One may create sadness while the other creates unease.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>You only have 10 minutes to write, so aim for <strong>one detailed comparison paragraph</strong>. A single well-developed PEECPEED paragraph with embedded quotations from both poems, specific technique analysis, and clear comparison connectives will score higher than two separate analyses of each poem.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Worked Example: Comparing Two Poems About Loss</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Consider this question: <em>&ldquo;Both poems discuss nature, loss, and death. Compare the ways that poets present their feelings towards loss and death in &lsquo;Pictures of Memory&rsquo; and &lsquo;The Last Rose of Summer.&rsquo;&rdquo;</em> [8 Marks]</p>

    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n15">In &lsquo;Pictures of Memory,&rsquo; Cary uses metaphor to present strong feelings of positivity towards death. The &ldquo;gates of light&rdquo; metaphor could be alluding to the gates of heaven, suggesting the poet believes her brother is now at peace in a better place. The word &ldquo;light&rdquo; has warm, comforting connotations, indicating the speaker has reached a state of acceptance. This is further supported by references to nature throughout, revealing a tone of peace and purity. <strong>Similarly</strong>, in &lsquo;The Last Rose of Summer,&rsquo; Moore also uses metaphor to convey strong feelings towards death; <strong>however</strong>, these feelings are less positive and accepting. The &ldquo;last rose of Summer&rdquo; symbolises an elderly person waiting to die long after their friends have passed. &ldquo;Summer&rdquo; symbolises beauty and vitality, while &ldquo;rose&rdquo; has connotations of love and romance. Yet the semantic field of loss in &ldquo;last&hellip; alone&hellip; gone&hellip; pine&rdquo; outlines the narrator&rsquo;s negative emotions. <strong>Unlike</strong> Cary, Moore presents someone struggling to come to terms with loss rather than finding peace in it.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n16">Notice how the model paragraph moves between the two poems using connectives (&ldquo;Similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;however,&rdquo; &ldquo;Unlike&rdquo;). It identifies a shared method (metaphor) but explores how the poets use it differently to create contrasting attitudes.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n18">In the 5 minutes you have for reading and annotating the second poem, use a quick comparison grid:</p>

Under each poem, jot down: (1) a key quotation, (2) the main technique, (3) the tone/attitude. In the middle column, note whether these are similar or different. This takes 2&ndash;3 minutes and gives you a clear plan for your paragraph. For example: Poem 1: &ldquo;gates of light&rdquo; / metaphor / accepting of death. Poem 2: &ldquo;last rose of Summer&rdquo; / metaphor / struggling with loss. Middle: Both use metaphor but contrasting attitudes.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Common Comparison Mistakes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Writing about poems separately:</strong> The most common mistake. If you write one paragraph about Poem 1 and another about Poem 2, you are <em>analysing</em>, not <em>comparing</em>. The examiner will cap your mark at Level 1 (1&ndash;2 marks). <strong>Comparing content instead of methods:</strong> Saying &ldquo;both poems are about death&rdquo; is not analysis. You must compare <em>how</em> the poets present the theme &mdash; their techniques, word choices, structures, and effects. <strong>Ignoring the second poem:</strong> Some students spend too long on the first poem and barely reference the second. Aim for roughly equal coverage of both.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Finding Comparisons Quickly</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">When you first read the second poem, look for these quick comparison points: <strong>Tone:</strong> Is it the same or different from Poem 1? <strong>Imagery type:</strong> Do both poets use nature imagery, or does one use something else? <strong>Structure:</strong> Are the stanza patterns similar or contrasting? <strong>Speaker&rsquo;s attitude:</strong> Does the speaker in Poem 2 feel the same way as in Poem 1, or are they at a different emotional stage? <strong>Overall message:</strong> Do the poems reach the same conclusion about the theme?</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>You can compare both similarities AND differences in the same paragraph. In fact, this is the most effective approach: &ldquo;Both poets use [technique], <strong>however</strong> while Poet A uses it to suggest [X], Poet B uses it to create a contrasting sense of [Y].&rdquo; This single sentence is already a strong comparison.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Timing and Exam Strategy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Section C of Paper 2 gives you 45 minutes total for both questions. Use the time strategically: <strong>Question 1 (24 marks):</strong> 10 minutes annotating, 20 minutes writing. <strong>Question 2 (8 marks):</strong> 5 minutes annotating the second poem and planning your comparison, 10 minutes writing. Do not spend longer than 30 minutes on Question 1 &mdash; Question 2 is worth a third of Section C&rsquo;s marks and students routinely underperform on it simply by running out of time.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n27">For Question 2, the examiner is looking for <strong>genuine comparison</strong>, not two separate analyses. Every sentence should reference both poems or use a comparison connective. If your paragraph does not contain words like &ldquo;similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;however,&rdquo; &ldquo;whereas,&rdquo; or &ldquo;in contrast,&rdquo; you are probably not comparing. One well-structured PEECPEED paragraph with clear connectives, embedded quotations from both poems, and method-level analysis will comfortably reach Level 3 (7&ndash;8 marks).</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n28">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n29">Question 2 is worth 8 marks and demands genuine comparison &mdash; move between poems using connectives like &ldquo;similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;however,&rdquo; and &ldquo;in contrast.&rdquo;</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30">Compare methods (techniques), attitudes (what the poets think/feel), and effects (how the reader responds) &mdash; not just content or themes.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">Use the PEECPEED structure and a quick comparison grid to plan your paragraph in under 3 minutes, leaving 10 minutes for focused, comparative writing.</li></ul>
