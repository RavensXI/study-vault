# Reading & Analysing an Unseen Poem

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Unseen Poetry?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A poem you have never studied in class, given to you in Section C of Paper 2 for analysis under exam conditions.">Unseen poetry</dfn> is the final section of AQA English Literature Paper 2. You are given a poem you have probably never seen before and asked to analyse it. This section is worth 32 marks in total &mdash; 24 marks for Question 1 (analysing a single poem) and 8 marks for Question 2 (comparing two poems). That makes unseen poetry worth more marks than the 19th-century novel or the anthology poetry sections.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The good news is that unseen poetry is a <strong>skills-based</strong> section. You do not need to memorise quotations or revise specific texts. Instead, you need a reliable method for approaching any poem and writing a detailed, analytical response under timed conditions.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Section C is worth 32 marks out of 96 on Paper 2. Question 1 (24 marks) should take about 30 minutes: 10 minutes reading and annotating, 20 minutes writing. Question 2 (8 marks) should take about 15 minutes: 5 minutes reading and annotating, 10 minutes writing.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Six-Step Reading Method</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Many students panic when they see an unfamiliar poem. The following method gives you a clear, repeatable process that works on any poem. Follow these steps every time.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Step 1 &mdash; Read the question first.</strong> The question tells you what to look for. If it asks about &ldquo;feelings of sadness,&rdquo; you know to focus on tone, mood, and emotional language. The question is your compass.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Step 2 &mdash; Read the poem once for meaning.</strong> Do not worry about techniques yet. Just work out the basics: Who is speaking? What is happening? What is the subject of the poem? Highlight words and phrases that stand out to you.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Step 3 &mdash; Read again and label techniques.</strong> On your second reading, identify <dfn class="term" data-def="Methods a writer uses to create effects, including language devices (metaphor, simile, personification) and structural devices (enjambment, caesura, stanza breaks).">poetic techniques</dfn> such as metaphor, simile, personification, alliteration, and structural features like enjambment or caesura. Write the technique name next to the relevant line.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Step 4 &mdash; Read a third time and annotate effects.</strong> Now connect each technique to the question. Ask yourself: <em>Why</em> has the poet used this technique? What effect does it have on the reader? Write your ideas around the poem in a different colour.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Step 5 &mdash; Write your response.</strong> Aim for two detailed paragraphs for the 24-mark question. Each paragraph should follow a clear analytical structure (see PEEADEL below).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Step 6 &mdash; Proofread.</strong> Check your spelling (especially the poet&rsquo;s name and quoted words), quotation marks, and punctuation. Make sure you have covered all four mark scheme requirements.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme for unseen poetry rewards four things: (1) a clear focus on the question, (2) a range of quotations used in interpretations, (3) clear identification and exploration of the writer&rsquo;s techniques, and (4) exploration of the effect on the reader. Every paragraph you write should aim to hit all four.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">The PEEADEL Paragraph Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="A paragraph structure standing for Point, Evidence, Explain, Analyse, Develop, Effect, Link &mdash; used to write detailed analytical paragraphs about poetry.">PEEADEL</dfn> is a framework that helps you write detailed, mark-scheme-friendly paragraphs. Here is what each letter stands for:</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>P &mdash; Point:</strong> Answer the question directly by rewording it. Use a discourse marker (e.g. &ldquo;Furthermore,&rdquo; &ldquo;Significantly&rdquo;). <strong>E &mdash; Evidence:</strong> Support your point with an embedded quotation from the poem. <strong>E &mdash; Explain:</strong> Explain what the quotation means and how it links to the question. Use subject terminology. <strong>A &mdash; Analyse:</strong> Zoom in on a single word or phrase and explore its connotations in depth. <strong>D &mdash; Develop:</strong> Introduce a second quotation that reinforces your point. <h2 data-narration-id="n17">Worked Example: &lsquo;Spellbound&rsquo; by Emily Bront&euml;</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Let us apply the method to a real poem. Consider the opening lines of &lsquo;Spellbound&rsquo;: <em>&ldquo;The night is darkening round me, / The wild winds coldly blow; / But a tyrant spell has bound me / And I cannot, cannot go.&rdquo;</em></p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Step 1 (question):</strong> Imagine the question asks: &ldquo;How does Bront&euml; express feelings and attitudes towards nature?&rdquo; We know to look for how nature is presented and what emotions it evokes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Step 2 (meaning):</strong> The speaker is trapped somewhere in a wild storm at night. Despite the danger, something holds them in place &mdash; they &ldquo;cannot go.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Step 3 (techniques):</strong> We can spot personification (&ldquo;tyrant spell&rdquo;), repetition (&ldquo;cannot, cannot&rdquo;), and a semantic field of darkness and cold (&ldquo;darkening,&rdquo; &ldquo;coldly,&rdquo; &ldquo;wild&rdquo;).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Step 4 (effects):</strong> The <dfn class="term" data-def="A set of words within a text that are all related to the same topic or theme, creating a pattern of meaning.">semantic field</dfn> of darkness and hostility presents nature as threatening, yet the speaker is compelled to stay. The repetition of &ldquo;cannot&rdquo; emphasises helplessness and could suggest an internal conflict between fear and fascination.</p>

However, the choice of &ldquo;spell&rdquo; could also suggest enchantment and wonder, meaning the speaker may be captivated by nature&rsquo;s beauty even as they fear its power. This dual interpretation is reinforced by the repetition of &ldquo;cannot, cannot go,&rdquo; which emphasises the speaker&rsquo;s paralysis &mdash; caught between wanting to flee and being mesmerised. The reader feels a sense of unease but also admiration for the raw power of nature that Bront&euml; describes.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Poetry Is Subjective</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">One important principle to remember: poetry is <dfn class="term" data-def="Based on personal opinions, feelings, or interpretations rather than facts &mdash; in poetry analysis, multiple valid interpretations can coexist.">subjective</dfn>. Two readers can look at the same poem and reach completely different &mdash; but equally valid &mdash; interpretations. The examiner does not want one &ldquo;correct&rdquo; answer. They want to see that you can develop and justify your own ideas using evidence from the poem. Using modal verbs like &ldquo;could,&rdquo; &ldquo;may,&rdquo; and &ldquo;might&rdquo; shows the examiner that you understand multiple interpretations are possible.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Feature-spotting without analysis:</strong> Simply labelling techniques (&ldquo;this is a metaphor&rdquo;) earns no marks. You must explain <em>why</em> the technique has been used and its effect. <strong>Ignoring the question:</strong> Every sentence should link back to the question. If it asks about &ldquo;attitudes towards danger,&rdquo; every point must connect to danger. <strong>Retelling the poem:</strong> Do not summarise what happens. Instead, analyse how the poet creates meaning. <strong>Rushing annotation:</strong> The 10 minutes spent reading and annotating are the most important &mdash; they set up the quality of your written response.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n29">For the 24-mark question, always aim for two well-developed paragraphs rather than four thin ones. The examiner wants depth, not breadth. Spend time exploring the connotations of individual words and offering alternative interpretations using modal verbs. This is what separates Level 4 from Level 6 responses.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n30">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n31">Follow the six-step method: read the question, read for meaning, label techniques, annotate effects, write your response, and proofread.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">The mark scheme rewards four things: focus on the question, range of quotations, exploration of techniques, and effect on the reader &mdash; hit all four in every paragraph.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">Use modal verbs (could, may, might) to explore multiple interpretations &mdash; this is a top-level skill that moves you from Level 4 to Level 6.</li></ul>
