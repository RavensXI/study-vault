# Napoleon's Cult of Personality

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Napoleon&rsquo;s Cult of Personality</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">By Chapter Eight, Napoleon has transformed from a political leader into a figure of worship. He is no longer referred to simply as &lsquo;Napoleon&rsquo; but as &lsquo;our Leader, Comrade Napoleon.&rsquo; The pigs invent grandiose titles for him: &lsquo;Father of All Animals, Terror of Mankind, Protector of the Sheep-fold, Ducklings&rsquo; Friend.&rsquo; These <dfn class="term" data-def="Titles or descriptive phrases added to a person's name to glorify them, often used in propaganda.">epithets</dfn> are absurdly contradictory &mdash; he cannot be both &lsquo;Terror of Mankind&rsquo; and &lsquo;Ducklings&rsquo; Friend&rsquo; &mdash; and Orwell uses this to satirise how dictators construct false images of themselves.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Napoleon now rarely appears in public. When he does, he is &lsquo;attended not only by his retinue of dogs but by a black cockerel who marched in front of him and acted as a kind of trumpeter.&rsquo; He eats alone from the &lsquo;Crown Derby dinner service&rsquo; and has a gun fired on his birthday. Orwell builds a picture of a leader who has isolated himself from ordinary life &mdash; exactly the opposite of Old Major&rsquo;s vision of equality.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A <dfn class="term" data-def="Exaggerated adoration of a leader, often achieved through propaganda and media manipulation, designed to make the leader seem god-like.">cult of personality</dfn> is a common feature of <dfn class="term" data-def="A political system where the state holds total authority over society and controls every aspect of public and private life.">totalitarian</dfn> regimes. Napoleon&rsquo;s cult parallels Stalin&rsquo;s: portraits of Stalin were displayed in every Soviet building, cities were named after him, and he was credited with every national achievement.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Minimus&rsquo;s Poem &mdash; Propaganda as Art</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The pig Minimus composes a poem entitled &lsquo;Comrade Napoleon,&rsquo; which begins: &lsquo;Friend of fatherless! Fountain of happiness! Lord of the swill-bucket!&rsquo; The poem uses religious language (&lsquo;Thou art the giver of all that thy creatures love&rsquo;) and <dfn class="term" data-def="The treatment of a person as a god or divine being, often through excessive praise and worship.">deification</dfn> to elevate Napoleon to an almost god-like status. The line &lsquo;His first squeak should be &ldquo;Comrade Napoleon!&rdquo;&rsquo; suggests that loyalty to the leader should begin from birth.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Orwell&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="The use of humour, exaggeration, or ridicule to criticise and expose the failings of individuals, institutions, or society.">satire</dfn> is sharp here. The phrase &lsquo;Lord of the swill-bucket&rsquo; is deliberately bathetic &mdash; it deflates the grand, religious tone by reminding us that Napoleon is, after all, a pig eating from a trough. The poem is inscribed on the barn wall opposite the Seven Commandments, with a portrait of Napoleon painted by Squealer. This visual juxtaposition is powerful: Napoleon&rsquo;s image is literally placed above the rules that he has systematically broken.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n8">Stalin&rsquo;s cult of personality involved portraits in every home, statues in every city, and mandatory praise in all public speeches. Propaganda posters depicted him as a kindly father figure &mdash; &lsquo;Father of Nations&rsquo; &mdash; while he oversaw mass starvation and political murder. Similar cults were built around Mao Zedong in China, Kim Il-sung in North Korea, and Adolf Hitler in Nazi Germany. In each case, the leader used propaganda, art, and ritual to make themselves appear infallible and all-powerful.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9">Orwell understood that a cult of personality serves a specific purpose: it makes criticism of the leader feel like blasphemy. When Napoleon is presented as &lsquo;Father of All Animals,&rsquo; questioning him becomes an attack on everyone&rsquo;s father. This is why the animals give Napoleon credit for everything &mdash; even a hen remarks that &lsquo;under the guidance of our Leader, Comrade Napoleon, I have laid five eggs in six days.&rsquo;</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">The Battle of the Windmill</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">In Chapter Eight, Napoleon sells a stack of timber to Mr Frederick of Pinchfield Farm, only to discover that Frederick has paid with forged banknotes. Frederick then attacks Animal Farm with armed men who blow up the completed windmill with <dfn class="term" data-def="An explosive substance used in mining and demolition, here representing the destructive power of external enemies.">blasting powder</dfn>. Orwell writes that &lsquo;a mighty cry of vengeance went up&rsquo; from the animals, who fight back ferociously and eventually drive Frederick&rsquo;s men off the farm.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The destruction of the windmill &mdash; the product of years of backbreaking labour &mdash; is a devastating blow. Yet Napoleon immediately declares it a glorious victory: the &lsquo;Battle of the Windmill.&rsquo; He creates a new decoration, the &lsquo;Order of the Green Banner,&rsquo; which he awards to himself. Two days of celebrations follow. This is a masterclass in <dfn class="term" data-def="A deliberately misleading representation of events, used to control public perception.">spin</dfn>: turning a catastrophic failure into a propaganda triumph.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Frederick&rsquo;s betrayal and attack parallel <strong>Hitler&rsquo;s betrayal of the Nazi-Soviet Pact</strong> in 1941, when Germany invaded the Soviet Union despite their non-aggression treaty. Stalin, like Napoleon, had been deceived &mdash; and reframed the devastating early defeats as heroic Soviet resistance.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Orwell&rsquo;s Irony: The Pigs and Whisky</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">After the battle celebrations, the pigs discover a case of whisky in the farmhouse cellar. That night, &lsquo;the sound of loud singing&rsquo; comes from the farmhouse, and Napoleon is &lsquo;distinctly seen to emerge from the back door, gallop rapidly round the yard, and disappear indoors again&rsquo; &mdash; wearing Mr Jones&rsquo;s old bowler hat. The image of a pig drunkenly galloping in a human hat is both comic and deeply ironic: Napoleon is literally becoming the human tyrant he replaced.</p>

A &lsquo;cry of lamentation went up&rsquo; and the animals walk on tiptoe around the farmhouse. Napoleon&rsquo;s &lsquo;last act upon earth&rsquo; is to decree that drinking alcohol shall be punished by death. Of course, Napoleon is simply hungover. The Fifth Commandment is later changed to &lsquo;No animal shall drink alcohol <em>to excess</em>&rsquo; &mdash; another incremental erosion of the original principles. Orwell&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="A literary technique where the intended meaning is the opposite of what is literally stated, used to highlight contradictions or absurdity.">irony</dfn> here is exquisite: the pigs break a rule, then simply rewrite it to excuse their behaviour.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n19">Napoleon&rsquo;s cult of personality uses epithets, rituals, art (Minimus&rsquo;s poem), and isolation from ordinary animals to create an image of infallibility &mdash; paralleling Stalin, Mao, and other real-world dictators.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n20">The Battle of the Windmill shows Napoleon reframing catastrophic failure as glorious victory, mirroring Stalin&rsquo;s propaganda after Hitler&rsquo;s betrayal of the Nazi-Soviet Pact.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n21">Orwell uses irony and satire throughout Chapter Eight &mdash; Napoleon in Jones&rsquo;s bowler hat, &lsquo;Lord of the swill-bucket,&rsquo; the rewritten Fifth Commandment &mdash; to show the absurdity and hypocrisy of the pigs&rsquo; rule.</li></ul>
