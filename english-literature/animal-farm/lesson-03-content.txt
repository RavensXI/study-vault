# The Early Farm & Squealer's Propaganda

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Life After the Rebellion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Chapter 3 opens with the animals working harder than ever &mdash; but, crucially, working for themselves. Orwell writes that they &lsquo;toiled and sweated&rsquo; to bring in the harvest, yet the mood is optimistic. The farm is productive and the animals feel a sense of ownership. This brief period represents the early promise of the revolution, before corruption takes hold. However, Orwell immediately begins planting clues that this <dfn class="term" data-def="A perfect world, free of corruption and injustice.">utopia</dfn> will not last.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">From the very start of Chapter 3, inequality is visible. The pigs &lsquo;did not actually work, but directed and supervised the others.&rsquo; Orwell uses this parenthetical observation to show how intellectual superiority is being used to justify a new class system. The pigs position themselves as &lsquo;brainworkers&rsquo; whose management is essential, while animals like Boxer do all the physical labour. This mirrors how the Bolshevik leadership claimed special privileges because their intellectual work was vital to the state.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The pigs never do any physical work on the farm. Their role as organisers and supervisors mirrors the communist party officials who lived in luxury while ordinary Soviet citizens laboured in poverty.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Boxer: The Loyal Worker</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Boxer the carthorse emerges in Chapter 3 as the most dedicated worker on the farm. His personal motto &mdash; &lsquo;I will work harder&rsquo; &mdash; captures his absolute devotion to the cause. Orwell presents Boxer as both admirable and deeply vulnerable: his strength and loyalty are exploited by the pigs, who benefit from his labour without ever rewarding it. Boxer represents the <dfn class="term" data-def="The working class, especially in Marxist theory.">proletariat</dfn> &mdash; the loyal, hardworking ordinary people who are most exploited under totalitarian regimes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Benjamin the donkey provides a contrasting perspective. His cynical remark &mdash; &lsquo;Donkeys live a long time. None of you has ever seen a dead donkey&rsquo; &mdash; suggests he knows the revolution will not deliver on its promises. Benjamin represents those who see through political lies but do nothing to resist, making his silence a form of complicity.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">The Milk and Apples: Squealer&rsquo;s First Manipulation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">The milk that disappeared at the end of Chapter 2 is now mixed into the pigs&rsquo; mash. The windfall apples are also reserved exclusively for the pigs. When the other animals question this, Squealer is sent to explain. His speech in Chapter 3 is the first major example of <dfn class="term" data-def="To control or influence someone cleverly or unfairly.">manipulation</dfn> through language &mdash; and it establishes the template for how the pigs will control the farm throughout the novella.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Squealer&rsquo;s defence follows a clear pattern. First, he appeals to ethos: &lsquo;You do not imagine, I hope, that we pigs are doing this in a spirit of selfishness and privilege?&rsquo; The rhetorical question is structured to make disagreement seem unreasonable. Then he appeals to logos: &lsquo;Milk and apples (this has been proved by Science, comrades) contain substances absolutely necessary to the well-being of a pig.&rsquo; The parenthetical &lsquo;this has been proved by Science&rsquo; is a false appeal to authority &mdash; Squealer presents an unverifiable claim as established fact.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Squealer&rsquo;s most powerful weapon is fear. His speech always returns to the same threat: &lsquo;Jones would come back!&rsquo; This is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A person or group blamed for the wrongdoings of others.">scapegoat</dfn> tactic &mdash; by invoking the threat of the old regime, Squealer makes the animals accept any injustice as preferable to the return of Jones.</p>
</div>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13">Squealer employs several propaganda techniques in this speech. <strong>Fear</strong>: &lsquo;Jones would come back! Yes, Jones would come back!&rsquo; &mdash; the repetition intensifies the threat. <strong>False statistics</strong>: the claim that science proves pigs need apples is unverifiable. <strong>Euphemism</strong>: calling self-interest &lsquo;preserving our health&rsquo;. <strong>Appeal to authority</strong>: positioning the pigs as essential &lsquo;brainworkers&rsquo;. <strong>Emotional blackmail</strong>: &lsquo;It is for <em>your</em> sake that we drink that milk&rsquo; &mdash; the italics on &lsquo;your&rsquo; invert the truth, making the pigs&rsquo; greed seem like self-sacrifice. Orwell notes that &lsquo;it was agreed without further argument&rsquo; &mdash; the passive voice again hides who agreed, suggesting the animals are already losing their ability to challenge authority.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Squealer as Pravda</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Squealer represents the Soviet state media, particularly the newspaper <em>Pravda</em> (meaning &lsquo;truth&rsquo;). Just as <em>Pravda</em> presented Stalin&rsquo;s policies as beneficial and necessary, Squealer reframes the pigs&rsquo; greed as self-sacrifice for the common good. The irony is devastating: the character whose name suggests high-pitched noise and complaint is the one who silences all complaint. When Orwell describes him as being able to &lsquo;turn black into white&rsquo;, he captures the essence of propaganda &mdash; making the population believe the opposite of what is true.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>By Chapter 3, Orwell has established the three pillars of the pigs&rsquo; control: intellectual superiority (the pigs as &lsquo;brainworkers&rsquo;), propaganda (Squealer&rsquo;s speeches), and fear (&lsquo;Jones would come back&rsquo;). These mirror Stalin&rsquo;s use of the party elite, state media, and the threat of counter-revolution.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n17">When analysing Squealer&rsquo;s language, don&rsquo;t just identify techniques &mdash; explain their <em>effect</em> on the animals and their <em>purpose</em> in Orwell&rsquo;s wider argument. For example, don&rsquo;t just say &lsquo;Squealer uses a rhetorical question&rsquo; &mdash; explain that it is structured to make disagreement seem disloyal, which mirrors how totalitarian regimes frame dissent as treason. The best answers always connect language analysis to Orwell&rsquo;s political message and the real-world parallels with Soviet propaganda.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n19">Boxer&rsquo;s loyalty and motto (&lsquo;I will work harder&rsquo;) make him admirable but vulnerable &mdash; he represents the exploited working class whose dedication is rewarded with betrayal.</li><li data-narration-id="n20">Squealer&rsquo;s speech about the milk and apples establishes the propaganda template used throughout the novella: false logic, appeal to authority, and the fear of Jones&rsquo;s return.</li><li data-narration-id="n21">The simplification of the Seven Commandments to &lsquo;Four legs good, two legs bad&rsquo; shows how propaganda reduces complex ideas to controllable slogans.</li></ul>
