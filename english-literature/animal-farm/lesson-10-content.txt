# The Ending & the Moral

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Chapter Ten: The Passage of Time</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Chapter Ten opens with a time-jump. Years have passed. Many of the original animals who took part in the Rebellion are dead. Boxer, Snowball, Old Major, and Jones are all gone. Only Clover, Benjamin, Moses the raven, and &lsquo;a number of the pigs&rsquo; remain from the revolutionary generation. The farm has new animals who have never known anything other than Napoleon&rsquo;s rule &mdash; they accept it as normal.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Orwell&rsquo;s description of Napoleon and Squealer is pointed: both have grown enormously fat. Napoleon weighs &lsquo;twenty-four stone&rsquo; and Squealer is so obese his eyes are barely visible. Their physical transformation mirrors their <dfn class="term" data-def="A gradual decline in moral character, principles, or integrity, especially in those who hold power.">moral corruption</dfn> &mdash; they have literally fattened themselves on the labour of others. The farm now &lsquo;possessed&rsquo; three horses, a cart, and a plough &mdash; the word &lsquo;possessed&rsquo; is significant because it implies the animals are property once more.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The time-jump is a deliberate structural choice. Orwell skips forward to show the long-term consequences of the revolution&rsquo;s failure. The new generation of animals, who never knew Old Major&rsquo;s vision, accept tyranny as normal &mdash; Orwell warns that without historical memory, oppression becomes invisible.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Final Commandment</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The Seven Commandments have been progressively altered throughout the novel. Now, in Chapter Ten, they are replaced entirely by a single commandment painted on the barn wall in enormous letters:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>&lsquo;ALL ANIMALS ARE EQUAL BUT SOME ANIMALS ARE MORE EQUAL THAN OTHERS.&rsquo;</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">This <dfn class="term" data-def="A statement that contains two contradictory ideas, creating an apparent absurdity that reveals a deeper truth.">paradox</dfn> is the novel&rsquo;s most famous line. It is a logical impossibility &mdash; if some are &lsquo;more equal,&rsquo; then none are truly equal. Orwell uses this contradiction to expose the fundamental lie at the heart of the pigs&rsquo; rule: they have claimed to uphold equality while systematically destroying it. The commandment also serves as Orwell&rsquo;s critique of the Soviet Union, where the Communist Party&rsquo;s promise of a classless society became a justification for a new ruling elite.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">The Pigs Become Humans</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">The pigs now walk on their hind legs. Napoleon emerges from the farmhouse carrying a whip &mdash; the very symbol of Jones&rsquo;s human <dfn class="term" data-def="Prolonged cruel or unjust treatment or control, especially by those in power.">oppression</dfn> that the animals rebelled against in Chapter Two. The sheep, retrained by Squealer, now bleat &lsquo;Four legs good, two legs <em>better</em>&rsquo; &mdash; a complete inversion of the original slogan. The animals who remember the old days are too few, too old, and too afraid to object.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">In the novel&rsquo;s climactic scene, Napoleon invites neighbouring farmers &mdash; Mr Pilkington of Foxwood &mdash; to dinner at the farmhouse. The animals peer through the window and watch pigs and humans playing cards, drinking, and laughing together. Pilkington congratulates Napoleon on running a farm where &lsquo;the lower animals did more work and received less food than any animals in the county.&rsquo; Napoleon announces that &lsquo;Animal Farm&rsquo; will henceforth be known simply as &lsquo;the Manor Farm&rsquo; &mdash; its original name under Jones.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The renaming of Animal Farm back to Manor Farm is the final symbol of the revolution&rsquo;s complete failure. The word &lsquo;<dfn class="term" data-def="A complete turn, meaning a return to the starting point. Orwell uses this literal meaning to show that the revolution has come full circle.">revolution</dfn>&rsquo; literally means &lsquo;a complete turn&rsquo; &mdash; the farm has turned all the way back to where it started.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">The Final Image</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">The novel ends with one of the most powerful closing lines in English literature:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>&lsquo;The creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which.&rsquo;</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">This image is devastating. The pigs have become physically and morally indistinguishable from the humans they overthrew. Orwell&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="A story, poem, or picture that can be interpreted to reveal a hidden meaning, typically a moral or political one.">allegory</dfn> reaches its conclusion: the revolution has produced a new class of oppressors who are identical to the old ones. The &lsquo;creatures outside&rsquo; &mdash; the ordinary animals, the working class &mdash; are left staring through a window at a world of power and privilege from which they are permanently excluded.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Pessimistic:</strong> The revolution has achieved nothing. The animals are worse off than under Jones. There is no hope of further rebellion &mdash; the new generation accepts tyranny as normal. Power always corrupts.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Realistic:</strong> Orwell was a <dfn class="term" data-def="A political philosophy that combines personal freedom with social equality, advocating for democratic control of the economy rather than authoritarian state control.">democratic socialist</dfn> who believed in equality but saw how revolutionary ideals were betrayed in practice. The ending reflects his view that Stalinism specifically &mdash; not socialism itself &mdash; had failed.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Optimistic (arguably):</strong> The quarrel between pigs and humans at the card game suggests that the alliance between the ruling classes is unstable. The animals are still watching through the window &mdash; perhaps awareness is the first step toward future resistance.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Orwell&rsquo;s Moral</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Orwell described <em>Animal Farm</em> as a <dfn class="term" data-def="A short story, typically with animals as characters, that teaches a moral or practical lesson.">fable</dfn>. Like all fables, it carries a moral. Orwell&rsquo;s warnings are multiple: that revolutions are vulnerable to hijacking by those who seek personal power; that propaganda and the manipulation of language can make people accept any injustice; that political apathy enables tyranny; and that without education, literacy, and the freedom to question authority, ordinary people will always be exploited by those who rule them.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n26">The ending of <em>Animal Farm</em> appears frequently in exams. When analysing the final line (&lsquo;it was impossible to say which was which&rsquo;), explain the <strong>allegorical meaning</strong> (the revolution has failed; the pigs are the new oppressors) and the <strong>structural significance</strong> (the novel comes full circle, fulfilling Orwell&rsquo;s fable structure). For questions about the Seven Commandments, trace the full journey from original to final paradox. For the 34-mark essay, always state Orwell&rsquo;s purpose: he is not simply telling a story about animals &mdash; he is warning readers about the dangers of totalitarianism, propaganda, and political apathy.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n27">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n28">&lsquo;All animals are equal but some animals are more equal than others&rsquo; &mdash; this paradox encapsulates the complete betrayal of the revolution. The Seven Commandments, once the foundation of Animalism, have been systematically destroyed.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29">The final image of pigs and humans becoming indistinguishable completes the allegory: the revolutionary leaders have become identical to the oppressors they replaced. Napoleon carrying a whip and renaming the farm &lsquo;Manor Farm&rsquo; confirms the revolution has come full circle.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30">Orwell&rsquo;s moral is that revolution without education, accountability, and democratic safeguards will always be hijacked by those who seek power for themselves.</li></ul>
