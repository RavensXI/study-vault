# Napoleon's Rise to Power

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Power Struggle</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Chapter 5 is the turning point of Animal Farm. The tension between Snowball and Napoleon that has been building since the rebellion finally erupts. Orwell presents the two pigs as fundamentally opposed: Snowball wants to build a windmill to improve the animals&rsquo; lives through technology and reduced labour, while Napoleon dismisses the plan and argues that food production should be the priority. Their disagreement is not really about windmills &mdash; it is about the kind of society Animal Farm will become.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Orwell makes the allegorical parallel explicit. Snowball, representing Trotsky, believes in spreading the revolution and improving conditions through innovation. Napoleon, representing Stalin, is focused on consolidating his own power. In the Soviet Union, Trotsky advocated &lsquo;permanent revolution&rsquo; (exporting communism worldwide), while Stalin pushed &lsquo;socialism in one country&rsquo; (strengthening the USSR internally). The windmill debate mirrors this real ideological conflict.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The windmill debate represents the real power struggle between Trotsky and Stalin after Lenin&rsquo;s death. Orwell shows that Napoleon never cared about the windmill itself &mdash; he opposed it only because Snowball supported it, then claimed it as his own idea after expelling Snowball.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Mollie&rsquo;s Departure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Before the climactic expulsion, Orwell includes the departure of Mollie, the vain white mare who loves sugar lumps and ribbons. Mollie represents the <dfn class="term" data-def="The middle class, typically with reference to materialism or capitalist values.">bourgeoisie</dfn> &mdash; those who benefited from the old system and have no interest in revolutionary ideals. She is described from Chapter 1 as &lsquo;foolish&rsquo; and materialistic, more concerned with ribbons and sugar than with freedom. Her defection to a human owner shows that some members of society will always prioritise personal comfort over collective justice.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Orwell&rsquo;s presentation of Mollie is deliberately unsympathetic, but it also raises a genuine question: can people be blamed for leaving a revolution that increasingly demands sacrifice without delivering its promises? Mollie departs just before Napoleon seizes absolute power &mdash; ironically, she escapes the worst of the <dfn class="term" data-def="A political system where the state holds total authority over society.">totalitarian</dfn> regime that follows.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Snowball&rsquo;s Expulsion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">The expulsion of Snowball is the most dramatic moment in the novella so far. During a debate about the windmill, just as Snowball is winning the argument with &lsquo;his brilliant speeches&rsquo;, Napoleon lets out a &lsquo;high-pitched whimper&rsquo; and nine enormous dogs &mdash; the puppies he secretly reared &mdash; burst into the barn and chase Snowball from the farm. The animals are terrified. The dogs, described as &lsquo;huge&rsquo; and &lsquo;fierce-looking&rsquo;, represent Stalin&rsquo;s secret police (the NKVD), who enforced his rule through intimidation, imprisonment and execution.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The nine dogs represent Stalin&rsquo;s NKVD (secret police). Napoleon&rsquo;s &lsquo;high-pitched whimper&rsquo; is a deliberate signal &mdash; he has been planning this coup from the moment he took the puppies away to &lsquo;educate&rsquo; them. Force, not argument, is how he seizes power.</p>
</div>
<!-- DIAGRAM -->
<p data-narration-id="n11">The expulsion parallels Trotsky&rsquo;s exile from the Soviet Union in 1929. Just as Stalin used the apparatus of the state to remove his rival, Napoleon uses the dogs to remove his. Crucially, Napoleon does not defeat Snowball in argument &mdash; he defeats him through violence. Orwell&rsquo;s message is clear: in a totalitarian system, reasoned debate is no match for brute force.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Squealer&rsquo;s Role</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Squealer&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="Biased or misleading information used to promote a political cause.">propaganda</dfn> reaches a new level in Chapter 5. He tells the animals that Snowball was &lsquo;always a traitor&rsquo; and that Napoleon had to protect them. When Boxer objects, saying &lsquo;He fought bravely at the Battle of the Cowshed&rsquo;, Squealer responds that &lsquo;loyalty and obedience are more important&rsquo; than bravery. This is a chilling moment: Squealer is teaching the animals that loyalty to Napoleon matters more than truth. The narrator notes that &lsquo;the three dogs who happened to be with [Squealer] growled so threateningly&rsquo; that Boxer falls silent. Propaganda backed by violence &mdash; this is the formula of totalitarian control.</p>

It parallels Stalin&rsquo;s treatment of Lenin&rsquo;s body, which was embalmed and placed in a mausoleum in Red Square for public veneration. In both cases, the original leader&rsquo;s ideas are hollowed out and turned into a tool of <dfn class="term" data-def="Exaggerated adoration of a leader, often achieved through propaganda and media manipulation.">cult of personality</dfn>. Napoleon claims to continue Old Major&rsquo;s legacy while doing the exact opposite of what Old Major taught.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>After Snowball&rsquo;s expulsion, Boxer adopts a second motto: &lsquo;Napoleon is always right.&rsquo; Combined with &lsquo;I will work harder&rsquo;, these two maxims capture the tragedy of the working class under totalitarianism &mdash; unquestioning loyalty to a leader who will ultimately betray them.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n17">Questions about Napoleon&rsquo;s rise to power often use the phrase &lsquo;how far&rsquo; or &lsquo;to what extent&rsquo;, requiring a balanced argument. Consider whether Napoleon is <em>only</em> interested in power, or whether he genuinely believes his methods are necessary. The strongest responses acknowledge that Orwell presents Napoleon as calculated from the start (taking the puppies, opposing Snowball&rsquo;s plans) but also explore how the other animals&rsquo; passivity enables his rise. Always link Napoleon&rsquo;s actions to Stalin and the NKVD for strong contextual marks.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n19">Snowball&rsquo;s expulsion by the dogs is the novella&rsquo;s turning point. It shows that in totalitarian systems, force defeats reasoned debate &mdash; paralleling Stalin&rsquo;s exile of Trotsky.</li><li data-narration-id="n20">Napoleon consolidates power by cancelling democratic meetings, claiming Snowball&rsquo;s ideas as his own, and using Squealer&rsquo;s propaganda backed by the threat of the dogs.</li><li data-narration-id="n21">Boxer&rsquo;s second motto &mdash; &lsquo;Napoleon is always right&rsquo; &mdash; shows how loyalty and hard work, without critical thinking, can be exploited by those in power.</li></ul>
