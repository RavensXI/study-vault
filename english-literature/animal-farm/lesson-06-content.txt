# The Windmill & Boxer's Exploitation

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Building the Windmill</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">After Snowball&rsquo;s expulsion, Napoleon announces that the windmill will be built after all &mdash; despite having opposed it. <dfn class="term" data-def="A person or group unfairly blamed for problems caused by others.">Scapegoating</dfn> Snowball allows Napoleon to claim the windmill idea as his own, rewriting history to maintain power. Squealer explains that Napoleon had never really opposed the windmill but had pretended to in order to get rid of Snowball &mdash; a masterpiece of propaganda.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The animals work gruelling sixty-hour weeks. Orwell writes that &lsquo;all that year the animals worked like slaves&rsquo; &mdash; a deeply ironic simile, since the entire purpose of the Rebellion was to free them from slavery. The windmill becomes a symbol of the animals&rsquo; exploited labour: they sacrifice everything for a project that benefits only the pigs.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The windmill represents Stalin&rsquo;s Five-Year Plans &mdash; ambitious industrial projects that demanded backbreaking labour from Soviet workers, often with little reward. Orwell uses the windmill to show how leaders exploit workers&rsquo; hope for a better future.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Boxer: The Hero Worker</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><dfn class="term" data-def="The cart-horse who represents the loyal, hardworking proletariat in Orwell's allegory.">Boxer</dfn> is the driving force behind the windmill. Orwell writes that &lsquo;nothing could have been achieved&rsquo; without him and that his strength was &lsquo;equal to that of all the rest of the animals put together.&rsquo; The vivid image of &lsquo;the tips of his hoofs clawing at the ground&rsquo; conveys extreme physical effort, suggesting someone at the very edge of their endurance.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Boxer adopts the personal motto &lsquo;Napoleon is always right&rsquo; alongside his original &lsquo;I will work harder.&rsquo; This combination of blind loyalty and relentless labour makes Boxer both admirable and tragic. He represents the <dfn class="term" data-def="The working class, especially those who perform manual labour, in Marxist theory.">proletariat</dfn> &mdash; the working class who pour everything into the system but are ultimately betrayed by those in power.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">The Changing Commandments</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">In Chapter Six, the pigs move into the farmhouse and begin sleeping in beds &mdash; a direct violation of the Fourth Commandment. When Clover checks the barn wall, the commandment now reads: &lsquo;No animal shall sleep in a bed <em>with sheets</em>.&rsquo; Orwell writes that &lsquo;curiously enough, Clover had not remembered that the Fourth Commandment mentioned sheets.&rsquo; This <dfn class="term" data-def="When the audience or reader understands more about a situation than the characters do.">dramatic irony</dfn> highlights the animals&rsquo; inability to challenge the pigs&rsquo; lies.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Squealer justifies every change by arguing that the pigs need comfort to do their &lsquo;brainwork.&rsquo; He is always &lsquo;attended by two or three dogs&rsquo; &mdash; a subtle reminder that propaganda is backed by the threat of violence. The gradual alteration of the commandments is one of Orwell&rsquo;s most powerful devices: it shows how totalitarian regimes change the rules bit by bit, so slowly that people fail to notice until it is too late.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Each commandment change mirrors a real betrayal of communist ideals under Stalin. The beds represent luxury; trading with humans mirrors the USSR&rsquo;s alliances with capitalist nations. Orwell shows that revolutionary principles are always the first casualty of power.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Engagement with Humans</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Napoleon hires <dfn class="term" data-def="A human solicitor who acts as intermediary between Animal Farm and the outside world, representing useful idiots who assist totalitarian regimes.">Mr Whymper</dfn> as a solicitor to handle trade with neighbouring farms. This directly contradicts the original principles of Animalism, which forbade all contact with humans. Squealer insists the arrangement is &lsquo;not of course for any commercial purpose&rsquo; &mdash; a laughable euphemism that Orwell uses to expose the gap between the pigs&rsquo; words and their actions.</p>

Napoleon uses the destruction to intensify hatred of Snowball and to rally the animals to rebuild, extracting even more labour from them. ## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n17">When writing about Boxer, always connect him to the <strong>allegory</strong>. Don&rsquo;t just describe what Boxer does &mdash; explain what Orwell is saying about the exploitation of the working class through Boxer. Use the phrase &lsquo;Orwell uses Boxer to suggest that&hellip;&rsquo; to show you understand the writer&rsquo;s purpose. For the 34-mark essay, embed context throughout your response rather than bolting it on at the end &mdash; link Boxer to Stakhanov and the proletariat within your analysis of specific quotations.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n19">The windmill symbolises the exploitation of workers&rsquo; labour under totalitarianism, paralleling Stalin&rsquo;s Five-Year Plans. Boxer&rsquo;s relentless work represents the proletariat&rsquo;s blind loyalty.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n20">The gradual changing of the commandments shows how totalitarian regimes erode freedoms incrementally, making it hard for citizens to notice or resist.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n21">Napoleon&rsquo;s engagement with humans and use of Snowball as a scapegoat demonstrate how revolutionary leaders betray their own ideals while blaming others for their failures.</li></ul>
