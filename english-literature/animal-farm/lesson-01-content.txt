# Context, Allegory & Orwell's Purpose

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Kind of Story Is Animal Farm?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Animal Farm is not simply a story about animals on a farm. Orwell deliberately chose the form of a <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of folk tale, often with animals as characters, that teaches a moral or lesson.">fable</dfn> &mdash; a short story with animal characters that teaches a moral lesson. By giving human qualities to animals (a technique called <dfn class="term" data-def="Giving human traits to animals or non-human entities.">anthropomorphism</dfn>), Orwell made complex political ideas accessible and memorable. The subtitle &lsquo;A Fairy Story&rsquo; is deliberately ironic: the content is anything but light-hearted.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Orwell subtitled Animal Farm &lsquo;A Fairy Story&rsquo; as an ironic joke. The novella is actually a dark political warning about how power corrupts revolutionary ideals.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n4">Fables have existed for thousands of years. The ancient Greek storyteller Aesop wrote fables like &lsquo;The Hare and the Tortoise&rsquo; and &lsquo;The Lion and the Mouse&rsquo;, each containing a moral message delivered through animal characters. Orwell follows this tradition but takes it much further, using the form to deliver a sustained political argument.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Allegory: The Hidden Meaning</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Animal Farm is an <dfn class="term" data-def="A story with a hidden political or moral meaning.">allegory</dfn> &mdash; a story where characters, events and settings represent real-world people and situations. Almost every element of the novella maps onto the history of the Russian Revolution and Soviet Union under Stalin. Understanding these connections is essential for the exam.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n7">Mr Jones represents Tsar Nicholas II, the incompetent ruler whose neglect triggered revolution. Old Major represents Karl Marx (the political theorist) and Vladimir Lenin (the revolutionary leader). Napoleon represents Joseph Stalin, the dictator who seized power through fear and violence. Snowball represents Leon Trotsky, the intellectual rival whom Stalin exiled and eventually had murdered. Squealer represents Soviet propaganda, particularly the state newspaper <em>Pravda</em> (meaning &lsquo;truth&rsquo;). Boxer represents the loyal, exploited working class.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Satire: Humour with a Serious Purpose</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Orwell also uses <dfn class="term" data-def="The use of humour or exaggeration to criticise or ridicule politics or society.">satire</dfn> throughout Animal Farm. Satire uses humour, exaggeration and irony to mock and criticise. When the pigs start sleeping in beds and then change the commandment to &lsquo;No animal shall sleep in a bed <em>with sheets</em>&rsquo;, the dark comedy exposes how those in power twist rules to suit themselves. The humour makes the political message more powerful because it highlights the absurdity of <dfn class="term" data-def="A political system where the state holds total authority over society.">totalitarianism</dfn>.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">The Russian Revolution: Essential Context</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Before 1917, Russia was ruled by Tsar Nicholas II, who had total control over a country where most people lived in extreme poverty. Anger over poverty, lack of political freedom, and Russia&rsquo;s failures in World War I led to two revolutions in 1917. In February, protests forced the Tsar to abdicate. In October, the Bolsheviks, led by Vladimir Lenin, seized power. They were inspired by the ideas of Karl Marx, who argued that the working class (the <dfn class="term" data-def="The working class, especially in Marxist theory.">proletariat</dfn>) should overthrow the ruling class and share wealth equally.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>After Lenin&rsquo;s death in 1924, Joseph Stalin took control of the Soviet Union. He ruled through fear, propaganda, purges and a cult of personality &mdash; exactly the pattern Orwell mirrors through Napoleon in Animal Farm.</p>
</div>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13">George Orwell (born Eric Arthur Blair, 1903) was a democratic socialist who believed in equality and freedom. His experience fighting in the Spanish Civil War (1936) was pivotal: he witnessed Soviet-backed communists attacking their own allies, showing him that revolutionary ideals could be betrayed by those seeking power. After Spain, Orwell became fiercely opposed to totalitarianism in all its forms. He wrote Animal Farm in 1945 specifically to show how Stalin had corrupted the ideals of the Russian Revolution. Crucially, Orwell was not attacking socialism itself &mdash; he was attacking its corruption under authoritarian rule.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Why Does This Context Matter in the Exam?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">AQA expects you to weave contextual knowledge throughout your essays, not bolt it on as a separate paragraph. When you analyse a quotation, connect it to what you know about the Russian Revolution, Orwell&rsquo;s beliefs, or the literary form. For example, when discussing Napoleon&rsquo;s use of the dogs, link this to Stalin&rsquo;s secret police (the NKVD). When analysing Squealer&rsquo;s speeches, connect them to <em>Pravda</em> and Soviet propaganda. This shows the examiner you understand Orwell&rsquo;s purpose.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A common misconception is that Orwell was attacking communism itself. In fact, he was a democratic socialist who criticised how revolutionary ideals were betrayed under Stalin&rsquo;s totalitarian regime.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n17">In the AQA English Literature exam, Animal Farm questions always ask you to write about both what happens in the text <strong>and</strong> how Orwell presents ideas. Context is not a bolt-on &mdash; embed it within your analysis of language, form and structure. When you identify a literary technique (allegory, satire, irony), always explain <em>why</em> Orwell uses it and what political message it conveys. For 34-mark essay questions, SPaG marks are awarded for accurate spelling, punctuation and use of specialist vocabulary like &lsquo;allegory&rsquo;, &lsquo;totalitarianism&rsquo; and &lsquo;propaganda&rsquo;.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n19">Animal Farm is a fable, an allegory of the Russian Revolution, and a satire of totalitarianism &mdash; understanding all three forms is essential for exam success.</li><li data-narration-id="n20">Each character maps onto a real historical figure or group: Napoleon = Stalin, Snowball = Trotsky, Squealer = Pravda, Boxer = the exploited working class.</li><li data-narration-id="n21">Orwell was a democratic socialist who wrote Animal Farm to warn against the corruption of revolutionary ideals, not to attack socialism itself.</li></ul>
