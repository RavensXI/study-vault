# Old Major's Speech & the Rebellion

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Old Major&rsquo;s Speech: Setting the Tone</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Chapter 1 opens with Old Major, the prize-winning boar, calling the animals together for a meeting. His speech is the catalyst for everything that follows. Orwell crafts it as a masterpiece of <dfn class="term" data-def="The art of using language to persuade an audience, using techniques such as ethos, pathos and logos.">rhetoric</dfn> &mdash; Old Major uses emotional appeals, logical arguments and personal authority to convince the animals that rebellion is both necessary and just.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Old Major begins by establishing his authority: &lsquo;I have had a long life, I have had much time for thought.&rsquo; This is an appeal to <dfn class="term" data-def="A rhetorical appeal to the speaker's character, credibility or authority.">ethos</dfn> &mdash; he asks the animals to trust his wisdom. He then shifts to <dfn class="term" data-def="A rhetorical appeal to the audience's emotions.">pathos</dfn>, painting a vivid picture of their suffering: &lsquo;our lives are miserable, laborious, and short.&rsquo; The tricolon (list of three) hammers home the bleakness of their existence. He addresses individual animals directly &mdash; Clover, the hens, Boxer &mdash; making each feel personally included in his argument.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Old Major represents both Karl Marx (whose ideas inspired the revolution) and Vladimir Lenin (who led the Bolsheviks to power). His speech mirrors the Communist Manifesto&rsquo;s call for workers to unite against their oppressors.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">The core of Old Major&rsquo;s argument is a logical appeal: &lsquo;Man is the only creature that consumes without producing.&rsquo; This is <dfn class="term" data-def="A rhetorical appeal to logic, reason and evidence.">logos</dfn> &mdash; he uses evidence and reasoning to identify Man as the source of all the animals&rsquo; suffering. His solution is clear: &lsquo;Remove Man from the scene, and the root cause of hunger and overwork is abolished for ever.&rsquo; The simplicity of this argument is what makes it so powerful &mdash; and so dangerous, because it ignores the possibility that the animals themselves could become oppressors.</p>

He also establishes rules that foreshadow the Seven Commandments: &lsquo;No animal must ever live in a house, or sleep in a bed, or wear clothes, or drink alcohol.&rsquo;</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">&lsquo;Beasts of England&rsquo;: The Anthem of Revolution</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Old Major&rsquo;s speech culminates in the song &lsquo;Beasts of England&rsquo;, which paints a <dfn class="term" data-def="A perfect world, free of corruption and injustice.">utopian</dfn> vision of a future without human oppression: &lsquo;Bright will shine the fields of England, / Purer shall its waters be.&rsquo; The song serves a crucial structural purpose &mdash; it becomes the emotional glue that binds the animals to the revolutionary cause. Its later banning by Napoleon (Chapter 7) symbolises how the revolution has been completely betrayed.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">The Rebellion and the Seven Commandments</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">In Chapter 2, the rebellion happens almost spontaneously when Jones forgets to feed the animals. The trigger is neglect, not planning &mdash; mirroring how the February Revolution in Russia was sparked by food shortages rather than organised revolt. After expelling Jones, the pigs immediately take charge. They have already developed Old Major&rsquo;s ideas into a system called &lsquo;Animalism&rsquo; and distilled the principles into Seven Commandments, painted on the barn wall.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n11">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The Seven Commandments are a structural device. Orwell uses their gradual alteration throughout the novella to track the pigs&rsquo; increasing corruption &mdash; from &lsquo;No animal shall kill any other animal&rsquo; to &lsquo;No animal shall kill any other animal <em>without cause</em>.&rsquo;</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The commandments include rules such as &lsquo;Whatever goes upon two legs is an enemy&rsquo; and &lsquo;All animals are equal.&rsquo; These encode Old Major&rsquo;s ideals in written form. However, the very act of writing them gives the pigs power &mdash; they are the only animals literate enough to read and interpret the rules, foreshadowing their manipulation of language to maintain control.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Early Signs of Corruption</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Orwell plants the seeds of corruption immediately. At the end of Chapter 2, Napoleon takes charge of the milk: &lsquo;Never mind the milk, comrades!&rsquo; he cries, before sending the animals away to work. When they return, &lsquo;it was noticed that the milk had disappeared.&rsquo; The <dfn class="term" data-def="A grammatical construction where the subject receives the action, often used to obscure who performed it.">passive voice</dfn> here is deliberate &mdash; Orwell avoids saying who noticed or who took the milk, mirroring how authoritarian regimes obscure their own corruption.</p>

The dogs snap at the rats during Old Major&rsquo;s speech, hinting at the violence that will later enforce Napoleon&rsquo;s rule. Moses the raven tells stories of &lsquo;Sugarcandy Mountain&rsquo; (representing religion as a tool to pacify the masses). The pigs&rsquo; immediate assumption of leadership &mdash; without being elected &mdash; mirrors the Bolsheviks&rsquo; seizure of power.</p>

  <p>The disappearance of the milk at the end of Chapter 2 is the first concrete sign of the pigs&rsquo; corruption. Napoleon uses distraction (&lsquo;The harvest is more important&rsquo;) to divert attention &mdash; a tactic that mirrors how authoritarian governments redirect public focus away from their abuses.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n19">Old Major&rsquo;s speech uses ethos, pathos and logos to inspire rebellion. He represents Marx and Lenin, and his idealistic vision contrasts sharply with what the revolution actually becomes.</li><li data-narration-id="n20">The Seven Commandments are a structural device &mdash; their gradual alteration tracks the corruption of the revolution throughout the novella.</li><li data-narration-id="n21">Orwell foreshadows the pigs&rsquo; betrayal from the very start: the passive voice, the disappearing milk, and Squealer&rsquo;s ability to &lsquo;turn black into white&rsquo; all signal what is to come.</li></ul>
