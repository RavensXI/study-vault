# The Purges & Terror

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Purges Begin</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Chapter Seven marks the darkest turning point in <em>Animal Farm</em>. Facing food shortages and growing unrest, Napoleon responds not with reform but with <dfn class="term" data-def="The violent removal of political opponents, often through execution or imprisonment, to consolidate a leader's power.">terror</dfn>. He summons all the animals to the yard and, with his dogs at his side, forces a series of confessions. The four pigs who had protested against Napoleon&rsquo;s abolition of Sunday meetings are the first to confess to conspiring with Snowball. The dogs &lsquo;promptly tore their throats out.&rsquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This triggers a cascade of confessions. Hens, geese, and sheep all come forward to admit to various crimes &mdash; stealing corn, urinating in the drinking pool, killing an old ram. Each confession is followed by immediate execution. Orwell writes that &lsquo;the air was heavy with the smell of blood, which had been unknown there since Jones was expelled.&rsquo; The sensory language creates an overwhelming atmosphere of horror, and the reference to Jones is devastating: the revolution has produced something just as brutal as what it replaced.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The purges in Chapter Seven directly parallel Stalin&rsquo;s <strong>Great Purge</strong> (1936&ndash;1938), during which millions of Soviet citizens were imprisoned, forced to confess to fabricated crimes, and executed. Public &lsquo;show trials&rsquo; were staged to create the illusion that Stalin was protecting the nation from internal enemies.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Language of Brutality</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Orwell uses visceral, violent language to convey the horror of the executions. The dogs &lsquo;seized four of the pigs by the ear&rsquo; and the victims came forward &lsquo;trembling, with guilt written on every line of their countenances.&rsquo; The pigs are described as &lsquo;squealing with pain and terror&rsquo; &mdash; a word choice that reduces them to their animal nature and strips them of dignity.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The phrase &lsquo;the dogs had tasted blood, and for a few moments they appeared to go quite mad&rsquo; is particularly chilling. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech in which human qualities are attributed to animals or objects.">personification</dfn> of the dogs as blood-crazed killers mirrors the behaviour of the NKVD, Stalin&rsquo;s secret police, who carried out executions with increasing brutality. &lsquo;A pile of corpses lying before Napoleon&rsquo;s feet&rsquo; creates a stark visual image of Napoleon as a tyrant surveying his victims.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Scapegoating Snowball</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Squealer&rsquo;s propaganda reaches its most manipulative in Chapter Seven. He announces that Snowball &lsquo;has sold himself to Frederick of Pinchfield Farm&rsquo; and was &lsquo;in league with Jones from the very start.&rsquo; This <dfn class="term" data-def="Unfairly blaming a person or group for problems caused by others, diverting attention from the real source of failure.">scapegoating</dfn> rewrites history entirely &mdash; the animals saw Snowball fight bravely at the Battle of the Cowshed, yet Squealer insists that was &lsquo;part of the arrangement.&rsquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Even Boxer questions this: &lsquo;I do not believe that. Snowball fought bravely at the Battle of the Cowshed. I saw him myself.&rsquo; But Squealer counters by invoking Napoleon&rsquo;s absolute authority: &lsquo;Our Leader, Comrade Napoleon, has stated categorically &mdash; categorically, comrade &mdash; that Snowball was Jones&rsquo;s agent from the very beginning.&rsquo; Boxer&rsquo;s response &mdash; &lsquo;If Comrade Napoleon says it, it must be right&rsquo; &mdash; is devastating. It shows how even the most loyal and well-meaning animals can be manipulated into accepting obvious lies.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Squealer&rsquo;s speech uses multiple <dfn class="term" data-def="Biased or misleading information used to promote a political cause or point of view.">propaganda</dfn> techniques: <strong>fear</strong> (&lsquo;Snowball&rsquo;s secret agents are lurking among us&rsquo;), <strong>scapegoating</strong> (blaming Snowball for all problems), <strong>appeal to authority</strong> (&lsquo;Comrade Napoleon has stated categorically&rsquo;), and <strong>loaded language</strong> (&lsquo;in sheer malignity&rsquo;). These parallel techniques used by Soviet newspaper <em>Pravda</em> under Stalin.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Banning &lsquo;Beasts of England&rsquo;</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">After the executions, the surviving animals huddle together in shock. Clover looks out over the farm and reflects that &lsquo;these scenes of terror and slaughter were not what they had looked forward to on that night when Old Major first stirred them to rebellion.&rsquo; This moment of clarity is heartbreaking &mdash; but also futile. Clover cannot articulate her thoughts and lacks the words to challenge the pigs.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n16">The animals begin singing &lsquo;Beasts of England&rsquo; &mdash; the revolutionary anthem that once inspired their dreams of freedom. Squealer announces that the song is now <strong>banned</strong>. His justification is that &lsquo;the Rebellion is now completed&rsquo; and the song is &lsquo;no longer needed.&rsquo; In reality, the song is dangerous because it reminds the animals of the revolution&rsquo;s original promises &mdash; promises that Napoleon has comprehensively broken.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17">The replacement anthem, composed by Minimus, is a hollow piece of propaganda praising &lsquo;Animal Farm, Animal Farm, never through me shalt thou come to harm.&rsquo; Where &lsquo;Beasts of England&rsquo; was about liberation, the new song is about obedience. Orwell shows how totalitarian regimes suppress symbols of resistance and replace them with state-approved alternatives.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n18">When analysing the purge scene, use Orwell&rsquo;s exact language. Don&rsquo;t write &lsquo;the animals were killed&rsquo; &mdash; write &lsquo;Orwell uses the phrase &ldquo;tore their throats out&rdquo; to convey the savage brutality of the executions.&rsquo; For Squealer questions, identify specific propaganda techniques by name (fear, scapegoating, appeal to authority) and explain how each technique helps the pigs maintain control. Always link to context: the purges mirror Stalin&rsquo;s Great Terror of 1936&ndash;1938.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n19">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n20">The purges in Chapter Seven parallel Stalin&rsquo;s Great Purge. Orwell uses graphic, visceral language (&lsquo;tore their throats out,&rsquo; &lsquo;the air was heavy with the smell of blood&rsquo;) to convey the horror of <dfn class="term" data-def="Cruel and oppressive government or rule.">tyranny</dfn>.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n21">Squealer&rsquo;s scapegoating of Snowball uses multiple propaganda techniques &mdash; fear, loaded language, appeal to authority &mdash; to rewrite history and silence dissent.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n22">The banning of &lsquo;Beasts of England&rsquo; symbolises the death of the revolution&rsquo;s original ideals. Totalitarian regimes suppress symbols of resistance to prevent people from remembering what was promised.</li></ul>
