# The Death of Boxer & Betrayal

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Chapter Nine: The Decline of Animal Farm</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">By Chapter Nine, life on Animal Farm has deteriorated drastically. Rations are reduced for all animals except the pigs and dogs &mdash; Orwell notes this through the <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech where something is presented as less significant than it actually is, often for ironic effect.">understatement</dfn> that &lsquo;for the time being, certainly, it had been found necessary to make a readjustment of rations.&rsquo; The word &lsquo;readjustment&rsquo; is a <dfn class="term" data-def="The use of mild or indirect language to disguise something unpleasant or embarrassing.">euphemism</dfn> for a cut. Squealer reels off statistics to prove that food production has actually increased &mdash; but the animals are hungrier than ever.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The pigs, meanwhile, enjoy increasing <dfn class="term" data-def="Special rights or advantages available only to a particular group, often at the expense of others.">privileges</dfn>: a separate school for their piglets, beer rations (half a gallon for Napoleon, served in the Crown Derby soup tureen), and the right to walk on their hind legs within the farmhouse. Animal Farm is proclaimed a &lsquo;Republic&rsquo; with Napoleon as its unanimously elected President &mdash; the only candidate. Orwell&rsquo;s irony is devastating: a republic with a single candidate is a dictatorship by another name.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Orwell uses understatement throughout Chapter Nine to mirror Squealer&rsquo;s propaganda techniques. By presenting terrible events in mild, bureaucratic language, Orwell shows how totalitarian regimes minimise suffering through carefully chosen words. The reader sees through the understatement even when the animals cannot.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Boxer&rsquo;s Collapse</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Boxer has been working himself to destruction. His split hoof, sustained during the Battle of the Windmill, never fully heals, yet he continues labouring on the new windmill. His determination to see the windmill completed before his retirement age of twelve drives him to work even harder. One day, Boxer collapses while hauling a load of stone. He falls to the ground and cannot get up.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The other animals are distraught. Boxer himself remains optimistic: he tells Clover that he will recover and spend his retirement in the corner of the big pasture that was set aside for retired animals. Orwell creates <dfn class="term" data-def="A feeling of sadness and compassion caused by the suffering of another person or character.">pathos</dfn> through Boxer&rsquo;s innocent hope &mdash; the reader knows, through dramatic irony, that the pigs have no intention of honouring their promise of retirement.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">The Knacker&rsquo;s Van</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Squealer announces that Napoleon has arranged for Boxer to be treated at the hospital in Willingdon. A van arrives to collect him. But it is <dfn class="term" data-def="A person whose trade is the disposal of dead or unwanted animals, typically for rendering into glue or other products.">Benjamin</dfn> &mdash; the cynical donkey, who can read &mdash; who notices the lettering on the side of the van: &lsquo;Alfred Simmonds, Horse Slaughterer and Glue Boiler, Willingdon.&rsquo; For the first time in the novel, Benjamin breaks his silence and cries out: &lsquo;Fools! Fools! Do you not see what is written on the side of that van?&rsquo;</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n10">The animals rush after the van, screaming for Boxer to get out. Clover shouts, &lsquo;Boxer! Boxer! Get out! Get out quickly! They&rsquo;re taking you to your death!&rsquo; Boxer tries to kick his way out but &lsquo;his strength had left him&rsquo; &mdash; the animal who was once the most powerful on the farm is now too weak to save himself. The van drives away. Boxer is never seen again.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n11">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The <dfn class="term" data-def="A person who buys and slaughters old or unwanted horses, typically to render them into glue, leather, or animal feed.">knacker</dfn>&rsquo;s van is the novel&rsquo;s most emotionally devastating moment. Boxer &mdash; who gave everything to the farm, who worked when sick and injured, whose mottos were &lsquo;I will work harder&rsquo; and &lsquo;Napoleon is always right&rsquo; &mdash; is literally sold for slaughter. The pigs trade his body for whisky money. This is the ultimate <dfn class="term" data-def="The act of being disloyal to someone who trusted you, breaking faith or violating a commitment.">betrayal</dfn> of the working class.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Squealer&rsquo;s Final Deception</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Three days later, Squealer addresses the animals. He claims that Boxer died peacefully in hospital, with Squealer at his side. The van, Squealer explains, had previously belonged to a knacker but had been bought by the veterinary surgeon, who had not yet painted over the old lettering. The animals are &lsquo;enormously relieved&rsquo; by this explanation. Orwell&rsquo;s narration is deliberately flat and understated, forcing the reader to feel the outrage that the animals cannot.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n14">Boxer represents the <strong>proletariat</strong> &mdash; the loyal, hardworking masses who pour their labour into building the state. His death at the knacker&rsquo;s represents the totalitarian betrayal of workers: they are used, exploited, and discarded when they are no longer productive. In the Soviet Union, workers like the Stakhanovites were praised as heroes but received few real benefits. Those who became too old or sick to work were abandoned by the state they had served.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15">Orwell&rsquo;s message is clear: totalitarian leaders view workers as resources, not people. The pigs&rsquo; use of the money from Boxer&rsquo;s sale to buy whisky is the final, sickening proof &mdash; Boxer&rsquo;s life is worth nothing more than a case of alcohol.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Benjamin&rsquo;s Role</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Benjamin&rsquo;s reaction to the van is significant because he has spent the entire novel refusing to engage with the politics of the farm. His cynical motto &mdash; &lsquo;Donkeys live a long time. None of you has ever seen a dead donkey&rsquo; &mdash; suggests he always knew the revolution would fail. But he never used his intelligence or literacy to warn the others. Only when his closest friend is about to die does he finally speak up &mdash; and by then it is too late.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n18">Through Benjamin, Orwell warns that political apathy is as dangerous as active oppression. Benjamin could read, could think critically, and could see through the pigs&rsquo; lies &mdash; but he chose not to act. He represents the educated cynics who observe injustice but do nothing, believing change is impossible. Orwell suggests that their silence enables tyranny. Benjamin&rsquo;s anguished cry of &lsquo;Fools! Fools!&rsquo; is ultimately directed at himself as much as the other animals.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n19">Boxer&rsquo;s death is one of the most commonly examined moments in <em>Animal Farm</em>. When writing about it, focus on Orwell&rsquo;s <strong>methods</strong>: the dramatic irony (the reader understands the van&rsquo;s purpose before the animals do), the pathos (Boxer&rsquo;s hopeful talk of retirement), and the euphemism (Squealer&rsquo;s lie about the veterinary surgeon). Always connect Boxer to the allegory: his death represents the totalitarian state&rsquo;s betrayal of the working class. For the 34-mark essay, address the word &lsquo;sympathetic&rsquo; directly &mdash; explain why Orwell creates sympathy (to make the betrayal more powerful) and whether that sympathy is complicated by Boxer&rsquo;s own complicity in accepting the pigs&rsquo; lies.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n20">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n21">Boxer&rsquo;s sale to the knacker is the novel&rsquo;s most devastating moment, representing the totalitarian state&rsquo;s ultimate betrayal of loyal workers. The pigs use the money to buy whisky.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n22">Squealer&rsquo;s lie about the veterinary surgeon&rsquo;s van is the most brazen deception in the novel &mdash; the animals accept it because they cannot bear to face the truth.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n23">Benjamin represents the danger of political apathy: he had the intelligence to challenge the pigs but chose silence until it was too late. Orwell warns that inaction enables tyranny.</li></ul>
