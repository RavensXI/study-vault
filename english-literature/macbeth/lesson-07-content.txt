# The Witches Return & Tyranny

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Return to the Witches</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Act Four Scene One opens with the witches brewing their potion around a cauldron. The ingredients are grotesque: ‘poisoned entrails’, ‘eye of newt’, ‘toe of frog’, ‘finger of birth-strangled babe’. Shakespeare lists body parts from animals, mythical creatures, and even human corpses. For a Jacobean audience who genuinely believed in witchcraft, this scene would have been deeply unsettling. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A repeated line or set of lines in a poem or play, often used to create rhythm or emphasis.">refrain</dfn> ‘Double, double, toil and trouble; / Fire burn, and cauldron bubble’ creates a hypnotic, spell-like rhythm through its <dfn class="term" data-def="A metrical pattern of two stressed syllables in a row, used here to create a chanting, ritualistic effect.">trochaic tetrameter</dfn> — a rhythmic pattern that differs from the iambic pentameter used by human characters, marking the witches as unnatural.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The word ‘double’ is significant throughout <em>Macbeth</em>. It connects to <dfn class="term" data-def="The use of ambiguous language to conceal the truth or avoid committing to a clear meaning.">equivocation</dfn> — saying one thing and meaning another. Macbeth says Duncan is ‘here in double trust’ before murdering him. The witches’ prophecies have a ‘double sense’ that Macbeth fails to see until it is too late. Shakespeare weaves this idea of doubleness through the entire play to explore the theme of deception.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Macbeth now greets the witches as ‘you secret, black, and midnight hags’ and demands ‘Answer me.’ Compare this with his first meeting in Act 1 Scene 3: ‘Speak, if you can. What are you?’ The shift from uncertain questioning to commanding imperatives shows Macbeth is no longer afraid of the supernatural — he has become complicit in it.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Three Apparitions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The witches conjure three apparitions, each delivering a prophecy designed to give Macbeth false confidence.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>First Apparition — an armoured head:</strong> ‘Beware Macduff! / Beware the Thane of Fife!’ This confirms Macbeth’s existing fear. The armoured head foreshadows Macbeth’s own beheading at the hands of Macduff in Act Five. The audience may recognise this, creating dramatic irony.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Second Apparition — a bloody child:</strong> ‘None of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth.’ Macbeth interprets this as meaning he is invincible, since everyone is born from a woman. He does not consider that Macduff was ‘from his mother’s womb / Untimely ripp’d’ — delivered by Caesarean section. The bloody child likely represents Macduff at birth.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Third Apparition — a child crowned, with a tree in his hand:</strong> ‘Macbeth shall never vanquish’d be until / Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come against him.’ Macbeth laughs this off as impossible — trees cannot move. But Malcolm later orders his soldiers to cut branches from Birnam Wood as camouflage, fulfilling the prophecy. The crowned child represents Malcolm, the rightful king.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Dramatic Irony &amp; False Security</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The prophecies create powerful <dfn class="term" data-def="When the audience knows something that a character on stage does not, creating tension or emotional impact.">dramatic irony</dfn>. The audience can see that the apparitions are designed to deceive: each prophecy has a loophole that Macbeth cannot see because of his arrogance. Banquo warned in Act One: ‘oftentimes, to win us to our harm, / The instruments of darkness tell us truths, / Win us with honest trifles, to betray’s / In deepest consequence.’ This is exactly what happens. The witches give Macbeth truths that are technically accurate but profoundly misleading.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Shakespeare uses the concept of equivocation here — a hot topic in Jacobean England after the Gunpowder Plot of 1605. The Jesuit priest Henry Garnet argued that equivocation (telling half-truths) was morally acceptable. King James I was outraged. Shakespeare’s witches embody this dangerous art of telling the truth in a way designed to deceive.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>After hearing the prophecies, Macbeth resolves to murder Macduff’s entire family: ‘The castle of Macduff I will surprise, / Seize upon Fife, give to the edge o’ the sword / His wife, his babes.’ This marks Macbeth’s descent from targeted killing (Duncan, Banquo) to indiscriminate slaughter. He has become a full tyrant.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Macbeth’s Language: Commanding the Supernatural</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Throughout this scene, Macbeth uses imperatives to command the witches: ‘Answer me’, ‘Tell me’, ‘Let me know’, ‘Call ’em’, ‘I will be satisfied.’ He even uses the verb ‘I conjure you’, which means to summon spirits through magic. Shakespeare shows that Macbeth is no longer a passive recipient of the supernatural — he is actively participating in dark arts. This shift horrifies a Jacobean audience who believed that consorting with witches was a path to damnation.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">His language also reveals hubris. After hearing the second and third prophecies, he declares: ‘Then live, Macduff: what need I fear of thee?’ and laughs at the idea of Birnam Wood moving. Yet he still orders Macduff’s family killed — ‘to make assurance double sure.’ The word ‘double’ again links to equivocation. Macbeth’s overconfidence and simultaneous paranoia reveal a mind coming apart at the seams.</p>

Shakespeare wrote <em>Macbeth</em> partly to flatter James: the play features witches (James’s obsession), Scottish kingship (James was Scottish), and Banquo’s line of descendants (James’s claimed ancestry). ## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n19">When writing about the witches in the exam, always connect to context. Mention King James I, <em>Daemonologie</em>, and Jacobean beliefs about witchcraft. If the extract is from Act 4 Scene 1, analyse the apparitions individually — explain what each one says, what Macbeth thinks it means, and what it actually means. This three-part approach demonstrates understanding of dramatic irony. Use the term ‘equivocation’ to show sophisticated vocabulary and link to the Gunpowder Plot context.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n20">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n21">The three apparitions give Macbeth prophecies with hidden loopholes: ‘beware Macduff’, ‘none of woman born’ shall harm him, and Birnam Wood will not come to Dunsinane. All three are fulfilled in ways Macbeth does not expect.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n22">The cauldron scene shows Macbeth actively seeking out the supernatural (using imperatives like ‘I conjure you’) rather than being a passive victim — he has become complicit in his own destruction.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n23">Shakespeare uses equivocation (double meanings) throughout to link the witches’ deception to Jacobean anxieties about the Gunpowder Plot. Banquo’s warning about the ‘instruments of darkness’ is proved right.</li></ul>
