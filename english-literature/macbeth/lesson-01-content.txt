# Context & the Supernatural

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The World Shakespeare Wrote For</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">When Shakespeare wrote <em>Macbeth</em> around 1606, England was ruled by King James I — a monarch who was genuinely fascinated by, and terrified of, witchcraft. James had written his own book on the subject, <em>Daemonologie</em> (1597), and had personally overseen witch trials in Scotland. For a <dfn class="term" data-def="Relating to the reign of King James I of England (1603–1625), whose beliefs about witchcraft and kingship heavily influenced Shakespeare.">Jacobean</dfn> audience, the witches in <em>Macbeth</em> were not fantasy figures — they were a genuine, terrifying threat.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Understanding this context is essential for the AQA exam. The play was written to flatter James I and reflect his worldview. Every scene involving the supernatural would have provoked a stronger reaction from Shakespeare’s original audience than it does from a modern one.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>King James I wrote <em>Daemonologie</em> in 1597 and personally attended witch trials. Shakespeare wrote <em>Macbeth</em> partly to appeal to the new king’s interests and fears about witchcraft and the threat it posed to royal authority.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Jacobean Attitudes to Witchcraft</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">In the early seventeenth century, most people believed that witches were real. The 1604 Witchcraft Act made it a capital offence to invoke evil spirits. Witches were thought to be servants of the Devil who could predict the future, control the weather, cause disease, and destroy crops. Their <dfn class="term" data-def="Animal companions believed to be given to witches by the Devil, such as cats, toads, or ravens.">familiars</dfn> — animals like cats and toads — were considered proof of their pact with Satan.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Shakespeare taps directly into these fears. In Act 1, Scene 1, the witches’ familiars call to them: “I come, Graymalkin!” (a cat) and “Paddock calls” (a toad). A Jacobean audience would immediately recognise these as signs of demonic allegiance. The witches’ ability to predict the battle’s outcome — “When the hurlyburly’s done, / When the battle’s lost and won” — demonstrates supernatural knowledge that only the Devil could provide.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">The Divine Right of Kings</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Jacobean society was structured around the <dfn class="term" data-def="The belief that a monarch is chosen by God and that challenging or killing the king is a sin against God himself.">divine right of kings</dfn> — the belief that the monarch was God’s chosen representative on Earth. To kill a king (regicide) was not just murder; it was an act against God. This belief was supported by the <dfn class="term" data-def="The medieval and early modern belief that God created a strict hierarchy: God, angels, the king, nobles, commoners, animals. Disrupting this order invited divine punishment.">Great Chain of Being</dfn>, a strict hierarchy placing God at the top, then the king, then nobles, commoners, and animals.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">When Macbeth murders Duncan, he does not just commit a political crime — he tears apart the God-given order of the universe. Shakespeare makes the consequences immediate and dramatic: nature itself rebels. In Act 2, Scene 4, Ross and the Old Man describe how “dark night strangles the travelling lamp” (the sun is blocked), a falcon is killed by an owl (a lower creature defeating a noble one), and Duncan’s horses “turned wild in nature” and ate each other. These unnatural events show that regicide disrupts the entire Chain of Being.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Shakespeare’s Use of the Supernatural</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">The supernatural in <em>Macbeth</em> serves several dramatic purposes. First, it drives the plot: the witches’ prophecies ignite Macbeth’s ambition. Second, it reflects Jacobean fears: audiences genuinely believed that engaging with the supernatural invited damnation. Third, it raises the central question of the play — <dfn class="term" data-def="A key theme in Macbeth exploring whether events are predetermined by outside forces or the result of personal choices.">fate versus free will</dfn>. Are the witches controlling Macbeth, or simply revealing desires that already exist within him?</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n16">This line from the witches establishes the theme of appearance versus reality that runs through the entire play. What seems good (“fair”) may actually be evil (“foul”), and vice versa. Macbeth echoes this unknowingly in Act 1, Scene 3: “So foul and fair a day I have not seen.” His language mirrors the witches before he even meets them, suggesting he is already connected to the forces of darkness. Duncan’s line “There’s no art to find the mind’s construction in the face” (Act 1, Scene 4) further reinforces the idea that appearances cannot be trusted — he says this about the treacherous Thane of Cawdor, unaware that Macbeth will prove even more dangerous.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17">Shakespeare develops this motif through Lady Macbeth’s instruction to “look like the innocent flower, but be the serpent under’t” and Macbeth’s own admission: “False face must hide what the false heart doth know.” The entire play asks the audience to look beneath the surface.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">The Gunpowder Plot Connection</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Shakespeare likely wrote <em>Macbeth</em> in 1606, just a year after the Gunpowder Plot — a failed attempt to assassinate King James I by blowing up Parliament. The theme of regicide would have been incredibly topical. The play’s message is clear: those who kill kings are punished. Macbeth’s downfall reassures the audience (and the king) that the divine order will always be restored. The Porter’s reference to an “equivocator” in Act 2, Scene 3 is widely believed to be a direct allusion to Father Henry Garnet, a Catholic priest executed for his involvement in the Gunpowder Plot, who defended the use of equivocation (deliberately misleading answers) under oath.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">By connecting regicide to damnation and showing its perpetrators destroyed, Shakespeare flatters James I and reinforces the political message that the king’s authority is sacred and God-given.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n21">Context is assessed through AO3, but you must embed it within your analysis — never bolt it on as a separate paragraph. Instead of writing “Jacobeans believed in witchcraft,” link it to a quotation: “Shakespeare’s use of familiars like ‘Graymalkin’ and ‘Paddock’ would have immediately signalled demonic allegiance to a Jacobean audience.” For the 34-mark essay, context should appear naturally in every paragraph, not as a standalone section.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n22">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n23">Shakespeare wrote <em>Macbeth</em> for a Jacobean audience that genuinely feared witchcraft and believed in the divine right of kings — context that intensifies every supernatural scene.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n24">The witches’ paradox “Fair is foul, and foul is fair” establishes the central theme of appearance versus reality and is echoed by Macbeth before he even meets them.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25">Regicide disrupts the Great Chain of Being, and Shakespeare uses pathetic fallacy and unnatural events to show that killing a king has cosmic consequences.</li></ul>
