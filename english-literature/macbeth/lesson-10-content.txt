# The Ending & Key Themes

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Final Battle: Scenes Seven to Nine</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The closing scenes of <em>Macbeth</em> build rapidly towards the final confrontation. Shakespeare switches between short scenes inside Macbeth’s castle and scenes following Malcolm’s advancing army, creating a pace that generates tension and excitement. Macbeth compares himself to a bear tied to a stake: ‘They have tied me to a stake; I cannot fly, / But, bear-like, I must fight the course.’ This is a reference to <dfn class="term" data-def="A popular Elizabethan and Jacobean entertainment where a bear was chained to a post and attacked by dogs, held in arenas near theatres like the Globe.">bear-baiting</dfn>, a blood sport held in arenas next to the Globe Theatre. The bear is chained and cannot escape — it must fight or die. Macbeth recognises that he is trapped by fate, yet chooses to go down fighting rather than surrender.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">When Macbeth kills Young Siward, he boasts: ‘Thou wast born of woman. / But swords I smile at, weapons laugh to scorn, / Brandish’d by man that’s of a woman born.’ His confidence in the witches’ prophecy makes him arrogant and reckless. Shakespeare deliberately places this boast just before Macduff enters — the one man who was <em>not</em> ‘of woman born.’ The audience feels the dramatic irony tightening.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Birnam Wood comes to Dunsinane when Malcolm orders his soldiers: ‘Let every soldier hew him down a bough / And bear’t before him.’ The soldiers carry tree branches as camouflage. This fulfils the third apparition’s prophecy in a way Macbeth never imagined. Shakespeare shows that the witches told the truth — but in a way designed to deceive. This is <dfn class="term" data-def="When the outcome of a situation is the opposite of what was expected, often used to highlight a character’s misunderstanding.">situational irony</dfn>.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Macduff vs Macbeth</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">When Macduff confronts Macbeth, he calls him ‘Turn, hell-hound, turn!’ The compound noun ‘hell-hound’ combines the imagery of damnation with a hunting dog — Macbeth has become a demonic, predatory creature. A hound pursues and hunts relentlessly, suggesting Macbeth’s tyranny has been an unrelenting assault on Scotland. The alliteration in ‘hell-hound’ makes the insult sharp and percussive, heightening the drama of the moment.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Macbeth initially refuses to fight Macduff: ‘Of all men else I have avoided thee. / But get thee back; my soul is too much charged / With blood of thine already.’ The word ‘soul’ is significant — Macbeth still has enough conscience to acknowledge the weight of his crimes against Macduff’s family. But this could also be a taunt: ‘I have already destroyed your family; there is no need for more.’ Macduff’s response is devastatingly simple: ‘My voice is in my sword.’ Words are no longer enough; only action will deliver justice.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">The Prophecy Fulfilled: ‘Untimely Ripp’d’</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Macbeth declares ‘I bear a charmed life, which must not yield / To one of woman born.’ Macduff’s reply shatters his confidence: ‘Despair thy charm; / And let the angel whom thou still hast served / Tell thee, Macduff was from his mother’s womb / Untimely ripp’d.’ Macduff was delivered by Caesarean section — technically not ‘born’ of woman in the natural sense. The prophecy is fulfilled, but not as Macbeth expected.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">Macbeth’s reaction is a moment of <dfn class="term" data-def="The moment in a Greek tragedy when the tragic hero recognises the truth about their situation, often too late to change the outcome.">anagnorisis</dfn> — the tragic recognition. He curses the witches: ‘And be these juggling fiends no more believed, / That palter with us in a double sense; / That keep the word of promise to our ear, / And break it to our hope.’ The word ‘double’ echoes through the play — ‘double trust’, ‘double, double, toil and trouble’ — and now Macbeth finally understands that the witches’ equivocation has been his undoing all along. Banquo’s warning from Act One is proved right: ‘The instruments of darkness tell us truths, / Win us with honest trifles, to betray’s / In deepest consequence.’</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Malcolm’s Final Speech: Restoring Order</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Macduff enters carrying Macbeth’s severed head and declares: ‘Behold, where stands / The usurper’s cursed head: the time is free.’ The word ‘free’ signals the end of tyranny. Malcolm is proclaimed King of Scotland and delivers a speech that restores order. He promotes his thanes to earls — ‘the first that ever Scotland / In such an honour named’ — rewarding loyalty just as his father Duncan did. He calls home those exiled by ‘the snares of watchful tyranny.’</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Crucially, Malcolm describes Macbeth and Lady Macbeth as ‘this dead butcher and his fiend-like queen.’ ‘Butcher’ reduces Macbeth to a mindless killer, while ‘fiend-like’ associates Lady Macbeth with the Devil. These labels are Malcolm’s political narrative — but the audience knows the characters are more complex than these simple summaries. The play’s tragedy lies in the fact that Macbeth was once a ‘valiant’, ‘worthy gentleman’ and Lady Macbeth was once a devoted partner who was ultimately destroyed by guilt.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Malcolm’s coronation at ‘Scone’ — the traditional site of Scottish coronations — symbolises the restoration of the Great Chain of Being. The rightful king is back on the throne, Scotland is healed, and the natural order is restored. This ending would have pleased King James I, reinforcing the message that challenging the Divine Right of Kings leads to destruction.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Thematic Overview: The Whole Play</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">For the exam, you need to be able to discuss the play’s key themes across all five acts. Here is a summary of the most important themes and how they develop.</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Key Themes Across the Play</span>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Ambition:</strong> Macbeth’s <dfn class="term" data-def="A fatal flaw in a tragic hero’s character that leads to their downfall — from Aristotle’s theory of tragedy.">hamartia</dfn> (fatal flaw). The witches’ prophecies ignite it, Lady Macbeth fans it, and Macbeth acts on it. The play asks: is ambition itself evil, or only when it leads to immoral action?</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Kingship &amp; Tyranny:</strong> Three kings: Duncan (virtuous but naive), Macbeth (tyrannical), Malcolm (wise and just). Shakespeare presents a spectrum of rule, with Macbeth as the warning against disrupting the Divine Right.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>The Supernatural:</strong> Witches, prophecies, apparitions, ghosts, hallucinations. Do the witches cause Macbeth’s downfall, or does he choose his own path? The play balances fate and free will without giving a definitive answer.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Guilt:</strong> Tracked through the blood and hand-washing motifs. Macbeth’s guilt evolves from horror (‘Will all great Neptune’s ocean wash this blood?’) to resignation (‘I am in blood, stepp’d in so far’) to nihilism (‘signifying nothing’). Lady Macbeth’s guilt evolves from denial (‘A little water’) to madness (‘Out, damned spot!’).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Appearance vs Reality:</strong> ‘Fair is foul and foul is fair’, ‘look like the innocent flower but be the serpent under’t’, the witches’ equivocal prophecies, Malcolm’s test of Macduff. Nothing in <em>Macbeth</em> is what it seems.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Masculinity:</strong> Lady Macbeth equates manliness with violence (‘unsex me here’). Macbeth adopts this view. Macduff challenges it: ‘I must also feel it as a man.’ Shakespeare presents toxic masculinity as destructive and emotional honesty as true strength.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n26">In the exam, if you get an extract from the final scenes, make sure you discuss how the ending resolves the play’s key tensions. Link Malcolm’s ‘dead butcher and fiend-like queen’ back to the characters’ complexity throughout. If asked about fate vs free will, use the final battle to argue both sides: the prophecies are fulfilled (fate) but Macbeth chose to seek out the witches and act on their words (free will). Always end your essay with a clear, concise statement about Shakespeare’s overall message — e.g. ‘Shakespeare warns that unchecked ambition, combined with the corruption of the supernatural, leads to the destruction of both the individual and the state.’</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n27">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n28">The prophecies are fulfilled through situational irony: Birnam Wood ‘moves’ as camouflage, Macduff was ‘untimely ripp’d’ by Caesarean. Macbeth’s anagnorisis comes when he realises the witches ‘palter with us in a double sense.’</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29">Malcolm’s coronation restores order: he is the wise, just king that Scotland needs. His summary of Macbeth as ‘this dead butcher’ is politically convenient but overly simplistic — the audience has seen Macbeth’s full tragic arc.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30">For exam revision, know the six key themes (ambition, kingship, supernatural, guilt, appearance vs reality, masculinity), track each one across all five acts, and always link to Jacobean context (Divine Right, Great Chain, Gunpowder Plot, witchcraft beliefs).</li></ul>
