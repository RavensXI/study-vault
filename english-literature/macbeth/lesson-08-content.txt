# Macduff & Scotland's Suffering

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Act Four Scene Two: Macduff’s Family</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Act Four Scene Two takes place in Macduff’s castle after he has fled to England to join Malcolm. Lady Macduff is furious that her husband has abandoned the family. She complains that he ‘wants the natural touch’ — meaning he lacks the instinct to protect his loved ones. Shakespeare creates a powerful contrast: while Macbeth orders the murder of innocents, Macduff is seeking help to save Scotland but leaves his family defenceless.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The conversation between Lady Macduff and her young son is tender and darkly comic. The boy’s innocence is heartbreaking because the audience already knows, through <dfn class="term" data-def="When the audience knows something that the characters on stage do not, creating tension as the audience anticipates what will happen.">dramatic irony</dfn>, that murderers are on their way. When Lady Macduff asks her son how he will live without a father, he replies: ‘As birds do, mother.’ This simple line carries enormous weight — it echoes a Bible verse from the Gospel of Matthew: ‘Look at the birds of the air; they neither sow nor reap… and yet your heavenly Father feeds them.’ The boy’s faith in providence is about to be brutally destroyed.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>When the murderers arrive, the young son calls them ‘thou shag-hair’d villain’ and is stabbed on stage. The murderer dismisses him as ‘Young fry of treachery!’ and ‘What, you egg!’ The brutality of killing a child on stage would shock any audience. Shakespeare uses this moment to show Macbeth’s tyranny has reached its most extreme point.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Bird Motif</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Shakespeare uses the <dfn class="term" data-def="A recurring image, symbol, or idea throughout a literary text that develops or reinforces a theme.">motif</dfn> of birds throughout <em>Macbeth</em> to reflect the natural order and its disruption. In this scene, Lady Macduff says: ‘the poor wren, / The most diminutive of birds, will fight, / Her young ones in her nest, against the owl.’ The wren represents the small and vulnerable (Macduff’s family), while the owl represents the predator (Macbeth). Even the tiniest bird will defend its young — a pointed criticism of Macduff for leaving.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Act Four Scene Three: Malcolm’s Test</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Scene Three is the longest scene in <em>Macbeth</em> and takes place in England. Macduff has come to persuade Malcolm to raise an army against Macbeth. But Malcolm is suspicious: how does he know Macduff is not a spy sent by Macbeth? He devises a test — he pretends to be an even worse person than Macbeth, claiming he is lustful, greedy, and would ‘pour the sweet milk of concord into hell.’</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Macduff is devastated: ‘O Scotland, Scotland!’ His despair proves his loyalty — a true patriot would be horrified at the prospect of another tyrant. Malcolm reveals it was a test and lists the ‘king-becoming graces’: ‘justice, verity, temperance, stableness, / Bounty, perseverance, mercy, lowliness, / Devotion, patience, courage, fortitude.’ This list contrasts directly with Macbeth’s qualities and outlines what a good king should be. Shakespeare uses Malcolm to present an ideal of <dfn class="term" data-def="The theme of how power should be exercised, including the qualities of a good ruler versus a tyrant.">kingship</dfn> that Macbeth has corrupted.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Malcolm compares himself to a ‘weak poor innocent lamb’ offered to ‘an angry god’ (Macbeth). This biblical allusion connects Malcolm to Christ (the Lamb of God) and Macbeth to a false deity demanding sacrifice. Shakespeare positions Malcolm as Scotland’s saviour and Macbeth as a demonic tyrant.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Scotland’s Suffering Under Tyranny</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Macduff describes Scotland’s agony under Macbeth’s rule: ‘Each new morn / New widows howl, new orphans cry, new sorrows / Strike heaven on the face.’ The <dfn class="term" data-def="The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses for emphasis.">anaphora</dfn> of ‘new’ creates a relentless rhythm, emphasising that suffering compounds daily. Malcolm echoes this: ‘I think our country sinks beneath the yoke; / It weeps, it bleeds, and each new day a gash / Is added to her wounds.’ A yoke is a device used to restrain oxen — Scotland is enslaved, treated like an animal under Macbeth’s tyranny. The personification of Scotland as a bleeding, weeping woman reinforces the violation of the natural order.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Ross arrives from Scotland and delivers devastating news. When Macduff asks about his family, Ross uses euphemism: ‘they were well at peace when I did leave ’em.’ ‘At peace’ means dead. The dramatic irony is painful — the audience already knows the family has been murdered.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Macduff’s Grief &amp; Masculinity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">When the truth emerges, Macduff’s grief is raw: ‘All my pretty ones? / Did you say all? O hell-kite! All? / What, all my pretty chickens and their dam / At one fell swoop?’ The repetition of ‘all’ shows his disbelief. The bird imagery returns: his family were ‘pretty chickens’ slaughtered by a ‘hell-kite’ — Macbeth. ‘Fell swoop’ means a fierce, sudden attack by a bird of prey.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Malcolm tells Macduff to ‘Dispute it like a man.’ Macduff’s response is one of the most important lines on masculinity in the play: ‘I shall do so; / But I must also feel it as a man.’ This directly challenges the Macbeths’ version of manhood, where masculinity meant cruelty and suppressing emotion. Macduff shows that true manliness includes the capacity to grieve, to love, and to feel — and then to act with courage. This redefinition of masculinity is central to the play’s moral message.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n23">Act 4 Scene 3 is rich in exam material. If asked about kingship, compare Malcolm’s ‘king-becoming graces’ with Macbeth’s tyranny. If asked about masculinity, contrast Macduff’s ‘I must also feel it as a man’ with Lady Macbeth’s ‘unsex me here.’ For the bird motif, track it across at least three scenes. Always show you understand that Shakespeare is making deliberate choices — use phrases like ‘Shakespeare contrasts…’, ‘Shakespeare uses the motif of… to suggest…’</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n24">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n25">The murder of Macduff’s family (Act 4 Scene 2) represents the lowest point of Macbeth’s tyranny — he now slaughters innocent women and children. The bird motif (wren vs owl, chickens vs hell-kite) runs through the play to reflect the destruction of the natural order.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n26">Malcolm’s test of Macduff (Act 4 Scene 3) establishes the ‘king-becoming graces’ and positions Malcolm as the rightful, virtuous king who will restore Scotland.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n27">Macduff’s line ‘I must also feel it as a man’ redefines masculinity as strength through emotional honesty, directly challenging the toxic masculinity promoted by Macbeth and Lady Macbeth.</li></ul>
