# Ambition & the Prophecies

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Act 1, Scene 3 — The Prophecies</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Act 1, Scene 3 is one of the most important scenes in the play. The <dfn class="term" data-def="Three supernatural figures in Macbeth, also called the ‘weird sisters,’ who deliver prophecies that drive the plot. They represent fate, temptation, and the dangers of the supernatural.">witches</dfn> deliver three prophecies to Macbeth: that he will be Thane of Glamis (which he already is), Thane of Cawdor, and “King hereafter.” These prophecies act as the catalyst for the entire tragedy. Without them, Macbeth may never have considered regicide.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Before the witches appear, Shakespeare gives us a glimpse of their petty cruelty. The First Witch describes how she has shipwrecked a sailor’s husband because his wife refused to share her chestnuts. This small act of revenge establishes an important point: the witches are malicious, vengeful, and use their power to punish ordinary people. They then recite an <dfn class="term" data-def="A spoken charm or spell, often using rhythmic, repetitive language associated with witchcraft and dark magic.">incantation</dfn> before Macbeth and Banquo arrive.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The witches deliver three prophecies to Macbeth: (1) Thane of Glamis, (2) Thane of Cawdor, (3) “King hereafter.” Two come true almost immediately when Ross arrives to announce Macbeth has been given the title Thane of Cawdor — and this is what convinces Macbeth the third prophecy might also be fulfilled.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Macbeth’s First Words</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Macbeth’s very first line in the play is: “So foul and fair a day I have not seen.” This is deeply significant. He echoes the witches’ chant from Act 1, Scene 1 (“Fair is foul, and foul is fair”) before he has even met them. Shakespeare uses this verbal echo to suggest that Macbeth is already connected to the supernatural forces of darkness — or, at the very least, that his mind naturally gravitates toward the same moral ambiguity the witches represent.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">When Banquo first sees the witches, he is confused by their appearance: “What are these? So withered and so wild in their attire, that look not like th’ inhabitants o’ th’ earth, and yet are on’t.” The witches do not belong to the natural world. Banquo describes them as being “on” the earth but not “of” it — they exist outside the natural order, which connects them to the forces that oppose the Great Chain of Being.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n8">The witches also deliver three prophecies to Banquo, but his are deliberately <dfn class="term" data-def="Having a double meaning or being open to more than one interpretation, creating uncertainty for the audience.">ambiguous</dfn>: he will be “lesser than Macbeth, and greater,” “not so happy, yet much happier,” and “thou shalt get kings, though thou be none.” The paradoxical phrasing means Banquo will never be king himself, but his descendants will rule.     <p data-narration-id="n9">The ambiguity of the prophecies is central to the play’s exploration of fate versus free will. The witches never tell Macbeth to kill Duncan — they simply state what “will” happen. It is Macbeth who decides to act on their words, and this choice is what makes him a tragic figure rather than a mere victim of fate.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Macbeth’s Reaction — “Why Do I Yield to That Suggestion?”</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">When Ross and Angus arrive to confirm that Macbeth has been made Thane of Cawdor, two of the three prophecies have come true. Macbeth is initially confused — he asks why Ross dresses him “in borrowed robes” — but his thoughts quickly turn to the third prophecy. In an aside, he reveals his inner turmoil: “This supernatural soliciting / Cannot be ill, cannot be good.” He is caught between temptation and conscience.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The thought of murder enters his mind unbidden: “My thought, whose murder yet is but fantastical, / Shakes so my single state of man.” Shakespeare shows that the idea of killing Duncan is already forming in Macbeth’s imagination, yet it terrifies him. His “seated heart” knocks at his “ribs” — a visceral, physical reaction that reveals his moral conflict. Crucially, Macbeth then resolves to leave it to chance: “If chance will have me king, why, chance may crown me, / Without my stir.” At this point, he decides not to act. This resolution will not last.</p>

  <p>Macbeth’s <dfn class="term" data-def="A fatal flaw in a tragic hero’s character that leads to their downfall. In Macbeth, his hamartia is his unchecked ambition.">hamartia</dfn> (tragic flaw) is his ambition. The witches do not create his desire for the crown — they reveal a desire that already exists. Shakespeare makes this clear through the aside: the thought of murder is “but fantastical,” yet it already “shakes” him to his core.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Banquo as a Foil</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Shakespeare uses Banquo as a <dfn class="term" data-def="A character who contrasts with another character to highlight specific qualities. Banquo’s caution and morality highlight Macbeth’s ambition and moral weakness.">foil</dfn> for Macbeth. Where Macbeth is immediately drawn to the prophecies, Banquo is cautious and sceptical. He warns Macbeth: “And oftentimes, to win us to our harm, / The instruments of darkness tell us truths, / Win us with honest trifles, to betray’s / In deepest consequence.”</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">This is one of the most important warnings in the entire play. Banquo recognises that the witches (“instruments of darkness”) may tell small truths to gain trust, only to deceive when it matters most. He is proved right by the end of the play: the prophecies lead Macbeth to destruction. Banquo’s ability to resist temptation while Macbeth succumbs is what makes him Macbeth’s moral opposite.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n17">In Act 1, Scene 4, Duncan publicly names his son Malcolm as Prince of Cumberland — making Malcolm the official heir to the throne. This is the moment that transforms Macbeth’s passive ambition into active plotting. In an aside, Macbeth says: “Stars, hide your fires; / Let not light see my black and deep desires.” The light/dark imagery reflects the theme of appearance versus reality: Macbeth wants to conceal his murderous thoughts from the world and from God. The verb “hide” suggests he knows his desires are sinful.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18">Duncan’s trust in Macbeth is deeply ironic. He has just stated that “There’s no art to find the mind’s construction in the face” — a comment about the previous Thane of Cawdor’s betrayal — and then immediately places his full trust in Macbeth, the new Thane of Cawdor. Shakespeare uses this <dfn class="term" data-def="When the audience knows something that a character on stage does not, creating tension and emotional engagement.">dramatic irony</dfn> to show Duncan’s fatal weakness: he is too trusting, which ultimately costs him his life.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">The Seeds of Tragedy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">By the end of Act 1, Scene 4, all the ingredients for tragedy are in place. Macbeth has been tempted by the prophecies, his ambition has been inflamed by Malcolm’s appointment as heir, and he has already begun to conceal his true intentions. Banquo’s warning has gone unheeded. The witches have set the trap, and Macbeth is walking into it. The question the audience must consider is whether Macbeth had any real choice — or whether his ambition made this outcome inevitable from the moment the witches spoke.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n21">When analysing the witches’ prophecies, always discuss the theme of fate versus free will. The witches never tell Macbeth to kill Duncan — he chooses to act. This distinction is crucial for top-band responses. Use Banquo’s warning about “instruments of darkness” as a counter-argument: if Banquo can resist, Macbeth’s downfall is a result of character, not just fate.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n22">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n23">The witches’ prophecies act as a catalyst for the tragedy, but they never instruct Macbeth to kill — his ambition drives his choices, raising the central question of fate versus free will.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n24">Banquo serves as Macbeth’s moral foil: he hears the same prophecies but resists temptation, warning that “the instruments of darkness tell us truths” to betray us.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25">Duncan’s decision to name Malcolm as heir is the trigger that turns Macbeth’s passive ambition into active plotting, revealed through his “black and deep desires” aside.</li></ul>
