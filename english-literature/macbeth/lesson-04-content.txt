# The Murder of Duncan

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Act 2, Scene 1 — Banquo and Fleance</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Act 2 opens in darkness. Banquo and his son Fleance walk through Macbeth’s castle late at night, with Fleance carrying a torch. The torch is symbolic: it represents truth, hope, and the future. Fleance is Banquo’s heir — the child through whom the witches’ prophecy (“thou shalt get kings, though thou be none”) will be fulfilled. Shakespeare uses the light of the torch to contrast with the darkness of what Macbeth is about to do.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Banquo tells Fleance: “There’s husbandry in heaven; / Their candles are all out.” The stars have been extinguished — <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of extended metaphor where an object or idea represents something beyond its literal meaning. Throughout Macbeth, darkness symbolises evil, guilt, and the concealment of sin.">symbolically</dfn>, heaven has turned its lights off, leaving the world in moral darkness. This echoes Macbeth’s own call in Act 1, Scene 4: “Stars, hide your fires.” The imagery suggests that God has withdrawn, leaving space for evil to act.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Banquo confesses to “cursed thoughts” that disturb his sleep. In Jacobean England, bad dreams were believed to be caused by demons. Unlike Macbeth, Banquo prays to “merciful powers” for protection — while the Macbeths ask dark spirits for help. This contrast reinforces Banquo as Macbeth’s moral foil.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n5">When Macbeth enters, Banquo mentions he has “dreamt last night of the three weird sisters” and notes they have “shown some truth.” Macbeth lies, claiming he does not think about them — creating <dfn class="term" data-def="When the audience knows something that a character on stage does not. Here, the audience knows Macbeth is planning Duncan’s murder while Banquo does not.">dramatic irony</dfn>. Macbeth cryptically offers Banquo future rewards (“It shall make honour for you”), hinting at his plans without revealing them. The tension between the two men is palpable: Banquo suspects something, but cannot articulate what.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">The Dagger Soliloquy — “Is This a Dagger Which I See Before Me?”</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Once alone, Macbeth delivers one of Shakespeare’s most famous <dfn class="term" data-def="A speech delivered by a character alone on stage, revealing their inner thoughts directly to the audience. Macbeth’s dagger soliloquy reveals his psychological torment before the murder.">soliloquies</dfn>. He sees a dagger floating before him, its handle pointing toward his hand: “Is this a dagger which I see before me, / The handle toward my hand? Come, let me clutch thee.”</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">The hallucination raises the central question: is the dagger a supernatural sign leading Macbeth to murder, or a projection of his own guilty mind? Macbeth himself asks: “Art thou not, fatal vision, sensible / To feeling as to sight? Or art thou but / A dagger of the mind, a false creation, / Proceeding from the heat-oppressed brain?” He recognises it may be a hallucination caused by stress, yet he follows it anyway.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>“Come, let me clutch thee”</strong> — The imperative “Come” shows Macbeth’s desperation to seize the vision, while “clutch” suggests a grasping, almost violent desire to hold the weapon. He simultaneously wants to act and is terrified of doing so.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>“And on thy blade and dudgeon, gouts of blood”</strong> — The dagger becomes covered in blood before Macbeth has even committed the murder. This foreshadows the guilt that will consume both Macbeth and Lady Macbeth. Blood on the handle (“dudgeon”) as well as the blade suggests that the guilt will spread to everyone involved.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>“There’s no such thing”</strong> — Macbeth deliberately rejects the hallucination. This is a crucial turning point: by recognising the dagger as unreal, Shakespeare confirms that Macbeth is sane. He cannot blame his actions on madness or supernatural compulsion. What follows is a conscious choice.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>“Nature seems dead, and wicked dreams abuse / The curtained sleep”</strong> — Shakespeare creates a semantic field of evil and the supernatural. “Nature seems dead” foreshadows the disruption of the natural order that regicide will cause. “Wicked dreams” connects to Banquo’s “cursed thoughts,” but Macbeth embraces the darkness rather than resisting it.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Hecate, the Wolf, and Tarquin</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">As the soliloquy progresses, Macbeth draws on increasingly dark <dfn class="term" data-def="References within a text to other well-known stories, myths, or figures. Macbeth alludes to Hecate (Greek goddess of witchcraft), wolves, and Tarquin (a Roman tyrant) to align himself with evil forces.">allusions</dfn>. He references “pale Hecate’s offerings” — Hecate is the Greek goddess of witchcraft, magic, and the night. In the play, she directs the three witches and orchestrates Macbeth’s downfall. By mentioning Hecate, Macbeth aligns himself with the supernatural forces he should be resisting.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">He compares himself to a wolf (“withered murder, / Alarumed by his sentinel, the wolf”) and to Tarquin, a Roman who crept into a woman’s bedroom to commit rape. Both allusions associate Macbeth with predatory violence and moral corruption. Shakespeare builds a semantic field of evil — witchcraft, wolves, ghosts, darkness — that surrounds Macbeth as he moves toward Duncan’s chamber.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">The Bell and the Rhyming Couplet</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The soliloquy ends with a bell ringing — Lady Macbeth’s signal that the coast is clear. Macbeth’s final words are a <dfn class="term" data-def="A pair of rhyming lines that appear together, often used to create a sense of finality. Macbeth’s couplet about the bell seals Duncan’s fate.">rhyming couplet</dfn>: “I go, and it is done; the bell invites me. / Hear it not, Duncan, for it is a knell / That summons thee to heaven or to hell.” The word “knell” means a funeral bell — Macbeth is pronouncing Duncan’s death sentence. The rhyming couplet creates a sense of finality and inevitability. The juxtaposition of “heaven” and “hell” in the final line encapsulates the play’s moral conflict: Duncan will go to heaven as an innocent victim, while Macbeth is choosing hell.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Shakespeare never shows Duncan’s murder on stage. This was likely because regicide was considered too sinful to depict. The offstage murder forces the audience to imagine the act, which can be more powerful than seeing it. It also keeps the focus on Macbeth’s psychological torment rather than physical violence.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Act 2, Scene 2 — The Immediate Aftermath</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">After the murder, Macbeth returns to Lady Macbeth in a state of extreme distress. His first words are: “I have done the deed.” The simple, monosyllabic language contrasts sharply with the elaborate poetry of the dagger soliloquy — the reality of murder has stripped him of eloquence. He carries the daggers with him, having forgotten to leave them with the guards. Lady Macbeth chastises him: “Why did you bring these daggers from the place? / They must lie there.” When Macbeth refuses to return, she takes them herself: “Infirm of purpose! / Give me the daggers.”</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n21">One of the most psychologically revealing moments occurs when Macbeth describes hearing voices pray. Two people said “God bless us!” and “Amen,” but Macbeth could not join them: “I had most need of blessing, and ‘Amen’ / Stuck in my throat.” For a Jacobean audience, this was terrifying. If Macbeth cannot say “Amen,” he has been cut off from God’s grace. His act of regicide has severed his relationship with the divine. The physical metaphor of the word “stuck” in his throat suggests that his guilt is already manifesting physically.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22">Macbeth also hears a voice cry: “Sleep no more! / Macbeth does murder sleep.” Sleep represents innocence, peace, and the natural order. By killing Duncan, Macbeth has murdered his own ability to rest. This prophecy comes true: Macbeth is plagued by guilt and paranoia for the rest of the play, and Lady Macbeth’s sleepwalking in Act 5 shows that she, too, has lost the gift of peaceful sleep.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n23">The dagger soliloquy is a favourite extract for the exam. When analysing it, focus on the theme of fate versus free will: the dagger seems to lead Macbeth to Duncan’s chamber, but Macbeth himself says “There’s no such thing” — he recognises the hallucination and acts anyway. This proves his guilt is the result of choice, not madness. Always link the soliloquy’s imagery (darkness, blood, Hecate) to Jacobean beliefs about the supernatural and regicide.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n24">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n25">The dagger soliloquy reveals Macbeth’s inner turmoil: the hallucination raises the question of fate versus free will, but his rejection of it (“There’s no such thing”) confirms that the murder is a conscious choice.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n26">Shakespeare keeps Duncan’s murder offstage, focusing on Macbeth’s psychological breakdown — his inability to say “Amen,” the voice crying “Sleep no more!”, and his refusal to return the daggers.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n27">The allusions to Hecate, Tarquin, and the wolf build a semantic field of evil that surrounds Macbeth as he moves toward the murder, linking him to the supernatural forces he should resist.</li></ul>
