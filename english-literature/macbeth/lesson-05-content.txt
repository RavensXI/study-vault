# Guilt & Consequences

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Blood and Water — The Central Motif of Act 2</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">After the murder, Shakespeare uses the <dfn class="term" data-def="A recurring image, symbol, or idea that runs through a text, gaining significance each time it appears. Blood is the dominant motif in Macbeth, representing guilt, violence, and the consequences of sin.">motif</dfn> of blood to explore the Macbeths’ contrasting reactions to their crime. Macbeth stares at his hands in horror: “This is a sorry sight.” Lady Macbeth dismisses his anguish: “A foolish thought, to say a sorry sight.” The word “foolish” shows she still views emotional responses as weakness. But the blood on their hands will become the most important symbol in the play.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Macbeth’s despair reaches its peak with the line: “Will all great Neptune’s ocean wash this blood / Clean from my hand? No, this my hand will rather / The multitudinous seas incarnadine, / Making the green one red.” The <dfn class="term" data-def="Deliberate exaggeration used for emphasis or dramatic effect. Macbeth’s claim that his bloody hands would turn entire oceans red shows the overwhelming scale of his guilt.">hyperbole</dfn> is extraordinary: rather than the ocean cleaning his hands, his guilt is so enormous that his hands would turn the entire sea from green to red. The word “incardnadine” (to make red) is a Latinate, polysyllabic word that contrasts sharply with the monosyllabic Anglo-Saxon simplicity of “making the green one red” — Shakespeare moves from complex, elevated language to blunt, raw directness, as if the horror strips away Macbeth’s ability to be eloquent.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Macbeth appeals to Neptune, a <em>pagan</em> Roman god, rather than the Christian God. For a Jacobean audience, this signals that Macbeth has been spiritually cut off from Christianity by his act of regicide. He is already damned — he cannot pray, he cannot say “Amen,” and now he turns to a pagan deity for help that will never come.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Lady Macbeth’s Contrasting Response</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Lady Macbeth’s reaction to the blood is strikingly different. She returns from planting the daggers on the guards and says: “My hands are of your colour; but I shame / To wear a heart so white.” The colour imagery is crucial: “your colour” refers to the red of Duncan’s blood (guilt), while “a heart so white” implies cowardice. She is shaming Macbeth for his emotional response while claiming equal guilt but superior composure.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">She then declares: “A little water clears us of this deed.” This line is devastatingly ironic. Lady Macbeth believes that physical washing can remove moral guilt — that the crime is simply a practical problem. This <dfn class="term" data-def="A literary technique where a writer hints at future plot developments. Lady Macbeth’s casual attitude to blood foreshadows her obsessive hand-washing and guilt-driven madness in Act 5.">foreshadowing</dfn> is among the most powerful in the play. In Act 5, Scene 1, the sleepwalking Lady Macbeth will cry: “Out, damned spot! Out, I say!” and “All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand.” The woman who dismissed guilt so easily is destroyed by it.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Act 2, Scene 3 — The Porter</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">The Porter scene is often overlooked as <dfn class="term" data-def="A humorous scene or character introduced to relieve tension after an intense dramatic moment. The Porter provides comic relief after Duncan’s murder, but his speech also carries a darker, symbolic meaning.">comic relief</dfn>, but it is far more significant than simple humour. The Porter, drunk from the previous night’s celebrations, imagines himself as the gatekeeper of Hell: “Here’s a knocking indeed! If a man were porter of hell-gate, he should have old turning the key.”</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">This metaphor is deeply appropriate. Macbeth’s castle has literally become Hell — a place where a king has been murdered, where sin reigns, and where the natural order has been destroyed. The Porter imagines admitting three sinners to Hell: a farmer who hoarded grain (self-interest), an <dfn class="term" data-def="Someone who uses deliberately ambiguous language to deceive without technically lying. The Porter’s equivocator alludes to Father Henry Garnet, executed for involvement in the Gunpowder Plot.">equivocator</dfn> who committed treason (deception), and a tailor who stole cloth (theft). Each sinner mirrors an aspect of Macbeth’s crime: he has acted out of self-interest, used deception, and stolen the royal crown from Duncan.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The constant knocking at the gate symbolises fate approaching. Macduff — the man who will eventually kill Macbeth — waits on the other side. The knocking is persistent and unavoidable, just as Macbeth’s punishment is inevitable. The Porter’s parting words, “I pray you, remember the porter,” are almost prophetic: remember his warning about the road to Hell.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">The Discovery of Duncan’s Body</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Macduff enters the castle and discovers Duncan’s body. His reaction is one of genuine, visceral horror: “O horror, horror, horror! / Tongue nor heart cannot conceive nor name thee!” The triple repetition of “horror” may echo the Holy Trinity (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit), suggesting that Duncan’s murder is a sin of cosmic proportions that offends God himself. Macduff calls the castle to wake: “Shake off this downy sleep, death’s counterfeit, / And look on death itself!” The phrase “death’s counterfeit” (sleep as a fake version of death) connects to Macbeth’s earlier line about murdering sleep.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Macduff:</strong> “The fountain of your blood is stopped” — Duncan is the source (“fountain”) of Malcolm and Donalbain’s royal bloodline. The metaphor suggests that the source of life and royalty has been cut off by Macbeth’s act.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Macbeth:</strong> “His silver skin laced with his golden blood” — Macbeth describes Duncan in precious, noble colours. “Silver” and “golden” emphasise Duncan’s royalty and purity. The elaborate language feels performative — Macbeth is acting the role of a grieving subject while hiding his guilt. Lady Macbeth had earlier said she would “gild the faces of the grooms” with blood — “gild” puns on “guilt.”</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Banquo:</strong> “Let us meet / And question this most bloody piece of work” — Banquo is level-headed and practical, calling for investigation rather than hysteria. Shakespeare presents him as a natural leader, foreshadowing his credibility as a threat to Macbeth.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Donalbain:</strong> “There’s daggers in men’s smiles” — a metaphor that perfectly captures the theme of duplicity. The people surrounding them appear friendly but may be murderous. Malcolm and Donalbain flee — to England and Ireland respectively — which ironically makes them look guilty.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">The Disruption of Nature</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">In Act 2, Scene 4, the Old Man and Ross discuss the unnatural events that have accompanied Duncan’s death. The sun has been blocked (“dark night strangles the travelling lamp”), a falcon has been killed by an owl, and Duncan’s horses have eaten each other. These events are not coincidental — they are Shakespeare’s way of showing that regicide has disrupted the Great Chain of Being. The natural world has been thrown into chaos because God’s representative on Earth has been murdered.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">The Old Man says these events are “unnatural, / Even like the deed that’s done.” The falcon (a noble bird representing Duncan) killed by an owl (a lower creature representing Macbeth) is a direct metaphor for the murder: a lesser being has destroyed a greater one, overturning the natural hierarchy. For a Jacobean audience, this was powerful evidence that the universe itself punishes those who defy the divine order.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n23">When the body is discovered, both Macbeths perform roles to deflect suspicion. Macbeth admits he killed the guards in a supposed fit of grief: “Yet I do repent me of my fury, / That I did kill them.” This destroys the evidence, but Macduff’s sharp question — “Wherefore did you so?” — shows he already suspects Macbeth’s motives. Macduff’s keen eye for hidden motives establishes him as Macbeth’s moral opposite and future nemesis.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24">Lady Macbeth faints — or pretends to. This diversion redirects attention away from Macbeth’s suspicious behaviour. She uses feminine weakness as a shield, exploiting Jacobean expectations that a woman would collapse at such news. Whether the faint is real or staged, it demonstrates Lady Macbeth’s strategic intelligence: even in crisis, she controls the narrative.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n25">When writing about blood imagery, always compare Macbeth’s reaction (“Neptune’s ocean”) with Lady Macbeth’s (“a little water”) and then connect both to Act 5 (“out, damned spot”). This shows the examiner you can track motifs across the whole play. The Porter scene is frequently underused in essays — linking the three sinners to Macbeth’s own crimes (self-interest, equivocation, theft) will demonstrate sophisticated understanding.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n26">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n27">Macbeth’s “Neptune’s ocean” speech reveals overwhelming guilt, while Lady Macbeth’s “a little water” dismisses it — a contrast that foreshadows their eventual role reversal in Act 5.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n28">The Porter scene is not just comic relief: the three sinners (farmer, equivocator, tailor) mirror Macbeth’s crimes, and the knocking symbolises fate approaching through Macduff.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29">Nature’s rebellion (blocked sun, falcon killed by owl, cannibal horses) shows that regicide disrupts the entire Great Chain of Being, reinforcing the Jacobean belief in divine punishment.</li></ul>
