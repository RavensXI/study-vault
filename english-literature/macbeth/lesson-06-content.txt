# Banquo & Deception

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Banquo’s Soliloquy &amp; Macbeth’s Fear</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Act Three opens with <dfn class="term" data-def="A speech in a play where a character speaks their thoughts aloud to the audience, usually alone on stage.">soliloquy</dfn> from Banquo. He suspects that Macbeth has cheated his way to the crown: ‘Thou hast it now: king, Cawdor, Glamis, all, / As the weird women promised, and, I fear, / Thou play’dst most foully for’t.’ The verb ‘foully’ echoes the witches’ chant ‘fair is foul and foul is fair’, linking Macbeth’s rise to the supernatural corruption that set it in motion. Yet Banquo also admits his own hope: the witches called him ‘root and father of many kings’. Shakespeare shows that even the moral Banquo is tempted by the prophecies, though crucially he does not act on them.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Macbeth, now king, is consumed by fear of Banquo. In his own soliloquy he confesses: ‘Our fears in Banquo / Stick deep.’ He admires Banquo’s ‘royalty of nature’ and ‘wisdom that doth guide his valour’, yet this admiration turns to resentment. The witches placed a ‘fruitless crown’ on Macbeth’s head and a ‘barren sceptre’ in his grip. These adjectives — ‘fruitless’ and ‘barren’ — suggest that Macbeth’s kingship is sterile and without legacy. He has murdered Duncan only to benefit Banquo’s descendants.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Macbeth says he has given his ‘mine eternal jewel’ (his soul) to ‘the common enemy of man’ (the Devil). This shows he recognises the moral price of regicide but feels trapped — he has already damned himself, so he may as well continue.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Theme of Deception in Act Three</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A past paper question asks: ‘Starting with Banquo’s soliloquy in Act 3 Scene 1, how does Shakespeare present ideas about deception?’ This is a key exam focus. Macbeth’s conversation with Banquo before the banquet is loaded with <dfn class="term" data-def="The underlying meaning behind what a character says, rather than the literal meaning of their words.">subtext</dfn>. He asks casually, ‘Is’t far you ride?’ — seemingly polite small talk, but actually establishing whether Banquo will be far enough from the castle to be ambushed. The instruction ‘Fail not our feast’ is darkly ironic: Macbeth knows Banquo will never return alive.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Shakespeare contrasts Banquo’s honest suspicion with Macbeth’s calculated deception. Where Banquo voices his fears openly to the audience, Macbeth conceals his murderous intentions behind friendly formality. This links to the wider theme of ‘appearance versus reality’ established in Act One with Lady Macbeth’s instruction to ‘look like the innocent flower, / But be the serpent under’t.’</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Act Three Scene Two: The Changing Relationship</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">In Scene Two, the relationship between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth shifts dramatically. Lady Macbeth reveals her own despair in a rhyming couplet: ‘Nought’s had, all’s spent, / Where our desire is got without content.’ Despite being queen, she has gained nothing of value. The word ‘spent’ suggests exhaustion — emotionally, morally, and spiritually drained. She also says ‘’Tis safer to be that which we destroy / Than by destruction dwell in doubtful joy’ — meaning it would be better to be dead like Duncan than to live with this guilt.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">Macbeth tells his wife ‘O, full of scorpions is my mind, dear wife!’ The metaphor of scorpions conveys a mind swarming with poisonous, stinging thoughts — paranoia, guilt, and fear. Yet he now excludes her from his plans, telling her to ‘Be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck.’ The affectionate ‘dearest chuck’ is a significant downgrade from ‘dearest partner of greatness’ in Act One. Shakespeare signals that their partnership of equals is collapsing: Macbeth now acts alone.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">The Banquet Scene: Banquo’s Ghost</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Act Three Scene Four is widely regarded as the <dfn class="term" data-def="The point in a drama where the main character's fortunes change decisively, usually from good to bad in a tragedy.">turning point</dfn> of the play. Macbeth hosts a royal banquet, a public display of his new power. When a murderer confirms Banquo’s death but reveals that Fleance has escaped, Macbeth’s composure cracks. Then Banquo’s ghost appears and sits in Macbeth’s seat — a powerful visual symbol that Banquo’s lineage, not Macbeth’s, will ultimately hold the throne.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Macbeth’s reaction is visceral: ‘Thou canst not say I did it: never shake / Thy gory locks at me.’ The word ‘gory’ is graphic and violent, reminding the audience of the murder. His desperate denial — ‘I did not do it’ — is both a literal claim (he did not personally kill Banquo) and an attempt to deny moral responsibility. The ghost is visible only to Macbeth, raising the question of whether it is a supernatural visitation or a hallucination born of guilt, much like the dagger in Act Two.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>After the ghost vanishes, Macbeth declares: ‘I am in blood / Stepp’d in so far that, should I wade no more, / Returning were as tedious as go o’er.’ This metaphor of wading through a river of blood shows he believes he has gone too far to turn back. It marks his commitment to continued violence.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Lady Macbeth at the Banquet</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Lady Macbeth tries to control the situation, just as she did after Duncan’s murder. She challenges Macbeth’s masculinity: ‘Are you a man?’ and calls him ‘Quite unmann’d in folly.’ This echoes her tactic from Act One (‘When you durst do it, then you were a man’). She also dismisses the ghost as ‘the very painting of your fear’ and compares it to the air-drawn dagger, trying to rationalise what she cannot see.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">However, her control is slipping. She tells her husband ‘You lack the season of all natures, sleep’ — foreshadowing her own sleepwalking scene in Act Five. She eventually dismisses the guests abruptly: ‘Stand not upon the order of your going, / But go at once.’ The public banquet has become a public humiliation. The scene marks the moment Lady Macbeth loses her influence over her husband and begins her own decline.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Blood Motif &amp; Macbeth’s Resolve</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">After the guests leave, Macbeth makes a chilling declaration: ‘It will have blood; they say, blood will have blood.’ This proverbial statement suggests that murder breeds more murder — a cycle of violence that cannot be stopped. The repetition of ‘blood’ hammers home the motif that runs through the entire play: from Duncan’s ‘golden blood’ to Macbeth’s bloody hands to the river of blood he now wades through. Shakespeare shows the audience that Macbeth’s tyranny will only deepen from this point forward.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n22">For the AQA Macbeth question (Paper 1, Section A), you will be given an extract and asked to write about the extract <em>and</em> the play as a whole. When writing about the banquet scene, always link to other moments: compare Macbeth’s guilt here with the dagger hallucination in Act Two, or contrast Lady Macbeth’s control here with her breakdown in Act Five. Use the phrase ‘Shakespeare presents…’ to show you understand the writer is making deliberate choices. Quote short, embedded quotations (‘gory locks’, ‘fruitless crown’) and analyse individual word choices.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n23">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n24">Banquo’s soliloquy reveals his suspicion that Macbeth ‘play’dst most foully’ for the crown, while Macbeth’s soliloquy shows his fear of Banquo’s prophesied lineage and his ‘fruitless crown’.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25">The banquet scene (Act 3 Scene 4) is the turning point: Banquo’s ghost destroys Macbeth’s composure, Lady Macbeth loses control, and Macbeth commits to further violence (‘I am in blood, stepp’d in so far’).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n26">Act Three marks the collapse of the Macbeths’ partnership — from ‘dearest partner of greatness’ to ‘dearest chuck’ to exclusion — and the deepening of the blood and deception motifs.</li></ul>
