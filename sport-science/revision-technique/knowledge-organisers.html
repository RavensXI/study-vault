Summarising

Knowledge Organisers

Condense each topic onto one page

What the Research Says

A knowledge organiser is a single-page summary of everything you need to know about one topic. It forces you to select, prioritise, and organise information — all of which strengthen understanding. The act of creating one from memory is itself a powerful retrieval exercise. For Sport Science, where every topic has key terms, injury types, treatment protocols, risk factors, and assessment procedures, a well-made knowledge organiser gives you a complete revision tool on one sheet.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Summarising rated LOW utility as a passive technique — but active summarisation from memory is far more effective Create from memory, not by copying
Chi et al. (1994) Self-explanation — students who organised and explained material to themselves learned more deeply Organising information aids understanding
Fiorella & Mayer (2016) Generative learning — creating your own summaries produces better retention than studying pre-made ones Making it yourself is the point

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Choose one topic — Pick a single topic such as “Acute Injuries” or “Warm-Up and Cool-Down”. One topic per page — no more.
  2. 2
    Create from memory first — Before opening any notes, write down everything you know about the topic. This is a brain dump in an organised format. Use sections: Key Terms, Types/Categories, Treatment/Protocol, Risk Factors, Diagram.
  3. 3
    Fill the sections — Key terms with definitions. Types or categories with brief explanations. Treatment protocols step by step. Risk factors grouped by intrinsic/extrinsic. One diagram or flowchart.
  4. 4
    Check and fill gaps — Open your notes and add anything you missed in a different colour. These gaps tell you what to focus on next time.
  5. 5
    Use it for retrieval — In future sessions, try to recreate the knowledge organiser from memory. Each time, you should need to check less.

One knowledge organiser takes about 25 minutes. Aim to create one per topic over the course of your revision. By exam time, you should have a complete set that you can recreate from memory.

Key Terms

Acute injury — a sudden injury caused by a specific event or trauma during sport. Fracture — a partial or complete break in a bone. Sprain — overstretching or tearing of a ligament. Strain — overstretching or tearing of a muscle or tendon. Dislocation — a bone forced out of its normal position at a joint. Concussion — a brain injury caused by a blow to the head.

Types of Acute Injury

Fractures: Simple (closed — bone doesn’t break skin), compound (open — bone pierces skin), stress (hairline crack from repetitive impact). Sprains: Ligament damage, commonly ankle or knee. Strains: Muscle/tendon damage, commonly hamstring or calf. Dislocations: Shoulder (rugby, contact sports), finger (netball, basketball). Concussion: Symptoms include dizziness, confusion, headache, nausea, blurred vision.

Treatment — PRICE Protocol

P — Protection (prevent further injury). R — Rest (stop activity immediately). I — Ice (apply for 10–15 min, wrapped in cloth, reduces swelling). C — Compression (bandage to limit swelling). E — Elevation (raise above heart level to reduce blood flow to area). Used for sprains, strains, and soft tissue injuries in the first 48–72 hours.

Risk Factors

Extrinsic: Playing surface (wet, uneven), equipment (worn-out boots), weather conditions, coaching (poor technique instruction), rules not enforced. Intrinsic: Age, fitness level, previous injuries, flexibility, fatigue, not warming up properly.

Fit this on one page: Use a grid layout with boxes for each section. Include a small diagram of the PRICE steps on the right-hand side. The discipline of fitting everything onto a single A4 sheet forces you to select the most important information.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Copying straight from notes — If you just transcribe your textbook, you’re re-reading, not learning. Always create from memory first, then check.
  • ❌
    Trying to include everything — A knowledge organiser is not a complete set of notes. It’s the essential definitions, types, treatments, and risk factors you need for the exam. If it spills onto two pages, cut the least important material.
  • ❌
    Forgetting treatment details — Sport Science examiners reward precise protocol knowledge. “Put ice on it” is vague. “Apply ice wrapped in cloth for 10–15 minutes to reduce swelling” is precise. Always include the specific steps.
  • ❌
    Making it once and never returning — The real value is in recreating it from memory in future sessions. Use your knowledge organiser as a retrieval practice tool, not a decoration for your folder.
