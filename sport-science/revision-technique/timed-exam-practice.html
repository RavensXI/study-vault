Timed Exam Practice

Build speed, accuracy, and exam confidence

Why Timed Practice Matters

Knowing the content is only half the battle. In the exam, you need to recall it quickly, apply it to unfamiliar scenarios, and write it under strict time limits. Students who never practise under timed conditions often run out of time on the highest-value questions — the 6-mark and 9-mark extended answers that carry the most marks. Timed practice builds the speed and discipline you need to perform on the day.

OCR R180 Exam Structure

Paper Content Time Marks
R180 Sport Science — Reducing the Risk of Sports Injuries and Dealing with Common Medical Conditions 1 hour 15 min 80 marks

Rule of thumb: roughly 1 minute per mark. A 6-mark question should take about 8 minutes (including planning time). A 9-mark question should take about 12 minutes. That leaves a few minutes for checking at the end.

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Start with single questions — Don’t jump straight to full papers. Pick one 6-mark or 9-mark question, set a timer, and write your answer under timed conditions. No notes allowed.
  2. 2
    Plan before you write — Spend 1–2 minutes planning. For a 6-mark question: jot down 3 developed points. For a 9-mark question: identify the key areas to cover (e.g. identification, treatment, risk factors, prevention) and plan one strong paragraph for each.
  3. 3
    Self-mark against the mark scheme — Download OCR mark schemes from their website. Read the level descriptors carefully. Be honest — did you hit Level 3? What was missing? Highlight the specific phrases in the mark scheme that your answer did or didn’t include.
  4. 4
    Rewrite the weak parts — If your answer was Level 2, identify exactly what you needed for Level 3. Write the improved version. This is where the learning happens.
  5. 5
    Build to a full paper — Once comfortable with single questions, attempt a full R180 paper under timed conditions (1 hour 15 minutes). In the last 2 weeks before the exam, do at least 2 full timed papers.

Question: “A basketball player sprains their ankle during training. Describe how the PRICE protocol should be applied to treat this injury.” [6 marks]

This is an application question. You need to describe each step AND link it to the specific injury.

Level 1–2 answer (1–3 marks)

The player should use PRICE. This means protection, rest, ice, compression, and elevation. They should put ice on the ankle and wrap it in a bandage. They should rest and keep their foot up.

Level 3 answer (5–6 marks)

The basketball player has sprained their ankle, which means the ligaments in the ankle joint have been overstretched or torn. The PRICE protocol should be applied immediately to reduce swelling and promote recovery.

Protection — the player should be carefully helped off the court to prevent any further damage to the already weakened ligaments. They should not continue playing.

Rest — the player must stop all physical activity. Continuing to play on a sprained ankle risks turning a minor sprain into a more serious tear.

Ice — apply an ice pack wrapped in a cloth to the ankle for 10–15 minutes. The cold causes blood vessels to constrict (vasoconstriction), reducing blood flow to the area and limiting swelling and inflammation. Never apply ice directly to the skin.

Compression — wrap an elasticated bandage firmly around the ankle. This applies external pressure to prevent excess fluid building up in the joint, which would increase pain and slow recovery.

Elevation — raise the ankle above heart level by resting it on a bench or bag. Gravity helps drain excess fluid away from the injury, further reducing swelling.

Key differences: The Level 3 answer identifies the injury correctly (ligament damage), explains each PRICE step with a reason (vasoconstriction, gravity, external pressure), and links back to the specific scenario (basketball court, ankle ligaments). The Level 1–2 answer listed the steps but did not explain how or why they work.

  • OCR website — Search “OCR Cambridge National Sport Science past papers R180”. Free access to papers and mark schemes from previous years.
  • Your teacher — Ask for additional practice papers or specimen papers.
  • ExamBuilder (if your school has access) — OCR’s tool for creating custom question sets by topic.

Always use the mark scheme alongside the paper. Self-marking is only useful if you are comparing against the actual level descriptors, not just guessing your grade.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Spending too long on low-mark questions — A 1-mark “state” question should take 30 seconds, not 3 minutes. Save your time for the 6 and 9-mark questions where the most marks are available.
  • ❌
    Not planning extended answers — Without a plan, you ramble. One minute of planning ensures you cover all the required points and use proper terminology.
  • ❌
    Listing instead of explaining — Writing “Protection, Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation” as a list earns 1–2 marks at best. Explaining each step with reasons and linking to the scenario earns 5–6 marks.
  • ❌
    Never doing a full timed paper — Practising individual questions is good, but the real challenge is sustaining concentration across 75 minutes. Do at least two full timed papers before the real exam.
