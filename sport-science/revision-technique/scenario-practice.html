Scenario Practice

Apply your knowledge to realistic sporting situations

Why Scenarios Matter

The R180 exam does not just test whether you know definitions — it tests whether you can apply your knowledge to realistic situations. A typical question might describe an athlete getting injured during training and ask you to explain what the first aider should do, what risk factors were involved, or how the injury could have been prevented. Students who only memorise facts struggle with these applied questions. Practising with scenarios bridges the gap between knowing and applying.

How Scenarios Appear in the Exam

Question Style What It Looks Like What It Tests
Injury identification “A netball player lands awkwardly and her ankle swells up immediately. Identify the likely injury.” Recognising injury types from symptoms
Treatment application “Describe how a first aider should treat this injury using the PRICE protocol.” Applying treatment steps to a specific case
Risk factor analysis “Explain two extrinsic risk factors that may have contributed to this injury.” Linking risk factors to specific contexts
Prevention planning “Describe a warm-up that would help prevent this type of injury.” Designing practical solutions using theory

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Write your own scenarios — Create 2–3 realistic sporting scenarios per topic. Include the sport, the situation, and a specific injury or problem. Write them on cards or in a notebook.
  2. 2
    Read the scenario cold — Pick a scenario at random. Read it once, then close it. You should not know in advance which topic it will test.
  3. 3
    Write a full answer from memory — Treat it like an exam question. Identify the injury, explain the treatment, analyse the risk factors, or describe prevention. Use proper terminology.
  4. 4
    Check against your notes — Did you use the correct terms? Did you include all the steps of the protocol? Did you link the answer back to the specific scenario, not just give a generic response?
  5. 5
    Swap scenarios with a friend — Write scenarios for each other. Answering someone else’s scenarios is harder because you can’t predict what’s coming — just like the real exam.

One scenario takes about 15 minutes. Do 2–3 per revision session. This technique is most effective in the final 2–3 weeks before the exam, once you have a solid foundation of knowledge from retrieval practice and flashcard drills.

Scenario

A football player twists their ankle during a match on a wet pitch. Their ankle swells up immediately and they are unable to put weight on it. The first aider comes onto the pitch.

Weak answer

The player has hurt their ankle. The first aider should put ice on it and tell them to rest. They should also wrap it up with a bandage.

Strong answer

The likely injury is a sprain — overstretching or tearing of the ligaments in the ankle joint. The immediate swelling and inability to bear weight suggest at least a moderate sprain.

The first aider should apply the PRICE protocol: Protect the ankle by helping the player off the pitch to prevent further damage. Rest — the player must stop playing immediately. Apply Ice wrapped in a cloth for 10–15 minutes to reduce blood flow and limit swelling. Apply Compression using an elasticated bandage to provide external pressure and prevent further fluid build-up. Elevate the ankle above heart level (e.g. on a bench) so gravity helps drain excess fluid from the injury site.

The extrinsic risk factor here is the wet pitch — a slippery surface increases the risk of the foot sliding or turning unexpectedly, placing abnormal stress on the ankle ligaments.

Key difference: The strong answer identifies the injury type correctly, applies every PRICE step with an explanation of why it works, and links back to the specific scenario (wet pitch as a risk factor). This is what earns full marks.

Scenario

During a school cross-country race, a runner suddenly collapses and is unresponsive. A teacher who is a qualified first aider reaches them within 30 seconds.

Strong answer

The first aider should follow the DRABC protocol:

Danger — check the area is safe. Ensure there are no hazards (traffic, other runners about to pass) before approaching.

Response — try to get a response. Tap the runner’s shoulders and ask “Can you hear me?” loudly. If no response, shout for help and ask someone to call 999.

Airway — tilt the head back and lift the chin to open the airway. Check for obstructions in the mouth.

Breathing — look, listen, and feel for breathing for up to 10 seconds. If breathing, place in the recovery position. If not breathing, begin CPR.

Circulation — begin CPR (30 chest compressions to 2 rescue breaths). If an AED is available, use it as soon as possible. Continue until the ambulance arrives.

The runner may have a pre-existing medical condition such as a heart condition, asthma, or epilepsy. The first aider should check for a medical alert bracelet and inform the paramedics of any findings.

Scenario

A school rugby team is about to play a match on a cold November afternoon. The coach asks you to design a warm-up. Describe the four components and give specific examples relevant to rugby.

Strong answer

1. Pulse raiser (5–10 min) — Light jogging around the pitch, gradually increasing pace. Shuttle runs with changes of direction to mimic match movements. This raises heart rate, increases blood flow to muscles, and raises body temperature — especially important on a cold day when muscles are tight.

2. Stretching (5 min) — Dynamic stretches are more appropriate before a match: leg swings (hamstrings and hip flexors), walking lunges (quadriceps and glutes), arm circles (shoulders for tackling). Avoid static stretching before explosive activity as it can temporarily reduce power.

3. Skill rehearsal (5–10 min) — Passing drills along a line, catching high balls, tackle pad work at controlled intensity. This rehearses the specific movement patterns players will use in the match.

4. Mental preparation (2–3 min) — Team huddle, visualising key plays, reviewing set-piece calls. Helps players focus and builds team confidence before kick-off.

This warm-up reduces injury risk by increasing muscle temperature and flexibility, rehearsing sport-specific movements, and mentally preparing the players for the demands of the match.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Giving a generic answer — Writing about PRICE without linking it to the specific injury in the scenario. Always refer back to the person, the sport, and the situation described.
  • ❌
    Forgetting to explain why — “Apply ice” is a step. “Apply ice to reduce blood flow to the area, limiting swelling and inflammation” is an explanation. The exam rewards the explanation.
  • ❌
    Only practising injury scenarios — The exam also tests warm-up design, risk factor analysis, and medical condition management. Use scenarios that cover the full specification.
  • ❌
    Not using correct terminology — Writing “twisted ankle” instead of “sprain” or “pulled muscle” instead of “strain” costs marks. Use the precise terms from your flashcard drills.
