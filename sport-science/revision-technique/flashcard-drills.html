Terminology

Flashcard Drills

Master the terminology that earns marks

Why Terminology Matters

Sport Science is packed with specific terminology. The R180 exam rewards students who use precise language — writing “epicondylitis” instead of “elbow pain”, or “extrinsic risk factor” instead of “things outside the body”. Flashcard drills are the fastest way to lock in definitions, acronyms, and technical vocabulary so they come to you automatically under exam conditions.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Kornell (2009) Flashcards with spaced retrieval outperformed re-reading for vocabulary learning Ideal for terminology-heavy subjects
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Practice testing (including flashcard self-testing) rated HIGH utility One of the two most effective revision methods
Leitner (1972) The Leitner box system uses increasing intervals for correct answers, focusing time on weaker cards Efficient use of limited revision time

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Create your cards — One term per card. Front: the term or acronym. Back: the definition, what it stands for, and a brief sport example. Keep the back to 2–3 lines maximum.
  2. 2
    Set up 3 boxes — Label them Box 1 (daily), Box 2 (every 3 days), Box 3 (weekly). All new cards start in Box 1.
  3. 3
    Test yourself — Read the front, say the answer out loud before flipping. Be strict — a partial answer counts as wrong.
  4. 4
    Sort by result — Correct answer → move to the next box. Wrong answer → back to Box 1. This focuses your time on the terms you find hardest.
  5. 5
    Reverse the cards — Once comfortable, flip the cards around: read the definition and recall the term. This tests the knowledge from both directions.

When and How Often

Spend 10–15 minutes per session on flashcard drills. Do Box 1 every day. Check Box 2 every 3 days. Review Box 3 once a week. The goal is to move all cards to Box 3 before the exam.

Card 1

Front: PRICE

Back: Protection, Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation — used for acute soft tissue injuries (sprains, strains) in the first 48–72 hours to reduce swelling.

Card 2

Front: SALTAPS

Back: See, Ask, Look, Touch, Active movement, Passive movement, Strength testing — a pitch-side injury assessment procedure used by first aiders to evaluate the severity of an injury.

Card 3

Front: DRABC

Back: Danger, Response, Airway, Breathing, Circulation — the emergency first aid protocol used when someone is unresponsive. Check the scene is safe before approaching.

Card 4

Front: Extrinsic risk factor

Back: A risk factor from outside the body — e.g. playing surface, weather, equipment, coaching quality, other players. Can often be controlled or reduced.

Card 5

Front: Epicondylitis

Back: A chronic overuse injury causing inflammation of the tendons at the elbow. Tennis elbow (lateral) or golfer’s elbow (medial). Caused by repetitive arm/wrist movements.

Card 6

Front: Sprain

Back: Overstretching or tearing of a ligament (connects bone to bone). Common sites: ankle, knee. Example: a footballer twisting their ankle on uneven ground.

Card 7

Front: Strain

Back: Overstretching or tearing of a muscle or tendon (connects muscle to bone). Common sites: hamstring, calf. Example: a sprinter pulling their hamstring.

Card 8

Front: Tendonitis

Back: A chronic overuse injury — inflammation of a tendon caused by repetitive movement. Example: Achilles tendonitis in a long-distance runner. Treat with rest, ice, and anti-inflammatory medication.

Card 9

Front: Concussion

Back: A brain injury caused by a blow to the head. Symptoms: dizziness, confusion, headache, nausea, blurred vision. Must stop playing immediately. Requires medical clearance before returning to sport.

Card 10

Front: Compound fracture

Back: An open fracture where the broken bone pierces through the skin. High risk of infection. More serious than a simple (closed) fracture. Requires immediate hospital treatment.

Category Terms to Make Cards For
Acronyms PRICE, SALTAPS, DRABC
Acute injuries Fracture (simple, compound, stress), sprain, strain, dislocation, concussion, abrasion
Chronic injuries Tendonitis, epicondylitis, stress fracture, shin splints
Risk factors Extrinsic, intrinsic, overtraining, individual variables
Warm-up / cool-down Pulse raiser, stretching (static/dynamic), skill rehearsal, mental preparation
Medical conditions Asthma, diabetes, epilepsy — symptoms and management in sport
First aid CPR, recovery position, AED, wound management

Aim for about 30–40 cards for the full R180 specification. If your teacher has highlighted additional terms, add those too.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Too much on each card — If the back has a full paragraph, it’s too long. Stick to the definition plus one sport-specific example. You should be able to read it in 5 seconds.
  • ❌
    Only going front-to-back — The exam might give you a definition and ask for the term, or give you a scenario and ask which protocol to use. Test in both directions.
  • ❌
    Being too lenient with yourself — If you got it “mostly right” but missed a key detail, that’s wrong. Back to Box 1. Precision is what earns marks.
  • ❌
    Not using the Leitner system — Randomly flipping through all cards wastes time on terms you already know. The box system ensures you spend the most time on your weakest areas.
