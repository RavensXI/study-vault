The Aims of the Peacemakers & the Fourteen Points

Study Notes

Britain's Prime Minister David Lloyd George initially campaigned with aggressive rhetoric, stating Germany needed squeezing "until the pip squeaks," though privately he believed Germany required sufficient strength for British trade. He positioned himself between France's Clemenceau and America's Wilson, who held contrasting views on German treatment.

Georges Clemenceau of France had witnessed German attacks in both 1870 and 1914, experiencing personal loss when his son was wounded. Determined to prevent future invasions, he initially pushed for Germany's dismantling into smaller states, ultimately securing many demands through forceful negotiation.

Woodrow Wilson presented an idealist's vision with his Fourteen Points for peace, published pre-war as the surrender basis. Germans assumed this would guarantee lenient treatment. However, American isolationism created domestic pressure against continued European involvement.

Key Ideas in Wilson's Fourteen Points:

  • End secret treaties between nations
  • Allow freedom of seas for all nations in peace and war
  • Promote free trade through removing economic barriers
  • Encourage universal disarmament
  • Promote self-determination for empire populations
  • Create League of Nations for political independence assurance

Reception

Germany accepted the Armistice expecting fair treatment. Lloyd George rejected freedom-of-seas concepts threatening British naval dominance. Both he and Clemenceau recognized self-determination threatened their own empires. Clemenceau viewed points as excessively lenient.

In each question for longer terms that you know you will have to repeat, consider abbreviating after you have defined them once.

Conclusion

Mastering the Treaty of Versailles requires understanding its authors, exact terms, and immediate German impact, establishing foundations for tracking reactions, League of Nations development, and economic troubles transforming disappointment into disaster.

02

The Terms of the Treaty of Versailles

Study Notes

The Treaty of Versailles, signed June 28, 1919, officially concluded the First World War between Germany and Allied powers. Germany was excluded from negotiations; the 'Big Three' decided terms presented for signature.

Military Terms:

  • Army limited to 100,000 men
  • Conscription banned
  • Forbidden air force, submarines, armoured vehicles
  • Navy restricted to 6 battleships
  • Rhineland demilitarized with no German troop presence

Territorial Terms:

  • Alsace-Lorraine returned to France
  • Saar coalfields given to France for 15 years
  • Posen and West Prussia given to Poland
  • Polish Corridor cut off East Prussia from Germany
  • Overseas colonies taken and distributed as mandates
  • Anschluss with Austria forbidden

Economic Terms:

  • Reparations set at £6.6 billion (1921 figure)
  • Industrial territory losses damaged wealth generation
  • Coal required deliveries to France, Belgium, Italy

Article 231: The War Guilt Clause

This article forced Germany and allies accepting total sole responsibility for starting the First World War. The clause morally justified other punishments, enabling reparations and disarmament. For Germans, this represented national humiliation, becoming a focal point for treaty anger.

When analysing the treaty, link the different types of terms together to show deeper understanding. For example, you could explain how territorial losses of industrial areas like the Saar directly impacted Germany's ability to meet economic reparations terms.

Conclusion

The Treaty dismantled German power through severe military, territorial, and economic penalties. Article 231 forced Germany accepting full war blame. The dictated peace's harsh nature created deep national resentment poisoning international relations for twenty years.

03

Reactions to the Treaty

Study Notes

German Objections

The treaty received universal outrage as a national humiliation, termed the 'Diktat' (dictated peace).

  • War Guilt Clause was most hated; Germans rejected sole responsibility
  • £6.6 billion reparations seemed impossibly high and economically destructive
  • Polish Corridor separated East Prussia, violating self-determination principles
  • Military restrictions caused national pride injury and vulnerability feelings
  • Germans fumed over exclusion from negotiations despite Fourteen Points agreement

Allied Reactions

Georges Clemenceau (France): Unhappy, believing terms insufficient. He wanted Germany broken into smaller states and complete Rhineland removal. Forced compromise frustrated him; he feared Germany's recovery and revenge. French voters agreed, voting him from office in 1920.

David Lloyd George (Britain): Mixed feelings characterized his position. Public perception showed him as heroic, pleased securing British interests through empire expansion and naval reduction. Privately, he believed the treaty was excessively harsh, famously stating: "We shall have to fight another war again in 25 years time." While initially popular, his fears proved accurate regarding long-term consequences.

Woodrow Wilson (USA): Extremely disappointed, viewing the treaty as betraying his Fourteen Points and excessively punitive. He disliked War Guilt Clauses and high reparations. Congress refused ratification; Americans wanted isolationism avoiding European entanglement. The USA never joined the League, devastating the organization from inception.

A strong answer will move beyond simply describing reactions and will offer an overall judgement on the treaty. You could argue that the treaty was fundamentally flawed because it was an unstable compromise satisfying no one.

Conclusion

The Treaty failed creating lasting peace, meeting deep resentment in Germany, undermining the new Weimar Republic from birth. Allied reactions were negative: Clemenceau found it too weak, Lloyd George feared another war, Wilson saw failed opportunity for fairness. USA isolationism crippled the League, leaving reluctant Britain and fearful France enforcing the flawed treaty.

The Wider Peace Settlement (Other Treaties & New States)

Study Notes

While Versailles dealt specifically with Germany, separate treaties addressed Germany's war partners: Austria, Hungary, Bulgaria, and Turkey. These treaties aimed dismantling old, multi-ethnic empires and redrawing Europe's map, guided by self-determination principles, though creating numerous new problems.

The Other Treaties

Treaty of St Germain (Austria, 1919):

  • Formally dissolved the Austro-Hungarian Empire
  • Austria lost vast lands to newly created states: Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, Poland
  • Army limited to 30,000 men
  • Anschluss with Germany strictly forbidden

Result: Austria became a small, landlocked country with shattered economy, as most industrial land was given away.

Treaty of Trianon (Hungary, 1920):

  • Particularly harsh for Hungary
  • Lost over two-thirds of former territory to Czechoslovakia, Romania, Yugoslavia
  • Approximately 3 million Hungarians found themselves in foreign countries
  • Army limited to 35,000 men

Result: Hungary became bitter and resentful, determined to revise the treaty and reclaim lost lands and people.

Treaty of Neuilly (Bulgaria, 1919):

  • Bulgaria lost territory to Yugoslavia and Greece
  • Lost Aegean Sea access
  • Army limited to 20,000 men
  • Forced to pay £100 million reparations

Result: Considered harsh but less severe than treaties imposed on former allies.

Treaty of Sevres (Turkey, 1920) & Lausanne (1923):

Sevres was brutally harsh, dismantling the Ottoman Empire and distributing Middle Eastern lands (Palestine, Iraq) as British and French 'mandates'. This humiliation sparked nationalist uprising under Mustafa Kemal Ataturk.

Ataturk's forces successfully drove Allies out and overthrew the Sultan, forcing renegotiation. The Treaty of Lausanne was substantially fairer: Turkey regained most land, faced no military limits, and avoided reparations.

Result: This represented the only example of defeated nations successfully challenging and overturning post-war treaty terms.

The New States & Their Problems

The old empires' break-up created weak new countries like Poland, Czechoslovakia, and Yugoslavia facing major challenges:

  1. Minority Groups: Hastily drawn borders trapped large minority populations (millions of Germans in Czechoslovakia and Poland), creating ethnic tension and instability.
  2. Economic Issues: These nations, previously part of larger economic units, struggled establishing stable economies, new currencies, and effective trade relationships.
  3. Political Instability: Without democratic governance history, their political systems were fragile, surrounded by resentful neighbors wanting territory reclamation.

When discussing the wider peace settlement, critically evaluate the principle of 'self-determination'. While used justifying new states like Czechoslovakia, you achieve higher marks pointing out hypocrisy. For example, the principle was completely ignored when millions of Germans were placed under Polish or Czech rule, and when Austria was forbidden uniting with Germany.

Conclusion

The wider peace settlement redrew central and eastern European maps, dismantling old empires and creating new nations. However, the treaties were deeply flawed. They left defeated powers bitter and resentful, while newly created states were economically weak and ethnically divided. Turkey's successful challenge to Sevres demonstrated the entire post-war settlement's fragility and its terms' non-permanence.

The League of Nations: Aims, Structure & Powers

Study Notes

The League of Nations, founded in 1920 following the First World War, was a pioneering international organization. US President Woodrow Wilson envisioned a forum where countries could discuss problems and prevent global conflict. Headquarters were in Geneva, Switzerland. However, the USA refused joining, critically damaging its prestige and power from inception.

The Aims of the League

The Covenant's 26 articles established main goals:

  1. Stop war through diplomacy and collective security, where members would act together against aggressive nations
  2. Improve lives and jobs by tackling global issues like poor working conditions, human trafficking, disease
  3. Achieve disarmament by encouraging armed forces reduction
  4. Enforce Treaty of Versailles by ensuring compliance with treaty terms

The Structure of the League

The Assembly: Like the League's parliament, every member country had one representative and one vote. It met annually deciding budgets and admitting new members. A key weakness: all decisions required unanimity.

The Council: A smaller group meeting frequently, holding real power. Initially had permanent members (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) and temporary members. Major weakness: permanent members held vetoes, allowing any resolution blocking.

The Permanent Court of International Justice: Based in The Hague, designed settling international legal disputes. However, it lacked enforcement mechanisms and could only act if both involved countries agreed accepting its decision.

The Secretariat: The League's civil service, responsible for administration, paperwork, and report preparation.

Commissions and Agencies: Special committees achieving humanitarian aims, including the International Labour Organisation (ILO), Health Committee, and Slavery Commission, often representing the League's most successful work.

The Powers of the League (Collective Security)

The League employed a three-step process addressing aggressor nations:

  1. Moral Condemnation: The Council would first condemn aggressive acts, hoping world opinion pressure would force backing down.
  2. Economic Sanctions: If condemnation failed, the League could impose economic sanctions, stopping all member trading with aggressors.
  3. Military Force: The final resort where the League could request member troops contributing to collective forces.

A fatal flaw: the League lacked its own army, relying completely on member goodwill for soldiers, with no obligation requirements.

When analysing the League, focus on its weaknesses from the start. For top marks, don't just describe the structure; explain how it was flawed. For example, 'The League's power was fundamentally undermined by its own structure. The requirement for unanimous Assembly decisions, combined with permanent Council member veto power, meant decisive aggression action was often impossible.'

Conclusion

The League of Nations founded with noble ambitions ensuring global peace and improving human lives. However, potential was crippled from the beginning. Key weaknesses included absent powerful countries like the USA, decision-making structures making swift action nearly impossible, and lacking its own army for enforcement. While humanitarian commissions achieved notable successes, its primary war prevention mission faced severe tests.

The League in the 1920s: Successes and Failures

Study Notes

The 1920s brought mixed fortunes for the new League. It scored notable victories settling smaller country disputes and achieved great things through humanitarian commissions. However, the decade exposed critical League weaknesses, particularly regarding powerful nation defiance.

Successes in the 1920s

Aaland Islands (1921):

Dispute: Sweden and Finland both claimed the islands, approaching war.

League Action: Investigated and ruled the islands belonged to Finland, protecting Swedish language and culture.

Outcome: Clear success; both accepted the League's decision, avoiding war.

Upper Silesia (1921):

Dispute: A rich industrial border region between Germany and Poland was claimed by both.

League Action: Organized peaceful plebiscite for inhabitants. Very close results led to League partition - Germany received most land, Poland received the main industrial zone.

Outcome: Successful compromise; both nations accepted the new border, stopping potential conflict.

Bulgaria (1925):

Dispute: Following a border incident, Greece invaded Bulgaria.

League Action: Swiftly condemned invasion, ordering Greek withdrawal and compensation payment.

Outcome: Success for League authority; Britain and France backed the decision; Greece complied. However, Greeks complained of double standards regarding Italy in the Corfu incident.

Humanitarian Work

Perhaps the League's greatest successes came from its commissions and agencies.

  • The Refugee Commission helped return approximately 400,000 prisoners of war home.
  • The Health Organisation began vital work combating global diseases like malaria and leprosy.
  • The International Labour Organisation (ILO) successfully campaigned improving working conditions, including lead paint bans and child labour limitations.
  • The Slavery Commission worked abolishing slavery globally, freeing 200,000 enslaved people in Sierra Leone.

Failures in the 1920s

Vilna (1920):

Dispute: Poland invaded and seized Vilna, Lithuania's capital.

League Action: Lithuania appealed for help; the League condemned Poland's actions but did nothing enforcing withdrawal.

Outcome: Major failure. France saw Poland as a key German-countering ally and refused acting. Britain would not act alone. Poland kept Vilna, proving aggressive nations with powerful friends could ignore the League.

Corfu (1923):

Dispute: An Italian general was murdered on the Greek-Albanian border. Italy's fascist leader Benito Mussolini blamed Greece and invaded the Greek island Corfu.

League Action: Greece appealed to the League, which condemned Mussolini's invasion. However, Mussolini refused accepting League authority, turning instead to the Conference of Ambassadors, a powerful country group he knew would be sympathetic.

Outcome: Disastrous failure. The Conference sided with Italy, ordering Greece apologizing and paying compensation. Mussolini withdrew troops, boasting his success. This showed powerful nations could use aggression getting what they wanted, completely undermining the League.

Diplomacy Outside the League

In the 1920s, several important international agreements were made outside the League, such as the Locarno Treaties (1925) and the Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928). This suggested that for major international issues, powerful countries preferred making deals among themselves rather than trusting the League, damaging its prestige.

It is not necessary for the exam to know ALL the successes or failures of the League in the 1920's. Pick one of each and learn it inside out! Then briefly look over the others. You are unlikely to be asked a direct question about this, but being able to reference these successes in other questions shows the examiner that you have good contextual knowledge.

Conclusion

In the 1920s, the League of Nations mixed promise and disappointment. It proved its worth as a humanitarian organisation and as smaller nation dispute mediator. However, Vilna and Corfu failures were ominous. They created dangerous precedent that the League's collective security commitment was weak and that aggressive major powers could act without consequences. These were lessons dictators would remember in the 1930s.

Diplomacy Outside the League: Locarno & Kellogg-Briand Pact

Study Notes

During the mid-to-late 1920s, growing optimism and cooperation seemed to characterize Europe. While the League handled some disputes, the most significant international agreements were made directly between great powers, outside the League's framework. These pacts, celebrated at the time, also revealed fundamental lack of faith in the League's collective security system.

The Locarno Treaties (1925)

This series of agreements was seen as a major peace breakthrough and turning point in Germany relations.

What was agreed?

  • Germany, France, and Belgium promised respecting their shared western borders as laid out in the Treaty of Versailles. This was the first time Germany voluntarily accepted these terms.
  • The Rhineland was to remain a demilitarised zone permanently.
  • Britain and Italy acted as guarantors, promising using their armies defending any nation attacked violating these agreements.
  • Crucially, Germany did not agree to its eastern borders with Poland and Czechoslovakia, making those new countries feel very insecure.

The 'Spirit of Locarno'

The treaties were hailed as great success, creating a new cooperation period known as the "Locarno Honeymoon." Germany was allowed joining the League of Nations in 1926, seemingly healing many war wounds. However, this was a great power deal for their own security. It showed that for major issues, Britain and France trusted specific treaties more than the League's vague 'collective security' promise.

The Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928)

This pact represented an even grander, more ambitious world peace attempt.

What was agreed?

  • The pact was eventually signed by 62 countries, including Germany, France, and the USA.
  • The signatories promised not using war as an international dispute solution method. In simple terms, they agreed outlawing war.

Impact and Weaknesses

The pact was seen as humanity's huge step forward and the post-war peace desire's symbol. However, it had a fatal flaw: there was no enforcement mechanism. It didn't state what would happen if a country broke the promise. It was an idealistic statement rather than a serious treaty. It also did not cover self-defence wars, creating loopholes that any aggressor could exploit.

The fact that it was co-authored by the USA, a League non-member, again showed that the most important diplomacy was happening outside of Geneva.

For a top-level analysis, explain the contradictory nature of these agreements. Argue that while on the surface, Locarno and Kellogg-Briand were peace successes aligning with League goals, they were actually signs of its underlying weakness. The fact that powerful nations felt needing these separate deals proved they lacked real faith in the League's collective security protection.

Conclusion

The Locarno Treaties and the Kellogg-Briand Pact marked the 1920s international cooperation high point. They created genuine optimism promising a war-free future. However, their very existence outside the League of Nations highlighted a crucial problem: when it came to their own security, the world's most powerful nations trusted each other more than they trusted the League. This lack of League faith had disastrous 1930s consequences when truly tested.

The Great Depression and the Manchurian Crisis

Study Notes

The 1920s ended with fragile peace, shattered by two connected events changing the world. The Great Depression beginning in 1929 was a global economic disaster creating desperation and fuelling extremism. This harsh international climate set the stage for the Manchurian Crisis, Japan's aggression act exposing the League as powerless and fatally undermining collective security.

The Great Depression (1929)

The Depression began with the Wall Street Crash in October 1929 USA and quickly spread globally with devastating consequences.

Global Impact:

  1. Trade collapsed: American banks recalled loans; economies crumbled. Countries adopted protectionism (import taxing) protecting their industries, bringing international trade to standstill.
  2. Mass Unemployment: Businesses went bankrupt, leading to millions losing their jobs.
  3. Rise of Extremism: Facing poverty and hardship, many lost democracy faith. They turned to extremist political parties, like the Nazis in Germany and Japan's military leaders, who promised easy solutions through aggressive nationalism and expansion.

Impact on the League of Nations:

  1. Reduced Cooperation: Nations became preoccupied with domestic problems. The 1920s international cooperation spirit evaporated as countries focused on self-interest.
  2. Reluctance to Act: The Depression made countries unwilling imposing economic sanctions on aggressors, as this would further damage their own struggling trade.
  3. Military Cuts: Britain and France, the League's main powers, cut military spending. They lacked both funds and foreign conflict involvement desire.

The Manchurian Crisis (1931-33)

This was the first major League test by its own leading member, and it failed completely.

Background: Japan's economy was devastated by the Depression, as it relied on silk exporting to the USA. The military, which had great influence over government, saw new territory conquest as the solution. Manchuria, a resource-rich Chinese province, was their target.

The Invasion:

The Mukden Incident (Sept 1931): The Japanese army staged an explosion on the South Manchurian Railway, which they controlled. They blamed the incident on Chinese nationalists, using it as pretext for full-scale Manchuria invasion.

By 1932, Japan had conquered the entire province and renamed it 'Manchukuo', installing a puppet ruler.

The League's Response:

China appealed to the League for help. The League's response was extremely slow. It sent the Lytton Commission to investigate, but over a year passed before its report publication. The report found Japan acted unlawfully, ordering Manchuria withdrawal. The League's Assembly voted 42-1 approving the report. Japan was the only dissenting vote.

The Outcome:

Japan responded voting against the vote by walking out of the Assembly and formally withdrawing from the League of Nations in March 1933. The League did nothing more. Economic sanctions were not used, and military action was never even considered. The crisis was too far away, and Britain and France were unwilling risking their forces or economies. Japan kept Manchuria.

There is a good chance you could be asked about Manchuria specifically. The 'Write an Account' question is a good candidate for this. Even if this is not asked, the Manchurian Crisis is an excellent example of the League's self-interest by countries, and somehow, someway you will be able discussing this in the paper.

Conclusion

The Manchurian Crisis was a disastrous League of Nations failure. It proved that a determined and powerful member could commit aggression facing no serious consequences. The failure sent a clear and dangerous message to other expansionist dictators, particularly Mussolini in Italy and Hitler in Germany: the League was a 'paper tiger', and aggression would be met with words, not action. This was a crucial step on the path to the Second World War.

The Abyssinian Crisis and the Collapse of the League

Study Notes

If the Manchurian Crisis had severely wounded the League of Nations, the Abyssinian Crisis of 1935-36 was the final, fatal blow. This was a clear-cut case of a large, powerful European nation, Italy, invading a smaller, weaker member state, Abyssinia. The League's complete failure protecting Abyssinia destroyed its credibility as a peacekeeping organisation and is widely seen as the moment it collapsed in all but name.

Background to the Crisis

Mussolini's Ambitions: Benito Mussolini, the fascist dictator of Italy, wanted building a great new Roman Empire. He wanted expanding Italy's territory, gaining valuable resources, and distracting his people from Great Depression economic problems.

The Target: Abyssinia (modern-day Ethiopia) was one of Africa's only independent nations. It was conveniently located between two existing Italian colonies and was rich in natural resources. For Mussolini, it was an ideal target beginning his new empire.

The Trigger: In December 1934, a clash between Italian and Ethiopian soldiers at the Wal-Wal oasis gave Mussolini the pretext he needed. He began building forces for full-scale invasion, despite Abyssinia's emperor Haile Selassie appealing to the League for help.

The League's Response & Failure

From the very beginning, the League's leading members, Britain and France, mishandled the crisis. Their priority was not the League, but their own political interests.

Playing for Time: Britain and France were desperate keeping Mussolini as an ally against the growing Adolf Hitler threat in Germany. They signed the Stresa Front with Italy in April 1935, a pact standing against German rearmament. They deliberately avoided mentioning Abyssinia, which Mussolini interpreted as a green light for invasion.

Invasion and Sanctions (Oct 1935):

Italy invaded Abyssinia in October 1935. The League swiftly condemned the invasion and imposed economic sanctions. However, the sanctions were fatally flawed:

  • They did not include oil, steel, or coal - the key resources needed waging war.
  • The Suez Canal, owned by Britain and France, was left open to Italian supply ships. Closing it would have stopped the invasion in its tracks, but Britain and France feared it might lead to war with Italy.
  • Non-member states like the USA continued trading with Italy.

The Hoare-Laval Pact (Dec 1935):

This was the ultimate betrayal. The British and French foreign ministers, Samuel Hoare and Pierre Laval, secretly drew up a plan offering Mussolini two-thirds of Abyssinia if he would call off invasion. The plan was leaked to the press, sparking public outcry in Britain and France. It was seen as a treacherous act rewarding aggression. The pact was abandoned, but the damage was done. It proved Britain and France were willing sacrificing a fellow League member for their own political gain.

The Outcome & Consequences:

  • Italy Conquers Abyssinia: With no effective opposition, Italy completed its brutal Abyssinia conquest by May 1936.
  • The League is Finished: The League's credibility was completely destroyed. It had failed in its primary duty protecting a member from aggression. After this, no one took the League's authority seriously again.
  • Mussolini and Hitler Unite: The crisis pushed Mussolini away from Britain and France into an alliance with Hitler. In 1936, they signed the Rome-Berlin Axis, a key step towards the Axis Powers' WWII creation.
  • Hitler's Gamble: While the League was distracted by Abyssinia, Hitler took his own massive risk in March 1936. He marched his troops into the demilitarised Rhineland, a direct Treaty of Versailles violation. Britain and France, paralysed by Abyssinian failure, did nothing.

For a top-level answer, compare the Manchuria and Abyssinia failures. Argue that Abyssinia was the more significant and damaging failure. This is because the crisis was on Europe's doorstep; Italy was a major European League Council power; and Britain and France had power to stop invasion (by closing Suez Canal or imposing oil sanctions) but deliberately chose not to.

Conclusion

The Abyssinian Crisis was the final League of Nations coffin nail. Britain and France's actions showed their own national interests came far before collective security principles. They destroyed the very organisation they were supposed leading. After Abyssinia, the League was seen as irrelevant, and the war path was wide open as aggressive dictators knew nothing could stop them.

Hitler's Aims and Steps to Rearmament (1933-1935)

Study Notes

When Adolf Hitler became Chancellor in January 1933, it marked a pivotal moment for Europe. The international system, already weakened by the Great Depression and Manchurian Crisis, now faced a leader openly determined destroying the post-war peace settlement. Hitler had clear aggressive foreign policy goals, and from 1933 to 1935, he took increasingly bold steps challenging the Treaty of Versailles and rebuilding German military might.

Hitler's Foreign Policy Aims

Hitler outlined his aims clearly in his book, Mein Kampf ('My Struggle'). His primary goals were:

Abolish the Treaty of Versailles: This was his central and most popular aim. He saw the treaty as national humiliation (a 'Diktat') and vowed reversing its terms, including War Guilt Clause, reparations, and disarmament.

Expand German Territory (Lebensraum): Hitler believed German people needed 'Lebensraum' (living space). His plan was conquering a vast new empire in Eastern Europe, primarily by seizing land from Poland and the Soviet Union.

Unite all German-speaking people: He aimed creating a 'Greater Germany' by bringing all ethnic Germans (Volksdeutsche) into the Reich. This included Austria, Czechoslovakia, and Poland populations, directly violating the treaty's Anschluss (union) ban with Austria.

Defeat Communism: Hitler saw communism as a Jewish invention and the Soviet Union as Germany's ultimate enemy. He believed a war destroying the USSR was essential for securing Lebensraum.

First Steps (1933-1935)

Hitler began cautiously, testing Allied resolve with defiance and diplomacy mixture.

1. Withdrawal from the League of Nations (1933): At the World Disarmament Conference, Hitler proposed all nations should disarm to Germany's level. When France refused, Hitler used this as an excuse withdrawing Germany from both the conference and the League of Nations, claiming they were unfair.

2. Non-Aggression Pact with Poland (1934): This was a cunning move. By signing a 10-year non-aggression pact, Hitler made himself looking like a peace man. It reassured Poland and Britain, but in reality, it was just a tactic buying time for rearmament and weakening the France-Poland alliance.

3. Failed Anschluss with Austria (1934): Hitler encouraged Austrian Nazis staging a coup. They murdered Austrian Chancellor Engelbert Dollfuss. However, the coup failed when Italy's Mussolini, who saw Austria as part of his own sphere of influence, moved his troops to the Austrian border. Hitler's army was not yet strong enough challenging Italy, so he was forced backing down.

4. The Saar Plebiscite (1935): As promised by the Treaty of Versailles, a plebiscite (public vote) was held in the Saar region deciding its future. An overwhelming 90% of the population voted to rejoin Germany. This was a huge and legitimate propaganda victory for Hitler.

Rearmament and Conscription (1935)

Feeling more confident, Hitler made his most direct treaty challenge yet.

  • 'Freedom to Rearm' Rally (March 1935): Hitler openly announced he was reintroducing conscription and planned building an army of 550,000 men. He also revealed Germany's new air force existence, the Luftwaffe.
  • The Stresa Front: In response, Britain, France, and Italy formed the Stresa Front. They condemned Hitler's actions and promised standing united protecting Austrian independence.
  • The Anglo-German Naval Agreement (June 1935): The Stresa Front collapsed almost immediately. Britain, without consulting its allies, signed a naval deal with Germany. This allowed Hitler building a navy up to 35% of Royal Navy size. This act showed Britain's self-interest and proved to Hitler that the Stresa Front was not a serious threat.

For a top-level answer, you should characterise Hitler's early foreign policy as a mix of caution and aggression. Explain how actions like the Poland pact were designed deceiving opponents, while the failed Austria coup showed he was not yet strong enough acting without restraint.

Conclusion

Between 1933 and 1935, Hitler successfully began his campaign dismantling the Treaty of Versailles. He skilfully combined moments of defiance with peaceful gestures confusing and dividing opponents. The Allied response was weak and inconsistent, culminating in the Anglo-German Naval Agreement, which shattered Stresa Front unity. This failure presenting strong, united opposition emboldened Hitler, convincing him Britain and France lacked the will to stop him.

Escalating Tension: Remilitarisation of the Rhineland (1936)

Study Notes

The Rhineland remilitarisation in March 1936 was Hitler's boldest and most audacious gamble to date. It was a direct violation of both the Treaty of Versailles and the Locarno Treaties, which Germany had signed voluntarily. The complete failure of Britain and France to respond marked a crucial turning point on the war road, dramatically shifting European power balance and convincing Hitler that the Allies lacked the will to stop him.

What was the Rhineland?

The Rhineland was Germany's industrial heartland, located on its western border with France and Belgium.

  • A Demilitarised Zone: Under the Treaty of Versailles, the area was to remain demilitarised. This meant Germany was forbidden placing any troops, weapons, or fortifications there. It was intended being a permanent buffer zone, making Germany launching a surprise France attack impossible.
  • Locarno Guarantee: Germany freely accepted this demilitarised status as a permanent arrangement in the Locarno Treaties of 1925. This meant Hitler could not claim he was overturning an unfair 'Diktat'; he was breaking a willingly signed treaty.

Hitler's Gamble (March 1936)

On March 7, 1936, Hitler ordered German soldiers marching back into the Rhineland.

This was an enormous gamble. The new German army was still weak and no match for the far larger and better-equipped French army. Hitler later admitted: "If the French had marched into the Rhineland, we would have had to withdraw with our tails between our legs."

He had given his generals orders to retreat at the first sign of French opposition. A failure would have been a humiliating defeat for Hitler, potentially ending his rule.

The Allied Response: Why did they do nothing?

France: Was paralysed by political division. With an election approaching, no leader was willing risking plunging the country into war. Their military chiefs overestimated German strength, saying it would require full-scale, costly mobilisation to intervene. Crucially, they refused acting without British support.

Britain: Many British politicians and public members felt Germany had a moral right putting troops in its own country. Popular opinion was that Hitler was merely "marching into his own backyard." The British government was also against any action that might lead to war, and the military was in no shape to fight, having been cut back during the Depression.

The League of Nations: The League was completely preoccupied with the Abyssinian Crisis. It condemned Hitler's action but was powerless doing anything else, as its leading members, Britain and France, were unwilling using force.

Consequences of the Remilitarisation

  1. Hitler's Prestige Soars: He had faced down the Allies and won. His position in Germany became unchallengeable, and he became convinced Britain and France would never stand up to him.
  2. End of the Security System: The Treaty of Versailles was now effectively dead. The Locarno Treaties, supposed guaranteeing French security, were proven worthless.
  3. Strategic Disaster for France: Germany could now begin building the Siegfried Line, a series of fortifications along its France border. This meant if Hitler chose expanding eastwards into Austria or Czechoslovakia, it would be almost impossible for France using its army to stop him.
  4. New Alliances: The crisis cemented Mussolini's growing Hitler friendship. They would go on to sign the Rome-Berlin Axis in late 1936.

For a top-level answer, you must explain why the Rhineland remilitarisation was a crucial turning point. Argue that it represented the last real opportunity for Britain and France to stop Hitler without waging a full-scale war.

Conclusion

Hitler's Rhineland remilitarisation was a calculated risk paying off spectacularly. The timid and divided Britain and France response gave Hitler a massive political and strategic victory. It shattered Treaty of Versailles remaining authority, emboldened Hitler pursuing further aggression, and made it significantly harder for the Allies containing him in the future.

The Policy of Appeasement (incl. Anschluss & Sudetenland)

Study Notes

Appeasement was the policy of giving in to an aggressor's demands in order to avoid conflict. From 1937, this was the official British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain policy towards Hitler's Germany. Buoyed by Rhineland success, Hitler's confidence grew, and in 1938 he set his sights on uniting all German-speaking peoples. This led to two major international crises, in Austria and Czechoslovakia, where Chamberlain's appeasement policy was put to the ultimate test.

What was Appeasement & Why was it followed?

From the late 1930s Britain perspective, appeasement seemed like a logical and necessary policy for several reasons.

  1. Fear of another war: The First World War trauma was still fresh. Britain and France were desperate avoiding a repeat of that slaughter.
  2. The Treaty of Versailles was seen as unfair: Many British politicians felt the treaty had been too harsh on Germany and that some Hitler demands, like uniting with German speakers, were reasonable.
  3. Threat of Communism: Many in Britain's upper classes saw Hitler as a vital buffer against the greater evil of Stalin's Soviet Union. A strong Germany could stop communism spreading westward.
  4. Economic Problems: The Great Depression had hit Britain hard. The country could not afford the massive rearmament and potential war cost.
  5. USA's Isolationism: Britain knew it could not fight Germany alone, and the USA had made it clear it would not get involved in another European war.
  6. Buying time to rearm: The British army was not ready for 1938 war. Appeasement gave Britain valuable time building up its armed forces, particularly the Royal Air Force (RAF).

Case Study 1: Anschluss with Austria (March 1938)

The union of Germany and Austria (Anschluss) was Hitler's first major objective.

  • The Build-up: Hitler, himself an Austrian, encouraged the Austrian Nazi Party creating unrest. He pressured the Austrian Chancellor, Schuschnigg, accepting Nazi government members. When Schuschnigg called for an Austrian independence vote resisting Hitler, Hitler became furious.
  • The Invasion: On March 12, 1938, Hitler sent his troops into Austria. They faced no opposition and were greeted by cheering crowds. Anschluss was achieved without a single shot fired. A rigged vote was held a month later, showing 99.75% union support.
  • The Reaction: Britain and France did nothing. Chamberlain stated Austria and Germany had a union right and that the Treaty of Versailles had wrongly separated them. The League of Nations, now seen irrelevant, was not consulted.

Case Study 2: The Sudetenland Crisis (Sept 1938)

Having successfully taken Austria, Hitler's next target was the Sudetenland.

  • The Target: This was a western Czechoslovakia region with 3 million ethnic Germans. It was also vital to Czechoslovakia's security, containing most of its border fortifications and key industries.
  • The Crisis: Hitler used the same tactics as in Austria, encouraging local Nazis causing trouble and then claiming he needed "protecting" the Sudeten Germans from Czech oppression. He threatened invading, and Europe was pushed to war's brink.
  • The Munich Agreement: To avert war, Chamberlain flew to meet Hitler three times. The crisis culminated in the Munich Agreement on September 29, 1938. The meeting was attended by Hitler (Germany), Chamberlain (Britain), Mussolini (Italy), and Daladier (France). Crucially, Czechoslovakia's leaders and the Soviet Union were not invited. They agreed giving the Sudetenland to Germany. Chamberlain flew home a hero, famously declaring he had secured "peace for our time."

When writing about appeasement, avoid the simple trap of calling it a cowardly policy. For top marks, you must explain the historical context. Argue that while in hindsight appeasement was a disaster making Hitler stronger, for Chamberlain in 1938, it seemed a moral and pragmatic choice.

Conclusion

Appeasement was a policy born from the First World War trauma. In 1938, it led to both the Anschluss and Czechoslovakia's carving up at the Munich Conference. These were huge Hitler victories, who gained land, resources, and soldiers without having to fight. While Chamberlain believed he had prevented a war, the policy had in fact betrayed a democratic nation, alienated the Soviet Union, and powerfully convinced Hitler that Britain and France would never have the courage standing up to him.

The End of Appeasement & the Nazi-Soviet Pact (1939)

Study Notes

Neville Chamberlain's "peace for our time" promise lasted less than six months. In March 1939, Hitler tore up the Munich Agreement and invaded the rest of Czechoslovakia. This shocking aggression act finally proved to Britain and France that Hitler could not be trusted and brought the appeasement policy to an abrupt and humiliating end. With war now looking inevitable, the final diplomatic puzzle piece fell into place with the stunning and cynical Nazi-Soviet Pact.

Stalin's Dilemma

With Europe on war's brink, all eyes turned to Soviet Union leader Joseph Stalin. He was deeply suspicious of both sides.

Why he distrusted Britain and France:

  • He had been deliberately excluded from the Munich Conference, which he saw as a Western conspiracy pushing Hitler eastwards towards the USSR.
  • He believed Britain and France were secretly happy to see Nazism and Communism destroying each other.
  • He felt their Poland guarantee was weak and that they could not be relied upon fighting for Eastern Europe.

Why he distrusted Hitler:

  • Hitler had always written in Mein Kampf about his Communism hatred and his aim to seize Lebensraum (living space) from the Soviet Union. Stalin knew Hitler was his ultimate ideological enemy.

Stalin received alliance offers from both Britain/France and Germany. He had to decide which deal offered him the most security and time.

The Nazi-Soviet Pact (August 1939)

On August 23, 1939, the world was stunned by the news that the sworn enemies, Nazi Germany and the Communist USSR, had signed a non-aggression pact (also known as the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact).

Why Hitler signed it:

  1. It gave him the green light invading Poland without having to worry about a Soviet attack.
  2. Critically, it ensured he would not have to fight a two-front war (against Britain and France in the West and the USSR in the East), which had been the strategic nightmare leading to Germany's WWI defeat.

Why Stalin signed it:

  1. He gained time to rearm. Stalin knew that war with Hitler was inevitable, but his army was weak after his purges. The pact bought him valuable time preparing.
  2. The pact contained a secret deal to divide Poland between Germany and the USSR. It also gave Stalin a sphere of influence over the Baltic states, creating a buffer zone between Germany and the Soviet Union.

To achieve a high grade, you must analyse why Stalin, a sworn fascism enemy, signed a Hitler deal. Explain that from Stalin's perspective, it was a logical, if cynical, choice. He had been alienated by Western powers at Munich and did not trust their guarantees.

Conclusion

The Czechoslovakia invasion marked appeasement's death and led Britain and France to guarantee Poland's independence. This set the war stage. The final act was the Nazi-Soviet Pact, a cynical convenience marriage that stunned the world. It removed the USSR threat, giving Hitler the confidence to invade Poland, and in doing so, made a second world war an absolute certainty.

The Invasion of Poland & the Outbreak of War

Study Notes

After the Nazi-Soviet Pact secured his eastern flank, Hitler was ready for his next and final pre-war move: Poland's conquest. Confident that Britain and France were weak and would back down one last time, he manufactured a crisis and unleashed a new, terrifying warfare form. This time, however, he had miscalculated. The Poland invasion was the aggression act that finally triggered the Second World War.

The Final Target: Poland

Throughout summer 1939, Hitler increased his Poland pressure.

  • Hitler's Demands: He demanded Danzig city's return, which was mostly German-speaking, and the abolition of the Polish Corridor, the strip of land dividing the main Germany part from East Prussia.
  • The Polish Response: Supported by the British and French independence guarantee, the Polish government stood firm and refused Hitler's demands.
  • The Pretext for War: As he had done before, Hitler needed an invasion excuse. On August 31, 1939, in the Gleiwitz Incident, German SS soldiers dressed in Polish uniforms staged a fake attack on a German radio station on the border. Hitler used this manufactured "Polish aggression" as his war justification.

The Invasion of Poland (1st September 1939)

  1. Blitzkrieg: At dawn on September 1, 1939, the German army invaded Poland from the west. They used a new tactic called 'Blitzkrieg' (Lightning War). This involved overwhelming the enemy with coordinated, fast-moving attacks using tanks (Panzers) and dive-bombers (Stukas), which caused panic and chaos.
  2. Soviet Invasion: On September 17, as agreed in the Nazi-Soviet Pact secret clauses, Stalin's Red Army invaded Poland from the east.
  3. Poland is Crushed: Caught in a pincer movement between two of the world's most powerful armies, the outdated Polish forces stood no chance. Warsaw, the capital, surrendered on September 27. Poland was once again wiped off the map, divided between Germany and the Soviet Union.

Britain and France Declare War

The Ultimatum: Chamberlain did not immediately declare war. On September 2, he issued an ultimatum to Hitler: begin German troops withdrawing from Poland by 11 am on September 3, or Britain would be at war with Germany.

War: Hitler, still believing Britain and France were bluffing, ignored the deadline. At 11:15 am on September 3, 1939, Chamberlain announced in a radio broadcast to the nation that Britain was at war with Germany. France followed suit shortly after. The Second World War had begun.

The specification requires you to consider who was responsible for the 1939 war outbreak. For a top-level answer, you must assess the key individuals' roles. Argue that while Hitler's relentless aggression was the primary cause, Stalin's cynical Nazi-Soviet Pact signing was the crucial event making Poland invasion inevitable.

Conclusion

Hitler's September 1, 1939 Poland invasion was the direct Second World War cause. His brutal and effective Blitzkrieg use, combined with the eastern Soviet invasion, sealed Poland's fate. This time, Britain and France honoured their promise. When Hitler ignored their final ultimatum, they declared war on Germany, ending a decade of international tension and plunging Europe into its second catastrophic 20th-century conflict.

Responsibility for the Outbreak of WWII

Study Notes

The Second World War's September 1939 outbreak was not a single person or event fault. It was the culmination of twenty years of international tension, flawed treaties, failed institutions, and disastrous political decisions. Historians continue debating the exact responsibility level held by different individuals and policies. This lesson assesses the main arguments for who, or what, was ultimately to blame for the conflict.

Argument 1: Hitler was Responsible

This is the most direct and widely accepted argument. It holds that the war was a direct result of Adolf Hitler's aggressive, expansionist foreign policy.

His Aims: From the beginning, Hitler's goals, as outlined in Mein Kampf, were to destroy the Treaty of Versailles, expand German territory for 'Lebensraum', and unite all German peoples. These aims could not be achieved without war.

His Actions: He systematically broke international agreements, from rearmament to the Rhineland remilitarisation and the Austria Anschluss.

The Final Trigger: His September 1939 Poland invasion was the direct and immediate cause of Britain and France war declarations.

Hitler's deliberate and relentless 1933-1939 actions were the primary and undeniable war cause.

Argument 2: Chamberlain & Appeasement was Responsible

This argument suggests that while Hitler was the aggressor, the appeasement policy led by Neville Chamberlain encouraged him and made war more likely.

Rewarding Aggression: By repeatedly giving in to Hitler's demands over the Rhineland, Anschluss, and Sudetenland, the policy showed Hitler that he could act without facing consequences.

Emboldening Hitler: Each appeasement victory made Hitler more popular at home, strengthened his position, and made him more confident and willing to take bigger risks.

A Miscalculation: It sent a clear message that Britain and France lacked the will to fight, leading Hitler to miscalculate that they would not honour their Poland guarantee.

Chamberlain's policy, though intended preventing war, was a disastrous failure empowering Hitler and making a larger conflict inevitable.

Argument 3: Stalin was Responsible

This argument focuses on the crucial Soviet Union role in the war's final days.

The Decisive Action: The Nazi-Soviet Pact was the event that sealed Poland's fate and unleashed the war.

Enabling Hitler: By signing the pact, Stalin removed the two-front war threat for Hitler, which had been his greatest fear. It was the green light Hitler needed for Poland invasion.

Cynical Self-Interest: Stalin was not tricked; he made a cynical deal to gain territory for himself (eastern Poland) and to buy time for the USSR to prepare for a future Germany war.

While Hitler lit the match, Stalin poured the petrol. His Hitler pact was the key unlocking the door to September 1939 war.

Argument 4: Wider Factors were Responsible

This argument suggests that the war was caused by deep-rooted problems going far beyond a few individuals' actions.

The Treaty of Versailles (1919): The harsh and humiliating terms created a deep grievance in Germany, which Hitler masterfully exploited to gain power and public support for his actions. It can be considered the long-term war cause.

The Failure of the League of Nations: The League's inability to stop aggression in Manchuria (1931) and Abyssinia (1935) proved that collective security was an empty promise and that no international body existed to stop a determined aggressor.

The Great Depression (1929): The global economic collapse created poverty and desperation conditions leading to the rise of extremist, nationalist leaders like Hitler in Germany and the military in Japan. It also weakened democratic nations' resolve to stand up to them.

This is a classic 'who was to blame?' essay question. The best answers create a balanced, multi-causal argument. Start agreeing that Hitler was the main war architect. Then, broaden your argument.

Conclusion

There is no single, simple Second World War cause. Hitler's limitless ambition was undoubtedly the main engine driving Europe towards conflict. However, his journey was enabled and accelerated by a series of critical international community failures: a flawed peace treaty creating lasting grievance, a weak League of Nations failing to provide security, a disastrous appeasement policy emboldening aggression, and a cynical Stalin pact giving Hitler the freedom to act. The war was the ultimate consequence of a collective diplomatic failure stretching back two decades.
