# Writing an Evaluation Response

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is an Evaluation Question?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 4 on AQA Paper 1 is the highest-value reading question, worth <strong>20 marks</strong>. It asks you to <dfn class="term" data-def="To judge the value or quality of something by weighing up evidence for and against a statement.">evaluate</dfn> a statement about the text. The question will always give you a specific claim &mdash; for example, that a character is &ldquo;funny or threatening&rdquo; &mdash; and ask you: <em>To what extent do you agree or disagree?</em></p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This question tests <strong>AO4</strong>: &ldquo;Evaluate texts critically and support this with appropriate textual references.&rdquo; Unlike Q2 (language) and Q3 (structure), Q4 requires you to form your own <dfn class="term" data-def="A considered opinion or position that is supported with evidence from the text.">judgement</dfn> about the text and defend it with evidence. You are acting like a critic, not just a detective.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Q4 is worth 20 marks &mdash; more than Q2 (8 marks) and Q3 (8 marks) combined. You should spend approximately 20&ndash;25 minutes on this question. It targets the second half of the source text (from a specified line to the end).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">How the Question Is Worded</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The 2026 sample paper gives us a clear example of what Q4 looks like. Here is the real question from the AQA 8700/1 sample assessment:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><em>&ldquo;For this question focus on the second part of the source, from line 20 to the end. In this part of the source, the hyena could be seen as funny or threatening. To what extent do you agree or disagree with this statement?&rdquo;</em></p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">The question then gives you bullet-point prompts:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n9">Consider your impressions of how the hyena behaves.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n10">Comment on the <dfn class="term" data-def="The techniques and choices a writer makes, such as language, imagery, structure, and tone, to create particular effects.">methods</dfn> the writer uses to present the hyena as funny or threatening.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n11">Support your response with references to the text.</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Notice three things: (1) you are told exactly which lines to focus on; (2) the statement contains <strong>two sides</strong> &mdash; funny <em>or</em> threatening; (3) the phrase &ldquo;to what extent&rdquo; means you can agree, disagree, or do both. You do not have to pick one side completely.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Understanding the Mark Scheme Levels</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">AQA uses a four-level mark scheme for Q4. Understanding the difference between levels is the single most important thing you can do to improve your mark. Each level is defined by <dfn class="term" data-def="Words or phrases in a mark scheme that describe the quality of response expected at each level, such as &lsquo;perceptive&rsquo; or &lsquo;simple&rsquo;.">skills descriptors</dfn> and key words:</p>

You analyse the effects of the writer&rsquo;s methods, select judicious (well-chosen) textual detail, and develop a convincing and critical response to the statement.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Level 3 (11&ndash;15 marks) &mdash; Clear, relevant:</strong> You show a clear and relevant evaluation. You explain clearly how methods are used, select relevant textual detail, and make a clear and relevant response to the statement.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Level 2 (6&ndash;10 marks) &mdash; Some, attempts:</strong> You show some evaluation. You attempt to comment on the effect of methods, select some appropriate detail, and make some response to the statement.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Level 1 (1&ndash;5 marks) &mdash; Simple, limited:</strong> You show simple, limited evaluation. You offer simple comments on the effect of methods, select simple references, and make a simple response to the statement.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n19">The biggest jump in marks comes between Level 2 and Level 3. At Level 2, students <em>attempt</em> to comment on methods but don&rsquo;t explain the effects clearly. At Level 3, students <em>explain clearly</em> how and why the writer&rsquo;s choices create the effect described in the statement. To reach Level 4, your comments must be <em>perceptive</em> &mdash; showing insight, subtlety, or an original angle the examiner hasn&rsquo;t seen a hundred times before.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">The PEEL Structure for Evaluation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">A strong evaluation paragraph follows a clear pattern. Use <strong>PEEL</strong> to keep each paragraph focused:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n22"><strong>P &mdash; Point:</strong> State your evaluative judgement. Do you agree or disagree with the statement for this particular moment in the text? Use phrases like &ldquo;I agree that the hyena is presented as threatening here because&hellip;&rdquo;</li>
<li data-narration-id="n23"><strong>E &mdash; Evidence:</strong> Embed a short, well-chosen quotation from the specified lines.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n24"><strong>E &mdash; Explain:</strong> Analyse the writer&rsquo;s method. Name the technique and explain what effect it creates on the reader. This is where you show you understand <em>how</em> the writer achieves the effect, not just <em>what</em> happens.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25"><strong>L &mdash; Link back:</strong> Explicitly link your analysis back to the statement. Does this evidence support or challenge the claim? Say so.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The mark scheme rewards quality over quantity. One perceptive, well-developed paragraph is worth more than three simple ones. It is not the number of references but the quality of comments that determines the level.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Model Paragraph: Applying PEEL to the Sample Paper</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Let&rsquo;s apply this to the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract from the 2026 sample paper. The statement says the hyena could be seen as &ldquo;funny or threatening.&rdquo; Here is a Level 4 style paragraph:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Point:</strong> &ldquo;I agree that the hyena is initially presented as threatening in this section.&rdquo; <strong>Evidence:</strong> &ldquo;The writer describes &lsquo;the beating of its legs against the benches made the whole boat shake, and its claws were loudly clicking on their surface.&rsquo;&rdquo; <strong>Explain:</strong> &ldquo;The onomatopoeic verb &lsquo;clicking&rsquo; and the physical force conveyed by &lsquo;made the whole boat shake&rsquo; emphasise the hyena&rsquo;s raw power in the confined space. The reader shares Pi&rsquo;s claustrophobic fear because the threat is inescapable &mdash; the hyena is not just nearby, it is literally shaking the ground beneath him.&rdquo; <strong>Link:</strong> &ldquo;This strongly supports the idea that the hyena is threatening, as the writer makes the reader feel physically trapped alongside the narrator.&rdquo;</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Students lose marks on Q4 for the same avoidable errors every year:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Retelling the story:</strong> Q4 is not a comprehension question. The examiner does not want a summary of what happens. Every sentence should include analysis or evaluation.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Ignoring the statement:</strong> Some students write a general language analysis as if it were Q2. You <em>must</em> refer back to the statement. Use the exact words from the question &mdash; &ldquo;funny,&rdquo; &ldquo;threatening&rdquo; &mdash; in your response.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Writing about the wrong lines:</strong> The question specifies &ldquo;from line 20 to the end.&rdquo; If you write about language outside these lines, the mark scheme says your response should be placed at the bottom of the level.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Spotting techniques without explaining effects:</strong> Naming a metaphor is not enough. You need to explain <em>what effect</em> it has on the reader and <em>how</em> it connects to the statement.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n38">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>According to the AQA mark scheme, a student must address the correct focus of the question to achieve Level 3 or above. If you write about the wrong lines or ignore the statement, you cannot score higher than Level 2 (10 marks maximum), no matter how good your analysis is.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Suggested Response Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40">For a 20&ndash;25 minute response, aim for <strong>3&ndash;4 developed paragraphs</strong>. Here is a suggested plan:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Paragraph 1:</strong> Agree with one side of the statement. Analyse a method that supports it.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42"><strong>Paragraph 2:</strong> Explore the other side. Find evidence that supports the alternative reading.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n43"><strong>Paragraph 3:</strong> Show where both sides overlap or where the writer shifts between them. This is your most analytical paragraph.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Paragraph 4 (optional):</strong> A short concluding judgement. To what extent do you agree overall? A single sentence is enough.</li>
</ul>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n51">Q4 is the only reading question that asks for your personal response. The command phrase &ldquo;to what extent do you agree&rdquo; means you <strong>must</strong> state your position. Do not sit on the fence without ever committing &mdash; use phrases like &ldquo;I strongly agree&rdquo; or &ldquo;I partially agree&rdquo; to anchor each paragraph. The examiner is looking for a critical voice, not a neutral summary. Remember: always refer back to the exact words in the statement, always stay within the specified lines, and always explain the <em>effect</em> of methods on the reader. Quality beats quantity &mdash; three perceptive paragraphs will outscore five simple ones every time.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n52">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n53">Q4 is worth 20 marks and tests AO4 &mdash; your ability to evaluate critically. You must form a judgement about the statement and support it with evidence and analysis of the writer&rsquo;s methods.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n54">Engage with both sides of the statement to reach Level 3 and above. The best responses explore how the writer creates competing effects simultaneously.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n55">Stay within the specified lines, refer back to the statement throughout, and focus on explaining the <em>effect</em> of methods on the reader &mdash; not just identifying techniques.</li>
</ul>
