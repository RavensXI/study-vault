# The 'Big 5' Language Techniques

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Language Techniques Matter on Paper 1</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">On AQA Paper 1, Question 2 asks you to analyse how a writer uses language. Question 4 also rewards you for commenting on the writer&rsquo;s methods. In both cases, the examiners want you to identify specific language techniques, quote them accurately, and &mdash; most importantly &mdash; explain their <dfn class="term" data-def="The impact a writer's choice of language has on the reader's thoughts, feelings, or understanding.">effect</dfn> on the reader. You cannot reach Level 3 or Level 4 without doing this.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This lesson focuses on five language techniques that appear again and again in fiction extracts. We call them the &ldquo;Big 5&rdquo; because they are the techniques examiners most commonly reward &mdash; and the ones writers rely on most heavily. Learn to spot them, name them, and explain their effect, and you will have a toolkit that works on any unseen text.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>AQA&rsquo;s mark scheme for Paper 1, Question 2 (AO2) assesses your ability to &ldquo;explain, comment on and analyse how writers use language and structure to achieve effects and influence readers, using relevant subject terminology to support their views.&rdquo; To reach Level 3 (clear, relevant), you must explain the effects of the writer&rsquo;s language choices clearly and use subject terminology accurately.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Technique 1: Metaphor</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech that describes something as if it literally is something else, creating a comparison without using 'like' or 'as'.">metaphor</dfn> describes one thing as though it is something else. Unlike a simile, it does not use &ldquo;like&rdquo; or &ldquo;as.&rdquo; This creates a direct comparison that can be powerful and surprising.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">In the AQA 2026 sample Paper 1 extract from <em>Life of Pi</em>, the narrator describes the hyena&rsquo;s spots as &ldquo;the symptoms of a skin disease.&rdquo; This is a metaphor that transforms the animal&rsquo;s natural markings into something repulsive and unhealthy. The effect is to make the reader share Pi&rsquo;s disgust and fear &mdash; the hyena is not just unattractive, it is presented as contaminated and dangerous.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">In the 2026 sample Paper 2 extract, Peter Fleming uses the metaphor &ldquo;cannonaded down&rdquo; to describe his suitcase falling during the train crash. The verb <em>cannonaded</em> turns an ordinary suitcase into a weapon of war, exaggerating the violence of the crash and creating a darkly comic tone as the writer pretends he is under military bombardment.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">Technique 2: Personification</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of figurative language where human qualities, emotions, or actions are given to non-human things such as objects, animals, or abstract ideas.">Personification</dfn> gives human qualities to something non-human. Writers use it to make objects feel alive, threatening, or emotional.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">In the Peter Fleming extract, the train engine is described as having &ldquo;a defiant and naughty look&rdquo; and being &ldquo;definitely conscious of indiscretion.&rdquo; The engine is given the personality of a mischievous child who knows it has done something wrong. This personification transforms a serious train crash into something absurd and humorous, reflecting the writer&rsquo;s sardonic, mocking tone.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Similarly, in Fanny Kemble&rsquo;s letter (Source B, Paper 2), the steam engine is described as a &ldquo;snorting little animal&rdquo; and a &ldquo;brave little she-dragon.&rdquo; Here, personification (combined with <dfn class="term" data-def="A technique where an animal is given human characteristics, or where something non-animal is described using animal qualities.">zoomorphism</dfn>) makes the new technology feel alive and exciting rather than cold and mechanical, reflecting Kemble&rsquo;s sense of wonder.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Technique 3: Simile</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A comparison between two things using the words 'like' or 'as', helping the reader visualise or understand something by linking it to something familiar.">simile</dfn> compares two things using &ldquo;like&rdquo; or &ldquo;as.&rdquo; It works by connecting something unfamiliar to something the reader already understands.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">In <em>Life of Pi</em>, the narrator describes the hyena&rsquo;s spots as &ldquo;like the symptoms of a skin disease.&rdquo; The simile compares the animal&rsquo;s natural appearance to illness, which creates connotations of contamination and decay. The reader is positioned to see the hyena as something diseased rather than simply unattractive. The writer also describes the hyena&rsquo;s ears as &ldquo;ridiculously mouse-like&rdquo; &mdash; the simile undercuts any sense of threat by making the ears sound comically small and out of proportion, adding a note of dark humour to the description.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Many students confuse metaphors and similes. Remember: a simile uses &ldquo;like&rdquo; or &ldquo;as&rdquo; to compare (&ldquo;her eyes were like diamonds&rdquo;), while a metaphor states that something <em>is</em> something else (&ldquo;her eyes were diamonds&rdquo;). In the exam, always name the correct technique &mdash; misidentifying it can cost you marks.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Technique 4: Hyperbole</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><dfn class="term" data-def="Deliberate and obvious exaggeration used for emphasis or effect. It is not meant to be taken literally.">Hyperbole</dfn> is deliberate exaggeration. Writers use it to emphasise emotions, create humour, or make a point more dramatic.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">When the suitcase hits Peter Fleming during the train crash, he thinks: &ldquo;This is the end of the world &hellip; and in addition they have broken both my legs.&rdquo; Neither statement is true &mdash; the world has not ended and his legs are not broken. The hyperbole exaggerates his shock in the moment of impact, but the comedic tone (&ldquo;in addition&rdquo;) shows the writer is already laughing at himself. This creates the sardonic, understated humour that runs throughout the passage.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">In <em>Life of Pi</em>, the description of the hyena as &ldquo;ugly beyond redemption&rdquo; is also hyperbolic. The phrase suggests the animal is so hideous that nothing could ever save or improve its appearance. The religious connotation of &ldquo;redemption&rdquo; intensifies the exaggeration, implying the hyena&rsquo;s ugliness is almost a moral failing.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Technique 5: Adjectives and Adverbs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">This might seem basic, but careful analysis of <dfn class="term" data-def="Words that describe or modify nouns (adjectives) or verbs (adverbs). Writers choose these carefully to shape the reader's impression of a character, setting, or event.">adjectives and adverbs</dfn> is one of the most reliable ways to reach Level 3 and Level 4. Examiners reward students who can explain <em>why</em> a specific word was chosen over any other.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24">In <em>Life of Pi</em>, the hyena&rsquo;s coat is described as &ldquo;shaggy&rdquo; and &ldquo;coarse.&rdquo; Both adjectives suggest roughness and a lack of care &mdash; this is not a sleek, graceful animal. The adjective &ldquo;bungled&rdquo; (describing the mix of colours) implies the hyena was badly made, as if a mistake has been made in its creation. This is a much more interesting word than simply saying the colours are &ldquo;mixed&rdquo; or &ldquo;messy.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">In Peter Fleming&rsquo;s account, the adverbs are particularly powerful. The train &ldquo;sprawled foolishly&rdquo; and the last carriage remained &ldquo;primly&rdquo; on the rails. &ldquo;Foolishly&rdquo; makes the crashed train look ridiculous and embarrassed, while &ldquo;primly&rdquo; gives the surviving carriage an air of smug superiority. These adverbs work alongside the personification to create the writer&rsquo;s humorous, detached tone.</p>

Connotations are the associations and feelings a word carries beyond its dictionary definition. For example, the AQA mark scheme for the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract notes that &ldquo;redemption&rdquo; has &ldquo;connotations of being saved from evil, as if the hyena&rsquo;s ugliness is symbolic of a deeper sinister nature.&rdquo; That is a Level 4 comment because it goes beyond the surface meaning and explores the deeper implications of the word choice.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27">Try this formula: <strong>Identify the technique &rarr; Quote it &rarr; Explain the literal meaning &rarr; Explore the connotations &rarr; Link to the writer&rsquo;s purpose or the reader&rsquo;s response.</strong></p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Putting It All Together: The Analysis Chain</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Knowing the Big 5 is only half the battle. The mark scheme rewards you for how you write about techniques, not just for spotting them. The AQA levels work like this:</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>AQA Paper 1 Q2 Level Descriptors Explained</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Level 1 (1&ndash;2 marks):</strong> You spot a technique and quote it, but only offer a simple comment. Example: &ldquo;The writer says the hyena is &lsquo;ugly beyond redemption&rsquo;. This is hyperbole as it is just describing its appearance, but he uses this to emphasise how ugly it is.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Level 2 (3&ndash;4 marks):</strong> You attempt to comment on the effect and use some subject terminology. Example: &ldquo;The writer uses adjectives to make the hyena seem unpleasant. &lsquo;Ugly beyond redemption&rsquo; suggests it is so horrible that nothing could be done about it.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Level 3 (5&ndash;6 marks):</strong> You clearly explain the effects and use accurate terminology. Example: &ldquo;The short sentence &lsquo;it is ugly beyond redemption&rsquo; is deliberately blunt and the adjective &lsquo;ugly&rsquo; introduces the animal as hideous. &lsquo;Beyond redemption&rsquo; exaggerates further, claiming nothing could ever improve its appearance.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Level 4 (7&ndash;8 marks):</strong> You analyse effects perceptively and explore connotations. Example: &ldquo;The adjective &lsquo;ugly&rsquo; labels the hyena as grotesque, emphasised by the harshness of the short sentence. &lsquo;Beyond redemption&rsquo; has connotations of being saved from evil, as if the hyena&rsquo;s ugliness is symbolic of a deeper sinister nature that cannot be reversed.&rdquo;</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n34">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The difference between Level 2 and Level 3 is the quality of your explanation, not the number of techniques you spot. One well-analysed quotation with a clear explanation of effect will score higher than three techniques listed with no analysis. Quality over quantity &mdash; always.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n35">Use this approach for every quotation you analyse: <strong>name the technique</strong>, <strong>embed a short quotation</strong>, <strong>explain what it literally means</strong>, and then <strong>explore the deeper connotations or the effect on the reader</strong>. This is what the mark scheme calls &ldquo;analysing the effects of the writer&rsquo;s choices.&rdquo;</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n36">On Paper 1, Question 2 is worth 8 marks and you should spend about 10 minutes on it. The biggest mistake students make is &ldquo;feature spotting&rdquo; &mdash; listing techniques without explaining their effect. The mark scheme is clear: you must analyse <em>effects</em> and use terminology <em>accurately</em>. Aim for 2&ndash;3 well-developed points rather than 5 shallow ones. Always check the line references in the question &mdash; if you write about language outside the given lines, the AQA mark scheme caps you at Level 1 or Level 2. Finally, avoid saying &ldquo;this makes the reader want to read on&rdquo; &mdash; examiners see this thousands of times and it earns no marks. Instead, be specific about what the reader thinks, feels, or imagines.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n37">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n38">The &ldquo;Big 5&rdquo; language techniques &mdash; metaphor, personification, simile, hyperbole, and adjectives/adverbs &mdash; appear in almost every fiction extract and are the techniques examiners most commonly reward.</li><li data-narration-id="n39">Naming the technique is not enough. You must explain its <strong>effect</strong> on the reader and explore the <strong>connotations</strong> of key words to move beyond Level 2.</li><li data-narration-id="n40">Quality beats quantity: 2&ndash;3 detailed, perceptive points will outscore 5 shallow observations every time.</li></ul>
