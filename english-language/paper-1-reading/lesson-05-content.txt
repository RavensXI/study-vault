# Writing a Structure Analysis Response

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Does Paper 1, Question 3 Actually Ask?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 3 on AQA Paper 1 is worth <strong>8 marks</strong> and asks you to analyse how a writer has used <dfn class="term" data-def="The way a writer organises and arranges a text to create effects on the reader, including shifts in focus, changes in pace, and narrative perspective.">structure</dfn> across the whole source to achieve a specific effect. Unlike Question 2, which focuses on language in a short extract, Question 3 requires you to look at the <strong>whole text</strong> and explain how the writer has deliberately organised it to shape the reader&rsquo;s experience.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The question will always give you a specific focus &mdash; for example, &ldquo;How has the writer structured the text to create tension?&rdquo; or &ldquo;How has the writer structured the text to interest you as a reader?&rdquo; Everything you write must link back to this focus. You are assessed on <strong>AO2</strong>: your ability to explain, comment on and analyse how writers use structure to achieve effects and influence readers, using relevant subject terminology.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 3 is about structure, NOT language. You should discuss how the text is organised and arranged &mdash; not individual word choices. Think about what happens where, why the writer has ordered things this way, and how that order creates a specific effect on the reader.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">What Counts as a &ldquo;Structural Feature&rdquo;?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Many students struggle with Question 3 because they don&rsquo;t know what to look for. Structure is about the <em>arrangement</em> of a text &mdash; the decisions a writer makes about what to show the reader and when. Here are the key structural features the AQA mark scheme expects you to discuss:</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Key Structural Features to Look For</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Shifts in focus:</strong> Does the writer move between different subjects, characters, settings or time periods? A shift from action to description, or from one character to another, changes the pace and directs the reader&rsquo;s attention.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Changes in mood or tone:</strong> Does the atmosphere of the text change? A sudden shift from calm to chaos, or from humour to fear, creates a powerful effect.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A hint or warning about something that will happen later in the text, used to build anticipation or tension.">Foreshadowing</dfn>:</strong> Does the writer drop early hints about what will happen later? This builds anticipation and makes the reader uneasy.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The moment in a text where a significant change occurs, such as a turning point in the action, mood or focus.">Pivotal point</dfn> / turning point:</strong> Is there a key moment where the action, mood or direction of the text changes dramatically?</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Narrative pace:</strong> Does the writer speed up or slow down the action? Short sentences and rapid events increase pace; long descriptions and reflective passages slow it down.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Narrative perspective:</strong> Whose eyes do we see the events through? A first-person narrator forces us to share their fear, excitement or confusion directly.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Opening and ending:</strong> How does the text begin and end? Does it start in the middle of the action? Does it end on a cliffhanger, an anti-climax, or a resolution?</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Juxtaposition:</strong> Does the writer place two contrasting ideas, images or scenes side by side to highlight differences?</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">The Mark Scheme: What Examiners Want</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">The AQA mark scheme for Question 3 uses four levels. Understanding these levels is essential because they tell you exactly what separates a basic answer from a top-level one.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Breaking Down the Four Levels</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Level 1 (1&ndash;2 marks) &mdash; Simple, limited:</strong> The student offers simple comments on structure. They might identify what happens at the beginning and the end, but they only describe events rather than analysing the writer&rsquo;s choices. Subject terminology is simple and not always used correctly.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Level 2 (3&ndash;4 marks) &mdash; Some, attempts:</strong> The student attempts to comment on the effect of structural features. They select some appropriate examples but their explanations are incomplete or emerging. Subject terminology is used, mainly appropriately.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Level 3 (5&ndash;6 marks) &mdash; Clear, relevant:</strong> The student clearly explains the effects of the writer&rsquo;s structural choices. They select a range of relevant examples and use subject terminology clearly and accurately. Their response is directly focused on the question.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Level 4 (7&ndash;8 marks) &mdash; Perceptive, detailed:</strong> The student analyses the effects of the writer&rsquo;s structural choices with insight. They select judicious examples and make sophisticated, accurate use of subject terminology. Their response is exploratory and analytical.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n21">The biggest difference between Level 2 and Level 3 is the word <strong>&ldquo;explains.&rdquo;</strong> At Level 2, students attempt to comment but don&rsquo;t fully develop their ideas. At Level 3, every point is clearly explained &mdash; the student says <em>what</em> the structural feature is, <em>where</em> it appears, and <em>why</em> it creates a specific effect on the reader. At Level 4, the student goes further by analysing how structural choices work together and exploring subtle or layered effects.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>According to the AQA mark scheme, it is not the <em>number</em> of references that determines the level, but the <em>quality</em> of the comments. One perceptive, well-developed paragraph can score higher than four shallow points. Always prioritise depth over quantity.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">A Step-by-Step Method for Question 3</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">You should spend roughly <strong>8&ndash;10 minutes</strong> on Question 3 (including reading time). Here is a reliable method:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Step 1: Re-read and divide the text into three sections.</strong> Think of the source as having a beginning, middle and end. What happens in each section? What changes between them? Annotate the text with notes about shifts in focus, mood or pace.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Step 2: Identify the question&rsquo;s focus.</strong> The question will always specify an effect (e.g. tension, interest, sympathy). Every point you make must link back to this focus. If the question asks about tension, don&rsquo;t just describe what happens &mdash; explain how the structure builds, maintains or releases tension.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Step 3: Write 2&ndash;3 developed paragraphs.</strong> Each paragraph should cover a different part of the text (beginning, middle or end) and analyse a specific structural choice. Use this pattern:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Structural feature &rarr; Evidence &rarr; Effect on the reader</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Name the structural technique, give a brief reference to the text, and then explain the effect this has on the reader in relation to the question&rsquo;s focus.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Applying the Method: The <em>Life of Pi</em> Extract</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">In the 2026 AQA Paper 1 sample assessment, Question 3 asks: <strong>&ldquo;How has the writer structured the text to create tension?&rdquo;</strong> The source is an extract from the middle of <em>Life of Pi</em> by Yann Martel, in which the narrator Pi is trapped in a lifeboat with a hyena. Let&rsquo;s apply the method to this real exam question.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Model Paragraph 1: The Opening (Foreshadowing)</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n32">The writer establishes tension from the very first sentence: &ldquo;It was the hyena that worried me.&rdquo; By introducing Pi&rsquo;s fear immediately, the writer <dfn class="term" data-def="A hint or warning about something that will happen later in the text, used to build anticipation or tension.">foreshadows</dfn> the threatening encounter that follows. Instead of moving straight to the action, the writer then deliberately slows the pace by including detailed background information about hyenas &mdash; their hunting methods, their persistence, their cleverness. This creates a sense of dread because the reader now understands exactly how dangerous the hyena is before it has even appeared. The structural choice to delay the action while building the threat forces the reader to share Pi&rsquo;s anxious anticipation.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Model Paragraph 2: The Middle (Pivotal Point &amp; Rising Action)</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n33">The pivotal point occurs when the hyena suddenly &ldquo;leapt over the zebra&rdquo; and began running in circles. After the slow, descriptive opening, this sudden burst of movement dramatically increases the pace. The writer uses repetition of the hyena&rsquo;s laps &mdash; &ldquo;one lap-two-three-four-five-and onwards&rdquo; &mdash; to convey frantic, relentless movement that feels chaotic and unpredictable. The tension escalates because the reader, sharing Pi&rsquo;s first-person perspective, cannot predict whether the hyena will keep circling or attack. Each time it pauses at the stern bench and looks at Pi, the tension peaks momentarily before dropping back as it resumes running. This cycle of rising and falling tension keeps the reader on edge.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Model Paragraph 3: The Ending (Anti-climax)</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n34">The ending of the extract provides an unexpected <dfn class="term" data-def="A disappointing or underwhelming conclusion to a sequence of events that had been building towards a climax. Used deliberately by writers to subvert reader expectations.">anti-climax</dfn> that undercuts the escalating tension. After all the frantic circling and Pi&rsquo;s repeated preparation for &ldquo;my end,&rdquo; the hyena simply vomits and collapses. The juxtaposition between the terrifying predator described at the start and this vulnerable, sick animal at the end is structurally significant. However, the final sentence &mdash; &ldquo;It did not move from the restricted space for the rest of the day&rdquo; &mdash; does not fully resolve the tension. The phrase &ldquo;the rest of the day&rdquo; implies tomorrow is still uncertain, leaving the reader with lingering unease.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n35">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Mistake 1: Retelling the story.</strong> Many students simply describe what happens in the text without analysing <em>why</em> the writer has arranged events in this order. Saying &ldquo;At the start, the hyena is mentioned&rdquo; is retelling. Saying &ldquo;The writer introduces the hyena immediately to foreshadow the threatening encounter and create a sense of dread&rdquo; is analysis.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Mistake 2: Writing about language instead of structure.</strong> Avoid analysing individual words, metaphors or similes in Question 3. You should discuss <em>organisation</em>: what the writer chooses to focus on, when and why. If you mention a technique like repetition, link it to pace or tension &mdash; not to the connotations of individual words.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Mistake 3: Using the &ldquo;beginning, middle, end&rdquo; template without analysis.</strong> Simply saying &ldquo;At the beginning&hellip; In the middle&hellip; At the end&hellip;&rdquo; is a useful framework, but only if each section contains genuine analysis of a structural feature and its effect. Examiners can spot mechanical responses that follow a template without real engagement.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Mistake 4: Ignoring the focus of the question.</strong> The AQA mark scheme states that you must address the correct focus to achieve Level 3 or above. If the question asks about tension, every paragraph must explain how the structure creates, builds or releases <em>tension</em> specifically.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n40">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The mark scheme says students can reach a given level by making just <strong>one or more</strong> comments at that standard. Two well-developed, perceptive paragraphs are far better than five shallow ones. Quality always beats quantity in Question 3.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n41">When answering Question 3, always name the structural feature using correct <dfn class="term" data-def="The technical vocabulary used to describe how writers construct texts, such as foreshadowing, pivotal point, juxtaposition and narrative pace.">subject terminology</dfn> (e.g. foreshadowing, pivotal point, anti-climax) before explaining its effect. Avoid simply tracking through the text saying &ldquo;at the start&hellip; then&hellip; at the end.&rdquo; Instead, choose 2&ndash;3 structural features and develop each one fully. Remember: the question asks &ldquo;how has the writer structured&rdquo; &mdash; the command word is &ldquo;how,&rdquo; so you need to explain the method and its effect, not just describe what happens. Spend around 8&ndash;10 minutes on this question and aim for 2&ndash;3 paragraphs of clear, focused analysis.</p>

## Conclusion

<ul><li data-narration-id="n43">Question 3 is worth 8 marks and assesses your ability to analyse <strong>structural features</strong> (not language) across the <strong>whole</strong> text, linking every point to the specific focus of the question.</li><li data-narration-id="n44">Use correct subject terminology &mdash; foreshadowing, pivotal point, shifts in focus, narrative pace, juxtaposition, anti-climax &mdash; and explain <em>how</em> each structural choice creates a specific effect on the reader.</li><li data-narration-id="n45">Quality beats quantity: 2&ndash;3 well-developed analytical paragraphs will score higher than five shallow observations.
