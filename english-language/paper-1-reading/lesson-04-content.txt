# Structural Features and the 'Big 5' for Structure

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Structure in English Language?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">When we talk about <dfn class="term" data-def="The way a writer organises and arranges their text to shape the reader's experience, including what is revealed, when, and how focus shifts.">structure</dfn> in English Language, we are not talking about individual words or phrases. Instead, we are looking at how the writer has organised the whole text. Think of it like the architecture of a building &mdash; the blueprint that decides what the reader sees first, what comes next, and how everything connects together.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">On AQA Paper 1, Question 3 is worth <strong>8 marks</strong> and asks you to analyse how a writer has <strong>structured</strong> a text to interest the reader. This means you need to look at the big picture: how the text begins, how it develops, and how it ends. You also need to comment on specific structural choices the writer makes along the way.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4">Many students lose marks on this question because they write about language instead of structure. If you find yourself analysing a metaphor or an adjective, you have slipped into a language answer. Structure is about <em>what</em> the writer focuses on and <em>when</em>, not the specific words they choose.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 3 on Paper 1 assesses AO2: &ldquo;Explain, comment on and analyse how writers use language and <strong>structure</strong> to achieve effects and influence readers, using relevant subject terminology to support their views.&rdquo; It is the only reading question on Paper 1 that focuses exclusively on structure.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">The &lsquo;Big 5&rsquo; Structural Features</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">To help you identify and analyse structural choices, we use the &lsquo;Big 5&rsquo; &mdash; five key structural features that writers use across fiction and non-fiction. Learning these gives you a reliable toolkit to approach any Question 3 confidently.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">1. Focus Shifts</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A change in what the writer directs the reader's attention to, such as moving from one character to another, from setting to action, or from external events to internal thoughts.">focus shift</dfn> is when the writer moves the reader&rsquo;s attention from one thing to another. This could be a shift from one character to another, from description to action, from the external world to a character&rsquo;s internal thoughts, or from the present to the past.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">For example, in the AQA sample paper extract from <em>The Life of Pi</em>, the writer begins by focusing on Pi&rsquo;s background knowledge about hyenas &mdash; what his father told him, how they hunt. The focus then shifts to a detailed description of the hyena&rsquo;s appearance, before shifting again to the hyena&rsquo;s sudden, erratic behaviour. Each shift changes the reader&rsquo;s experience: first we feel informed, then disgusted, then alarmed.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">When you spot a focus shift, always explain <strong>why</strong> the writer moves the reader&rsquo;s attention. What effect does this change have? Does it build tension, create surprise, or provide contrast?</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">2. Foreshadowing</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><dfn class="term" data-def="A structural technique where the writer plants clues or hints early in the text about events that will happen later, creating anticipation or unease in the reader.">Foreshadowing</dfn> is when a writer drops hints about what is going to happen later. It creates a sense of anticipation or dread and makes the reader want to keep reading.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">In the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract, the very first sentence &mdash; &ldquo;It was the hyena that worried me&rdquo; &mdash; foreshadows the threatening behaviour that follows. By telling us about his worry <em>before</em> anything dangerous happens, the writer makes us expect danger. This means that when the hyena finally leaps out, our tension has already been building.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">3. Pivotal Point (Turning Point)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A key moment in a text where something significant changes — the mood shifts, the action escalates, or new information alters the reader's understanding.">pivotal point</dfn> is a moment where the text changes direction. It might be a sudden event, a revelation, or a dramatic shift in mood or tone. The pivotal point often divides the text into a &lsquo;before&rsquo; and &lsquo;after&rsquo;.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">In the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract, the pivotal point comes when the hyena &ldquo;leapt over the zebra and onto the stern bench.&rdquo; Before this moment, the extract is dominated by Pi&rsquo;s thoughts and description. After it, the extract becomes fast-paced and action-driven. The mood shifts from uneasy anticipation to real, immediate danger.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Identifying the pivotal point is one of the most effective things you can do in a Question 3 response. It shows the examiner you understand the overall shape of the text.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">4. Narrative Pace</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><dfn class="term" data-def="The speed at which a text moves. Writers speed up pace with short sentences and rapid action, or slow it down with long descriptions and detailed reflection.">Narrative pace</dfn> refers to how fast or slow the text feels. Writers control pace through sentence length, the amount of description, and the balance between action and reflection.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Short sentences and quick actions speed the pace up, creating excitement or urgency. Long, detailed descriptions slow the pace down, allowing the reader to absorb setting, character, or atmosphere. A shift in pace is always worth commenting on because it signals a deliberate choice by the writer to change the reader&rsquo;s experience.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">In the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract, the pace accelerates dramatically once the hyena starts running in circles. The use of the hyphenated counting &mdash; &ldquo;one lap-two-three-four-five-and onwards&rdquo; &mdash; mimics the frantic, breathless speed of the hyena&rsquo;s movement. The pace then slows right down at the end of the extract when the hyena stops and vomits, and &ldquo;did not move from the restricted space for the rest of the day.&rdquo; This deceleration creates an <dfn class="term" data-def="A moment where expected excitement or climax does not materialise, often creating an unexpected sense of deflation or relief.">anti-climax</dfn>, releasing the tension that had been building.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme for Question 3 rewards students who comment on &ldquo;narrative pace&rdquo; as a structural feature. Identifying where pace speeds up or slows down &mdash; and explaining <em>why</em> &mdash; is a quick way to access Level 3 and Level 4 marks.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">5. Opening and Ending</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">The way a text begins and ends is always a deliberate structural choice. Writers may open with a dramatic hook, a mysterious detail, or a calm establishing shot that lulls the reader before disruption. Endings might resolve tension, leave the reader with unanswered questions, or circle back to the opening.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">In the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract, the opening immediately names the source of threat &mdash; &ldquo;It was the hyena that worried me&rdquo; &mdash; plunging the reader straight into Pi&rsquo;s anxiety. The ending, however, is surprisingly calm: the hyena collapses and does not move for the rest of the day. This contrast between the tense, fear-driven opening and the quiet, deflated ending creates a sense of anti-climax. The threat dissolves rather than explodes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">When writing about openings and endings, always consider the <strong>relationship between them</strong>. Does the ending mirror the opening? Does it contrast with it? Has the mood changed? Examiners love to see students tracking how the text has shifted from beginning to end.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Mistake 1: Writing about language.</strong> If you mention a metaphor, simile, or adjective, you are analysing language, not structure. Keep your focus on <em>what</em> the writer shows the reader and <em>when</em>, not the specific words used.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Mistake 2: Simply retelling the story.</strong> Describing what happens in the text without analysing <em>why</em> the writer has structured it that way will keep you at Level 1. Always explain the <strong>effect</strong> on the reader.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Mistake 3: Not using subject terminology.</strong> The mark scheme specifically rewards &ldquo;accurate use of subject terminology.&rdquo; Terms like <em>foreshadowing</em>, <em>pivotal point</em>, <em>focus shift</em>, <em>narrative pace</em>, and <em>anti-climax</em> show the examiner you understand structural analysis.</p>

Saying &ldquo;the writer uses foreshadowing to create anticipation because the reader expects the threat to materialise&rdquo; is Level 3.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n42">Question 3 is only worth 8 marks, so spend around <strong>8&ndash;10 minutes</strong> on it. Don&rsquo;t try to cover all five structural features &mdash; pick two or three and analyse them well. Use the beginning &rarr; middle &rarr; end approach to ensure you comment on &ldquo;the whole structure of the source.&rdquo; Always name the structural feature (e.g. foreshadowing, pivotal point) and then explain the <strong>effect on the reader</strong>. The command word is &ldquo;How,&rdquo; so every point must explain how the structural choice creates a specific effect. Avoid writing about language &mdash; this is the most common mistake students make on this question.</p>

## Conclusion

<ul><li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>The Big 5 structural features</strong> are: focus shifts, foreshadowing, pivotal point, narrative pace, and opening/ending. Learn these and you can approach any Question 3 confidently.</li><li data-narration-id="n45"><strong>Structure is about organisation, not words.</strong> If you find yourself analysing metaphors or adjectives, you are writing a language answer. Focus on <em>what</em> the writer shows the reader and <em>when</em>.</li><li data-narration-id="n46"><strong>Always explain the effect on the reader.</strong> Identifying a structural feature earns Level 1&ndash;2 marks. Explaining <em>why</em> the writer has used it and <em>how</em> it shapes the reader&rsquo;s experience pushes you to Level 3&ndash;4.</li></ul>
