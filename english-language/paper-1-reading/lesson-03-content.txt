# Writing a Language Analysis Response

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Does a Language Analysis Question Look Like?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">On AQA Paper 1, Question 2 asks you to analyse how a writer uses language. On Paper 2, Question 3 asks the same thing. Both questions test <dfn class="term" data-def="Assessment Objective 2: Explain, comment on and analyse how writers use language and structure to achieve effects and influence readers, using relevant subject terminology.">AO2</dfn> &mdash; your ability to explain how a writer&rsquo;s language choices create specific effects on the reader. The key word is <strong>how</strong>. You are not being asked <em>what</em> happens. You are being asked how the writer uses words, phrases, language features, language techniques and sentence forms to achieve effects.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">On Paper 1, Question 2 is worth 8 marks. On Paper 2, Question 3 is worth 12 marks. Despite the different mark totals, both questions require the same core skill: selecting a quotation, identifying a language technique, and analysing its effect in detail.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The mark scheme uses four levels. Level 1 is &ldquo;simple, limited&rdquo; comment. Level 4 is &ldquo;perceptive, detailed&rdquo; analysis. The difference between these levels is not about how many quotations you use &mdash; it is about the <strong>quality</strong> of your comments on each quotation. One perceptive comment is worth more than five simple ones.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Understanding the Levels</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The AQA mark scheme describes what examiners are looking for at each level. Understanding these descriptions helps you see exactly what you need to do to move up.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Four Levels Explained</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Level 1 (Simple, limited):</strong> You offer simple comments on the effect of language. You select simple references. You make simple use of <dfn class="term" data-def="The specialist vocabulary used to describe language features, such as metaphor, simile, personification, alliteration, or hyperbole.">subject terminology</dfn>, not always appropriately. A Level 1 response often just identifies a technique and restates the quotation in different words.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Level 2 (Some, attempts):</strong> You attempt to comment on the effect of language. You select some appropriate textual detail. You make some use of subject terminology, mainly appropriately. A Level 2 response begins to explain <em>why</em> the writer chose a word, but the explanation is incomplete or surface-level.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Level 3 (Clear, relevant):</strong> You explain clearly the effects of the writer&rsquo;s choices of language. You select a range of relevant textual detail. You make clear and accurate use of subject terminology. At Level 3, your explanation is complete in itself &mdash; it makes sense and is directly focused on the question.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Level 4 (Perceptive, detailed):</strong> You <em>analyse</em> the effects of the writer&rsquo;s choices of language. You select judicious textual detail. You make sophisticated and accurate use of subject terminology. At Level 4, your comments are insightful and exploratory &mdash; you consider layers of meaning, connotations, and the deeper implications of language choices.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n11">Notice the key difference. At Level 2, you <em>attempt to comment</em>. At Level 3, you <em>explain clearly</em>. At Level 4, you <em>analyse</em>. The jump from Level 2 to Level 3 is about completing your explanation. The jump from Level 3 to Level 4 is about exploring <dfn class="term" data-def="The associations or feelings that a word suggests beyond its literal meaning. For example, 'cannonaded' literally means fired like a cannon, but connotes violence, warfare, and being under attack.">connotations</dfn> and layers of meaning.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">The WHAT &rarr; HOW &rarr; WHY Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The most effective way to structure a language analysis paragraph is to follow this three-step pattern:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>WHAT</strong> &mdash; What has the writer done? Identify the technique and embed your quotation.<br/><strong>HOW</strong> &mdash; How does this technique work? Explain what the specific word or phrase suggests, focusing on connotations.<br/><strong>WHY</strong> &mdash; Why has the writer made this choice? What is the effect on the reader? What deeper meaning is being created?</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n15">This structure prevents the most common mistake students make: identifying a technique and then simply restating what the quotation says. If your analysis paragraph does not contain the word &ldquo;suggests,&rdquo; &ldquo;implies,&rdquo; &ldquo;connotes&rdquo; or &ldquo;conveys,&rdquo; you are probably still at Level 1 or 2.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Examiners do not count how many points you make. They look at the <strong>quality</strong> of your analysis. Three well-developed paragraphs that explore connotations and effects will score higher than six paragraphs that only identify techniques and restate meaning.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Worked Example: From Level 1 to Level 4</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">The AQA 2026 sample Paper 2 Question 3 asks: &ldquo;How does the writer use language to describe the train crash?&rdquo; referring to Source A (Peter Fleming&rsquo;s <em>One&rsquo;s Company</em>), lines 12 to 23. Let&rsquo;s look at how four different responses to the same quotation move through the levels.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>The quotation:</strong> &ldquo;my heaviest suitcase was cannonaded down&rdquo;</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>See All Four Levels in Action</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Level 1 response:</strong> &ldquo;The writer describes the train crash as &lsquo;my heaviest suitcase was cannonaded down&rsquo;. This is hyperbole as it is just a suitcase hitting his legs, but he uses this to emphasise how heavy it was.&rdquo; &mdash; This simply identifies the technique and restates the obvious meaning. It does not explore <em>why</em> the word &ldquo;cannonaded&rdquo; was chosen.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Level 2 response:</strong> &ldquo;The writer uses the hyperbole &lsquo;my heaviest suitcase was cannonaded down&rsquo; to show how the force of the crash was powerful enough to move a heavy object so that it smashed into his knees.&rdquo; &mdash; This attempts to explain the effect but only discusses the literal meaning of force. It does not explore the connotations of &ldquo;cannonaded.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Level 3 response:</strong> &ldquo;The writer uses the hyperbole &lsquo;my heaviest suitcase was cannonaded down&rsquo; to describe how the train crash was so forceful that it felt like he was being bombarded. The metaphor of &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; suggests that the impact of the train crash turns the suitcase into a weapon being fired which creates the idea of the writer being under attack.&rdquo; &mdash; This clearly explains the effect and engages with the connotations of warfare. The explanation is complete in itself.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Level 4 response:</strong> &ldquo;The writer uses the hyperbole &lsquo;my heaviest suitcase was cannonaded down&rsquo; to describe how the impact of the train crash was like a deliberate act of warfare. The use of the military verb &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; conveys the writer&rsquo;s sense of being victimised as he describes the simple dislodging of his suitcase by physical force as a metaphorical attack by the train, as it has turned his own possession into a weapon to be used against him.&rdquo; &mdash; This analyses multiple layers: the military connotation, the idea of the train as aggressor, and the irony that his own possession becomes a weapon. It is insightful and exploratory.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n24">The difference is clear. Each level adds a deeper layer of thinking. Level 4 does not use more quotations &mdash; it explores one quotation more thoroughly.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Key Language Features to Look For</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">You need to be confident identifying a range of language features. In the AQA 2026 sample paper, the mark scheme for Question 3 (Paper 2) lists these features as possible content:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Words and phrases:</strong> adjectives (&ldquo;frightful,&rdquo; &ldquo;spectacular&rdquo;), verbs (&ldquo;cannonaded,&rdquo; &ldquo;tilted,&rdquo; &ldquo;disposed&rdquo;), adverbs (&ldquo;drunkenly,&rdquo; &ldquo;primly,&rdquo; &ldquo;foolishly&rdquo;).<br/><strong>Language techniques:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Giving human qualities or characteristics to non-human things, such as objects, animals, or abstract ideas. Fleming personifies the train engine as having a 'defiant and naughty look'.">personification</dfn> (&ldquo;sprawled foolishly,&rdquo; &ldquo;defiant and naughty look&rdquo;), metaphor (&ldquo;cannonaded,&rdquo; &ldquo;little world&rdquo;), <dfn class="term" data-def="Deliberate exaggeration used for emphasis or comic effect, not meant to be taken literally. Fleming uses hyperbole when he says 'This is the end of the world'.">hyperbole</dfn> (&ldquo;end of the world,&rdquo; &ldquo;broken both my legs&rdquo;), <dfn class="term" data-def="The repetition of the same consonant sound at the beginning of nearby words, used for emphasis or to create a particular mood. 'Fearful force' uses alliteration to stress the violent impact.">alliteration</dfn> (&ldquo;fearful force&rdquo;), onomatopoeia (&ldquo;crash&rdquo;).<br/><strong>Sentence forms:</strong> short sentences (&ldquo;It was six o&rsquo;clock. I began to dress. I felt very much annoyed.&rdquo;) to create a blunt, matter-of-fact tone.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Putting It Into Practice: Paper 1 Question 2</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Paper 1 Question 2 works in exactly the same way. The AQA 2026 sample asks about <em>Life of Pi</em>, lines 10&ndash;19: &ldquo;How does the writer use language here to describe the hyena&rsquo;s appearance?&rdquo; The mark scheme highlights features including the adjective &ldquo;ugly,&rdquo; the simile &ldquo;like the symptoms of a skin disease,&rdquo; and the antithesis &ldquo;doglike, but like no dog anyone would want as a pet.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">A Level 4 response on the simile might read: &ldquo;The writer compares the hyena&rsquo;s spots to &lsquo;the symptoms of a skin disease,&rsquo; which transforms a natural marking into something pathological and repulsive. The word &lsquo;symptoms&rsquo; has medical connotations, implying the hyena&rsquo;s appearance is not simply unattractive but almost diseased, as though its ugliness is an illness that cannot be cured. This connects to the earlier phrase &lsquo;beyond redemption,&rsquo; reinforcing the idea that the hyena is irredeemably grotesque.&rdquo;</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Common Mistakes to Avoid</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Feature-spotting without analysis:</strong> Writing &ldquo;The writer uses a metaphor&rdquo; and then moving on. You must explain the <em>effect</em> of the metaphor &mdash; what it makes the reader think, feel, or picture.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Retelling the story:</strong> This question is not about what happens. If your paragraph could be answered without ever mentioning a language technique, you are describing content, not analysing language.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Writing about the wrong lines:</strong> The mark scheme states that if you write about language outside the given lines, your response will be placed at the bottom of the level it would otherwise achieve. Always check the line references before you start.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Using quotations that are too long:</strong> You do not need to copy out whole sentences. Embed short, precise quotations of one to four words within your own sentences. This shows you can select <dfn class="term" data-def="Carefully chosen and precisely relevant textual detail that supports your point. The mark scheme uses the word 'judicious' at Level 4, meaning your quotations are well-selected and purposeful.">judicious</dfn> detail.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n35">A Step-by-Step Approach for the Exam</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36">When you sit down to answer a language analysis question, follow these steps:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Read the question carefully. Underline the line references and the focus (e.g. &ldquo;to describe the train crash&rdquo; or &ldquo;to describe the hyena&rsquo;s appearance&rdquo;). Everything you write must connect to this focus.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Step 2:</strong> Re-read the specified lines. As you read, underline or highlight three to four short quotations where you can see a clear language technique at work.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Step 3:</strong> For each quotation, write a WHAT &rarr; HOW &rarr; WHY paragraph. Identify the technique, explore the connotations of specific words, and explain the effect on the reader.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Step 4:</strong> Check that every paragraph links back to the focus of the question. If the question asks about the train crash, every paragraph should connect to how the crash is described &mdash; not to the writer&rsquo;s feelings about bread or his sleeping arrangements.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n41">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>For Paper 1 Question 2 (8 marks), aim to write three strong paragraphs in about 10&ndash;12 minutes. For Paper 2 Question 3 (12 marks), aim for four strong paragraphs in about 12&ndash;15 minutes. Quality always beats quantity.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n42">The command word in these questions is &ldquo;How,&rdquo; which means you must focus on the writer&rsquo;s methods, not the content of the story. A useful test: if you could delete all the quotations and technique names from your answer and it would still make sense as a summary, you are retelling rather than analysing. Always zoom in on individual words within your quotation and explore what they <em>connote</em> &mdash; this is what pushes you from Level 2 into Level 3 and beyond. Remember that the mark scheme rewards the quality of your comments, not the number. Three detailed, perceptive paragraphs will outscore six shallow ones every time.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n43">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n44">Language analysis questions test AO2: how writers use language to achieve effects. Always focus on the <strong>how</strong>, not the <strong>what</strong>.</li><li data-narration-id="n45">Use the WHAT &rarr; HOW &rarr; WHY structure: identify the technique, explore connotations of specific words, then explain the effect on the reader.</li><li data-narration-id="n46">The difference between levels is about depth, not quantity. Level 4 responses explore layers of meaning and make insightful, exploratory comments on carefully chosen quotations.</li></ul>
