# Paper 1 Reading Practice — Full Section A

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What This Lesson Covers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">This is your chance to put everything together. In this lesson, you will work through a complete Paper 1 Section A using the AQA 2026 sample assessment materials. The source text is an extract from <em>Life of Pi</em> by Yann Martel, in which the narrator, Pi, is trapped on a lifeboat with a hyena, a zebra and an orang-utan. You will practise all four reading questions &mdash; from the 4-mark <dfn class="term" data-def="A question where you select correct answers from a list of options, testing your ability to identify explicit and implicit information.">multiple choice</dfn> to the 20-mark evaluation &mdash; using the real question wording, real mark schemes and real examiner expectations.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Paper 1 Section A is worth <strong>40 marks</strong> and you should spend approximately <strong>1 hour</strong> on it, including reading time. The four questions test three <dfn class="term" data-def="The skills that examiners assess in your answer. Paper 1 Reading uses AO1 (information and ideas), AO2 (language and structure) and AO4 (evaluation).">assessment objectives</dfn>: AO1, AO2 and AO4. Every question targets a different skill, so your approach must change for each one.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Paper 1 Section A timing guide: Q1 = 5 minutes, Q2 = 10 minutes, Q3 = 10 minutes, Q4 = 20&ndash;25 minutes, plus 10&ndash;15 minutes reading the source at the start. Stick to these timings or you will run out of time for Question 4, which is worth the most marks.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Step 1: Read the Source Carefully</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Before you touch the questions, spend 10&ndash;15 minutes reading the source text. Read the introduction first &mdash; it tells you that Pi is a teenage boy in a lifeboat on the Pacific Ocean with a hyena, a zebra and an orang-utan. This context is essential. Then read the full extract slowly, underlining or annotating key details. As you read, think about the writer&rsquo;s choices: Why has Martel used this word? Why does the focus shift here? What effect does this create?</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The source runs from line 1 to line 50. Notice how it divides into sections: lines 1&ndash;9 introduce Pi&rsquo;s fear and background information about hyenas; lines 10&ndash;19 describe the hyena&rsquo;s ugly appearance; lines 20&ndash;35 show the hyena&rsquo;s frantic circling; and lines 36 onwards show the anti-climactic ending where it collapses. Understanding this structure will help you with every question.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Question 1: Multiple Choice (4 marks, AO1)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">The real question reads: <em>&ldquo;Read again the first part of the source, from lines 1 to 9. Tick one box for each question.&rdquo;</em> There are four sub-questions, each worth 1 mark. You must select the correct answer from four options. This question tests whether you can identify <dfn class="term" data-def="Information stated directly in the text (explicit) and information you must work out from clues in the text (implicit).">explicit and implicit information</dfn>.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Question 2: Language Analysis (8 marks, AO2)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">The real question reads: <em>&ldquo;Look in detail at this extract, from lines 10 to 19 of the source. How does the writer use language here to describe the hyena&rsquo;s appearance?&rdquo;</em> You are given bullet-point prompts: words and phrases, language features and techniques, sentence forms.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">This question tests your ability to analyse <dfn class="term" data-def="The specific words, phrases, techniques and sentence forms a writer selects to create particular effects on the reader.">language choices</dfn> and their effects. You must do three things for every point: <strong>identify</strong> the technique, <strong>quote</strong> the evidence, and <strong>analyse</strong> the effect on the reader.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Key language features in lines 10&ndash;19 to analyse:</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">The mark scheme highlights: adjectives (&ldquo;ugly&rdquo;, &ldquo;shaggy&rdquo;, &ldquo;coarse&rdquo;), verbs (&ldquo;bungled&rdquo;), contrast with the leopard (&ldquo;none of the classy ostentation&rdquo;), simile (&ldquo;like the symptoms of a skin disease&rdquo;), adverbs of excess (&ldquo;too massive&rdquo;, &ldquo;ridiculously mouse-like&rdquo;, &ldquo;too big&rdquo;), humour and hyperbole (&ldquo;suffering from a receding hairline&rdquo;), short declarative sentences (&ldquo;The nostrils are too big.&rdquo;), and the antithesis at the end (&ldquo;doglike, but like no dog anyone would want as a pet&rdquo;). You do not need to cover all of these &mdash; it is quality, not quantity, that determines your level.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>For Q2, aim for 3&ndash;4 well-developed analytical points. Each point should follow the pattern: identify the technique &rarr; embed a short quotation &rarr; analyse the effect on the reader. One perceptive point is worth more than five simple ones.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Question 3: Structure Analysis (8 marks, AO2)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">The real question reads: <em>&ldquo;You now need to think about the structure of the source as a whole. The text is from the middle of a novel. How has the writer structured the text to create tension?&rdquo;</em> You are given bullet prompts about how tension increases or decreases, structural effects, and features like changes in mood, tone or perspective.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">This question covers the <strong>whole source</strong>. You need to discuss structural features &mdash; not individual words. Think about: how the extract begins and ends, where the <dfn class="term" data-def="A turning point in the text where the focus, mood or direction of the narrative changes significantly.">pivotal point</dfn> occurs, shifts in focus between action and description, narrative pace, foreshadowing and juxtaposition.</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Opening &amp; foreshadowing:</strong> The first sentence &mdash; &ldquo;It was the hyena that worried me&rdquo; &mdash; immediately introduces Pi&rsquo;s fear and foreshadows the hyena&rsquo;s threatening behaviour later in the extract.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Exposition:</strong> Background information about hyenas&rsquo; hunting ability (lines 1&ndash;9) allows the reader to appreciate the danger, building anticipation before the hyena actually appears.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Pivotal point:</strong> The hyena leaps over the zebra (line 20). This sudden action shifts the text from description to frantic action, and the pace accelerates dramatically.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Rising action &amp; pace:</strong> The hyena&rsquo;s repetitive circling (&ldquo;one lap&ndash;two&ndash;three&ndash;four&ndash;five&rdquo;) increases pace through listing and dashes. The repetition of &ldquo;yip yip yip yip yip&rdquo; creates a relentless, manic rhythm.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>First-person perspective:</strong> The reader shares Pi&rsquo;s thoughts and fears directly, which heightens tension because we can only see what he sees.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Anti-climax:</strong> The ending undercuts the threat &mdash; the hyena vomits and collapses. This sudden release of tension through bathos juxtaposes the earlier menace with unexpected vulnerability.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Common mistake:</strong> Many students write about language in Q3. The examiners are looking for <strong>structural</strong> analysis &mdash; how the text is organised, how focus shifts, how pace changes, how the beginning connects to the ending. Use structural terminology: foreshadowing, pivotal point, narrative perspective, juxtaposition, rising action, anti-climax.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n35">Question 4: Evaluation (20 marks, AO4)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36">The real question reads: <em>&ldquo;For this question focus on the second part of the source, from line 20 to the end. In this part of the source, the hyena could be seen as funny or threatening. To what extent do you agree or disagree with this statement?&rdquo;</em></p>
<p data-narration-id="n37">This is the highest-tariff reading question. You must <dfn class="term" data-def="To make a judgement about a text by weighing up evidence and considering different interpretations, rather than simply describing what happens.">evaluate</dfn> the statement by considering both sides &mdash; how the hyena is presented as funny <em>and</em> how it is presented as threatening &mdash; and give your own personal response supported by textual evidence. You do not have to pick one side. The best answers explore <strong>both</strong> interpretations and show how the writer creates each effect.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Threatening evidence:</strong> &ldquo;The beast was going at a good clip, and it was no small animal&rdquo; &mdash; the noun &ldquo;beast&rdquo; and emphasis on size creates danger. &ldquo;I prepared for my end&rdquo; &mdash; Pi genuinely believes he is about to die. The hyena&rsquo;s &ldquo;blank and frank&rdquo; look is unsettling because it shows no emotion, making it unpredictable. The verb &ldquo;seized&rdquo; in &ldquo;I was seized by fear&rdquo; suggests Pi is paralysed, a physical response to real danger.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Funny evidence:</strong> The hyena runs in repetitive circles, which is absurd rather than dangerous. The onomatopoeia &ldquo;yip yip yip yip yip&rdquo; sounds ridiculous rather than terrifying. The phrase &ldquo;a twenty-five-foot indoor track&rdquo; compares the lifeboat to a running track, creating a comical image. The anti-climactic ending where the hyena vomits and &ldquo;dropped into what it had just produced&rdquo; is deliberately bathetic. The phrase &ldquo;exploring the furthest confines of animal anguish&rdquo; gives the hyena almost human suffering, which is both humorous and sympathetic.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Structure your answer:</strong> For each point, state your evaluation (e.g. &ldquo;I agree that the hyena seems threatening here because&hellip;&rdquo;), then quote evidence, then analyse the writer&rsquo;s method and its effect. Aim for 4&ndash;5 well-developed paragraphs.</p>

</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n41">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Q4 is marked on a scale of Levels 1&ndash;4. To reach Level 4 (16&ndash;20 marks), you must show a &ldquo;perceptive and detailed&rdquo; evaluation with &ldquo;convincing and critical&rdquo; personal response. This means exploring alternative interpretations and analysing how the writer&rsquo;s methods create specific effects &mdash; not just saying what happens.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n42">Putting It All Together: Timing &amp; Strategy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n43">Here is the recommended approach for the full Section A, based on the mark allocations and the AQA timing advice:</p>

<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n44">
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">0&ndash;15 min</div>
    <h4>Read the Source</h4>
    <p>Read the introduction and the full extract. Annotate key details, interesting language and structural shifts. Note the line references for each question.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">15&ndash;20 min</div>
    <h4>Question 1 (4 marks)</h4>
    <p>Multiple choice. Read lines 1&ndash;9 again carefully. Select the correct answers. Do not spend more than 5 minutes here.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">20&ndash;30 min</div>
    <h4>Question 2 (8 marks)</h4>
    <p>Language analysis on lines 10&ndash;19. Write 3&ndash;4 analytical paragraphs using the identify &rarr; quote &rarr; analyse pattern.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">30&ndash;40 min</div>
    <h4>Question 3 (8 marks)</h4>
    <p>Structure analysis on the whole source. Discuss how the text is organised to create tension. Use structural terminology.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">40&ndash;60 min</div>
    <h4>Question 4 (20 marks)</h4>
    <p>Evaluation of lines 20 to end. Explore both &ldquo;funny&rdquo; and &ldquo;threatening&rdquo; interpretations. 4&ndash;5 developed paragraphs with personal response and method analysis.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n45">Notice how Q4 gets the most time because it is worth the most marks. Many students lose marks by spending too long on Q1&ndash;Q3 and then rushing Q4. Practise strict timing &mdash; set a timer for each question.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n47"><strong>Q2:</strong> Feature-spotting without analysis. Saying &ldquo;the writer uses a simile&rdquo; is not enough. You must explain <em>why</em> the simile works and what effect it has on the reader.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n48"><strong>Q3:</strong> Writing about language instead of structure. If you find yourself analysing individual words, stop and refocus on the <em>organisation</em> of the text &mdash; shifts in focus, pace, perspective and narrative position.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n49"><strong>Q4:</strong> Retelling the story instead of evaluating. You must engage with the statement and give a critical, personal response. Use phrases like &ldquo;I agree that&hellip;&rdquo;, &ldquo;This suggests that&hellip;&rdquo;, &ldquo;The writer creates a sense of&hellip;&rdquo; to keep your response evaluative.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n50">In the exam, always check which <strong>lines</strong> each question refers to. Q1 and Q2 specify exact line references &mdash; writing about text outside these lines will limit your marks. Q3 covers the <strong>whole source</strong>, and Q4 specifies a section but expects you to consider how it connects to the rest of the text. For Q4, the command word is &ldquo;to what extent&rdquo; &mdash; this means you should weigh up both sides of the statement and give a balanced, evaluative response rather than picking one side and ignoring the other. Always support every point with a short, embedded quotation.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n51">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n52">Paper 1 Section A has four questions testing AO1 (information and ideas), AO2 (language and structure) and AO4 (evaluation) &mdash; each question requires a different approach and different terminology.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n53">Timing is critical: spend 5 minutes on Q1, 10 on Q2, 10 on Q3 and 20&ndash;25 on Q4 &mdash; Q4 is worth half the reading marks, so give it the most time and the most developed analysis.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n54">Quality beats quantity in every question &mdash; one perceptive, well-analysed point at Level 4 earns more marks than five simple points at Level 1.</li>
