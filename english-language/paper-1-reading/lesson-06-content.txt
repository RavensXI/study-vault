# Evaluation — The Synoptic Question

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is the Evaluation Question?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The evaluation question is Question 4 on AQA Paper 1. It is worth <strong>20 marks</strong> and is the highest-tariff reading question on the paper. The question gives you a statement about a specific section of the fiction source and asks you: &ldquo;To what extent do you agree or disagree?&rdquo; This means you must form a <dfn class="term" data-def="A reasoned judgement or opinion about a text, supported by evidence and analysis. In the evaluation question, your critical response shows whether you agree or disagree with the given statement.">critical response</dfn> to the text &mdash; not just describe what happens, but judge how effectively the writer achieves an effect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This question is sometimes called the &ldquo;synoptic question&rdquo; because it draws together all your reading skills: identifying information (AO1), analysing language and structure (AO2), and critically evaluating the text (AO4). You need to show the examiner that you can think like a reader and a critic at the same time.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 4 assesses AO4: &ldquo;Evaluate texts critically and support this with appropriate textual references.&rdquo; This is the <strong>only</strong> question on Paper 1 that tests AO4. It is worth 20 marks &mdash; a quarter of the entire reading section.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Understanding the Assessment Objective</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><dfn class="term" data-def="Assessment Objective 4 (AO4) requires students to evaluate texts critically and support this with appropriate textual references. It is tested only in Paper 1, Question 4.">AO4</dfn> requires you to do two things: (1) make a critical judgement about the text, and (2) support that judgement with evidence. Notice the word &ldquo;critically&rdquo; &mdash; this means you cannot simply say &ldquo;I agree&rdquo; and then retell the story. You must explain <em>how</em> and <em>why</em> the writer&rsquo;s choices create the effect described in the statement.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The mark scheme uses four levels to judge your response. Understanding these levels is essential for knowing what the examiner is looking for:</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Level 4 (16&ndash;20 marks) &mdash; Perceptive, detailed:</strong> You show a perceptive understanding of the writer&rsquo;s methods. You analyse the effects of language, structure, or form in a detailed and exploratory way. Your evaluation is convincing because it is supported by well-chosen (judicious) textual references. Your personal response is assured and confident.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Level 3 (11&ndash;15 marks) &mdash; Clear, relevant:</strong> You show a clear understanding of the writer&rsquo;s methods. You explain the effects clearly and select relevant textual detail. Your evaluation is thoughtful and your personal response is clearly expressed.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Level 2 (6&ndash;10 marks) &mdash; Some, attempts:</strong> You show some understanding but your comments may be incomplete or partially correct. You attempt to respond to the statement but your textual references may not always support your point effectively.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Level 1 (1&ndash;5 marks) &mdash; Simple, limited:</strong> You offer simple, obvious comments. You may paraphrase the text rather than evaluate it. Your references are basic and your response shows limited engagement with the statement.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">How the Question Looks on the Paper</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Let&rsquo;s look at the real Question 4 from the AQA 2026 sample paper, which uses an extract from <em>Life of Pi</em> by Yann Martel. The source describes a teenage boy, Pi, trapped on a lifeboat with a hyena:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>&ldquo;For this question focus on the second part of the source, from line 20 to the end. In this part of the source, the hyena could be seen as funny or threatening. To what extent do you agree or disagree with this statement?&rdquo;</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Notice the key features of this question: it directs you to a <strong>specific section</strong> of the text; it gives you a <strong>statement</strong> containing two possible interpretations (funny <em>or</em> threatening); and it uses the command phrase <strong>&ldquo;to what extent&rdquo;</strong>, which invites a nuanced response rather than a simple yes or no.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">A Step-by-Step Approach</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Follow these four steps to write a strong evaluation response:</p>

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Step 1: Decide Your Position</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Read the statement carefully and decide whether you agree, disagree, or &mdash; most commonly &mdash; think the answer is more complex than a simple yes or no. For the <em>Life of Pi</em> question, you might argue that the hyena is presented as <em>both</em> funny and threatening, and that the writer shifts between these effects. This is a more <dfn class="term" data-def="Containing subtle differences or shades of meaning. A nuanced response considers multiple interpretations rather than taking a single, simple position.">nuanced</dfn> position, which is exactly what Level 4 rewards.</p>

<h3 data-narration-id="n20">Step 2: Select Your Evidence</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Choose 4&ndash;5 quotations from the specified lines that support your position. Pick evidence that allows you to discuss the writer&rsquo;s methods &mdash; not just what happens, but <em>how</em> the writer creates the effect. For example, from the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract, the phrase &ldquo;transforming the stern bench&hellip; into a twenty-five-foot indoor track&rdquo; uses a humorous <dfn class="term" data-def="A comparison between two unlike things to explain or clarify an idea. Unlike a simile, a metaphor states that one thing is another.">metaphor</dfn> that makes the hyena&rsquo;s behaviour seem absurd, like an athlete on a running track. Meanwhile, &ldquo;I prepared for my end&rdquo; is a blunt, chilling short sentence that conveys genuine terror.</p>

<h3 data-narration-id="n22">Step 3: Analyse the Writer&rsquo;s Methods</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n23">This is where most marks are won or lost. For each piece of evidence, you must explain <em>what</em> method the writer uses, <em>how</em> it creates the effect described in the statement, and <em>why</em> it supports your evaluation. Use this sentence structure as a guide: &ldquo;The writer presents the hyena as [funny/threatening] through [method], which creates the effect of [explanation], and this supports the idea that [link back to statement].&rdquo;</p>

<h3 data-narration-id="n24">Step 4: Maintain Your Evaluative Voice</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Throughout your response, keep returning to the statement and your personal judgement. Use evaluative phrases such as: &ldquo;This effectively conveys&hellip;&rdquo;, &ldquo;The writer successfully creates&hellip;&rdquo;, &ldquo;I agree that this moment is [funny/threatening] because&hellip;&rdquo;, or &ldquo;To some extent, the writer achieves this effect, however&hellip;&rdquo; These phrases show the examiner that you are constantly <em>evaluating</em>, not just analysing.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The difference between Level 2 and Level 3 is the difference between <strong>commenting</strong> and <strong>evaluating</strong>. A Level 2 response says &ldquo;The writer uses a metaphor here.&rdquo; A Level 3 response says &ldquo;The writer&rsquo;s metaphor effectively makes the hyena seem ridiculous, which I agree creates a comedic tone.&rdquo; Always link your analysis back to the statement.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Students lose marks on Question 4 for predictable reasons. Here are the most common pitfalls:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>1. Retelling the story:</strong> Many students summarise what happens in the extract without evaluating anything. Remember, the examiner already knows what happens &mdash; they want to know what you <em>think</em> about how the writer presents it.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>2. Ignoring the statement:</strong> Some students treat Question 4 like a language analysis question (Q2) and write about methods without ever referring back to the statement. Every paragraph should explicitly connect to the statement&rsquo;s claim.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>3. Only agreeing:</strong> The phrase &ldquo;to what extent&rdquo; invites you to explore the complexity of the statement. If you only agree (or only disagree), your response may lack the nuance needed for Level 4. Consider moments where you partly agree and partly disagree.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>4. Writing outside the specified lines:</strong> The mark scheme states that if you write about language outside the given lines, your response will be placed at the bottom of the appropriate level. Always check which lines you are directed to.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n36">The Level 4 descriptor uses the word <dfn class="term" data-def="Carefully chosen to be exactly right for the purpose. In the mark scheme, judicious references are quotations that perfectly support the point being made.">judicious</dfn> to describe the best textual references. A judicious reference is a short, carefully chosen quotation that perfectly supports your evaluative point. It is <em>not</em> a long, copied-out sentence. Compare these two approaches:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Weak:</strong> &ldquo;The writer says &lsquo;It started running in circles again&rsquo; which shows the hyena is moving around.&rdquo; (This just restates what happens.)</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Judicious:</strong> &ldquo;The hyena&rsquo;s &lsquo;maniacal&rsquo; circling creates an almost absurd image of uncontrolled frenzy.&rdquo; (This selects a single, powerful word and evaluates the effect it creates.)</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Planning Your Response in the Exam</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40">You are advised to spend about 20&ndash;25 minutes on Question 4. Spend 3&ndash;4 minutes reading the specified lines and planning before you write. A quick plan might look like this:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Position:</strong> Both funny and threatening &mdash; writer keeps shifting between them.<br/><strong>Point 1 (agree &mdash; funny):</strong> &ldquo;yip yip yip&rdquo; + &ldquo;indoor track&rdquo; &rarr; onomatopoeia + metaphor = absurd, comedic<br/><strong>Point 2 (agree &mdash; threatening):</strong> &ldquo;I prepared for my end&rdquo; + &ldquo;my heart jumped&rdquo; &rarr; short sentences + first person fear = genuine danger<br/><strong>Point 3 (both):</strong> &ldquo;maniacal beast&rdquo; + vomiting scene &rarr; threatening language + anti-climax = funny ending undercuts threat<br/><strong>Point 4 (overall judgement):</strong> Writer succeeds because humour and fear work together, not against each other.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n42">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The strongest responses write 3&ndash;4 developed paragraphs, each with a clear evaluative point, a judicious quotation, analysis of the writer&rsquo;s method, and an explicit link back to the statement. Quality beats quantity &mdash; the mark scheme rewards the <em>quality</em> of your comments, not the number of references.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n43">Question 4 is worth 20 marks and carries the most weight of any reading question on Paper 1. The command phrase &ldquo;to what extent&rdquo; is your signal to be nuanced: don&rsquo;t just agree &mdash; explore where you agree, where you disagree, and where the answer is complex. The easiest way to boost your mark from Level 2 to Level 3 is to use evaluative language: phrases like &ldquo;the writer effectively creates&hellip;&rdquo;, &ldquo;I agree that this conveys&hellip;&rdquo;, and &ldquo;this successfully suggests&hellip;&rdquo; prove to the examiner that you are <em>evaluating</em>, not just identifying methods. Finally, watch your timing &mdash; spend no more than 25 minutes here so you have enough time for the writing section.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n44">Key Takeaways</h3><ul><li data-narration-id="n45">Question 4 tests AO4 (critical evaluation) and is worth 20 marks. You must form a personal judgement about the statement and support it with evidence and analysis of the writer&rsquo;s methods.</li><li data-narration-id="n46">The best responses are <strong>nuanced</strong>: they consider where the statement is true, where it is not, and how the writer creates these effects through specific choices of language and structure.</li><li data-narration-id="n47">Always use evaluative language to show you are judging the text, not just describing it. Link every paragraph back to the statement and your overall position.</li></ul>
