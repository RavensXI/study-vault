# Sensory Language, Verbs and Show Don't Tell

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Sensory Language Matters in Creative Writing</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In AQA Paper 1, Section B, you are assessed on your ability to &ldquo;communicate clearly, effectively and imaginatively&rdquo; (AO5) and to use &ldquo;a range of vocabulary and sentence structures for clarity, purpose and effect&rdquo; (AO6). One of the most powerful ways to achieve both of these is through <dfn class="term" data-def="Language that appeals to the five senses: sight, sound, smell, taste and touch, helping the reader experience a scene physically.">sensory language</dfn>. Instead of telling your reader that a place is scary, you make them feel the cold air on their skin, hear the creak of a floorboard, and smell the damp stone walls. This is how you move from average writing to writing that genuinely impresses an examiner.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Many students rely almost entirely on sight when they describe a scene. They write what their character sees &mdash; and nothing else. But real experience involves all five senses: sight, sound, smell, taste and touch. The best creative writing draws on at least three of these senses to build an immersive world for the reader.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>AQA&rsquo;s mark scheme rewards &ldquo;extensive and ambitious vocabulary with sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; at Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks for AO5). Sensory language is one of the most effective linguistic devices you can use to reach this level.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Five Senses in Practice</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Let&rsquo;s look at how professional writers use sensory language. In the AQA Paper 1 2026 sample source, Yann Martel describes the hyena in <em>Life of Pi</em>. He doesn&rsquo;t just tell us the hyena is frightening. He makes us hear it: &ldquo;it went yip yip yip yip yip in a high-pitched way.&rdquo; He makes us feel the boat shake: &ldquo;The beating of its legs against the benches made the whole boat shake, and its claws were loudly clicking on their surface.&rdquo; Sound and touch work together to create fear without ever using the word &ldquo;scary.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Similarly, in Peter Fleming&rsquo;s <em>One&rsquo;s Company</em> (AQA Paper 2 2026 sample), the train crash is brought to life through sensory detail: &ldquo;a frightful jarring, followed by a crash&rdquo; (sound), &ldquo;catching me with fearful force on either knee-cap&rdquo; (touch/pain), and &ldquo;snorting steam&rdquo; (sound and sight). These details make the reader feel present in the scene.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Powerful Verbs: The Engine of Your Writing</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Verbs are the words that carry the action in your sentences. Weak, generic verbs produce flat writing. Powerful, specific verbs create vivid images and strong emotions. The difference between a Level 2 response and a Level 4 response often comes down to verb choices.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Compare these two sentences:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Weak:</strong> &ldquo;The suitcase came down from the rack and hit me.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Strong (Peter Fleming):</strong> &ldquo;My heaviest suitcase was <em>cannonaded</em> down, catching me with fearful force on either knee-cap.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The verb &ldquo;cannonaded&rdquo; transforms a simple falling suitcase into a violent military bombardment. The AQA mark scheme for the 2026 Paper 2 sample specifically highlights this as a <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to something it does not literally describe, creating an implicit comparison.">metaphor</dfn> &mdash; the suitcase becomes a weapon, and the writer becomes a victim under attack. One verb does all of that work.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme for the 2026 Paper 2 sample lists the following verbs as worthy of analysis in Fleming&rsquo;s train crash description: &ldquo;cannonaded,&rdquo; &ldquo;tilted,&rdquo; &ldquo;dug in,&rdquo; and &ldquo;disposed.&rdquo; Notice how every one of these verbs does more than describe movement &mdash; each carries an additional meaning or connotation. This is exactly what examiners want to see in your own creative writing.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n19">Here are some common weak verbs and their powerful replacements:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Walked</strong> &rarr; stumbled, trudged, staggered, crept, prowled<br/><strong>Said</strong> &rarr; whispered, barked, stammered, hissed, pleaded<br/><strong>Looked</strong> &rarr; glared, squinted, peered, scrutinised, glimpsed<br/><strong>Went</strong> &rarr; charged, drifted, lurched, bolted, retreated<br/><strong>Fell</strong> &rarr; plummeted, collapsed, crumpled, toppled, cannonaded</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Each replacement verb carries a different <dfn class="term" data-def="The feeling or idea suggested by a word beyond its literal meaning. For example, 'trudged' connotes exhaustion and reluctance.">connotation</dfn>. &ldquo;Trudged&rdquo; suggests exhaustion and reluctance. &ldquo;Prowled&rdquo; suggests predatory intent. Choose your verb based on the mood and meaning you want to create.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Show Don&rsquo;t Tell: The Golden Rule</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">&ldquo;Show don&rsquo;t tell&rdquo; is the most important principle in creative writing. It means you should never directly state an emotion or atmosphere. Instead, you should describe the physical details, actions and sensations that allow the reader to <em>feel</em> that emotion for themselves.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Compare these two approaches:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Telling:</strong> &ldquo;I was terrified of the hyena.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Showing (Yann Martel):</strong> &ldquo;I pushed myself away on the oar till only the tips of my feet were holding on to the boat.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Martel never uses the word &ldquo;terrified.&rdquo; Instead, he describes Pi&rsquo;s physical action &mdash; pushing himself to the very edge of the boat, clinging on with just his toes. The reader understands the terror through what Pi <em>does</em>, not through what Pi <em>says he feels</em>. This is far more powerful because it forces the reader to actively engage with the text and draw their own conclusions.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Show don&rsquo;t tell&rdquo; works through three main channels: (1) <strong>physical reactions</strong> &mdash; a racing heart, sweating palms, clenched fists; (2) <strong>actions</strong> &mdash; backing away, hiding, running; (3) <strong>sensory details</strong> &mdash; what the character sees, hears, smells, touches and tastes. If you use all three, you will almost never need to name the emotion directly.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n30">Here is a quick reference for common emotions and how to show them:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Fear:</strong> Shallow breathing, cold sweat, stomach dropping, eyes darting, backing away, trembling hands.<br/><strong>Anger:</strong> Jaw clenching, fists balling, blood rushing to the face, voice rising, sharp movements.<br/><strong>Sadness:</strong> Throat tightening, stinging eyes, heavy limbs, slow movements, staring blankly.<br/><strong>Joy:</strong> Warmth spreading through the chest, quickened heartbeat, uncontrollable smile, lightness in step.</p>

When you write creatively, personifying objects is a highly effective way to &ldquo;show&rdquo; atmosphere and tone.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n34">Putting It All Together</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n35">The best creative writing combines all three techniques from this lesson: sensory language, powerful verbs and show don&rsquo;t tell. They are not separate skills &mdash; they work together. When you choose a powerful verb like &ldquo;cannonaded,&rdquo; you are simultaneously using sensory language (we hear and feel the impact) and showing rather than telling (we understand the violence without being told &ldquo;it was violent&rdquo;).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36">Here is a before-and-after example to demonstrate all three techniques working together:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Before (telling, weak verbs, no sensory detail):</strong><br/>&ldquo;The storm was bad. I was scared. The wind was strong and the rain was heavy. I went inside.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>After (showing, powerful verbs, rich sensory detail):</strong><br/>&ldquo;The sky had darkened to the colour of a bruise. Wind howled through the gaps in the fence, whipping my hair across my face and stinging my eyes. Rain hammered the pavement, each drop exploding on impact, soaking through my coat until the cold gnawed at my skin. I stumbled towards the door, my fingers fumbling with the latch, the metallic taste of fear sharp on my tongue.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n39">The &ldquo;after&rdquo; version uses sight (&ldquo;colour of a bruise&rdquo;), sound (&ldquo;howled,&rdquo; &ldquo;hammered&rdquo;), touch (&ldquo;stinging,&rdquo; &ldquo;cold gnawed&rdquo;), and taste (&ldquo;metallic taste of fear&rdquo;). The verbs are powerful and specific: &ldquo;howled,&rdquo; &ldquo;whipping,&rdquo; &ldquo;hammered,&rdquo; &ldquo;exploding,&rdquo; &ldquo;gnawed,&rdquo; &ldquo;stumbled,&rdquo; &ldquo;fumbling.&rdquo; The emotion (fear) is never named &mdash; we feel it through the character&rsquo;s shaking hands and the metallic taste in their mouth. This is Level 4 writing.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n40">In Paper 1, Section B, examiners are looking for &ldquo;sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; to reach Level 4. A common mistake is to use sensory language in the opening paragraph and then forget about it. Make a conscious effort to weave sensory detail, powerful verbs and &ldquo;show don&rsquo;t tell&rdquo; throughout your entire response &mdash; not just at the start. Before you begin writing, quickly note down one detail for each sense (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) related to your scenario. This takes 30 seconds and transforms the quality of your writing. Also, avoid naming emotions directly. Every time you catch yourself writing &ldquo;I felt scared&rdquo; or &ldquo;she was angry,&rdquo; stop and replace it with a physical reaction or sensory detail that shows the emotion instead.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n41">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n42"><strong>Sensory language</strong> uses all five senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) to create immersive descriptions &mdash; aim for at least three senses in any descriptive paragraph.</li><li data-narration-id="n43"><strong>Powerful verbs</strong> carry meaning and connotation beyond basic action &mdash; replace generic verbs like &ldquo;walked,&rdquo; &ldquo;said&rdquo; and &ldquo;went&rdquo; with specific, expressive alternatives.</li><li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Show don&rsquo;t tell</strong> means using physical reactions, actions and sensory details to convey emotions instead of naming them directly &mdash; this is what separates Level 3 from Level 4 writing.</li></ul>
