# Crafting Similes and Figurative Language

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Figurative Language?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="Language that uses words or expressions with a meaning different from the literal interpretation, creating vivid imagery or comparisons.">Figurative language</dfn> is any language that goes beyond the literal meaning of words to create a picture, comparison, or effect in the reader&rsquo;s mind. In AQA Paper 1 Section B (the creative writing task), your use of figurative language is assessed under AO5 (communicating clearly, effectively and imaginatively) and AO6 (range of vocabulary and sentence structures for clarity, purpose and effect). Strong figurative language is one of the fastest ways to move your writing from Level 2 into Level 3 or Level 4.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The key types of figurative language you should be confident using in your creative writing are: similes, metaphors, personification, and extended metaphors. This lesson focuses on how to craft <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech that compares two different things using the words 'like' or 'as', e.g. 'the sea was like a sheet of glass'.">similes</dfn> that feel original and purposeful &mdash; not lazy or clich&eacute;d &mdash; and how to weave other figurative devices into your descriptive and narrative writing.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA Paper 1 mark scheme rewards &ldquo;extensive and ambitious vocabulary with sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; at Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks for content and organisation). A single, well-crafted simile is worth more than five generic ones. Quality over quantity.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Why Similes Matter in Creative Writing</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A simile compares two things using &ldquo;like&rdquo; or &ldquo;as&rdquo;. It helps the reader see, hear, or feel something by connecting an unfamiliar or abstract idea to something concrete and vivid. The best similes do three things at once: they create a visual image, they convey a mood or emotion, and they reveal something about the narrator&rsquo;s perspective.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Look at this example from the AQA sample Paper 2 insert, where Peter Fleming describes a train crash: &ldquo;The Trans-Siberian Express train sprawled foolishly down the embankment.&rdquo; While this particular example is personification rather than simile, notice how it combines a visual image (the crashed train) with a tone (humorous, mocking) and a perspective (the writer finds the crash absurd rather than terrifying). Your similes need to do similar work &mdash; they should always serve a purpose beyond simple decoration.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">The Anatomy of a Strong Simile</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">A strong simile has three parts:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>1. The subject</strong> &mdash; what you are describing (e.g. the storm, the character&rsquo;s fear, the building).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>2. The comparison</strong> &mdash; what you are comparing it to (this should be unexpected but logical).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>3. The effect</strong> &mdash; what the comparison makes the reader think or feel. If you can&rsquo;t explain why you chose that comparison, it probably isn&rsquo;t working hard enough.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">How to Generate Original Similes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">The trick to writing original similes is to avoid reaching for the first comparison that comes to mind. That first instinct is almost always a clich&eacute;. Instead, use this three-step process:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Step 1: Identify the quality.</strong> What exactly are you trying to convey? Not just &ldquo;it was scary&rdquo; but what <em>kind</em> of scary? Creeping dread? Sudden shock? Paralysing terror? Be specific about the feeling or sensory detail.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Step 2: Think sideways.</strong> Instead of comparing within the same category (a storm to another weather event), jump to a completely different field. Compare the storm to something from music, cooking, warfare, childhood, machinery, or nature. The bigger the leap, the more original the simile &mdash; as long as it still makes sense.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Step 3: Test the effect.</strong> Read your simile back. Does it create an image? Does it match the mood you want? Does it reveal something about the character or narrator? If the answer to all three is yes, you have a strong simile.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Examiners are looking for &ldquo;conscious crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; (Level 3) and &ldquo;sustained crafting&rdquo; (Level 4). One or two carefully chosen similes placed at key moments in your writing are far more effective than scattering figurative language in every sentence. Overuse feels forced and actually lowers your mark.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Beyond Similes: Other Figurative Techniques</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">Similes are just one tool in your figurative language toolkit. The strongest creative writing at GCSE blends several techniques together. Here are the key devices to combine with your similes:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech that states one thing IS another, without using 'like' or 'as'. E.g. 'the corridor was a throat, swallowing us whole.'">Metaphor</dfn>:</strong> A metaphor is bolder than a simile &mdash; it says something <em>is</em> something else rather than saying it is <em>like</em> something else. &ldquo;The corridor was a throat, swallowing us whole&rdquo; is more direct and intense than &ldquo;The corridor was like a throat.&rdquo; Use metaphors when you want to create a stronger, more immersive effect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A metaphor that is developed over several sentences or an entire paragraph, with multiple related comparisons building on the same image.">Extended metaphor</dfn>:</strong> This is where you take a single comparison and develop it across several sentences or even a whole paragraph. For example, if you compare a storm to an argument, you might describe the thunder as &ldquo;shouting,&rdquo; the lightning as &ldquo;accusations,&rdquo; and the silence after as &ldquo;the cold truce of morning.&rdquo; Extended metaphors impress examiners because they show sustained, deliberate crafting.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of figurative language where non-human things are given human qualities or actions. E.g. 'the wind howled' or 'the house groaned under the weight of years.'">Personification</dfn>:</strong> Giving human qualities to non-human things. Look at how both Peter Fleming and Fanny Kemble use personification in the AQA sample inserts: Fleming describes the crashed engine as having &ldquo;a defiant and naughty look&rdquo; and being &ldquo;definitely conscious of indiscretion,&rdquo; while Kemble calls the steam engine a &ldquo;snorting little animal&rdquo; she &ldquo;felt rather inclined to pat.&rdquo; Both writers use personification to reveal their attitude to the train &mdash; Fleming is amused, Kemble is enchanted.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n31">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>1. Clich&eacute;s.</strong> &ldquo;Cold as ice,&rdquo; &ldquo;quiet as a mouse,&rdquo; &ldquo;black as night&rdquo; &mdash; these are dead phrases. They show no originality and will keep your writing in Level 2 at best. If you&rsquo;ve heard it before, don&rsquo;t write it.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>2. Mixed metaphors.</strong> Don&rsquo;t start one comparison and then switch to a completely different one in the same sentence. &ldquo;The storm was a furious beast, hammering nails of rain into the roof&rdquo; mixes animal imagery with construction imagery. Pick one and commit to it.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>3. Overloading.</strong> If every sentence contains a simile or metaphor, the writing becomes exhausting to read. The mark scheme rewards &ldquo;crafting&rdquo; &mdash; which means deliberate, controlled use. Two or three powerful figurative moments across a 300&ndash;400 word response is ideal.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>4. Similes that don&rsquo;t match the mood.</strong> If you are describing a tense, frightening scene, a cheerful or humorous simile will break the atmosphere. Every figurative choice must serve the overall tone of your piece.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>The final sentence:</strong> Ending on a powerful image stays in the reader&rsquo;s (and examiner&rsquo;s) mind. A well-placed metaphor in your closing line can elevate the whole piece.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n40">Putting It Into Practice</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n41">Here is an example of a short descriptive paragraph that uses figurative language at Level 4 standard:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n42">&ldquo;The house stood at the end of the lane like a held breath &mdash; still, expectant, refusing to exhale. Its windows were lidded eyes, half-closed against the morning. Somewhere inside, a door knocked against its frame with the patient, rhythmic insistence of a heart that would not stop.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n43">Notice three things about this example. First, the simile (&ldquo;like a held breath&rdquo;) is original &mdash; it compares a building to a physical sensation, which is unexpected. Second, the personification (&ldquo;lidded eyes,&rdquo; &ldquo;refusing to exhale&rdquo;) is sustained across the paragraph, creating an extended metaphor of the house as a living body. Third, the figurative language serves the mood: everything suggests tension, anticipation, and unease. This is what &ldquo;sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; looks like in practice.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n44">In Paper 1 Question 5, examiners reward quality over quantity when it comes to figurative language. The Level 4 descriptor says &ldquo;extensive and ambitious vocabulary with sustained crafting of linguistic devices.&rdquo; The word &ldquo;crafting&rdquo; is key &mdash; it means your similes and metaphors should feel deliberate and purposeful, not thrown in randomly. Aim for two or three strong figurative moments across your whole response. Before writing a simile, ask yourself: does this create an image, match the mood, AND reveal something about the narrator or character? If it does all three, keep it. If it only does one, rethink it. And never, ever use a clich&eacute; &mdash; it signals to the examiner that you are writing on autopilot.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n45">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n46">A strong simile does three things: creates an image, conveys a mood, and reveals perspective. If it only does one, it isn&rsquo;t working hard enough.</li><li data-narration-id="n47">Avoid clich&eacute;s by rejecting your first instinct and &ldquo;thinking sideways&rdquo; &mdash; compare across different fields (nature to machinery, emotion to weather, buildings to bodies).</li><li data-narration-id="n48">Place figurative language at key moments (openings, mood shifts, endings) rather than in every sentence. Two or three well-crafted devices will score higher than ten generic ones.</li></ul>
