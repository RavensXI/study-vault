# Narrative Structure — Drop, Shift, Zoom, Link

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Structure Matters in Paper 1, Section B</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Many students focus all their revision energy on language techniques &mdash; metaphors, similes, sensory detail &mdash; but forget that structure carries just as many marks. AQA&rsquo;s mark scheme for the creative writing question (AO5) explicitly rewards students who &ldquo;organise information and ideas, using structural and grammatical features to support coherence and cohesion of texts.&rdquo; In other words, <strong>how you arrange your writing matters as much as what you put in it</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This lesson teaches you four powerful <dfn class="term" data-def="A deliberate choice about how to arrange, order, or present content within a piece of writing to create specific effects on the reader.">structural moves</dfn> that examiners look for in top-level responses: <strong>Drop</strong>, <strong>Shift</strong>, <strong>Zoom</strong>, and <strong>Link</strong>. These are not rigid rules &mdash; they are tools you can deploy in any combination to give your narrative a clear, purposeful shape that impresses the examiner.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>AO5 (content and organisation) is worth 24 marks on the Paper 1 creative writing question. Half of those marks are about organisation &mdash; how you structure paragraphs, control pace, and create cohesion. Strong structural choices can lift a response from Level 2 to Level 3 or even Level 4.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Move 1: Drop &mdash; Throw the Reader Straight In</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A structural technique where a piece of writing begins in the middle of the action or at a moment of high tension, without preamble or background explanation.">Drop</dfn> opening means starting your writing in the middle of the action. Instead of slowly building up background information, you plunge the reader into a moment of tension, conflict, or emotion. This is sometimes called <em>in medias res</em> (Latin for &ldquo;in the middle of things&rdquo;).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Look at how Yann Martel opens the extract from <em>Life of Pi</em> used in the AQA 2026 sample paper: &ldquo;It was the hyena that worried me.&rdquo; There is no slow introduction. No explanation of how Pi got on the lifeboat. The reader is dropped straight into his fear. This single sentence immediately raises questions: <em>Why is the hyena worrying him? What will it do?</em> The reader is hooked.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">To use a Drop opening in your own writing:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n9">Start with a short, punchy sentence that introduces tension or emotion.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n10">Avoid &ldquo;I woke up&rdquo; or &ldquo;It was a normal day&rdquo; beginnings &mdash; these are the opposite of a Drop.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n11">Withhold context. Let the reader work out where they are and what is happening gradually.</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Move 2: Shift &mdash; Change the Gear</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A deliberate change in time, place, mood, tone, or perspective within a piece of writing, used to create contrast or move the narrative forward.">Shift</dfn> is any deliberate change in your writing &mdash; a change in time, place, mood, tone, or perspective. Shifts prevent your writing from feeling flat or one-note. They show the examiner that you are making conscious, controlled choices about how your narrative moves.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">In the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract, Martel shifts between action and reflection. The hyena leaps out and runs in frantic circles (action), then the narrative pauses while Pi watches helplessly and thinks about the horizon (reflection). Later, the mood shifts from terrifying to almost absurd when the hyena suddenly vomits and collapses. This unexpected tonal shift &mdash; from threat to anti-climax &mdash; creates a complex, layered effect.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n15">Common types of Shift you can use:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Time shift:</strong> Jump forward or backward (&ldquo;Three hours earlier&hellip;&rdquo; or a single paragraph break to imply time passing).</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Mood/tone shift:</strong> Move from calm to chaotic, or from threatening to peaceful.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Perspective shift:</strong> Move from a character&rsquo;s internal thoughts to an external, detached description of the setting.</li>
</ul>

A student writing at Level 2 might keep describing the storm for the entire piece. A Level 4 student would <strong>Shift</strong>. They might insert a quiet, reflective paragraph: &ldquo;I remembered the last time the lights went out. I was six. My mother had lit candles and told me it was an adventure.&rdquo; This time shift and mood shift creates contrast, adds depth to the character, and shows the examiner you are in control of your structure.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Move 3: Zoom &mdash; Control Your Focus</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A structural technique where the writer narrows or widens the focus of their description, moving between wide-angle views and close-up details.">Zoom</dfn> is a change in the level of detail. Think of it like a camera: you can zoom out to show the whole landscape, or zoom in to show a single raindrop on a leaf. Moving between wide and narrow focus keeps your writing visually dynamic and shows sophisticated control.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Peter Fleming uses Zoom brilliantly in the Source A extract from the AQA 2026 Paper 2 sample. After the train crash, he zooms out to describe the entire scene: &ldquo;The Trans-Siberian Express train sprawled foolishly down the embankment.&rdquo; He describes the mail van, dining car, and sleeping cars in a wide shot. Then he zooms in to the engine, giving it personality: &ldquo;It had a defiant and naughty look; it was definitely conscious of indiscretion.&rdquo; This shift from panoramic overview to close-up detail creates a vivid, cinematic effect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Similarly, in the <em>Life of Pi</em> extract, Martel zooms in on tiny physical details of the hyena: its &ldquo;receding hairline,&rdquo; its &ldquo;ridiculously mouse-like&rdquo; ears, its nostrils that are &ldquo;too big.&rdquo; This extreme close-up makes the reader feel uncomfortably close to the animal, building tension through proximity.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme for structure (Paper 1, Q3) explicitly rewards students who can identify &ldquo;shifts in focus&rdquo; and &ldquo;narrative pace.&rdquo; When you use Zoom in your creative writing, you are demonstrating exactly the kind of structural awareness that examiners reward in both reading and writing questions.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Move 4: Link &mdash; Connect Your Beginning and End</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A structural technique where an image, phrase, or idea from the beginning of a piece is repeated or echoed at the end, creating a sense of completeness.">Link</dfn> (sometimes called a cyclical or circular structure) means returning to something from your opening at the end of your piece. This might be repeating a word, image, or phrase &mdash; but with a difference. The repetition creates a satisfying sense of completeness, while the difference shows how something has changed.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">For example, if your story opens with the sentence &ldquo;The door was red,&rdquo; you might end with &ldquo;The door was still red, but nothing else was the same.&rdquo; The repeated image of the red door provides cohesion, but the added clause shows that the character or situation has been transformed by the events of the narrative.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Fleming uses a version of Link in Source A. At the start, he describes the train as a controlling, oppressive force that has &ldquo;bullied&rdquo; and &ldquo;annoyed&rdquo; them. By the end, the train is &ldquo;a broken, buckled toy&rdquo; &mdash; a thing of no use. The structural contrast between the train&rsquo;s dominance at the start and its destruction at the end creates a deeply satisfying arc.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n29">The key to a successful Link is planning. Before you start writing, decide on <strong>one image, object, or phrase</strong> that you will use in both your opening and your ending. Write it at the top of your plan. As you write, keep this image in mind &mdash; you might weave small references to it through the middle of your piece too. When you reach your final paragraph, return to the image but change something about it: the tone, the meaning, or the context. This shows the examiner that your structure is deliberate and controlled from beginning to end.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30">Quick planning example for a story about a storm:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Opening image: &ldquo;The sky was the colour of a bruise.&rdquo;</li>
      <li>Middle: The storm arrives, causes damage, a character faces a challenge.</li>
      <li>Ending image: &ldquo;The sky was the colour of a bruise &mdash; but lighter now, yellowing at the edges, the way bruises do when they begin to heal.&rdquo;</li>
    </ul>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n31">Putting the Four Moves Together</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n32">You do not need to use all four moves in every piece of writing. However, the best responses typically use at least two or three. Here is a simple five-paragraph structure that combines them:</p>

<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n33">
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Paragraph 1</div>
    <h4>DROP</h4><p>Start in the middle of the action. Short, tense sentences. Raise a question in the reader&rsquo;s mind.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Paragraph 2</div>
    <h4>ZOOM OUT</h4><p>Pull back to describe the wider setting. Slow the pace. Use longer, more descriptive sentences.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Paragraph 3</div>
    <h4>SHIFT</h4><p>Change the mood, time, or perspective. Move to a memory, a contrasting emotion, or a different character&rsquo;s viewpoint.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Paragraph 4</div>
    <h4>ZOOM IN</h4><p>Focus on a single, specific detail. Use this to build tension or create a moment of emotional intensity.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Paragraph 5</div>
    <h4>LINK</h4><p>Return to your opening image or idea, but with a difference. Show how the situation or character has changed.</p>
  </div>
</div>

    <ul>
      <li><strong>Level 1 (1&ndash;6 marks):</strong> &ldquo;Evidence of simple structural features&rdquo; and &ldquo;one or two relevant ideas, simply linked.&rdquo; This is writing that goes from start to finish with no deliberate structural choices.</li>
      <li><strong>Level 2 (7&ndash;12 marks):</strong> &ldquo;Some use of structural features&rdquo; and &ldquo;some linked and relevant ideas.&rdquo; The student is beginning to organise their work but the structure is not fully controlled.</li>
      <li><strong>Level 3 (13&ndash;18 marks):</strong> &ldquo;Effective use of structural features&rdquo; and &ldquo;engaging, using a range of clear, connected ideas.&rdquo; The writing has deliberate shifts, controlled pace, and coherent paragraphs.</li>
      <li><strong>Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks):</strong> &ldquo;Varied and inventive use of structural features&rdquo; and &ldquo;compelling, incorporating a range of convincing and complex ideas.&rdquo; Drop, Shift, Zoom, and Link are exactly the kind of &ldquo;varied and inventive&rdquo; structural features that reach Level 4.</li>
    </ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n35">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A common mistake is writing a creative piece that is all action or all description from start to finish. The four structural moves prevent this by forcing you to vary your pace and focus. Think of your writing as a piece of music: it needs loud and quiet sections, fast and slow moments, close-up solos and wide ensemble passages.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n36">In the Paper 1 creative writing question, spend 5 minutes planning your structure before you write a single sentence. Jot down which structural moves you will use in each paragraph (D, S, Z, L). Examiners can tell the difference between writing that was planned and writing that was made up as it went along. A clear, purposeful structure is the fastest way to move from Level 2 into Level 3 or Level 4 on AO5. Remember: the mark scheme rewards &ldquo;varied and inventive use of structural features&rdquo; at the top level &mdash; so use at least two or three of the four moves in every response.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n37">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Drop</strong> your reader straight into the action &mdash; avoid slow, generic openings. Start with tension, emotion, or a question.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Shift</strong> between time, mood, tone, or perspective to keep your writing dynamic and show the examiner you are in control.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Zoom</strong> in and out to vary your focus &mdash; wide-angle descriptions of setting and close-up details of objects or characters create a cinematic effect.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Link</strong> your ending back to your opening with a repeated image or phrase, but change something to show development.</li>
</ul>
