# Structuring an Argument

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Structure Matters in Transactional Writing</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In AQA Paper 2, Question 5, you are asked to write a piece of <dfn class="term" data-def="Writing that has a clear purpose, audience, and form — such as a speech, article, letter, or essay. It aims to communicate ideas persuasively or informatively.">transactional writing</dfn> worth 40 marks. Of those 40 marks, 24 are awarded for content and organisation (AO5). This means the way you structure your argument is just as important as what you say. A brilliant point buried in a disorganised response will not reach the higher levels of the mark scheme.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The AQA mark scheme rewards writing that has &ldquo;varied and inventive use of structural features&rdquo; and &ldquo;fluently linked paragraphs with seamlessly integrated discourse markers&rdquo; at the top level (Level 4, 19&ndash;24 marks). At the lower levels, examiners describe writing with &ldquo;random paragraph structure&rdquo; and &ldquo;one or two unlinked ideas.&rdquo; The difference between these levels often comes down to one thing: did the student plan their structure before they started writing?</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>24 out of 40 marks on Question 5 are for content and organisation (AO5). A well-structured argument with clear paragraphing and discourse markers is essential for reaching Level 3 or above.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Five-Part Argument Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A strong argumentative piece follows a clear pattern. You do not need to reinvent the wheel every time you write &mdash; you need a reliable framework that you can adapt to any topic. The five-part structure below works for speeches, articles, letters, and essays.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>1. The Opening &mdash; Hook and Thesis</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7">Your opening paragraph must do two things: grab the reader&rsquo;s attention and state your <dfn class="term" data-def="The main argument or point of view that a writer establishes at the start of their piece and then supports throughout.">thesis</dfn> (your overall point of view). Start with a hook &mdash; this could be a rhetorical question, a striking statistic, a bold statement, or a short anecdote. Then make your position clear. The examiner should know within the first few sentences what you are arguing.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8">For example, if the question asks you to argue about public transport (as in the 2026 sample paper), you might open with: &ldquo;Every morning, millions of commuters sit trapped in traffic, burning fuel and burning time &mdash; while half-empty buses crawl past them.&rdquo; This creates a vivid image and immediately signals your argument.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>2&ndash;4. The Body Paragraphs &mdash; Three Developed Points</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n9">Aim for three body paragraphs, each developing a separate reason that supports your thesis. Three paragraphs give you enough scope to show a &ldquo;range of clear, connected ideas&rdquo; (Level 3) without running out of time. Each paragraph should follow this internal structure:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Point</strong> &mdash; State your argument clearly in the topic sentence.<br/><strong>Evidence / Example</strong> &mdash; Support your point with a specific detail, example, or scenario.<br/><strong>Explanation</strong> &mdash; Explain why this matters and link it back to your overall thesis.<br/><strong>Crafted language</strong> &mdash; Include at least one deliberate rhetorical or linguistic technique per paragraph.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11">The mark scheme at Level 4 rewards &ldquo;detailed/complex ideas&rdquo; and &ldquo;fluid sequencing of ideas/argument.&rdquo; This means each paragraph should not just list a new point &mdash; it should build on the previous one, creating a sense of momentum.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>5. The Counter-argument and Conclusion</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n12">Before your conclusion, include a <dfn class="term" data-def="An opposing viewpoint that a writer raises in order to acknowledge it and then argue against it, strengthening their own position.">counter-argument</dfn>. This is where you acknowledge the other side of the debate &mdash; and then dismantle it. This shows the examiner that you can think in a balanced way, even while arguing one position. Phrases like &ldquo;Some may argue that&hellip;&rdquo; or &ldquo;Of course, opponents will claim&hellip;&rdquo; signal the counter-argument before you rebut it.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n13">Your conclusion should not simply repeat your opening. Instead, it should leave a lasting impression. Return to your thesis, but elevate it &mdash; perhaps by broadening the scope, making a call to action, or ending with a powerful image or short sentence. The mark scheme rewards a &ldquo;convincing argument&rdquo; that feels complete and purposeful.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Discourse Markers: The Glue of Your Argument</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="Words or phrases that connect ideas between and within paragraphs, guiding the reader through the argument. Examples include 'furthermore,' 'however,' 'consequently,' and 'in contrast.'">Discourse markers</dfn> are the words and phrases that link your paragraphs and ideas together. They signal to the reader (and the examiner) that your argument has a logical flow. The AQA mark scheme specifically references them at every level:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Level 4:</strong> &ldquo;Fluently linked paragraphs with seamlessly integrated discourse markers.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Level 3:</strong> &ldquo;Coherent paragraphs with integrated discourse markers.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Level 2:</strong> &ldquo;Some use of paragraphs and some use of discourse markers.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Level 1:</strong> &ldquo;No paragraphs.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The key word at Level 4 is <em>seamlessly</em>. This means your discourse markers should not feel bolted on. Avoid starting every paragraph with &ldquo;Furthermore&rdquo; or &ldquo;Moreover&rdquo; &mdash; this becomes repetitive and mechanical. Instead, vary your connectives and embed them within sentences. For example, instead of writing &ldquo;Furthermore, buses are cheaper,&rdquo; try: &ldquo;The financial argument is equally compelling: buses cost a fraction of what drivers spend on petrol alone.&rdquo;</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>At Level 4, discourse markers should be &ldquo;seamlessly integrated&rdquo; &mdash; woven naturally into your sentences rather than placed mechanically at the start of every paragraph. Vary your connectives and use topic sentences to signal shifts in your argument.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Paragraphing for Effect</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Paragraphing is not just about organisation &mdash; it is a structural tool you can use deliberately for effect. The mark scheme rewards &ldquo;varied and inventive use of structural features&rdquo; at Level 4. Here are three ways to use paragraphing strategically:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>The short, punchy paragraph:</strong> A one- or two-sentence paragraph can create emphasis or dramatic pause. For example, after a long paragraph detailing problems with public transport, you might write: &ldquo;And yet, we do nothing.&rdquo; This isolation forces the reader to stop and reflect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>The list paragraph:</strong> A paragraph that uses a trio of examples or a list with parallel structure creates a sense of accumulation and builds momentum. The rule of three is particularly effective here.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>The pivot paragraph:</strong> This is your counter-argument paragraph. It deliberately shifts the direction of your argument before you turn it back. This creates variety in your structure and shows sophistication.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Planning Your Argument in the Exam</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">You are advised to spend about 45 minutes on Question 5. The AQA exam paper itself tells you: &ldquo;You are advised to plan your answer to Question 5 before you start to write.&rdquo; Spending 5 minutes planning will save you time in the long run and result in a more coherent, higher-scoring response.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Here is a quick planning method that works under timed conditions:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Step 1 &mdash; Read the statement carefully.</strong> Identify the topic (e.g. cars vs public transport) and the form (e.g. speech). Underline the key instruction: &ldquo;argue your point of view.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Step 2 &mdash; Decide your position.</strong> You do not need to believe it &mdash; you need to argue it convincingly. Pick whichever side you can write the most about.<br/><strong>Step 3 &mdash; Jot down three main points</strong> that support your argument. Number them in order of strength, saving the strongest for last.<br/><strong>Step 4 &mdash; Note one counter-argument</strong> and how you will rebut it.<br/><strong>Step 5 &mdash; Write a one-line idea for your opening hook</strong> and your closing image or call to action.</p>

However, we need to use them less by making public transport such as trains, trams and buses cheaper, more reliable and easier to access.&rdquo; Write a speech to be given at a meeting of your local council in which you argue your point of view on this statement.</em></p>

    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Position:</strong> Agree &mdash; we must invest in public transport.<br/><strong>Hook:</strong> Rhetorical question about sitting in traffic while a bus drives past.<br/><strong>Point 1:</strong> Environmental &mdash; car emissions contribute to climate change and poor air quality in towns.<br/><strong>Point 2:</strong> Economic &mdash; running a car costs thousands per year; affordable transport helps poorer families.<br/><strong>Point 3:</strong> Community &mdash; better public transport connects isolated communities and reduces congestion for everyone.<br/><strong>Counter:</strong> &ldquo;Cars give freedom&rdquo; &mdash; rebut by saying freedom is meaningless if you&rsquo;re stuck in a traffic jam.<br/><strong>Ending:</strong> Direct address &mdash; &ldquo;Councillors, the choice is yours.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n31">This plan takes about 3&ndash;5 minutes and gives you a clear roadmap for your writing. Every paragraph has a purpose, and you know where you are heading before you write a single sentence of your response.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Sequencing Your Ideas for Maximum Impact</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">The order of your points matters. A common and effective approach is to sequence your arguments from weakest to strongest. This creates a sense of building momentum &mdash; each point hits harder than the last. The mark scheme at Level 4 describes this as &ldquo;fluid sequencing of ideas/argument&rdquo; and &ldquo;subtle variation of pace.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34">Another approach is to open with your most emotionally engaging point (to hook the reader), then provide your most logical or evidence-based point in the middle, and end with your most powerful or call-to-action point. Whichever order you choose, make sure there is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A clear logical flow from one idea to the next, so that the reader can follow the writer's argument without confusion.">coherent progression</dfn> from one paragraph to the next. Each paragraph should feel like it belongs where it is, not like it could be moved anywhere else.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n35">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme caps your content and organisation mark at the top of Level 2 (12 out of 24) if you &ldquo;do not directly address the focus of the task.&rdquo; Always refer back to the specific statement and form given in the question.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n36">In Question 5, examiners can tell immediately whether a student has planned. An unplanned response tends to repeat the same point in different words, drift off-topic, or run out of ideas halfway through. Spend 5 minutes planning your five-part structure before you write. Use the specific form given in the question &mdash; if it says &ldquo;speech,&rdquo; include direct address (&ldquo;Ladies and gentlemen&rdquo;), rhetorical questions, and a formal but persuasive tone. If it says &ldquo;article,&rdquo; include a headline and subheadings. Matching your structure to the form shows the examiner that your &ldquo;tone, style and register are assuredly matched to purpose and audience&rdquo; (Level 4). Finally, always leave 5 minutes at the end to proofread &mdash; the 16 marks for technical accuracy (AO6) can make the difference between a grade 5 and a grade 7.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n37">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n38">Use the five-part structure (hook + thesis, three body paragraphs, counter-argument + conclusion) as a reliable framework for any Question 5 response.</li><li data-narration-id="n39">Discourse markers must be seamlessly integrated, not bolted onto the start of every paragraph &mdash; this is the difference between Level 3 and Level 4.</li><li data-narration-id="n40">Always spend 5 minutes planning before you write. Decide your position, list three points in order of strength, note a counter-argument, and plan your opening hook and closing image.</li></ul>
