# Writing Speeches and Essays

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Does Paper 2 Question 5 Ask You to Do?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Paper 2 Question 5 is the only writing question on this paper. It is worth <strong>40 marks</strong> &mdash; half the marks for the entire paper &mdash; split into 24 marks for content and organisation (AO5) and 16 marks for technical accuracy (AO6). You will be given a statement and asked to write in a specific <dfn class="term" data-def="The type of text you are asked to produce, such as a speech, essay, article or letter.">form</dfn>, such as a speech or essay, in which you argue your point of view.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">In the 2026 sample paper, the question reads:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Cars are convenient, comfortable and save time. However, we need to use them less by making public transport such as trains, trams and buses cheaper, more reliable and easier to access.&rdquo; Write a speech to be given at a meeting of your local council in which you argue your point of view on this statement.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Notice three things the question gives you: a <strong>topic</strong> (cars vs public transport), a <strong>form</strong> (a speech), and an <strong>audience</strong> (your local council). Your job is to match your <dfn class="term" data-def="The level of formality and the style of language chosen to suit the audience and purpose of the writing.">register</dfn>, tone and style to all three.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Speeches vs Essays: What&rsquo;s the Difference?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Although speeches and essays both argue a point of view, they differ in how they connect with the audience. Understanding these differences is essential for matching <dfn class="term" data-def="The attitude or feeling conveyed by the writer's word choices, such as formal, passionate, sarcastic or earnest.">tone</dfn> and style to form.</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n8">A speech is written to be <strong>spoken aloud</strong> to a live audience. This means you should:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Address the audience directly.</strong> Use second person (&ldquo;you&rdquo;) and collective pronouns (&ldquo;we&rdquo;, &ldquo;us&rdquo;, &ldquo;our&rdquo;) to build a connection. Open with a greeting: &ldquo;Members of the council, thank you for this opportunity to speak.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Use rhetorical devices deliberately.</strong> Speeches rely on the power of the spoken word, so techniques such as <dfn class="term" data-def="A question asked for effect, not to receive an answer. Used in speeches to make the audience think or to emphasise a point.">rhetorical questions</dfn>, lists of three (tricolon), repetition, emotive language and direct address are expected.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Vary your pace and tone.</strong> Short, punchy sentences create emphasis. Longer sentences allow you to develop a point. A good speech alternates between the two to keep the audience engaged.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>End with a call to action.</strong> Tell the audience what you want them to do: &ldquo;I urge this council to invest in our public transport network &mdash; not next year, not next month, but now.&rdquo;</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Features of an Essay</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13">An essay is a more formal, structured piece of writing. While it still argues a viewpoint, it does so through a balanced, logical approach rather than through the emotional immediacy of a speech.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Use a clear thesis.</strong> State your argument in the introduction and return to it throughout. Each paragraph should develop one aspect of your argument.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Acknowledge counterarguments.</strong> Show maturity by considering the opposing view and then explaining why your position is stronger: &ldquo;While some argue that cars offer unmatched convenience, this overlooks the environmental cost borne by all.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Maintain a formal, measured tone.</strong> Avoid the direct audience address of a speech. An essay persuades through evidence and reasoning rather than emotional appeal alone.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Planning Your Response</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">The exam paper advises you to <strong>plan your answer</strong> before you start writing. A five-minute plan is not wasted time &mdash; it produces a more coherent and convincing response. Here is a simple planning structure that works for both speeches and essays:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Step 1: Decide your viewpoint.</strong> Do you agree, disagree, or partially agree with the statement? You do not have to agree. You can argue any position as long as it is sustained and convincing.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Step 2: Generate 3&ndash;4 main points.</strong> Each point becomes a paragraph. For the sample question, you might argue: (1) public transport reduces pollution, (2) cars isolate communities, (3) investment creates jobs, (4) counterargument &mdash; rural areas still need cars.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Step 3: Choose your rhetorical devices.</strong> Note which techniques you will use and where. Planning your devices prevents you from stuffing them all into the first paragraph and forgetting them later.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Step 4: Plan your opening and closing.</strong> Your opening line hooks the reader. Your closing line drives home your argument. Plan both before you write a single word of the response.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme states that to reach Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks for AO5), your communication must be &ldquo;convincing and compelling and directly relevant to focus and task&rdquo; with &ldquo;tone, style and register assuredly matched to purpose and audience.&rdquo; This means the examiner is looking for a response that sounds like a real speech or essay, not a generic piece of writing.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Crafting a Compelling Opening</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Your opening sentence sets the tone for the entire piece. Avoid weak openings like &ldquo;I am going to talk about&hellip;&rdquo; or &ldquo;In this essay I will argue&hellip;&rdquo; Instead, aim for one of these approaches:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>A bold statement:</strong> &ldquo;Every morning, millions of us sit alone in metal boxes, crawling through traffic, burning fuel and burning time &mdash; and we call this progress.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>A rhetorical question:</strong> &ldquo;How many hours of your life have you wasted stuck in traffic? How much money have you poured into a petrol tank? And for what?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>A striking statistic or fact:</strong> &ldquo;In this country, transport is now the largest source of carbon emissions. That is not a future problem. It is today&rsquo;s crisis.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">For a speech, follow your opening with a direct greeting: &ldquo;Council members, I stand before you today to argue that&hellip;&rdquo; This grounds your writing in the correct form immediately.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Building the Middle: Paragraphing and Discourse Markers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Each paragraph in the body of your response should focus on <strong>one clear point</strong>. Begin with a topic sentence, develop it with evidence or examples, and link it back to your argument. Use <dfn class="term" data-def="Words or phrases that connect paragraphs and ideas, guiding the reader through the argument. Examples include 'furthermore', 'however', 'consequently' and 'in contrast'.">discourse markers</dfn> to create cohesion between paragraphs.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32">The mark scheme rewards &ldquo;fluently linked paragraphs with seamlessly integrated discourse markers&rdquo; at Level 4. This means avoiding mechanical connectives like &ldquo;Firstly&hellip; Secondly&hellip; Thirdly&hellip;&rdquo; Instead, weave your links naturally:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33">&ldquo;The environmental case is clear. But the benefits extend beyond the air we breathe. Consider the economic argument&hellip;&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34">Notice how this transitions from one point to the next without using a clunky connective. The short sentence &ldquo;The environmental case is clear&rdquo; concludes the previous idea, while &ldquo;But the benefits extend beyond&rdquo; pivots to the next one.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n35">Rhetorical Devices That Earn Marks</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36">The mark scheme rewards &ldquo;extensive and ambitious vocabulary with sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; at Level 4, and &ldquo;increasingly sophisticated vocabulary and phrasing, chosen for effect with a range of successful linguistic devices&rdquo; at Level 3. Here are the most effective devices for speeches and essays:</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Key Rhetorical Devices with Examples</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Tricolon (list of three):</strong> &ldquo;Cheaper fares, reliable timetables, accessible stations &mdash; that is what this council should deliver.&rdquo; Three items create a satisfying rhythm and build emphasis.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Anaphora (repetition at the start of sentences):</strong> &ldquo;We deserve clean air. We deserve affordable travel. We deserve a council that listens.&rdquo; Repetition of &ldquo;We deserve&rdquo; hammers the point home.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Emotive language:</strong> &ldquo;Children gasping for breath on polluted school runs&rdquo; appeals directly to the audience&rsquo;s emotions and makes the issue personal.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Counterargument and rebuttal:</strong> &ldquo;Some will say this is too expensive. But what is the cost of doing nothing?&rdquo; Acknowledging the other side and then dismantling it shows maturity and control.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Direct address:</strong> &ldquo;You have the power to change this&rdquo; makes the audience feel responsible. This is especially powerful in speeches.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n42">Writing a Powerful Conclusion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n43">Your conclusion should <strong>not</strong> simply repeat what you have already said. Instead, it should leave the reader (or listener) with a lasting impression. Effective conclusions often:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Circle back to the opening.</strong> If you opened with a striking image of traffic jams, return to it: &ldquo;Tomorrow morning, millions will sit in those metal boxes once again. But with your support, that does not have to be our future.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n45"><strong>End with a call to action (speeches):</strong> &ldquo;Vote for this investment. Fund our buses. Build our future.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n46"><strong>End with a thought-provoking statement (essays):</strong> &ldquo;The question is not whether we can afford to improve public transport. It is whether we can afford not to.&rdquo;</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n47">Technical Accuracy: The 16 Marks That Matter</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n48">AO6 is worth 16 marks &mdash; 40% of the question. Many students focus entirely on content and forget that <dfn class="term" data-def="The correctness of spelling, punctuation, grammar and sentence construction in a piece of writing.">technical accuracy</dfn> carries almost as many marks. To reach Level 4 (13&ndash;16 marks) for AO6, the mark scheme requires:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n49"><strong>Sentence demarcation:</strong> Consistently secure and accurate. Every sentence must start with a capital letter and end with a full stop, question mark or exclamation mark.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n50"><strong>Wide range of punctuation:</strong> Semicolons, colons, dashes, brackets and ellipses &mdash; used correctly and for effect, not just full stops and commas.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n51"><strong>Full range of sentence forms:</strong> Simple, compound and complex sentences, used deliberately. A one-word sentence (&ldquo;Enough.&rdquo;) or a minor sentence can be powerful if used sparingly.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n52"><strong>Accurate spelling:</strong> Including ambitious vocabulary. If you are unsure how to spell a word, choose a synonym you can spell correctly.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n53">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Leave at least 5 minutes at the end to proofread your response. Check for missing full stops, comma splices (using a comma where you need a full stop), and homophones (their/there/they&rsquo;re, your/you&rsquo;re, to/too/two). These basic errors can drop you from Level 3 to Level 2 for AO6.</p>
</div>

    <span>Common Mistakes to Avoid</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n54"><strong>Ignoring the form.</strong> If the question says &ldquo;Write a speech,&rdquo; your response must read like a speech. If it reads like a generic essay, the mark scheme caps AO5 because your tone, style and register are not matched to purpose and audience.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n55"><strong>Losing focus on the statement.</strong> Every paragraph should connect back to the given statement. If the statement is about public transport vs cars, do not spend three paragraphs writing about cycling or walking. The mark scheme states: &ldquo;If a candidate does not directly address the focus of the task, their communication cannot be clear or convincing and therefore the mark for AO5 is capped at the top of Level 2 &mdash; 12 marks.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n56"><strong>Overloading rhetorical devices.</strong> Using a rhetorical question in every single paragraph becomes repetitive. Vary your techniques. The mark scheme rewards &ldquo;sustained crafting,&rdquo; not random scattering.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n57"><strong>Forgetting paragraphs.</strong> A wall of text with no paragraphs will struggle to get above Level 2 for organisation. Each new point needs a new paragraph.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n58">For Question 5, always spend 5 minutes planning before you write. Identify the <strong>form</strong> (speech, essay, article, letter), the <strong>audience</strong> (who you are writing to) and the <strong>purpose</strong> (argue, persuade, advise). Then check your plan: does every paragraph link back to the statement? Have you varied your rhetorical devices? Have you matched your tone and register to the form? Remember, if you don&rsquo;t address the focus of the task, AO5 is capped at 12 out of 24 marks. Finally, leave 5 minutes to proofread &mdash; AO6 is worth 16 marks and basic errors in spelling and punctuation are the easiest marks to lose and the easiest to save.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n59">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n60"><strong>Match form, audience and purpose.</strong> A speech must sound like a speech (direct address, rhetorical devices, call to action). An essay must be formal, structured and balanced. The mark scheme rewards writing that is &ldquo;assuredly matched to purpose and audience.&rdquo;</li><li data-narration-id="n61"><strong>Plan before you write.</strong> Decide your viewpoint, generate 3&ndash;4 clear points, choose your devices, and plan your opening and closing. A planned response is always more coherent and convincing.</li><li data-narration-id="n62"><strong>Technical accuracy is worth 16 marks.</strong> Vary your punctuation, use a range of sentence forms, spell ambitiously but accurately, and always proofread. These marks are the most reliable way to boost your grade.</li></ul>
