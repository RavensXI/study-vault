# Transactional Writing Practice — Full Response

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Practise Full Responses?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 5 on AQA Paper 2 is worth 40 marks &mdash; half of the entire paper. It tests your ability to write a <dfn class="term" data-def="Writing that has a clear purpose, audience and form, such as a speech, letter, article or essay.">transactional</dfn> piece that argues a point of view. You will be given a statement and asked to write in a specific form (a speech, letter, article or essay) for a specific audience. Getting this question right can make the difference between grades.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">In this lesson, you will work through a full response from start to finish. We will use the real AQA 2026 sample question to practise planning, structuring and writing a complete answer under exam conditions. Think of this as your final rehearsal before the real thing.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">The Question: Breaking It Down</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Here is the actual AQA 2026 sample Question 5:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n6">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>&ldquo;Cars are convenient, comfortable and save time. However, we need to use them less by making public transport such as trains, trams and buses cheaper, more reliable and easier to access.&rdquo; Write a speech to be given at a meeting of your local council in which you argue your point of view on this statement. (24 marks for content and organisation. 16 marks for technical accuracy.) [40 marks]</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Before writing a single word, you must identify four things from the question:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Purpose:</strong> To argue your point of view. This means you need to persuade your audience, not just inform them.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Audience:</strong> Your local council &mdash; adults in positions of authority. This demands a formal, respectful tone.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Form:</strong> A speech. You must use speech conventions such as <dfn class="term" data-def="Speaking directly to the audience using words like 'you' or 'we' to create a connection and make arguments feel personal.">direct address</dfn>, rhetorical questions and a clear opening address.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Topic:</strong> Cars versus public transport. You can agree, disagree or take a balanced view &mdash; but you must have a clear position.</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Step 1: Planning (5 Minutes)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">You are advised to plan your answer before you start writing. A five-minute plan is not wasted time &mdash; it saves you from losing focus halfway through. A strong plan has three parts:</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Example Plan for This Question</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Position:</strong> Agree &mdash; public transport must be improved and made more affordable.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Paragraph plan:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Opening:</strong> Address the council. Acknowledge cars are convenient. State your argument clearly &mdash; we must invest in public transport.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Paragraph 2:</strong> Environmental argument &mdash; carbon emissions, air pollution, climate crisis. Use a statistic or striking image.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Paragraph 3:</strong> Social fairness &mdash; not everyone can afford a car. Young people, elderly, low-income families rely on buses and trains.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Paragraph 4:</strong> Counter-argument &mdash; acknowledge that cars are sometimes necessary (rural areas, disabilities). Argue this is why public transport needs to be better, not why we should give up.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Conclusion:</strong> Call to action. The council has the power to make a difference. End memorably.</li>
    </ul>
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Techniques to include:</strong> Rhetorical question, rule of three, direct address, anecdote, counter-argument, emotive language.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Step 2: Writing Your Opening</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Your opening paragraph must do three things: address the audience, establish your tone, and make your argument clear. Because the form is a speech, you should begin with a direct address such as &ldquo;Members of the council&rdquo; or &ldquo;Ladies and gentlemen of the council.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24">Here is an example opening:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><em>&ldquo;Members of the council, I stand before you today not to attack the humble car &mdash; we all know it is convenient, comfortable, and saves us precious time. But I am here to ask a simple question: at what cost? Our roads are choked, our air is poisoned, and our most vulnerable citizens are left stranded at bus stops that rarely see a bus. It is time to invest in public transport &mdash; not as an afterthought, but as a priority.&rdquo;</em></p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Notice how this opening acknowledges the statement in the question (cars are convenient), uses a <dfn class="term" data-def="A question asked for dramatic effect rather than to get an answer. It makes the audience think and engages them emotionally.">rhetorical question</dfn> (&ldquo;at what cost?&rdquo;), includes a rule of three (&ldquo;choked&hellip; poisoned&hellip; stranded&rdquo;), and states the argument clearly in the final sentence.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Step 3: Developing Your Middle Paragraphs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Each middle paragraph should focus on one clear point. Use the <dfn class="term" data-def="A paragraph structure: Point, Evidence, Explain, Link back to the argument. Keeps transactional writing focused and persuasive.">PEEL structure</dfn> loosely &mdash; make a point, support it with evidence or an example, explain why it matters, and link it back to your argument.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">The AQA mark scheme rewards &ldquo;a range of convincing and complex ideas&rdquo; at Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks for content and organisation). This means your paragraphs need developed reasoning, not just a list of points. Show the examiner you can think deeply about the issue.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n30">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme for Question 5 has two separate mark grids: AO5 (content and organisation, 24 marks) and AO6 (technical accuracy, 16 marks). You are marked on both WHAT you say and HOW accurately you write. A brilliant argument riddled with spelling errors will not reach the top levels.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n31">Here are techniques to weave into your middle paragraphs:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Counter-argument:</strong> Show the examiner you can see both sides. &ldquo;Some may argue that cars are essential in rural areas &mdash; and they are right. But this is precisely why we need buses that actually run on time.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Anecdote:</strong> A short, made-up but realistic story. &ldquo;I know a student who walks three miles to college because the bus was cut last year.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Emotive language:</strong> Words that stir feelings. &ldquo;Abandoned&rdquo; instead of &ldquo;left behind.&rdquo; &ldquo;Suffocating&rdquo; instead of &ldquo;polluted.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Statistics (invented but plausible):</strong> &ldquo;Studies suggest that a single bus can remove up to forty cars from the road during rush hour.&rdquo;</li>
</ul>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n36">According to the AQA mark scheme, Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks) requires:</p>
    <ul>
      <li data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Convincing and compelling communication</strong> that is directly relevant to the focus and task.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Tone, style and register</strong> assuredly matched to purpose and audience &mdash; so your speech must sound like a speech, and it must sound formal enough for a council meeting.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Extensive and ambitious vocabulary</strong> with sustained crafting of linguistic devices &mdash; this means deliberate, effective use of persuasive techniques throughout, not just one rhetorical question in the opening.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Varied and inventive structural features</strong> with fluently linked paragraphs and seamlessly integrated <dfn class="term" data-def="Words or phrases that connect paragraphs and ideas, such as 'furthermore', 'however', 'consequently'. They guide the reader through the argument.">discourse markers</dfn>.</li>
    </ul>
    <p data-narration-id="n41">In short: every paragraph must feel purposeful, every sentence must earn its place, and the whole piece must flow as one connected argument.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n42">Step 4: Writing Your Conclusion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n43">A strong conclusion is crucial in a speech. It is your last chance to leave an impression. Effective techniques include:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Circular structure:</strong> Return to an image or idea from your opening. If you started with &ldquo;at what cost?&rdquo;, you might end with &ldquo;The cost of inaction is one we cannot afford.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n45"><strong>Call to action:</strong> Tell the audience exactly what you want them to do. &ldquo;I urge this council to commit to a five-year public transport plan.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n46"><strong>Powerful final sentence:</strong> Short, punchy, memorable. &ldquo;The wheels of change must start turning &mdash; and they must start here.&rdquo;</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n47">Step 5: Technical Accuracy (AO6 &mdash; 16 Marks)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n48">Technical accuracy is worth 16 of the 40 marks. That is 40% of your writing mark. Even a brilliant argument will be capped if your spelling, punctuation and grammar are weak. The mark scheme rewards:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n49"><strong>Sentence demarcation:</strong> Every sentence starts with a capital letter and ends with a full stop, question mark or exclamation mark. This sounds basic, but under pressure, students make mistakes.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n50"><strong>Range of punctuation:</strong> Use commas, semicolons, colons, dashes and brackets. A semicolon used correctly signals confidence to the examiner.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n51"><strong>Variety of sentence forms:</strong> Mix short sentences for impact with longer, complex sentences for developed ideas.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n52"><strong>Ambitious vocabulary:</strong> Choose precise words. &ldquo;Neglected&rdquo; is stronger than &ldquo;forgotten.&rdquo; &ldquo;Congested&rdquo; is better than &ldquo;busy.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n53"><strong>Accurate spelling:</strong> Especially of common words that are frequently misspelled: <em>government</em>, <em>environment</em>, <em>accessible</em>, <em>necessary</em>, <em>definitely</em>.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n54">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Leave at least 5 minutes at the end of the exam to proofread your writing. Check for missing full stops, homophones (there/their/they&rsquo;re, your/you&rsquo;re), and any sentences that don&rsquo;t make sense. Fixing even two or three errors can move you up a level in AO6.</p>
</div>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n55"><strong>1. Not matching the form.</strong> If the question asks for a speech, you must write a speech. Starting with &ldquo;Dear Sir/Madam&rdquo; when asked for a speech means you have written a letter. The mark scheme states that if you do not &ldquo;directly address the focus of the task,&rdquo; your AO5 mark is capped at Level 2 (maximum 12 out of 24).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n56"><strong>2. Listing points without development.</strong> Writing &ldquo;Also, public transport is good for the environment. Also, it helps old people. Also, it saves money&rdquo; is Level 1 writing. Each point needs explanation and evidence.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n57"><strong>3. Forgetting the audience.</strong> A speech to a local council should not include slang or overly casual language. Equally, it should not sound like a textbook. Aim for confident formality with moments of passion.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n58"><strong>4. Running out of time.</strong> You have about 45 minutes for Question 5. Spend 5 minutes planning, 35 minutes writing, and 5 minutes checking. Students who do not plan often write for 40 minutes and produce unfocused, rambling responses.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n59">Putting It All Together: A Checklist</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n60">Before you hand in your response, check it against this list:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n61">&squarf; Have I addressed the correct audience in the correct form?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n62">&squarf; Is my argument clear from the opening paragraph?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n63">&squarf; Does each paragraph focus on one main point?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n64">&squarf; Have I used at least three persuasive techniques (e.g. rhetorical question, rule of three, counter-argument, direct address, emotive language)?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n65">&squarf; Have I included a counter-argument to show balanced thinking?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n66">&squarf; Does my conclusion return to my main argument and end memorably?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n67">&squarf; Have I used discourse markers to link my paragraphs (&ldquo;Furthermore,&rdquo; &ldquo;However,&rdquo; &ldquo;Consequently&rdquo;)?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n68">&squarf; Have I varied my sentence structures and used ambitious vocabulary?</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n69">&squarf; Have I proofread for spelling, punctuation and grammar?</li>
</ul>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n70">In Question 5, the examiner is looking for two separate things: your ideas and how accurately you write. Treat them as two separate challenges. When planning, focus entirely on what you want to say and how to structure it. When proofreading, focus entirely on technical accuracy &mdash; full stops, commas, spelling, homophones. Many students lose marks on AO6 not because they cannot spell, but because they rush and do not check. Remember: if you do not write in the correct form (e.g. you write a letter when asked for a speech), your content mark is capped at Level 2 (12 out of 24). Always underline the form, purpose and audience in the question before you begin.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n71">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n72">Always identify the purpose, audience, form and topic before planning. Getting the form wrong caps your mark at 12 out of 24 for content.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n73">Spend 5 minutes planning, 35 minutes writing and 5 minutes proofreading. A planned response is always stronger than an unplanned one.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n74">Technical accuracy (AO6) is worth 16 marks &mdash; 40% of the writing mark. Use varied punctuation, ambitious vocabulary and check your work for errors before the exam ends.</li>
</ul>
