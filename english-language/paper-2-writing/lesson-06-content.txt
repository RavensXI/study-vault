# Technical Accuracy — Vocabulary, Sentences and Punctuation

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Technical Accuracy Matters on Paper 2</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 5 on AQA Paper 2 is worth 40 marks. Of those, 16 marks &mdash; a full 40% of the question &mdash; are awarded for <dfn class="term" data-def="The quality of your spelling, punctuation, grammar, vocabulary and sentence structures. Assessed under AO6 on AQA English Language.">technical accuracy</dfn> under AO6. That means even a brilliant argument with perfect structure can only score 24 out of 40 if your spelling, punctuation and sentences are weak. Technical accuracy is not an afterthought &mdash; it is almost half the battle.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">AO6 states that students must &ldquo;use a range of vocabulary and sentence structures for clarity, purpose and effect, with accurate spelling and punctuation.&rdquo; The examiner is looking at three things: your <dfn class="term" data-def="The words you choose in your writing. AQA rewards 'extensive and ambitious' vocabulary at Level 4.">vocabulary</dfn>, your sentence forms, and your punctuation. This lesson breaks down exactly what each level of the mark scheme demands and gives you practical strategies to push your mark higher.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AO6 technical accuracy mark scheme has four levels. Level 4 (13&ndash;16 marks) requires &ldquo;extensive and ambitious use of vocabulary,&rdquo; a &ldquo;wide range of punctuation&rdquo; used with &ldquo;a high level of accuracy,&rdquo; and a &ldquo;full range of appropriate sentence forms for effect.&rdquo; You must hit all three areas to reach the top level.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Four Levels of AO6</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Understanding what the examiner is looking for at each level helps you identify where you are now and what you need to do to improve. Here is a summary of the AQA mark scheme for technical accuracy:</p>

<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 1 (1&ndash;4)</div>
    <h4>Simple, Limited</h4>
    <p>Occasional sentence demarcation. Some evidence of conscious punctuation. Simple range of sentence forms. Accurate basic spelling. Simple use of vocabulary.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 2 (5&ndash;8)</div>
    <h4>Some Control</h4>
    <p>Sentence demarcation mostly secure and sometimes accurate. Some control of a range of punctuation. Attempts a variety of sentence forms. Some accurate spelling of more complex words. Varied use of vocabulary.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 3 (9&ndash;12)</div>
    <h4>Mostly Secure</h4>
    <p>Sentence demarcation mostly secure and mostly accurate. Range of punctuation used, mostly with success. Uses a variety of sentence forms for effect. Generally accurate spelling, including complex and irregular words. Increasingly sophisticated use of vocabulary.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 4 (13&ndash;16)</div>
    <h4>Consistently Secure</h4>
    <p>Sentence demarcation consistently secure and accurate. Wide range of punctuation with a high level of accuracy. Full range of sentence forms for effect. High level of accuracy in spelling, including ambitious vocabulary. Extensive and ambitious use of vocabulary.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Vocabulary: From Simple to Ambitious</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">The jump from Level 2 to Level 4 in vocabulary is the difference between &ldquo;varied&rdquo; and &ldquo;extensive and ambitious.&rdquo; Ambitious vocabulary does not mean using the longest word you can think of. It means choosing precise, effective words that match your purpose and audience. A speech to a local council about public transport should sound formal and authoritative &mdash; not like a thesaurus exploded on the page.</p>

    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n10">Try the &ldquo;swap and sharpen&rdquo; technique. When you proofread, look for vague or overused words and replace them with something more precise. For example:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Simple:</strong> &ldquo;Cars are bad for the environment.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Ambitious:</strong> &ldquo;Cars are <em>detrimental</em> to the environment.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Simple:</strong> &ldquo;Public transport is good for people.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Ambitious:</strong> &ldquo;Public transport is <em>indispensable</em> for communities.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12">You only need a handful of well-placed ambitious words per paragraph. One precise word is worth more than three fancy ones used incorrectly. If you are not 100% sure what a word means, use a simpler alternative &mdash; accuracy always beats ambition.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n13">The mark scheme at Level 3 uses the phrase &ldquo;increasingly sophisticated use of vocabulary.&rdquo; <dfn class="term" data-def="Language that is precise, nuanced and shows a mature command of word choice. At Level 3+, AQA expects vocabulary chosen deliberately for effect.">Sophisticated vocabulary</dfn> means selecting words for their connotations, not just their literal meaning. Saying a bus service is &ldquo;unreliable&rdquo; is clear; saying it is &ldquo;woefully inadequate&rdquo; adds emotional weight and shows a deliberate choice of register. In a speech, this kind of word choice signals confidence and authority to the examiner.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Sentence Forms: Variety for Effect</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">AQA does not just want you to write different sentence lengths. The mark scheme specifically says &ldquo;a full range of appropriate sentence forms <strong>for effect</strong>.&rdquo; This means every sentence type you use should be doing a job. Here are the main sentence forms you should be deploying in transactional writing:</p>

<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Simple sentences</strong> &mdash; short, punchy, one clause. Use for emphasis or dramatic effect. &ldquo;This must change.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Compound sentences</strong> &mdash; two clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (and, but, or, so). Use for balanced arguments. &ldquo;Cars offer convenience, but they choke our streets.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Complex sentences</strong> &mdash; a main clause with one or more subordinate clauses. Use for developing ideas and showing cause and effect. &ldquo;Although public transport exists, its unreliability forces commuters back into their cars.&rdquo;</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The difference between Level 2 (&ldquo;attempts a variety of sentence forms&rdquo;) and Level 4 (&ldquo;uses a full range of appropriate sentence forms for effect&rdquo;) is <strong>control</strong>. At Level 4, you are not just using different sentences &mdash; you are choosing them deliberately. A short sentence after three complex sentences creates emphasis. A rhetorical question changes pace. The examiner needs to see that you are in charge of your writing.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Advanced Sentence Openers for Speeches</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n18">Varying how you start your sentences is a quick way to demonstrate range. Try these openers:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Adverbial opener:</strong> &ldquo;Undeniably, our reliance on cars has spiralled out of control.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Prepositional opener:</strong> &ldquo;In every town across this country, bus routes are being cut.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Participial opener:</strong> &ldquo;Standing at a bus stop in the rain for forty minutes, one begins to understand why people drive.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Conditional opener:</strong> &ldquo;If we invested even a fraction of road-building budgets into rail, the results would be transformative.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Fronted conjunction:</strong> &ldquo;And yet, nothing changes.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20">Notice how each opener creates a different rhythm and tone. The examiner is not counting sentence types &mdash; they are responding to the overall effect of your writing. Varied openers create a confident, controlled voice.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Punctuation: Beyond Full Stops and Commas</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">At Level 1, the examiner sees &ldquo;some evidence of conscious punctuation&rdquo; &mdash; basically, you use full stops and capital letters most of the time. To reach Level 4, you need a &ldquo;wide range of punctuation used with a high level of accuracy.&rdquo; Here is a checklist of punctuation marks you should be comfortable with:</p>

<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Full stops and capital letters</strong> &mdash; <dfn class="term" data-def="The correct use of full stops, capital letters and other marks to show where sentences begin and end. AQA calls this 'sentence demarcation.'">Sentence demarcation</dfn> must be &ldquo;consistently secure&rdquo; at Level 4. Every sentence must start with a capital letter and end with appropriate punctuation. This sounds basic, but errors here cap your mark severely.<br/><strong>Commas</strong> &mdash; Use after fronted adverbials (&ldquo;However, &hellip;&rdquo;), in lists, and to separate clauses. Avoid comma splicing (joining two full sentences with just a comma).<br/><strong>Apostrophes</strong> &mdash; For possession (the council&rsquo;s budget) and contraction (it&rsquo;s = it is). Never use an apostrophe for plurals.<br/><strong>Semicolons</strong> &mdash; Join two closely related sentences: &ldquo;Cars clog our roads; buses could free them.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Colons</strong> &mdash; Introduce a list, explanation or elaboration: &ldquo;The solution is simple: invest in public transport.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Dashes</strong> &mdash; For parenthetical information or dramatic emphasis: &ldquo;Every morning &mdash; rain or shine &mdash; thousands of commuters are stranded.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Exclamation marks and question marks</strong> &mdash; Use sparingly and purposefully, especially in speeches.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Common Punctuation Errors That Cost Marks</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Comma splicing:</strong> &ldquo;Cars pollute the air, we must act now.&rdquo; This is two sentences joined incorrectly with a comma. Fix it with a full stop, semicolon, or conjunction: &ldquo;Cars pollute the air; we must act now.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Missing apostrophes in possession:</strong> &ldquo;The governments policy&rdquo; should be &ldquo;The government&rsquo;s policy.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Overusing exclamation marks:</strong> In a speech, one or two are powerful. Five in a paragraph looks like you are shouting rather than arguing. The mark scheme rewards control, not volume.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Incorrect semicolon use:</strong> Both clauses must be able to stand alone as full sentences. &ldquo;Although it is raining; I will walk&rdquo; is wrong because &ldquo;Although it is raining&rdquo; is not a complete sentence.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Putting It All Together: A Before and After</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Look at how upgrading vocabulary, sentence forms and punctuation transforms a paragraph from Level 2 to Level 4 quality:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Level 2 version:</strong> &ldquo;Cars are really bad for the planet and they make lots of pollution. We should use buses more because they are better. The council should make buses cheaper so people use them more.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Level 4 version:</strong> &ldquo;Cars are, without question, one of the most significant contributors to environmental degradation in our towns and cities. Every morning, thousands of engines idle in congested streets &mdash; pumping carbon into the air our children breathe. The solution is not radical; it is remarkably straightforward: make public transport affordable, reliable, and accessible. If this council genuinely values the health of its community, it must act now.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32">Notice the differences. The Level 4 version uses ambitious vocabulary (&ldquo;degradation,&rdquo; &ldquo;congested&rdquo;), varied sentence forms (complex, compound, simple), and a range of punctuation (dashes, semicolons, colons). Every choice is deliberate and controlled.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n33">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Always leave 5 minutes at the end of the exam to proofread your writing. Check sentence demarcation first (capital letters and full stops), then look for comma splices, spelling errors in common words, and missing apostrophes. Even fixing two or three errors can move you up a level in AO6.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n34">Spelling: What the Examiner Expects</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n35">At Level 4, the mark scheme requires a &ldquo;high level of accuracy in spelling, including ambitious vocabulary.&rdquo; You do not need to spell every word perfectly, but you must spell common words correctly and show confidence with more complex ones. If you use a sophisticated word, spell it right. Misspelling &ldquo;environment&rdquo; as &ldquo;enviroment&rdquo; or &ldquo;government&rdquo; as &ldquo;goverment&rdquo; signals carelessness to the examiner.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36">A useful habit: if you are unsure how to spell a word, use a simpler synonym you <em>can</em> spell. &ldquo;Detrimental&rdquo; is impressive, but only if it is spelt correctly. &ldquo;Harmful&rdquo; is perfectly acceptable and avoids the risk. The mark scheme rewards accuracy, not just ambition.</p>

AQA requires Standard English in transactional writing.">Standard English</dfn> means avoiding non-standard grammar such as:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Subject-verb agreement errors:</strong> &ldquo;The council were wrong&rdquo; should be &ldquo;The council was wrong&rdquo; (in formal writing, treat collective nouns as singular).<br/><strong>Double negatives:</strong> &ldquo;They don&rsquo;t do nothing&rdquo; should be &ldquo;They do nothing&rdquo; or &ldquo;They don&rsquo;t do anything.&rdquo;<br/><strong>Informal contractions:</strong> In a formal speech, prefer &ldquo;cannot&rdquo; over &ldquo;can&rsquo;t&rdquo; and &ldquo;will not&rdquo; over &ldquo;won&rsquo;t&rdquo; most of the time. However, the occasional contraction can add natural rhythm to a speech.<br/><strong>Tense consistency:</strong> If you start a paragraph in the present tense, stay in the present tense.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n39">On Paper 2 Question 5, the 16 marks for technical accuracy are awarded <strong>separately</strong> from the 24 marks for content and organisation. This means you could write a compelling, well-structured argument and still only score 24/40 if your spelling, punctuation and grammar are weak. When you proofread, read your work <em>backwards</em> sentence by sentence &mdash; this forces you to focus on each sentence in isolation and spot demarcation errors, comma splices and spelling mistakes you would otherwise skim over. Aim to use at least three different punctuation marks beyond full stops and commas (semicolons, colons, dashes) to show range, but only use them if you are confident they are correct. One well-placed semicolon is worth more than five misused ones.</p>
