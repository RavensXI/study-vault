# Rhetorical Devices in Action

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Rhetorical Devices Matter in Paper 2</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Paper 2, Question 5 is worth 40 marks &mdash; half the marks for the entire paper. It asks you to write a <dfn class="term" data-def="Writing that has a clear purpose, audience, and form, such as a speech, article, letter, or essay. Paper 2 Question 5 always requires transactional writing.">transactional</dfn> piece such as a speech, article, letter, or essay. The AQA mark scheme rewards &ldquo;extensive and ambitious vocabulary with sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; at Level 4. In other words, examiners want to see you using rhetorical devices deliberately and effectively &mdash; not just scattering them randomly across the page.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Rhetorical devices are tools writers and speakers use to persuade, engage, or move their audience. Using them well can lift your writing from Level 2 (&ldquo;some use of rhetorical devices&rdquo;) to Level 4 (&ldquo;deliberate crafting of language and use of rhetorical devices&rdquo;). This lesson teaches you the most effective devices and &mdash; crucially &mdash; how to deploy them with purpose, not just for show.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme for Question 5 splits marks into two assessment objectives: AO5 (24 marks for content and organisation) and AO6 (16 marks for technical accuracy). Rhetorical devices contribute to AO5 because they show you are &ldquo;selecting and adapting tone, style and register for different forms, purposes and audiences.&rdquo;</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Essential Rhetorical Devices</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">You do not need dozens of devices. What matters is using a focused range with confidence. Below are the devices that examiners see used most effectively at the highest levels.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">1. The Rule of Three (Tricolon)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A list of three words, phrases, or clauses used for emphasis and rhythm. Also called a tricolon.">rule of three</dfn> groups ideas in threes for rhythm and emphasis. Three items feel complete and satisfying to a reader or listener. Look at the statement in the 2026 sample paper: &ldquo;Cars are convenient, comfortable and save time.&rdquo; That is a tricolon &mdash; three advantages listed for maximum impact.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9">You can use this device for building arguments: &ldquo;Public transport reduces pollution, cuts congestion, and saves families hundreds of pounds a year.&rdquo; Notice how the third item is the longest &mdash; this creates a climactic effect where each point builds on the last.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">2. Rhetorical Questions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A question asked for effect rather than to receive an answer. It encourages the audience to think and often implies the answer is obvious.">rhetorical question</dfn> is asked not to receive an answer but to make the audience think. It draws readers in by making them feel involved in the argument. For example: &ldquo;How much longer can we afford to ignore the crisis on our roads?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The best rhetorical questions feel natural, not forced. Place them at the start of a paragraph to hook the reader, or at the end to leave them reflecting. Avoid overuse &mdash; more than two or three in a piece makes your writing feel repetitive.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">3. Direct Address</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><dfn class="term" data-def="Speaking or writing directly to the audience using 'you' or 'we' to create a personal connection and make the argument feel relevant to them.">Direct address</dfn> uses &ldquo;you&rdquo; or &ldquo;we&rdquo; to speak straight to the audience. It creates a sense of shared responsibility and makes the reader feel personally involved. In a speech to a local council, for instance, you might write: &ldquo;We all know what it&rsquo;s like to wait forty minutes for a bus that never comes.&rdquo; This builds rapport and makes your argument feel relevant rather than abstract.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Using &ldquo;we&rdquo; is particularly powerful because it positions you alongside the audience, as if you are on the same team. This is called an <dfn class="term" data-def="Language that includes the audience in the speaker's position, such as 'we' and 'our', creating a sense of shared identity and purpose.">inclusive pronoun</dfn>.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">4. Repetition and Anaphora</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Deliberate repetition of a word or phrase hammers home your argument. <dfn class="term" data-def="The repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences, used to build rhythm and emphasis.">Anaphora</dfn> is a specific type of repetition where the same words begin consecutive sentences or clauses. Consider: &ldquo;We deserve better buses. We deserve safer cycle lanes. We deserve a transport system that actually works.&rdquo; The repeated &ldquo;We deserve&rdquo; creates rhythm, builds momentum, and reinforces the central demand.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">5. Emotive Language and Hyperbole</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><dfn class="term" data-def="Language deliberately chosen to provoke an emotional response in the reader, such as sympathy, anger, or guilt.">Emotive language</dfn> appeals to the audience&rsquo;s feelings rather than just their logic. Words like &ldquo;devastating,&rdquo; &ldquo;abandoned,&rdquo; or &ldquo;struggling&rdquo; trigger emotional responses. <dfn class="term" data-def="Deliberate exaggeration used for emphasis or dramatic effect, not meant to be taken literally.">Hyperbole</dfn> takes this further by deliberately exaggerating for effect: &ldquo;Millions of commuters are being held hostage by a transport system stuck in the last century.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Use emotive language sparingly. If every sentence is dripping with emotion, the effect wears off. Place your most powerful language at the start or end of a paragraph for maximum impact.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">6. Contrast and Antithesis</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><dfn class="term" data-def="Placing two opposite ideas side by side to highlight the difference between them and strengthen an argument.">Antithesis</dfn> places two contrasting ideas side by side to highlight the difference between them. This is particularly effective in argumentative writing where you want to expose a problem: &ldquo;While car manufacturers make billions in profit, bus passengers are left standing in the rain.&rdquo; The contrast between wealth and neglect makes the injustice feel vivid and undeniable.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>At Level 3 (13&ndash;18 marks for AO5), examiners expect &ldquo;effective use of rhetorical devices.&rdquo; At Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks), they want &ldquo;deliberate crafting of language and use of rhetorical devices.&rdquo; The difference is purpose: Level 3 students use devices correctly, but Level 4 students choose devices specifically to create a particular effect on the reader.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Putting Devices Together: Crafting a Paragraph</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">The secret to high marks is not using every device in every paragraph. Instead, layer two or three devices together within a single, well-structured paragraph. Here is an example response to the 2026 sample question about public transport:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">&ldquo;How much longer must we tolerate a system that punishes the poorest? <em>[rhetorical question + direct address]</em> Every morning, parents choose between feeding their children and paying for the bus to work. Every evening, elderly residents sit alone because they cannot afford the fare to visit their grandchildren. Every weekend, teenagers are trapped in their neighbourhoods with nowhere to go. <em>[anaphora &mdash; &lsquo;Every&rsquo; repeated three times, plus emotive language]</em> Cars may be convenient for those who can afford them, but for millions of people in this country, affordable public transport is not a luxury &mdash; it is a lifeline. <em>[antithesis + emotive language]</em>&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Notice how the devices work together: the rhetorical question draws the audience in, the anaphora builds momentum with specific examples, and the antithesis at the end delivers a punchy conclusion. This is what the mark scheme means by &ldquo;deliberate crafting.&rdquo;</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Overloading a single paragraph:</strong> Cramming five different devices into three sentences makes your writing feel forced and unnatural. Two or three devices per paragraph is plenty.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Using devices without substance:</strong> A rhetorical question with no argument behind it is empty. Every device should serve your argument, not decorate it. Ask yourself: &ldquo;What is this device doing for my reader?&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Forgetting audience and form:</strong> A speech to a local council needs a formal, respectful tone. Devices like direct address (&ldquo;you&rdquo;) and inclusive pronouns (&ldquo;we&rdquo;) are ideal. Aggressive hyperbole or sarcasm would be inappropriate for this audience.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n33">Matching Devices to Purpose and Audience</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n34">The 2026 sample question asks you to write a speech to a local council. This means your audience is formal and expects reasoned argument. Different audiences call for different devices:</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Newspaper article:</strong> Short, punchy sentences, contrast, a provocative opening, possibly a rhetorical question as a headline hook. The tone can be slightly less formal.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Letter to a headteacher:</strong> Respectful direct address, logical structure, emotive language balanced with reasoned points. Hyperbole should be used very carefully here.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n38">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme states that communication must be &ldquo;directly relevant to focus and task&rdquo; for Level 4. If you do not address the specific statement and audience given in the question, your AO5 mark is capped at the top of Level 2 (12 marks out of 24). Always check: who am I writing to, what form am I using, and what am I arguing?</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n39">In Question 5, examiners are looking for <strong>deliberate crafting</strong> &mdash; not a checklist of techniques. Do not try to cram every device you know into your response. Instead, choose three or four devices that suit your audience and form, and use them with purpose. A well-placed rhetorical question followed by a powerful tricolon will impress far more than ten devices used clumsily. Remember: the mark scheme rewards &ldquo;sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; at Level 4. The word &ldquo;sustained&rdquo; means consistent quality throughout, not one brilliant sentence surrounded by flat writing. Plan which devices you will use in your introduction, body paragraphs, and conclusion before you start writing.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n40">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Quality over quantity:</strong> Two or three well-deployed rhetorical devices per paragraph are far more effective than scattering every device you know across the page.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42"><strong>Purpose is everything:</strong> Every device should serve your argument. Ask yourself what effect each device has on the reader &mdash; this is what separates Level 3 (&ldquo;effective use&rdquo;) from Level 4 (&ldquo;deliberate crafting&rdquo;).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n43"><strong>Match devices to audience and form:</strong> A speech to a local council needs formal, inclusive language. Direct address, anaphora, and rhetorical questions are ideal. Always check the question to confirm who you are writing for and in what form.</li></ul>
