# Introduction to Rhetoric — Ethos, Logos, Pathos

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Rhetoric?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="The art of using language effectively to persuade, inform, or move an audience.">Rhetoric</dfn> is the art of persuasion through language. Every time you write to argue, persuade, or convince in your AQA Paper 2 Section B response, you are using rhetoric. The ancient Greeks studied rhetoric over 2,000 years ago, and the techniques they identified are still the foundation of every powerful speech, article, and letter written today.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">On Paper 2 Question 5, you will be asked to write a text &mdash; such as a speech, article, or letter &mdash; that argues a point of view. The AQA mark scheme rewards &ldquo;conscious crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; and &ldquo;extensive and ambitious vocabulary&rdquo; at the top levels. Understanding rhetoric gives you the tools to achieve this. It is not enough to simply state your opinion; you must deliberately craft your writing to influence your reader.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Paper 2 Question 5 is worth 40 marks &mdash; half of the entire paper. It is assessed on AO5 (content and organisation: 24 marks) and AO6 (technical accuracy: 16 marks). Effective use of rhetoric directly boosts your AO5 marks by demonstrating &ldquo;crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; and a tone that is &ldquo;matched to purpose and audience.&rdquo;</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Three Pillars: Ethos, Logos, Pathos</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The Greek philosopher Aristotle identified three modes of persuasion. He argued that every convincing argument uses a combination of these three appeals. Think of them as three tools in your writing toolkit &mdash; the strongest responses use all three together.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">Ethos &mdash; The Appeal to Credibility</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><dfn class="term" data-def="A rhetorical appeal that establishes the writer&rsquo;s credibility, authority, or trustworthiness to make the audience more likely to believe them.">Ethos</dfn> is about convincing your audience that you are trustworthy and worth listening to. If a reader does not trust the writer, even the best arguments will fail. Ethos answers the question: &ldquo;Why should I believe this person?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9">In your Paper 2 writing, you are unlikely to have genuine expertise on the topic. However, you can still build ethos. Here are practical ways to do it:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Adopt a confident, assured tone.</strong> Write as though you have authority. Avoid hedging phrases like &ldquo;I think maybe&rdquo; or &ldquo;I&rsquo;m not sure but.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Acknowledge the opposing view.</strong> Showing you understand both sides makes you appear fair and balanced, which builds trust. For example: &ldquo;Of course, some will argue that cars offer unmatched convenience &mdash; and they are right. But convenience alone cannot justify the damage we are doing.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Use formal, measured language.</strong> Match your register to your audience. If you are writing a speech to a local council (as in the AQA sample question), an appropriate level of formality signals that you belong in that setting.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Reference facts or authorities.</strong> Even invented statistics (&ldquo;Studies show that&hellip;&rdquo;) or appeals to respected figures can strengthen ethos in an exam context.</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Logos &mdash; The Appeal to Logic</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><dfn class="term" data-def="A rhetorical appeal that uses logic, reasoning, evidence, and facts to convince the audience that the argument is sound.">Logos</dfn> is the appeal to reason. It relies on evidence, facts, statistics, and logical arguments to convince the reader that your position makes sense. Logos answers the question: &ldquo;Does this argument hold up?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Strong logos in your writing might include:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Statistics</strong> (real or plausible): &ldquo;Transport for the North estimates that every &pound;1 invested in public transport generates &pound;4 in economic growth.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Cause-and-effect reasoning:</strong> &ldquo;If bus fares are halved, more commuters will switch from cars, reducing congestion and pollution simultaneously.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Counterargument and rebuttal:</strong> &ldquo;Critics claim that subsidising buses is too expensive. Yet the annual cost of road maintenance caused by heavy traffic far exceeds the proposed investment.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Examples and case studies:</strong> &ldquo;When Luxembourg made all public transport free in 2020, car usage dropped by 12% within a year.&rdquo;</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In the exam, you can invent statistics and examples &mdash; examiners are assessing your writing skill, not checking your facts. However, your invented evidence must be plausible and relevant. &ldquo;Studies show that 97% of people prefer buses&rdquo; sounds unconvincing. &ldquo;A recent study found that 60% of commuters would use public transport if services ran more frequently&rdquo; sounds credible.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Pathos &mdash; The Appeal to Emotion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><dfn class="term" data-def="A rhetorical appeal that targets the audience&rsquo;s emotions &mdash; such as sympathy, anger, fear, or hope &mdash; to persuade them.">Pathos</dfn> is the appeal to emotion. It makes the audience feel something: anger at injustice, sympathy for a victim, fear of consequences, or hope for a better future. Pathos answers the question: &ldquo;Why should I care?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">Pathos is often the most powerful of the three appeals because decisions are frequently driven by feeling, not logic. Techniques that create pathos include:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Emotive language:</strong> Choosing words with strong connotations. &ldquo;Children are choking on toxic fumes&rdquo; is more powerful than &ldquo;air quality is poor.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Anecdotes:</strong> Short, personal stories that put a human face on the issue. &ldquo;Imagine an elderly woman, standing alone at a rain-soaked bus stop, watching the third bus in a row sail past because it is already full.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Rhetorical questions:</strong> &ldquo;Is this the future we want for our children?&rdquo; forces the audience to engage emotionally.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Direct address:</strong> Using &ldquo;you&rdquo; and &ldquo;we&rdquo; pulls the audience into the argument and makes it personal.</li>
</ul>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n32">Using only one appeal creates weaknesses. A response that is all pathos (pure emotion with no evidence) feels manipulative and shallow. A response that is all logos (facts and statistics with no feeling) feels cold and fails to engage the reader. A response that is all ethos (just asserting authority) feels arrogant.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33">The AQA mark scheme at Level 4 (19&ndash;24 marks for AO5) rewards writing that is &ldquo;convincing and compelling&rdquo; with &ldquo;sustained crafting of linguistic devices.&rdquo; This means weaving all three appeals together throughout your response. Start a paragraph with a logical point (logos), support it with an emotional example (pathos), and maintain a confident, trustworthy voice throughout (ethos).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34">Think of it this way: <strong>logos</strong> convinces the head, <strong>pathos</strong> convinces the heart, and <strong>ethos</strong> makes the audience trust the person doing the convincing.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n35">Putting It Into Practice</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36">Let&rsquo;s look at how all three appeals might work together in a single paragraph, using the AQA 2026 sample question about public transport:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><em>&ldquo;I understand that budgets are tight &mdash; nobody in this room needs reminding of the difficult choices councils face every year</em> <strong>[ethos: acknowledging the audience&rsquo;s reality]</strong>. <em>But consider this: for every pound spent on improving bus routes, studies suggest the local economy gains four pounds in return</em> <strong>[logos: statistic and cause-and-effect reasoning]</strong>. <em>And behind every statistic is a real person &mdash; a teenager missing college because the bus didn&rsquo;t come, a nurse arriving late to a shift because there is no service after 9pm</em> <strong>[pathos: anecdotes creating sympathy]</strong>. <em>We can do better than this. We must.</em>&rdquo; <strong>[pathos: direct address and short, punchy sentences for emotional impact]</strong></p>

    <span>Common Mistakes to Avoid</span>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Mistake 1: Over-relying on pathos.</strong> Many students write responses that are entirely emotional &mdash; lots of exclamation marks, rhetorical questions in every paragraph, and dramatic language but no real argument. This caps your mark at Level 2 (&ldquo;some use of linguistic devices&rdquo;) because it lacks the variety and control needed for higher levels.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Mistake 2: Forgetting ethos entirely.</strong> Students often launch straight into their argument without establishing credibility. Taking a sentence or two to show you understand the complexity of the issue immediately lifts the quality of your response.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Mistake 3: Not matching tone to audience.</strong> The AQA mark scheme specifically assesses whether &ldquo;tone, style and register are matched to purpose and audience.&rdquo; A speech to a local council requires a different tone from an article for a school newspaper. Ethos demands you recognise who you are speaking to and adjust accordingly.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n41">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme does not use the words &ldquo;ethos,&rdquo; &ldquo;logos,&rdquo; or &ldquo;pathos.&rdquo; You do not need to label these techniques in your exam answer. However, understanding them helps you consciously deploy &ldquo;linguistic devices&rdquo; and create writing that is &ldquo;convincing and compelling&rdquo; &mdash; the exact language the mark scheme uses at Level 4.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n42">In Paper 2 Question 5, the examiner is looking for &ldquo;conscious crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; at the top levels. This means you should deliberately plan which rhetorical appeals you will use in each paragraph before you start writing. A useful planning strategy is to annotate your plan with &ldquo;E&rdquo; (ethos), &ldquo;L&rdquo; (logos), and &ldquo;P&rdquo; (pathos) next to each point, ensuring you use a balanced mix across your response. Remember: the command word in Question 5 will always require you to argue a point of view, so rhetoric is not optional &mdash; it is the core skill being tested.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n43">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Ethos</strong> builds trust by establishing credibility, acknowledging the audience, and using an appropriate register.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n45"><strong>Logos</strong> convinces through logic by using evidence, statistics, cause-and-effect reasoning, and counterarguments.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n46"><strong>Pathos</strong> moves the audience emotionally through emotive language, anecdotes, rhetorical questions, and direct address.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n47">The strongest Paper 2 responses weave all three appeals together &mdash; this is what the AQA mark scheme means by &ldquo;sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; at Level 4.</li>
</ul>
