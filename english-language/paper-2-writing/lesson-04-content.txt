# Writing Letters and Articles

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Does Paper 2 Question 5 Ask You to Do?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Paper 2, Section B is worth 40 marks &mdash; half the paper. You will be given a statement and asked to write a response in a specific <dfn class="term" data-def="The type of text you are asked to write, such as a letter, article, speech, or essay.">form</dfn>. The forms you could be asked to write in include a letter, an article, a speech, an essay, or a leaflet. This lesson focuses on the two most commonly tested forms: <strong>letters</strong> and <strong>articles</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The marks are split into two assessment objectives. AO5 awards up to 24 marks for <dfn class="term" data-def="How well your writing communicates ideas and is organised, including tone, style, register, paragraphs, and structural features.">content and organisation</dfn>. AO6 awards up to 16 marks for <dfn class="term" data-def="The accuracy of your spelling, punctuation, grammar, sentence structures, and vocabulary choices.">technical accuracy</dfn>. This means that what you say and how you structure it matters just as much as getting your spelling and punctuation right.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>AO5 (content and organisation) is worth 24 marks and AO6 (technical accuracy) is worth 16 marks. Together they make up 40 marks &mdash; exactly half of Paper 2. You should spend about 45 minutes on this question, including planning time.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Writing Letters: Format and Conventions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Letters are one of the most straightforward forms you could be asked to write. They require specific <dfn class="term" data-def="The expected features of a particular text type, such as addresses in letters or headlines in articles.">conventions</dfn> that signal to the examiner you understand the form. Getting these right is quick and easy &mdash; and failing to include them can cost you marks for matching form to purpose and audience.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n7">A formal letter (to a council, headteacher, newspaper editor, or MP) should include the following features:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Your address</strong> &mdash; top right of the page (you can invent this).</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Date</strong> &mdash; beneath your address.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Recipient&rsquo;s name or title</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;Dear Sir/Madam&rdquo; if you don&rsquo;t know the name, or &ldquo;Dear Mr/Mrs [Name]&rdquo; if you do.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Appropriate sign-off</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;Yours faithfully&rdquo; if you used &ldquo;Dear Sir/Madam&rdquo;; &ldquo;Yours sincerely&rdquo; if you used a name.</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n12">An informal letter (to a friend or family member) uses a more relaxed salutation (&ldquo;Dear [first name]&rdquo;) and sign-off (&ldquo;Best wishes&rdquo; or &ldquo;With love&rdquo;). However, even in an informal letter, your writing still needs to be well-organised and clearly argued. The examiner is not looking for slang or text-speak.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Formal letters</strong> use a serious, respectful tone. Avoid contractions (&ldquo;do not&rdquo; rather than &ldquo;don&rsquo;t&rdquo;). Use full titles and surnames. Structure your argument logically with discourse markers such as &ldquo;Furthermore,&rdquo; &ldquo;However,&rdquo; and &ldquo;In conclusion.&rdquo; The <dfn class="term" data-def="The level of formality in language, chosen to suit the audience and purpose of a text. Formal register avoids slang and uses complex vocabulary; informal register is more conversational.">register</dfn> should match the seriousness of the topic and the authority of your audience.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Informal letters</strong> allow a warmer, more personal tone. You can use first names, contractions, and a more conversational style. However, you must still write in full sentences, use paragraphs, and organise your ideas clearly. The examiner will still assess your technical accuracy, so do not mistake &ldquo;informal&rdquo; for &ldquo;careless.&rdquo;</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Writing Articles: Format and Conventions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Articles are written for a wider audience &mdash; typically for a newspaper, magazine, or website. They require a different set of conventions from letters. The key features of an article are:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n17"><strong>A headline</strong> &mdash; a short, attention-grabbing title. This could use alliteration, a pun, a question, or a bold statement. Keep it punchy.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n18"><strong>A subheading or strapline</strong> (optional but impressive) &mdash; a sentence beneath the headline that summarises the article&rsquo;s angle.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n19"><strong>An engaging opening paragraph</strong> &mdash; this should hook the reader immediately. A striking fact, a rhetorical question, or a bold opinion works well here.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Clear paragraphs</strong> &mdash; each developing a new point, linked by <dfn class="term" data-def="Words or phrases that connect ideas across sentences and paragraphs, guiding the reader through a text. Examples include 'however', 'furthermore', 'in contrast', and 'consequently'.">discourse markers</dfn>.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n21"><strong>A powerful conclusion</strong> &mdash; that leaves a lasting impression on the reader.</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Unlike a speech, you do <strong>not</strong> need to directly address the audience as &ldquo;ladies and gentlemen.&rdquo; However, you can use direct address (&ldquo;you&rdquo;) to draw the reader in and make your argument feel personal and relevant.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>For articles, always include a headline. The examiner needs to see that you understand the form. A missing headline is one of the most common mistakes students make &mdash; it signals that you have not matched your writing to the correct form, which limits your AO5 mark.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Matching Tone, Style, and Register</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">The AQA mark scheme at Level 3 and above requires your tone, style, and register to be &ldquo;consistently matched to purpose and audience.&rdquo; This means you need to think carefully about <strong>who</strong> you are writing for and <strong>why</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">If you are writing a letter to a local council (as in the 2026 sample paper), your register must be formal and respectful. You are writing to people in authority, so your tone should be measured and persuasive &mdash; not aggressive or rude. If you are writing a magazine article for teenagers, your register can be more relaxed and your tone more energetic.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The key principle is this: <strong>adapt your language to suit your reader</strong>. A letter to a headteacher sounds different from a letter to a friend. An article in The Guardian sounds different from an article in a student magazine. The examiner is looking for evidence that you have made deliberate, conscious choices about your language.</p>

At <strong>Level 3</strong> (13&ndash;18 marks), communication is &ldquo;consistently clear&rdquo; with tone, style, and register &ldquo;consistently matched.&rdquo; At <strong>Level 4</strong> (19&ndash;24 marks), communication is &ldquo;convincing and compelling&rdquo; with register &ldquo;assuredly matched.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29">The jump from Level 2 to Level 3 is where most students aim. To get there, you need to sustain your chosen register throughout the whole piece &mdash; not just in the opening paragraph. Consistency is key.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Structuring Your Response</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Whether you are writing a letter or an article, strong structure is essential. The mark scheme rewards &ldquo;varied and inventive use of structural features&rdquo; at the top level. Here is a practical structure that works for both forms:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Opening</strong> &mdash; State your position clearly. Hook the reader with a striking fact, anecdote, or rhetorical question. In a letter, this is your first paragraph after the salutation. In an article, this follows your headline.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Middle paragraphs (3&ndash;4)</strong> &mdash; Each paragraph should develop one main point. Use evidence, examples, and rhetorical devices. Link paragraphs with discourse markers to create <dfn class="term" data-def="The way ideas are linked and flow logically within and between paragraphs, creating a smooth and connected piece of writing.">cohesion</dfn>.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Counter-argument paragraph</strong> &mdash; Acknowledge the opposing viewpoint briefly, then dismiss it with a stronger point of your own. This shows sophisticated thinking and is rewarded at Level 3 and above.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Conclusion</strong> &mdash; Return to your main argument. End with a memorable final sentence &mdash; a call to action, a powerful image, or a short, punchy statement.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n36">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The mark scheme states that if a candidate &ldquo;does not directly address the focus of the task, their communication cannot be clear or convincing and therefore the mark for AO5 is capped at the top of Level 2 &mdash; 12 marks.&rdquo; Always re-read the question and make sure every paragraph links back to the statement you have been given.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n37">Language Techniques That Earn Marks</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n38">The mark scheme at Level 3 and above rewards &ldquo;increasingly sophisticated vocabulary and phrasing, chosen for effect with a range of successful linguistic devices.&rdquo; Here are the most effective techniques to use in letters and articles:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Rhetorical questions</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;How can we call ourselves a modern society when public transport fails millions every day?&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Tricolon (rule of three)</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;Public transport should be cheaper, more reliable, and more accessible.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Direct address</strong> &mdash; Using &ldquo;you&rdquo; and &ldquo;we&rdquo; to involve the reader and create a sense of shared responsibility.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n42"><strong>Statistics or facts</strong> &mdash; Even invented ones (&ldquo;According to recent studies&hellip;&rdquo;) can add authority if used convincingly.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n43"><strong>Emotive language</strong> &mdash; Carefully chosen words that appeal to the reader&rsquo;s emotions: &ldquo;abandoned,&rdquo; &ldquo;vulnerable,&rdquo; &ldquo;crisis.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Anecdotes</strong> &mdash; Brief personal stories that make abstract arguments feel real and relatable.</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n45">The key word in the mark scheme is <strong>&ldquo;crafting.&rdquo;</strong> At Level 4, the examiner wants to see &ldquo;sustained crafting of linguistic devices&rdquo; &mdash; not just one or two techniques scattered randomly, but deliberate, controlled use throughout your response.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n48">Technical Accuracy: The Other 16 Marks</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n49">AO6 is worth 16 marks and assesses your spelling, punctuation, grammar, sentence structures, and vocabulary. Many students lose marks here not because they cannot spell, but because they rush and do not check their work.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n50">At Level 3 (9&ndash;12 marks), the examiner expects &ldquo;mostly secure&rdquo; sentence demarcation, a &ldquo;range of punctuation used mostly with success,&rdquo; and &ldquo;a variety of sentence forms for effect.&rdquo; At Level 4 (13&ndash;16 marks), accuracy must be &ldquo;consistently secure&rdquo; with a &ldquo;wide range of punctuation used with a high level of accuracy.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n51">Practical tips for maximising your AO6 marks:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n52">Vary your sentence lengths deliberately &mdash; use short sentences for impact and longer sentences for developed explanations.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n53">Use semicolons, colons, dashes, and brackets as well as commas and full stops. A &ldquo;wide range of punctuation&rdquo; is rewarded.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n54">Leave 3&ndash;5 minutes at the end to proofread. Read your work aloud in your head &mdash; this helps you catch missing words and awkward sentences.</li>
</ul>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n55">In Paper 2 Question 5, the examiner will assess whether you have matched the correct <strong>form</strong> to the task. If you are asked to write a letter but produce an essay with no salutation or sign-off, your AO5 mark will be limited. Spend 30 seconds at the start adding the correct conventions (headline for articles, address and salutation for letters). Then focus on planning 4&ndash;5 strong paragraphs. Remember: the mark scheme caps AO5 at Level 2 (12 marks maximum) if you do not directly address the focus of the task &mdash; so keep re-reading the statement and make sure every paragraph links back to it. Use the command word &ldquo;argue&rdquo; or &ldquo;persuade&rdquo; to guide your approach.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n56">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n57">Letters need a salutation, formal or informal register matched to the audience, and an appropriate sign-off. Articles need a headline, an engaging opening, and clear paragraphs linked by discourse markers.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n58">Tone, style, and register must be consistently matched to your purpose and audience throughout your response &mdash; this is the difference between Level 2 and Level 3 on the AO5 mark scheme.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n59">Leave time to proofread. AO6 is worth 16 marks &mdash; varied punctuation, sentence forms, and accurate spelling can push your grade up significantly.</li>
</ul>
