# Language Analysis in Non-Fiction

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Language Analysis?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Language analysis means examining the specific words, phrases, and techniques a writer has chosen &mdash; and explaining <em>why</em> they chose them and <em>what effect</em> those choices have on the reader. On AQA Paper 2, this skill is tested directly in <strong>Question 3</strong>, which is worth <strong>12 marks</strong> and assesses <strong>AO2</strong>: &ldquo;Explain, comment on and analyse how writers use language and structure to achieve effects and influence readers, using relevant <dfn class="term" data-def="The technical vocabulary used to describe language features, such as metaphor, simile, alliteration, or hyperbole.">subject terminology</dfn> to support their views.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Question 3 will always direct you to a specific section of <strong>one</strong> of the two sources. It will ask something like: &ldquo;How does the writer use language to describe&hellip;?&rdquo; Your job is to pick out language features, quote precisely, and analyse what the writer&rsquo;s choices make the reader think, feel, or imagine.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 3 on Paper 2 is worth 12 marks. It focuses on <strong>language only</strong> (not structure). You should spend approximately 12&ndash;15 minutes on this question, analysing words, phrases, language features, language techniques, and sentence forms within the specified lines.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Difference Between Identifying and Analysing</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Many students lose marks because they <em>identify</em> a technique (&ldquo;The writer uses a metaphor&rdquo;) but do not <em>analyse</em> its effect. Identifying a technique on its own is only worth Level 1 (1&ndash;3 marks). To reach the higher levels, you must explain what the technique makes the reader think, feel, or picture &mdash; and ideally explore <em>why</em> the writer made that particular choice.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Think of it as a ladder with three rungs:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Identify</strong> &mdash; name the technique and quote it. (&ldquo;The writer uses the metaphor &lsquo;cannonaded down&rsquo;.&rdquo;)</li>
<li data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Explain</strong> &mdash; say what it suggests or what effect it creates. (&ldquo;This suggests the suitcase hit him with the force of a weapon being fired.&rdquo;)</li>
<li data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Analyse</strong> &mdash; explore deeper meanings, <dfn class="term" data-def="The associations or feelings that a word suggests beyond its literal dictionary meaning. For example, 'cannonaded' connotes warfare and deliberate attack.">connotations</dfn>, or the writer&rsquo;s purpose. (&ldquo;The military connotations of &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; convey the writer&rsquo;s sense of being victimised, as if the train deliberately turned his own possession into a weapon against him.&rdquo;)</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Key Language Features to Look For</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Non-fiction writers use all the same language tools as novelists and poets. Here are the features most commonly rewarded in AQA Paper 2 responses:</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Adjectives &amp; adverbs:</strong> Look at how descriptive words shape the reader&rsquo;s impression. In the 2026 sample Source A, Fleming uses adjectives like &ldquo;frightful,&rdquo; &ldquo;heaviest,&rdquo; and &ldquo;spectacular&rdquo; to shift from shock to almost admiring the crash scene. The adverb &ldquo;foolishly&rdquo; in &ldquo;sprawled foolishly&rdquo; makes the wrecked train seem clumsy and ridiculous rather than dangerous.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Verbs:</strong> Strong, specific verbs carry enormous weight. &ldquo;Cannonaded&rdquo; is far more powerful than &ldquo;fell&rdquo; &mdash; it transforms a falling suitcase into a military bombardment. &ldquo;Dug in&rdquo; makes the engine sound like a soldier in a trench, stubbornly refusing to move.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Nouns:</strong> Consider what the writer calls things. Kemble in Source B calls the steam engine a &ldquo;fire-horse,&rdquo; a &ldquo;she-dragon,&rdquo; and a &ldquo;snorting little animal.&rdquo; These noun choices reveal her wonder and her attempt to make sense of unfamiliar technology by comparing it to living creatures.</p>

    <span>Sentence-Level Features (Techniques &amp; Patterns)</span>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Metaphor &amp; <dfn class="term" data-def="A figure of speech that directly compares one thing to another by saying it IS something else, without using 'like' or 'as'. For example, calling a suitcase being flung down 'cannonaded' is a metaphor comparing it to a cannon attack.">personification</dfn>:</strong> Fleming personifies the engine as having a &ldquo;defiant and naughty look&rdquo; and being &ldquo;definitely conscious of indiscretion.&rdquo; By giving the train human qualities of guilt and defiance, he turns a mechanical failure into a comic character &mdash; as if the engine knows it has misbehaved.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Deliberate exaggeration used for emphasis or comic effect. The exaggeration creates dark humour &mdash; the reader knows the situation is serious, but the writer&rsquo;s overreaction is deliberately absurd.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Short sentences:</strong> &ldquo;It was six o&rsquo;clock. I began to dress. I felt very much annoyed.&rdquo; These blunt, factual sentences create a deadpan, understated tone. The writer has just survived a train crash, yet his main reaction is irritation &mdash; the contrast is humorous.</p>

The word &ldquo;crash&rdquo; is <dfn class="term" data-def="A word that imitates the natural sound of the thing it describes, such as 'crash', 'bang', or 'hiss'.">onomatopoeia</dfn>, making the reader hear the collision.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Listing &amp; repetition:</strong> Source A&rsquo;s opening repeats &ldquo;no more&rdquo; five times. This emphasises the writer&rsquo;s relief and reflects the monotony he felt on the journey.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">How to Write a Language Analysis Paragraph</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Use this structure for each paragraph. It is not a rigid formula, but it will help you include everything the examiner is looking for:</p>
<ol>
<li data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Quote</strong> &mdash; Select a short, specific quotation from within the given lines.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Identify the technique</strong> &mdash; Name the language feature using correct subject terminology.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Analyse the effect</strong> &mdash; Explain what this makes the reader think, feel, or picture. Zoom into individual words and explore their connotations.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Link to the writer&rsquo;s purpose</strong> &mdash; Why did the writer choose this? What impression are they trying to create?</li>
</ol>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n27">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme rewards <strong>quality over quantity</strong>. Three well-analysed quotations with detailed exploration of connotations will score higher than six quotations with surface-level comments. The examiner wants to see you &ldquo;zoom in&rdquo; on individual words.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Worked Example: Analysing Source A (Lines 12&ndash;23)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">The 2026 sample paper asks: <em>&ldquo;How does the writer use language to describe the train crash?&rdquo;</em> Here is an example paragraph written at Level 4 standard:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">&ldquo;Fleming personifies the derailed engine as having a &lsquo;defiant and naughty look&rsquo; and being &lsquo;definitely conscious of indiscretion.&rsquo; The adjective &lsquo;naughty&rsquo; reduces a massive, dangerous locomotive to a misbehaving child, which is absurdly comic given that the train has just crashed. The word &lsquo;indiscretion&rsquo; &mdash; typically used for a social faux pas, not a violent derailment &mdash; deliberately understates the severity of the event. This creates a sardonic, amused tone: Fleming treats the crash as entertainment rather than trauma, inviting the reader to find humour in what could have been a disaster.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Notice how this paragraph names the technique (<em>personification</em>), quotes precisely, zooms into individual word choices (&ldquo;naughty,&rdquo; &ldquo;indiscretion&rdquo;), explores connotations, and links back to the writer&rsquo;s overall purpose.</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Common Mistakes to Avoid</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Feature-spotting without analysis:</strong> Writing &ldquo;The writer uses alliteration in &lsquo;fearful force&rsquo;&rdquo; without explaining the effect is worth very little. Always answer the question &ldquo;So what?&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Quoting outside the given lines:</strong> The mark scheme states that if you write about language outside the specified lines, your response will be placed at the bottom of the level &mdash; even if it is good quality. Always check the line numbers.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Retelling the text:</strong> Narrating what happens is not analysis. The examiner knows what happens &mdash; they want to know <em>how</em> language creates effects.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Using vague phrases:</strong> Avoid &ldquo;this engages the reader&rdquo; or &ldquo;this makes the reader want to read on.&rdquo; Be specific about <em>what</em> the reader thinks or feels.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n36">Understanding the Level Descriptors</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n37">The AQA mark scheme for Question 3 uses four levels. Knowing what each level requires helps you aim for the top:</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Level Descriptors for Question 3 (12 marks)</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Level 4 (10&ndash;12 marks) &mdash; Detailed, perceptive:</strong> Analyses the effects of the writer&rsquo;s choices. Selects a range of judicious (well-chosen) textual detail. Makes sophisticated and accurate use of subject terminology.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Level 3 (7&ndash;9 marks) &mdash; Clear, relevant:</strong> Explains clearly the effects of the writer&rsquo;s choices. Selects a range of relevant textual detail. Makes clear and accurate use of subject terminology.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Level 2 (4&ndash;6 marks) &mdash; Some understanding:</strong> Attempts to comment on the effect of language. Selects some appropriate textual detail. Makes some use of subject terminology, mainly appropriately.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Level 1 (1&ndash;3 marks) &mdash; Simple, limited:</strong> Offers simple comment on the effect of language. Selects simple references. Makes simple use of subject terminology, not always appropriately.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n42">The key difference between Level 3 (&ldquo;explains clearly&rdquo;) and Level 4 (&ldquo;analyses&rdquo;) is depth. At Level 4, you explore connotations, consider alternative interpretations, and show how individual word choices connect to the writer&rsquo;s wider purpose or tone. You are not just explaining what something means &mdash; you are unpicking <em>how</em> and <em>why</em> it works.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n43">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>To move from Level 3 to Level 4, zoom into individual words within your quotation. Instead of analysing a whole phrase, pick out one key word and explore its connotations. For example, don&rsquo;t just analyse &ldquo;sprawled foolishly&rdquo; as personification &mdash; focus on &ldquo;foolishly&rdquo; alone and explain why that specific adverb makes the train seem ridiculous and harmless.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n44">Question 3 asks &ldquo;How does the writer use language to&hellip;?&rdquo; The command word is <strong>&ldquo;How&rdquo;</strong> &mdash; this means you must focus on the writer&rsquo;s methods and their effects, not simply describe what happens. Aim for three detailed analytical paragraphs rather than five or six short ones. Each paragraph should contain a short, precise quotation (even a single word is fine), the correct subject terminology, and a detailed explanation of the effect on the reader. Always check you are writing about the <strong>correct lines</strong> &mdash; quoting from outside the specified section will cap your mark. Finally, avoid &ldquo;the writer uses [technique] to hook the reader&rdquo; &mdash; be specific about what the reader thinks, feels, or imagines.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n45">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n46">Language analysis means identifying techniques, quoting precisely, and explaining the <strong>effect</strong> on the reader &mdash; always answer &ldquo;So what?&rdquo;</li>
<li data-narration-id="n47">To reach Level 4, zoom into individual words and explore their <strong>connotations</strong>, linking to the writer&rsquo;s overall purpose and tone.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n48">Quality beats quantity &mdash; three well-developed paragraphs will outscore six surface-level comments. Always stay within the specified line numbers.</li>
</ul>
