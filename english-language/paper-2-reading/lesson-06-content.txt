# Perspective and Comparison Skills

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Question 4 on Paper 2?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 4 is the highest-tariff reading question on AQA Paper 2. It is worth <strong>16 marks</strong> and assesses <dfn class="term" data-def="Assessment Objective 3: Compare writers' ideas and perspectives, as well as how these are conveyed, across two or more texts.">AO3</dfn> &mdash; your ability to compare writers&rsquo; ideas, perspectives, and the methods they use to convey them across two texts. You should spend approximately 20 minutes on this question.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The question always asks you to look at the <strong>whole</strong> of Source A and the <strong>whole</strong> of Source B. It will give you a focus &mdash; such as feelings about an experience, attitudes towards a place, or perspectives on a topic &mdash; and ask you to compare how the writers convey these. This means you must do two things at once: identify <em>what</em> each writer thinks or feels, and analyse <em>how</em> they communicate those thoughts and feelings through their writing methods.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 4 is the only reading question on Paper 2 that requires you to analyse both texts together. You must deal with both texts to reach Level 3 or above. If you only write about one text, the maximum mark you can achieve at Level 2 quality is 6 out of 16.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Understanding &ldquo;Perspective&rdquo;</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A writer&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="The particular attitude, opinion, or point of view a writer holds about a subject, shaped by their experiences, beliefs, and context.">perspective</dfn> is their point of view on a subject. It is shaped by who they are, when they are writing, and what they have experienced. Two writers can describe the same broad topic &mdash; train travel, for example &mdash; but hold completely different perspectives because of their different circumstances.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Consider the AQA sample sources. In Source A, Peter Fleming writes about the Trans-Siberian Railway in 1933. He has been on the train for over a week and is bored, frustrated, and relieved his journey is ending. His perspective is one of <strong>weariness and contempt</strong>. In Source B, Fanny Kemble writes about her first ride on a steam train in 1830. She is amazed, thrilled, and overwhelmed by the novelty. Her perspective is one of <strong>wonder and admiration</strong>. The same subject &mdash; train travel &mdash; produces opposite perspectives because of the writers&rsquo; very different experiences.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n8">Perspectives differ because of <strong>context</strong>. Fleming is writing in 1933 when long-distance train travel is routine and uncomfortable. Kemble is writing in 1830 when the steam engine has just been invented &mdash; trains are a technological marvel. Fleming has been trapped on the train for days; Kemble is experiencing her first ever ride. Fleming is writing a travel book for a general audience; Kemble is writing a private letter to a close friend, which makes her tone more personal and enthusiastic. Always consider <em>when</em> the text was written, <em>why</em> it was written, and <em>who</em> it was written for. These factors explain the differences you identify.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">The Skill of Comparison: Synthesis, Not Juxtaposition</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">The mark scheme makes a crucial distinction between <dfn class="term" data-def="Weaving together ideas from both texts in an integrated way, showing how they connect or contrast within the same point.">synthesis</dfn> and simple juxtaposition. At Level 1, students describe what one writer says, then describe what the other says, and leave the examiner to spot the link. This is <strong>juxtaposition</strong> &mdash; placing things side by side without connecting them. At Level 3 and above, students make <strong>like-for-like comparisons</strong> where both writers are discussed within the same point, with the contrast or similarity made explicit.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n11">Here is the difference in practice, using the sample texts:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Level 1 (juxtaposition):</strong> &ldquo;The writer of Source A doesn&rsquo;t like the train. He repeats &lsquo;no more&rsquo;. The writer of Source B likes the train. She says &lsquo;raptures&rsquo;.&rdquo; &mdash; These are two separate observations. The student has not explained how they connect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Level 3+ (synthesis):</strong> &ldquo;The writer of Source A feels that train travel is something to be endured, using repetition of &lsquo;no more&rsquo; to emphasise the many aspects he cannot wait to leave behind. In complete contrast, the writer of Source B is filled with admiration, using the noun &lsquo;raptures&rsquo; to suggest an almost spiritual level of joy, establishing from the outset how extraordinary her experience has been.&rdquo; &mdash; Both writers are compared within a single, focused point.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme uses four levels: Level 1 &mdash; Simple, limited (1&ndash;4 marks); Level 2 &mdash; Some, attempts (5&ndash;8 marks); Level 3 &mdash; Clear, relevant (9&ndash;12 marks); Level 4 &mdash; Perceptive, detailed (13&ndash;16 marks). To reach Level 3, you must deal with both texts and address the correct focus. To reach Level 4, your comparisons must be perceptive and your analysis of methods must be detailed.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Comparing Methods, Not Just Ideas</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">A common mistake is to focus only on <em>what</em> the writers think and feel without examining <em>how</em> they convey those thoughts and feelings. The question always prompts you to &ldquo;comment on the methods the writers use.&rdquo; Methods include:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Language features</strong> &mdash; personification, listing, repetition, word choice, metaphor, imagery</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Tone</strong> &mdash; sardonic, excited, contemptuous, admiring, explanatory, dramatic</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Structure</strong> &mdash; how the text is organised, shifts between action and reflection, what is placed at the beginning or end</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Form and audience</strong> &mdash; travel book vs. private letter, general audience vs. specific friend, use of direct address</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n21">For example, both Source A and Source B use <dfn class="term" data-def="A literary technique where human qualities are given to non-human things. Both Fleming and Kemble personify their trains, but to very different effects.">personification</dfn> of their trains. Fleming describes the train as a bully that &ldquo;had knocked us about&rdquo; and &ldquo;had been our prison,&rdquo; giving it a menacing, oppressive personality. Kemble describes her engine as a &ldquo;snorting little animal&rdquo; and a &ldquo;brave little she-dragon,&rdquo; giving it a charming, almost pet-like personality. Comparing how both writers use the same method for opposite effects is a powerful way to reach Level 4.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Structuring Your Answer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">You do not need to use a rigid formula, but a clear structure helps. A strong approach is to organise your answer by <strong>comparison points</strong>, not by text. Each paragraph should focus on one area of comparison and weave in references to both sources.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">A recommended structure for Question 4:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Paragraph 1:</strong> Compare the writers&rsquo; overall feelings or perspectives (e.g. boredom vs. excitement). Use a connective like &ldquo;In contrast&rdquo; or &ldquo;Conversely&rdquo; to link them. Analyse a method from each text.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Paragraph 2:</strong> Compare a specific method both writers use (e.g. personification, listing, tone). Show how the same technique creates different effects because of different perspectives.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Paragraph 3:</strong> Compare another method or a shift in perspective (e.g. how feelings change within each text, or differences in form/audience).</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Use comparative connectives throughout: &ldquo;In contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;Conversely,&rdquo; &ldquo;Similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;While Source A&hellip;, Source B&hellip;,&rdquo; &ldquo;Unlike Fleming, Kemble&hellip;&rdquo; These signal to the examiner that you are synthesising, not merely juxtaposing.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n33">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n34">The mark scheme highlights several pitfalls that keep students at lower levels:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Writing about one text only:</strong> At Level 2 quality, writing about one text caps your mark at 6. You <em>must</em> address both.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Ignoring the focus of the question:</strong> If the question asks about feelings and perspectives on train travel, do not write about something else. You must address the correct focus to reach Level 3.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Not analysing methods:</strong> Identifying what writers think without examining how they convey it limits you to Level 2. Always analyse language, tone, structure, or form.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Not comparing like-for-like:</strong> Making a point about listing in Source A and then an unrelated point about imagery in Source B is not effective comparison. Find shared ground &mdash; both use personification, both use first-person narrative, both shift tone &mdash; and compare how they use it differently.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n39">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>References to writers&rsquo; methods may be implicit &mdash; you do not have to name specific techniques by their technical term to get credit. However, using subject terminology accurately (e.g. &ldquo;personification,&rdquo; &ldquo;repetition,&rdquo; &ldquo;sardonic tone&rdquo;) demonstrates clear understanding and helps you access higher marks.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n40">Question 4 is worth 16 marks &mdash; the same as Questions 1, 2, and 3 combined. Spend around 20 minutes on it and write at least three developed paragraphs. The command word is &ldquo;Compare,&rdquo; which means every paragraph should mention both texts. Use comparative connectives (&ldquo;In contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;While,&rdquo; &ldquo;Conversely&rdquo;) to signal synthesis to the examiner. Always check you have addressed the specific focus of the question &mdash; not just &ldquo;what happens&rdquo; but the particular feelings or perspectives asked about. A strong approach is to find one method that both writers use (such as personification or listing) and compare how each writer uses it to convey their different perspective. This creates a &ldquo;like-for-like&rdquo; comparison, which is essential for Level 3 and above.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n41">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n42">Question 4 is worth 16 marks (AO3) and requires you to compare both texts &mdash; writing about only one text severely caps your mark.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n43">You must compare <em>what</em> each writer thinks or feels (their perspective) and <em>how</em> they convey it (their methods: language, tone, structure, form).</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n44">Organise by comparison points, not by text. Use comparative connectives to synthesise both sources within each paragraph, making like-for-like comparisons to reach Level 3 and above.</li>
</ul>
