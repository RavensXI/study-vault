# Paper 2 Reading Practice — Full Section A

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What This Lesson Covers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">This is your final lesson on Paper 2 Reading. Everything you have learned across the unit &mdash; identifying true statements, synthesising differences, analysing language, and comparing writers&rsquo; perspectives &mdash; comes together here. You will work through a complete Section A using the AQA 2026 sample assessment materials, with two real sources about train travel and the actual exam questions and mark schemes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The two sources are:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n4"><strong>Source A</strong> (20th century): Peter Fleming&rsquo;s <em>One&rsquo;s Company</em> (1933) &mdash; a travel writer describes the end of his journey on the <dfn class="term" data-def="A famous railway route running from Moscow to the Russian Far East, covering over 9,000 kilometres.">Trans-Siberian Railway</dfn>, including a dramatic train crash.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n5"><strong>Source B</strong> (19th century): Fanny Kemble&rsquo;s <em>Records of a Girlhood</em> (1878) &mdash; a letter describing her first ever ride on a steam train in 1830, accompanied by the inventor George Stephenson.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n6">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Paper 2 Section A is worth 40 marks and you should spend approximately 45 minutes on it &mdash; including 15 minutes of reading time at the start. There are four questions: Q1 (4 marks), Q2 (8 marks), Q3 (12 marks) and Q4 (16 marks). Budget your time according to marks.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">Question 1: True or False Statements (4 marks, AO1)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Question 1 asks you to read a specific section of Source A (lines 1&ndash;7) and choose four true statements from eight options. Each correct answer is worth 1 mark. This question tests whether you can <dfn class="term" data-def="Finding information that is directly stated in the text (explicit) or suggested but not directly stated (implicit).">identify and interpret explicit and implicit information</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Here is the actual question from the AQA 2026 sample paper:</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>A:</strong> The writer is at the start of his train journey. &mdash; <strong>FALSE</strong> (&ldquo;the journey was almost over&rdquo;)<br/><strong>B:</strong> The writer is looking forward to leaving the train. &mdash; <strong>TRUE</strong> (&ldquo;I felt very content&rdquo; about the end)<br/><strong>C:</strong> The writer will be on the train for three more days. &mdash; <strong>FALSE</strong> (&ldquo;After tomorrow&rdquo; = one more day)<br/><strong>D:</strong> The train has a carriage where meals are served. &mdash; <strong>TRUE</strong> (&ldquo;trips to the dining-car&rdquo;)<br/><strong>E:</strong> The writer thinks the bread has been delicious. &mdash; <strong>FALSE</strong> (&ldquo;rancid peat&rdquo; = disgusting)<br/><strong>F:</strong> The writer feels that he has been cut off from society while on the train. &mdash; <strong>TRUE</strong> (&ldquo;a monk&rsquo;s existence&rdquo;)<br/><strong>G:</strong> The writer has had many activities to keep him busy on the train. &mdash; <strong>FALSE</strong> (&ldquo;days entirely blank of action&rdquo;)<br/><strong>H:</strong> The writer does not have any plans for when he leaves the train. &mdash; <strong>TRUE</strong> (&ldquo;did not know what I was going to do&rdquo;)</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12">The four true statements are <strong>B, D, F and H</strong>. Notice that some require inference &mdash; &ldquo;a monk&rsquo;s existence&rdquo; is not the same wording as &ldquo;cut off from society,&rdquo; but you need to interpret its meaning.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Top tip for Q1:</strong> Read the specified lines carefully, then go through each statement one by one. Cross out any that are obviously wrong. Do not rush &mdash; 4 marks for about 5 minutes of work is excellent value.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Question 2: Synthesis &amp; Inference (8 marks, AO1)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Question 2 requires you to refer to <strong>both</strong> sources and <dfn class="term" data-def="Combining information from two different texts to draw comparisons or identify differences.">synthesise</dfn> information. The actual 2026 question asks: &ldquo;The writers in Source A and Source B are travelling on different types of trains. What can you infer about the differences between the two trains?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">This is not a language analysis question. You need to make <strong>inferences</strong> (work out what the text implies) and identify clear <strong>differences</strong> between the two trains. You must use evidence from both texts.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>To reach Level 3 or above on Q2, you MUST write about both texts and address the correct focus of the question. If you only write about one text, even at Level 2 quality, you are capped at 4 marks. Always structure your answer: Source A point &rarr; evidence &rarr; inference, then Source B point &rarr; evidence &rarr; inference &rarr; stated difference.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n18">The mark scheme identifies these areas of difference you could explore:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Size:</strong> Source A&rsquo;s train has multiple carriages; Source B&rsquo;s is a single engine and &ldquo;uncovered carriage.&rdquo;</li>
<li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Facilities:</strong> Source A has a &ldquo;mail van,&rdquo; &ldquo;dining-car&rdquo; and &ldquo;five sleeping cars&rdquo;; Source B has simple &ldquo;benches.&rdquo;</li>
<li data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Stage of development:</strong> Source A&rsquo;s train represents advanced rail travel; Source B&rsquo;s is the steam engine at its earliest stage of invention.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Purpose:</strong> Source A&rsquo;s train is for long-distance transport of people and mail; Source B&rsquo;s is a demonstration of new technology.</li>
</ul>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Level 2 (3&ndash;4 marks):</strong> &ldquo;The train in Source A is long and has several carriages including a &lsquo;mail van&rsquo;, &lsquo;a dining car&rsquo; and &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo; which shows that the train is used for many reasons. This is different from Source B, which has just a &lsquo;little engine&rsquo; and an &lsquo;uncovered carriage&rsquo; which shows it is very basic.&rdquo; &mdash; This describes features but the inferences are incomplete.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Level 4 (7&ndash;8 marks):</strong> &ldquo;The train in Source A reflects significant progress in train travel. It has a &lsquo;mail van&rsquo;, &lsquo;a dining car&rsquo; and &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo;, showing both the advancement of train manufacture and the purpose of rail travel to enable long journeys. This relative luxury is a complete contrast to Source B where the steam engine is an earlier model and therefore much simpler in its design &mdash; just a functional &lsquo;uncovered carriage&rsquo; with &lsquo;benches.&rsquo;&rdquo; &mdash; This makes perceptive inferences about what the differences tell us.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Question 3: Language Analysis (12 marks, AO2)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Question 3 focuses on Source A only, lines 12&ndash;23: &ldquo;How does the writer use language to describe the train crash?&rdquo; This is the biggest language analysis question on the paper. You must analyse <dfn class="term" data-def="The specific words, phrases, language features, techniques and sentence forms a writer selects to achieve particular effects on the reader.">writers&rsquo; choices of language</dfn> and their effects, using subject terminology accurately.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The key language features identified by the AQA mark scheme for this section include:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Hyperbole:</strong> &ldquo;This is the end of the world&rdquo; and &ldquo;broken both my legs&rdquo; &mdash; exaggerating the crash for comic and dramatic effect.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Metaphor:</strong> &ldquo;cannonaded down&rdquo; &mdash; turning the suitcase into a weapon, as if the crash were an act of warfare.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Personification:</strong> the engine &ldquo;snorting steam&rdquo; with a &ldquo;defiant and naughty look&rdquo; and being &ldquo;definitely conscious of indiscretion&rdquo; &mdash; treating the engine like a misbehaving child.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Adverbs:</strong> &ldquo;drunkenly,&rdquo; &ldquo;foolishly,&rdquo; &ldquo;primly&rdquo; &mdash; giving the carriages human behaviour and personality.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Short sentences:</strong> &ldquo;It was six o&rsquo;clock. I began to dress. I felt very much annoyed.&rdquo; &mdash; conveying the writer&rsquo;s matter-of-fact, understated reaction.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Alliteration:</strong> &ldquo;fearful force&rdquo; &mdash; emphasising the violence of the suitcase hitting him.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n34">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>To reach Level 4 on Q3 (10&ndash;12 marks), you must <strong>analyse</strong> the effects of language choices, not just identify them. The difference between Level 2 and Level 4 is depth. Level 2 says &ldquo;the writer uses hyperbole to show the crash was powerful.&rdquo; Level 4 says &ldquo;the military verb &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; conveys the writer&rsquo;s sense of being victimised, as if the train has turned his own possession into a weapon to be used against him.&rdquo;</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Q3 Paragraph Structure: What &rarr; How &rarr; Why</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n35">Use this structure for each paragraph of your Q3 response:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n36"><strong>WHAT:</strong> Identify the technique and embed the quotation. &ldquo;The writer uses the metaphor &lsquo;cannonaded down&rsquo; to describe the suitcase hitting him.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>HOW:</strong> Zoom in on a specific word and explain its connotations. &ldquo;The verb &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; has connotations of warfare and bombardment.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>WHY:</strong> Explain the effect on the reader or the writer&rsquo;s purpose. &ldquo;This suggests the writer feels he is under attack from his own luggage, creating a humorous tone as he exaggerates a minor injury into a battlefield experience.&rdquo;</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Question 4: Comparison of Perspectives (16 marks, AO3)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40">Question 4 is the highest-mark reading question. It asks you to use the <strong>whole of both sources</strong>: &ldquo;Compare how the writers convey their different feelings and perspectives about their experiences of travelling on a train.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n41">You must do three things: compare their <dfn class="term" data-def="The attitudes, opinions and viewpoints that writers express about a topic, shaped by their personal experiences and the time period they are writing in.">feelings and perspectives</dfn>, comment on the <strong>methods</strong> the writers use, and support your response with references to both texts.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n42">The mark scheme identifies these contrasting perspectives:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n43"><strong>Boredom vs intrigue:</strong> Fleming is weary after a week of monotonous travel; Kemble is fascinated by every detail of a new invention.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Contempt vs admiration:</strong> Fleming sees the train as a prison that &ldquo;bullied&rdquo; passengers; Kemble sees it as a &ldquo;wonderful beast&rdquo; and &ldquo;brave little she-dragon.&rdquo;</li>
<li data-narration-id="n45"><strong>Experience vs innocence:</strong> Fleming is a seasoned traveller, bored and cynical; Kemble is a 21-year-old on her first ever train ride, full of wonder.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n46"><strong>Familiarity vs novelty:</strong> Fleming cannot wait to escape the train; Kemble cannot contain her &ldquo;raptures.&rdquo;</li>
</ul>

<p data-narration-id="n47">Key methods to compare include:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n48"><strong>Repetition and listing:</strong> Fleming repeats &ldquo;no more&rdquo; to emphasise his relief at leaving; Kemble lists the engine&rsquo;s parts with wonder and excitement.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n49"><strong>Personification:</strong> Fleming personifies the train negatively (&ldquo;she had bullied us&rdquo;); Kemble personifies it positively as a living animal (&ldquo;snorting little animal&rdquo; she &ldquo;felt rather inclined to pat&rdquo;).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n50"><strong>Tone:</strong> Fleming is sardonic and mocking; Kemble is dramatic and excited.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n51"><strong>Audience:</strong> Fleming writes travel writing for a general readership with no direct address; Kemble writes a private letter to a friend, using &ldquo;you&rdquo; throughout.</li>
</ul>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Avoiding the Most Common Q4 Mistake</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n52">The biggest mistake on Q4 is writing about each source separately &mdash; one paragraph on Source A, then one on Source B &mdash; without making direct comparisons. The mark scheme calls this &ldquo;juxtaposition rather than synthesis&rdquo; and it limits you to Level 1 (1&ndash;4 marks).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n53">Instead, structure each paragraph around a <strong>point of comparison</strong>. For example: &ldquo;Both writers personify their trains, but Fleming does so to mock (&lsquo;sprawled foolishly&rsquo;) while Kemble does so to express affection (&lsquo;brave little she-dragon&rsquo;).&rdquo; This is a like-for-like comparison, which is what examiners reward at Level 3 and Level 4.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n58">Assessment Objectives Recap</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n59">Each question in Section A tests a specific <dfn class="term" data-def="The skills that AQA examiners are looking for in your answer. Each question targets a specific AO, and you should tailor your response accordingly.">assessment objective</dfn>:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n60"><strong>Q1 (AO1):</strong> Identify and interpret explicit and implicit information.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n61"><strong>Q2 (AO1):</strong> Select and synthesise evidence from different texts.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n62"><strong>Q3 (AO2):</strong> Explain, comment on and analyse how writers use language to achieve effects.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n63"><strong>Q4 (AO3):</strong> Compare writers&rsquo; ideas and perspectives, as well as how these are conveyed.</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n64">Knowing which AO is being tested helps you focus your answer. Q1 and Q2 are about <strong>what</strong> the texts say. Q3 is about <strong>how</strong> language works. Q4 is about comparing <strong>both what and how</strong> across both texts.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n65">In Paper 2 Section A, the most common reason students lose marks is poor time management &mdash; they spend too long on Q1 and Q2, then rush Q3 and Q4 which are worth the most marks. Stick to the timing plan: 5 minutes for Q1, 10 for Q2, 12 for Q3, and 18 for Q4. For Q3, remember the command word is &ldquo;How&rdquo; &mdash; this means analyse the effects of language, not describe what happens. For Q4, always use comparative connectives (&ldquo;In contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;Similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;Whereas&rdquo;) and write about methods as well as feelings. The mark scheme rewards &ldquo;like-for-like&rdquo; comparisons &mdash; if you discuss personification in Source A, compare it to personification (or another method) in Source B within the same paragraph.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n66">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n67">Paper 2 Section A has four questions testing AO1 (Q1 and Q2), AO2 (Q3), and AO3 (Q4). Always check which assessment objective is being tested so you know what skill to demonstrate.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n68">For Q2 and Q4, you <strong>must</strong> write about both texts to access Level 3 or above. Structure your answers around points of comparison, not separate paragraphs on each source.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n69">Quality matters more than quantity across all questions. One perceptive, well-developed comment can score higher than three surface-level points. Focus on depth of analysis, especially in Q3 and Q4.</li>
</ul>
