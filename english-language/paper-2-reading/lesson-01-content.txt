# True or False — Identifying Statements

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Question 1 on Paper 2?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 1 on AQA English Language Paper 2 is worth <strong>4 marks</strong> and should take you no more than <strong>5 minutes</strong>. You are given eight statements about Source A and must identify the four that are <strong>true</strong>. You shade the circle next to each true statement. That&rsquo;s it &mdash; no writing, no analysis, no quotations.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This question tests <dfn class="term" data-def="Assessment Objective 1: the ability to identify and interpret explicit and implicit information and ideas in a text.">AO1</dfn> &mdash; specifically your ability to <em>identify and interpret explicit and implicit information and ideas</em>. Some statements will test things stated directly in the text (<dfn class="term" data-def="Information that is clearly and directly stated in the text, leaving no room for doubt.">explicit</dfn> information), while others will test things you have to work out from clues in the text (<dfn class="term" data-def="Information that is suggested or hinted at rather than directly stated. The reader must infer the meaning from context.">implicit</dfn> information).</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 1 only refers to a short section of Source A &mdash; typically the first 5&ndash;10 lines. You do not need to read the whole text to answer it. The line references are given in the question.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">How the Question Works</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The question gives you eight statements labelled A to H. Exactly four are true and four are false. You must shade the circle next to each statement you believe is true. Here are the key rules:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Choose exactly four.</strong> If you shade more than four, the examiner marks only the first four you shaded &mdash; and if any of those are wrong, you lose that mark.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n8"><strong>If you make an error</strong>, cross out the whole box. If you then change your mind and want that statement back, draw a circle around the box.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Each correct answer = 1 mark.</strong> There are no half marks and no bonus for explaining your choice.</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Explicit vs. Implicit Statements</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">This is the most important distinction in Question 1. Some statements simply rephrase what the text says directly. Others require you to <dfn class="term" data-def="To reach a conclusion based on evidence and reasoning rather than from an explicit statement in the text.">infer</dfn> meaning &mdash; to read between the lines. Let&rsquo;s look at both types using the 2026 sample paper.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">The Full Answer: 2026 Sample Paper</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Here are all eight statements from the 2026 sample paper with their correct answers, based on Source A, lines 1&ndash;7:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The four TRUE statements are: <strong>B</strong> (the writer is looking forward to leaving the train), <strong>D</strong> (the train has a carriage where meals are served), <strong>F</strong> (the writer feels he has been cut off from society), and <strong>H</strong> (the writer does not have any plans for when he leaves the train). Statements A, C, E and G are all false.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n21">Let&rsquo;s look at why each false statement is wrong:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n22"><strong>A &mdash; &ldquo;The writer is at the start of his train journey.&rdquo;</strong> FALSE. The text opens with &ldquo;And now the journey was almost over&rdquo; &mdash; he is at the <em>end</em>, not the start.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n23"><strong>C &mdash; &ldquo;The writer will be on the train for three more days.&rdquo;</strong> FALSE. The text says &ldquo;After tomorrow&rdquo; &mdash; meaning one more day, not three.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n24"><strong>E &mdash; &ldquo;The writer thinks the bread has been delicious.&rdquo;</strong> FALSE. The bread is described as &ldquo;rancid peat&rdquo; &mdash; disgusting, not delicious.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n25"><strong>G &mdash; &ldquo;The writer has had many activities to keep him busy on the train.&rdquo;</strong> FALSE. The text says &ldquo;days entirely blank of action&rdquo; &mdash; no activities at all.</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Common Traps to Watch For</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">AQA designs the false statements to catch students who skim-read. Here are the four most common traps:</p>
<ol>
  <li data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Opposite meaning:</strong> A statement says the opposite of what the text says. For example, Statement A says &ldquo;start&rdquo; when the text says &ldquo;almost over.&rdquo;</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Wrong detail:</strong> A statement uses a correct topic but changes a specific detail. Statement C is about how long the journey will continue &mdash; the right topic &mdash; but says &ldquo;three more days&rdquo; instead of one.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Contradiction through tone:</strong> The statement uses a positive word where the text is negative (or vice versa). Statement E says &ldquo;delicious&rdquo; when the text clearly describes revolting food.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Misreading implication:</strong> A statement twists the implied meaning. Statement G sounds reasonable &mdash; long train journeys often have activities &mdash; but the text implies total boredom.</li>
</ol>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Step-by-Step Method</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Follow this process every time you answer Question 1:</p>
<ol>
  <li data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Step 1: Read the specified lines carefully.</strong> Do not skim. Read every word of the short section indicated in the question. In the 2026 sample, this is lines 1&ndash;7 of Source A only.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Step 2: Read all eight statements before shading anything.</strong> Get an overview first. Some statements are obviously true or false; others need more thought.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Step 3: Go statement by statement.</strong> For each one, find evidence in the text that either supports or contradicts it. If you can&rsquo;t find clear support, it is probably false.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Step 4: Check you have exactly four.</strong> Count your shaded circles before moving on. If you have three, you are leaving a free mark on the table. If you have five, one wrong answer will cancel out a right one.</li>
</ol>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n40">Question 1 is the easiest place to pick up marks on the whole paper &mdash; most students who lose marks here do so because they rush and shade five boxes instead of four, or because they do not read the lines carefully enough. Always go back to the text to check each statement. If a statement sounds &ldquo;nearly right&rdquo; but one detail is off, it is false. Remember: you are looking for statements that match the text, not statements that sound generally true about the world. Stick to exactly four shaded circles and you should get 4 out of 4 every time.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n41">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n42">Question 1 is worth 4 marks &mdash; shade exactly four true statements from the eight given. Each correct answer is 1 mark.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n43">Some statements test explicit information (directly stated) and others test implicit information (inferred from context). Read carefully for both.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n44">Watch for common traps: opposite meanings, wrong details, contradictory tone, and twisted implications. Always check each statement against the exact wording of the text.</li>
</ul>
