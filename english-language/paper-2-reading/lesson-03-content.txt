# Inference Writing — Extending Your Analysis

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Inference?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In AQA Paper 2, you will read two non-fiction texts and answer questions that test your ability to read beneath the surface. <dfn class="term" data-def="Reading between the lines to work out something that is suggested but not directly stated by the writer.">Inference</dfn> means working out what a writer is suggesting, even when they do not say it directly. It is the difference between what is on the page and what is meant by what is on the page.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Many students can spot basic inferences. The challenge &mdash; and where the higher marks live &mdash; is in <strong>extending</strong> your analysis. This lesson teaches you how to move from a simple, obvious inference to a perceptive, detailed one that reaches Level 3 and Level 4 on the AQA mark scheme.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>AQA&rsquo;s mark scheme uses four levels. Level 1 is &ldquo;simple, limited&rdquo; (paraphrase). Level 2 is &ldquo;some, attempts&rdquo; (incomplete ideas). Level 3 is &ldquo;clear, relevant&rdquo; (well explained). Level 4 is &ldquo;perceptive, detailed&rdquo; (insightful and exploratory). To reach the top levels, you must go beyond restating what the text says.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Inference Ladder: From Simple to Perceptive</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Think of inference as a ladder with three rungs. Each rung takes you closer to the top marks. Let&rsquo;s use Source A from the 2026 AQA sample paper &mdash; Peter Fleming&rsquo;s account of the Trans-Siberian Railway &mdash; to see this in action.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Rung 1 &mdash; Paraphrase (Level 1):</strong> You restate what the text says in your own words. For example: &ldquo;The train in Source A is big as it has lots of carriages because it says &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo;.&rdquo; This tells the examiner what you read, but not what you understood.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Rung 2 &mdash; Basic Inference (Level 2):</strong> You make a point that goes slightly beyond the surface. For example: &ldquo;The train in Source A has a &lsquo;mail van&rsquo;, &lsquo;a dining car&rsquo; and &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo;, which shows that the train is used for many reasons.&rdquo; You are starting to interpret, but the idea is incomplete.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Rung 3 &mdash; Perceptive Inference (Level 4):</strong> You explore what the detail reveals about a bigger idea. For example: &ldquo;The train in Source A reflects significant progress in train travel. It has a &lsquo;mail van&rsquo;, &lsquo;a dining car&rsquo; and &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo;, showing both the advancement of train manufacture and the purpose of rail travel to enable long journeys by providing eating and sleeping facilities.&rdquo; Here, you have connected the evidence to a wider point about the development of technology.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">The &ldquo;What &rarr; So &rarr; This Suggests&rdquo; Method</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">A reliable way to extend your inference is to follow three steps. This method works for Question 2 (the synthesis/inference question worth 8 marks) and is also useful when making points in Question 4 (comparison, 16 marks).</p>

<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Step 1 &mdash; WHAT does the text say?</strong> Select a short, relevant quotation or piece of textual detail. Keep it focused on the question&rsquo;s specific focus.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Step 2 &mdash; SO what does this tell us?</strong> Make an inference. What does this evidence suggest, imply, or reveal? Do not just reword the quotation. Ask yourself: what can I work out from this that the writer has not directly stated?</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Step 3 &mdash; THIS SUGGESTS something bigger.</strong> Push your inference further. What does it reveal about a broader idea &mdash; the writer&rsquo;s attitude, the context, the purpose of the text, or a wider theme? This is the step that separates Level 3 from Level 4.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme rewards quality over quantity. One or two perceptive inferences will score higher than five simple ones. Focus on depth, not on listing as many points as possible.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Applying the Method: Source B &mdash; Fanny Kemble</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Let&rsquo;s apply the &ldquo;What &rarr; So &rarr; This Suggests&rdquo; method to Source B from the 2026 sample paper. Fanny Kemble describes her first ride on a steam train in 1830.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>WHAT:</strong> Kemble describes the train as a &ldquo;snorting little animal, which I felt rather inclined to pat.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>SO:</strong> She compares the engine to a living creature, using <dfn class="term" data-def="Giving human or animal characteristics to a non-living object. Kemble personifies the train as a small, friendly animal.">personification</dfn> to present it as something tame and approachable rather than a powerful machine.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>THIS SUGGESTS:</strong> Because the steam engine was a brand-new invention in 1830, Kemble has no existing frame of reference for it. She can only understand and describe this unfamiliar technology by comparing it to something familiar &mdash; an animal. Her desire to &ldquo;pat&rdquo; it reveals not just curiosity but a childlike wonder; the train is so new to her experience that she relates to it with instinctive affection rather than fear. This reflects the novelty of early rail travel and the sense of amazement felt by those encountering it for the first time.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Common Mistakes That Limit Your Marks</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">Understanding what goes wrong is just as important as knowing what to do right. Here are the most common errors students make when attempting inference questions:</p>

<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>1. Paraphrasing instead of inferring.</strong> If your point could be understood by someone who had already read the text without any extra insight, you are paraphrasing. Paraphrase earns Level 1. Instead, ask: &ldquo;What does this <em>reveal</em> that is not obvious?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>2. Only writing about one text.</strong> The mark scheme is clear: if you only discuss one text in Question 2, you are capped at Level 2 (maximum 4 marks out of 8). Always address both sources.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>3. Ignoring the focus of the question.</strong> If the question asks about the differences between the <em>trains</em>, do not write about the writers&rsquo; feelings or the scenery. Every inference must link back to the question&rsquo;s specific focus. You cannot reach Level 3 or above without addressing the correct focus.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>4. Making unsupported claims.</strong> Every inference must be anchored to a specific piece of textual detail. If you claim something about the text, prove it with a quotation or reference.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n32">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The AQA mark scheme for Question 2 (2026) explicitly states: &ldquo;A candidate has to deal with both texts and address the correct focus of the question to achieve Level 3 or above.&rdquo; Breaking either of these rules caps your mark.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n33">Structuring a High-Level Inference Paragraph</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n34">When writing your answer to Question 2, aim for two or three well-developed paragraphs rather than five or six thin ones. Each paragraph should follow this pattern:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>1.</strong> Start with the focus &mdash; state the difference or similarity you are exploring, linked to the question.<br/><strong>2.</strong> Use evidence from Source A &mdash; embed a short, <dfn class="term" data-def="Selecting the most appropriate and precise piece of evidence from the text to support your point. The AQA mark scheme calls these 'judicious references' at Level 4.">judicious</dfn> quotation and make your inference.<br/><strong>3.</strong> Link to Source B &mdash; use a connective that shows contrast or comparison (e.g. &ldquo;In contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;This is completely different from&rdquo;) and provide evidence plus inference from Source B.<br/><strong>4.</strong> Push to the perceptive level &mdash; explain what both pieces of evidence together reveal about the broader topic (e.g. the stage of development, the purpose, the design).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Extending Analysis Beyond Question 2</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40">The skill of extending inference is not just for Question 2. In Question 4 (comparison, 16 marks), you need to infer writers&rsquo; <dfn class="term" data-def="A writer's attitudes, opinions, or viewpoints about a subject. In AQA Paper 2, you are asked to compare perspectives across two texts.">perspectives</dfn> before you can compare them. In Question 3 (language analysis, 12 marks), you need to infer the effect of language choices on the reader. In every reading question on Paper 2, the students who reach the highest levels are the ones who can move beyond the obvious and explore what the text implies.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n41">The &ldquo;What &rarr; So &rarr; This Suggests&rdquo; method works across all of these questions. The only difference is the focus: in Question 2 you infer <em>information and ideas</em>; in Question 3 you infer the <em>effect of language</em>; in Question 4 you infer <em>feelings and perspectives</em>. The process of digging deeper stays the same.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n42">In Question 2, you have roughly 10 minutes. Do not waste time writing a long introduction or conclusion. Go straight into your first point. Remember: the examiner is looking for the <strong>quality</strong> of your inferences, not the quantity. Two or three well-developed paragraphs that deal with both texts and address the question&rsquo;s focus will outscore five paragraphs of paraphrase. Always check: have I addressed both texts? Have I stayed on the question&rsquo;s focus? Have I gone beyond what the text literally says? If the answer to all three is yes, you are on track for Level 3 or above.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n43">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Inference means reading beyond the surface</strong> &mdash; paraphrasing what the text says will keep you at Level 1. You must explain what the text <em>suggests</em> or <em>reveals</em>.</li><li data-narration-id="n45"><strong>Use the &ldquo;What &rarr; So &rarr; This Suggests&rdquo; method</strong> to move from quotation to inference to perceptive analysis in a logical chain.</li><li data-narration-id="n46"><strong>Both texts, correct focus</strong> &mdash; for Question 2, you must address both sources and stick to the specific focus of the question to reach Level 3 or above.</li></ul>
