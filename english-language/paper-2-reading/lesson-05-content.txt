# Writing a Non-Fiction Language Analysis

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Does Question 3 Actually Ask You to Do?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 3 on AQA Paper 2 is worth 12 marks and tests <dfn class="term" data-def="Assessment Objective 2: Explain, comment on and analyse how writers use language and structure to achieve effects and influence readers, using relevant subject terminology.">AO2</dfn> &mdash; your ability to analyse how a writer uses language. The question will always direct you to a specific section of Source A or Source B and ask you how the writer uses language to describe, present, or convey something. For example, the 2026 sample paper asks: &ldquo;How does the writer use language to describe the train crash?&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This is a language-only question. You are analysing <strong>words, phrases, language features, language techniques, and sentence forms</strong>. You are not writing about structure here &mdash; that belongs elsewhere. You should spend roughly 12&ndash;15 minutes on this question.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 3 is marked using four levels. Level 4 (10&ndash;12 marks) requires you to <strong>analyse</strong> the effects of the writer&rsquo;s language choices, select <strong>judicious</strong> (well-chosen) textual detail, and use subject terminology in a <strong>sophisticated and accurate</strong> way. Level 1 (1&ndash;3 marks) only offers simple comments with simple references.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Difference Between Each Level</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The jump between levels is about the <strong>quality</strong> of your comments, not the quantity. Writing ten basic points will not score higher than three perceptive ones. Here is what the examiner is looking for at each level:</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Level 1 (Simple, limited &mdash; 1&ndash;3 marks):</strong> You identify a technique and quote the text, but your comment only says what happens rather than why the writer chose it. Example: &ldquo;The writer says &lsquo;cannonaded down&rsquo;. This is hyperbole because it is just a suitcase.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Level 2 (Some, attempts &mdash; 4&ndash;6 marks):</strong> You attempt to comment on <dfn class="term" data-def="The impact that a writer's language choice has on the reader's understanding, feelings, or imagination.">effect</dfn> but it is incomplete or doesn&rsquo;t go deep enough. Example: &ldquo;The writer uses the hyperbole &lsquo;cannonaded down&rsquo; to show how the force of the crash was powerful enough to move a heavy object.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Level 3 (Clear, relevant &mdash; 7&ndash;9 marks):</strong> You clearly explain the effect of the writer&rsquo;s choices. Your terminology is accurate and your textual detail is relevant. Example: &ldquo;The metaphor of &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; suggests that the impact turns the suitcase into a weapon being fired, creating the idea of the writer being under attack.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Level 4 (Detailed, perceptive &mdash; 10&ndash;12 marks):</strong> You analyse with insight, exploring layers of meaning. Your comments are developed and your terminology is sophisticated. Example: &ldquo;The military verb &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; conveys the writer&rsquo;s sense of being victimised as he describes the simple dislodging of his suitcase by physical force as a metaphorical attack by the train, turning his own possession into a weapon against him.&rdquo;</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n11">Notice how the Level 4 example does three things that lower levels do not: it explores the <strong>connotations</strong> of the word, it explains the <strong>effect on the reader</strong>, and it links back to the writer&rsquo;s <strong>purpose</strong>. This is what &ldquo;analysis&rdquo; means at GCSE level.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">A Step-by-Step Method: What &mdash; How &mdash; Why</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Use this three-part structure for each paragraph you write in your response. It keeps your analysis focused and stops you from simply identifying techniques without explaining them.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>WHAT:</strong> Identify the language feature or technique and embed a short, precise quotation. Do not copy out long chunks of text &mdash; select the most powerful word or phrase.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>HOW:</strong> Explain how the technique works. What does the word <dfn class="term" data-def="The associations or feelings that a word suggests beyond its literal dictionary meaning. For example, 'cannonaded' connotes warfare and deliberate attack.">connote</dfn>? What image does it create? How does the sentence form affect pace or tone?</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>WHY:</strong> Explore why the writer made this choice. What effect does it have on the reader? How does it shape our understanding of the subject being described?</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The mark scheme says that to reach Level 3 or above, you <strong>must address the correct focus of the question</strong>. If the question asks how language describes the train crash, every paragraph must link back to the train crash. Do not drift into general comments about the whole source.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">What Language Features Should You Look For?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">The mark scheme for the 2026 sample paper lists a wide range of features that could be analysed in Peter Fleming&rsquo;s description of the train crash (Source A, lines 12&ndash;23). These include:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Onomatopoeia</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;crash&rdquo; recreates the sound of the accident for the reader.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Metaphor</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;cannonaded&rdquo; and &ldquo;dug in&rdquo; use military imagery to convey violence and force.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Personification</strong> &mdash; the engine has a &ldquo;defiant and naughty look&rdquo; and is &ldquo;definitely conscious of indiscretion,&rdquo; making it seem like a misbehaving child rather than a dangerous machine.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Adverbs</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;foolishly,&rdquo; &ldquo;drunkenly,&rdquo; and &ldquo;primly&rdquo; give human qualities to the carriages, creating a comic tone.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Hyperbole</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;This is the end of the world&rdquo; and &ldquo;broken both my legs&rdquo; exaggerate the narrator&rsquo;s initial shock before the humorous tone takes over.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Short sentences</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;It was six o&rsquo;clock. I began to dress. I felt very much annoyed.&rdquo; These create a matter-of-fact, understated tone.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Juxtaposition</strong> &mdash; &ldquo;grotesque&rdquo; beside &ldquo;primly&rdquo; contrasts the chaos of the crash with the absurd dignity of the last carriage.</li>
</ul>

    <p data-narration-id="n27">Many students focus only on individual words and miss the marks available for analysing <dfn class="term" data-def="The way a sentence is constructed, including its length, type (declarative, interrogative, imperative, exclamatory), and the order of its clauses.">sentence forms</dfn>. In the train crash extract, Fleming uses a sequence of short, declarative sentences: &ldquo;It was six o&rsquo;clock. I began to dress. I felt very much annoyed.&rdquo; These clipped sentences create an understated, almost absurdly calm reaction to a major train derailment. The effect is comic &mdash; the narrator treats a catastrophic event as a mild inconvenience. Contrast this with the long, sprawling sentence that follows, describing the wreckage in detail. The shift from short to long mirrors the shift from the narrator&rsquo;s internal, controlled reaction to the chaotic external scene.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28">When writing about sentence forms, always link the form to the effect. Do not just say &ldquo;the writer uses short sentences for effect.&rdquo; Explain <strong>what</strong> effect: pace, tension, humour, emphasis, shock.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n33">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Feature-spotting without analysis:</strong> Writing &ldquo;The writer uses a metaphor&rdquo; without explaining what it means or why it is effective will keep you in Level 1. Always explain the effect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Writing about language outside the given lines:</strong> The mark scheme is clear: if you write about language from outside the specified lines, your response will be placed at the bottom of the level. Check the line numbers before you start writing.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Retelling the story:</strong> Question 3 is not asking what happens. It is asking <strong>how the writer uses language</strong>. Every sentence you write should be about the writer&rsquo;s choices, not a summary of the events.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Using vague effect phrases:</strong> Avoid empty phrases like &ldquo;this makes the reader want to read on&rdquo; or &ldquo;this creates a vivid image.&rdquo; Be specific about <strong>what</strong> image and <strong>what</strong> feeling the language creates.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n38">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Aim for 3&ndash;4 detailed analytical paragraphs rather than 6&ndash;7 shallow ones. The mark scheme rewards quality over quantity. One perceptive comment can reach Level 4; ten simple comments will stay in Level 1.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Planning Your Response in 2 Minutes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40">Before you write, spend two minutes reading the specified lines and underlining 3&ndash;4 language features that stand out. For each one, jot a word or two about its effect. Then write your paragraphs in order, spending roughly 3 minutes on each. This leaves time for a quick re-read at the end.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n41">You do <strong>not</strong> need an introduction or conclusion for this question. Start analysing immediately. Your first sentence should name a technique and quote from the text. Every second counts in a timed exam.</p>

However, using terminology alone is not enough. The mark scheme says terminology must be used to <strong>support your views</strong>, not just to label features. A student who writes &ldquo;the verb &lsquo;cannonaded&rsquo; conveys a sense of being under military attack&rdquo; will score higher than one who writes &ldquo;cannonaded is a metaphor and also onomatopoeia and also a verb.&rdquo;</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n44">In Question 3, examiners want to see you <strong>analyse</strong> rather than identify. The command word is &ldquo;How does the writer use language&hellip;&rdquo; &mdash; this means you must explain the <strong>effect</strong> of every technique you mention. Use the What&ndash;How&ndash;Why structure: name the technique, explore its connotations, and explain the effect on the reader. Aim for 3&ndash;4 well-developed paragraphs rather than many short ones. Always stay within the given line numbers, and never retell the story. If you can explore layers of meaning in a single word &mdash; for example, unpacking both the literal and metaphorical meaning of &ldquo;cannonaded&rdquo; &mdash; you are operating at Level 4.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n45">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n46">Question 3 is worth 12 marks and tests AO2 &mdash; your ability to analyse language choices, using relevant subject terminology to support your views.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n47">Use the What&ndash;How&ndash;Why method: identify the technique with a short quotation, explain how the language works (connotations, imagery, tone), and explore why the writer made this choice and its effect on the reader.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n48">Quality beats quantity &mdash; 3&ndash;4 perceptive paragraphs will outscore 6&ndash;7 shallow ones. Stay within the specified lines and never retell the story.</li>
</ul>
