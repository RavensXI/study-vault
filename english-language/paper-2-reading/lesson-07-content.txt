# Writing a Comparison Response

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Understanding Question 4 on Paper 2</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Question 4 is the biggest reading question on Paper 2. It is worth <strong>16 marks</strong> and tests AO3: your ability to compare writers&rsquo; ideas and perspectives, and the methods they use to convey them. You must write about <strong>both</strong> sources &mdash; if you only write about one, the maximum you can achieve is 6 marks out of 16.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The question will always ask you to <dfn class="term" data-def="To identify the similarities and differences between two texts, linking ideas together rather than writing about each text separately.">compare</dfn> something about the two sources. It might ask you to compare the writers&rsquo; feelings, attitudes, experiences, or perspectives on a shared topic. You will always be given bullet points to help guide your response.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>To reach Level 3 or above (9&ndash;16 marks), you <strong>must</strong> deal with both texts. Writing about only one text caps your mark at a maximum of 6 out of 16, regardless of quality.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">What the Examiner Is Looking For</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The mark scheme uses four levels. Understanding the difference between each level is the key to improving your grade. Here is how AQA describes each level:</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Four Levels Explained</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Level 4 (13&ndash;16 marks) &mdash; Perceptive, detailed:</strong> You <dfn class="term" data-def="To examine something in close detail, breaking it down into parts and explaining how each part works to create meaning.">analyse</dfn> how writers&rsquo; methods are used, select judicious (well-chosen) supporting detail from both texts, and show a detailed and perceptive understanding of the different ideas and perspectives.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Level 3 (9&ndash;12 marks) &mdash; Clear, relevant:</strong> You explain clearly how writers&rsquo; methods are used, select relevant detail from both texts, and show a clear understanding of the different ideas and perspectives. Comparisons are &ldquo;like-for-like.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Level 2 (5&ndash;8 marks) &mdash; Some, attempts:</strong> You make some comment on methods, select some appropriate detail (not always supporting), and show some understanding of differences. Comparisons may not be like-for-like.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Level 1 (1&ndash;4 marks) &mdash; Simple, limited:</strong> You make simple identification of methods, select simple references, and show simple awareness of ideas. You tend to <dfn class="term" data-def="Placing two things side by side without explaining the connection between them. In comparison writing, juxtaposition means writing about Source A then Source B separately, rather than weaving them together.">juxtapose</dfn> rather than compare &mdash; meaning you write about one text, then the other, without linking them.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n11">The crucial difference between Level 1/2 and Level 3/4 is the quality of your comparisons. At the lower levels, students tend to write a paragraph about Source A, then a separate paragraph about Source B. This is juxtaposition. At the higher levels, students weave both texts together, making direct, &ldquo;like-for-like&rdquo; comparisons within the same paragraph.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">How to Structure a Comparison Response</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">There is no single &ldquo;correct&rdquo; structure, but the most effective approach for Question 4 is to organise your response by <strong>comparison points</strong>, not by source. Each paragraph should discuss both texts, linked by a comparison.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n14">Here is a step-by-step approach:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Step 1: Identify 3&ndash;4 comparison points.</strong> Before you start writing, read the question carefully and think about the different feelings, perspectives, or attitudes the writers express. For example, if the question asks you to compare how writers convey their feelings about train travel, your comparison points might be: (1) excitement versus boredom, (2) admiration versus contempt for the train, (3) the tone each writer uses.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Step 2: Write one paragraph per comparison point.</strong> In each paragraph, discuss <strong>both</strong> sources. Start with one writer&rsquo;s idea, then use a comparison <dfn class="term" data-def="A word or phrase that links ideas together to show the relationship between them, such as contrast, similarity, or sequence. Examples include 'however', 'similarly', 'on the other hand', and 'in contrast'.">discourse marker</dfn> to move to the other writer&rsquo;s idea.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Step 3: Comment on methods.</strong> For each point, don&rsquo;t just say <em>what</em> the writers think &mdash; explain <em>how</em> they convey it. This means identifying language techniques, structural choices, or tone, and explaining their effect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Step 4: Use short, embedded quotations.</strong> Support every point with evidence from the texts. Keep quotations brief and weave them into your sentences.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n19">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The mark scheme states that &ldquo;references to the writers&rsquo; methods may be implicit without specific mention of the writer.&rdquo; This means you do not have to name every technique by its technical term &mdash; but you <strong>do</strong> need to show you understand <em>how</em> the writing works, not just <em>what</em> it says.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Comparison Connectives and Discourse Markers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">To write a fluent comparison, you need a strong toolkit of linking phrases. These help you move between texts smoothly and signal to the examiner that you are comparing, not just juxtaposing.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">For <strong>differences</strong>, use: &ldquo;In contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;Conversely,&rdquo; &ldquo;On the other hand,&rdquo; &ldquo;However,&rdquo; &ldquo;While Source A&hellip;, Source B&hellip;,&rdquo; &ldquo;Unlike the writer of Source A, Kemble&hellip;&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">For <strong>similarities</strong>, use: &ldquo;Similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;In the same way,&rdquo; &ldquo;Both writers&hellip;,&rdquo; &ldquo;Like Fleming, Kemble also&hellip;,&rdquo; &ldquo;This is echoed in Source B, where&hellip;&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24">For <strong>nuanced points</strong>, use: &ldquo;Although both writers&hellip;, the way they convey this differs because&hellip;,&rdquo; &ldquo;While there is a surface similarity in&hellip;, the underlying perspective is quite different.&rdquo;</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">A Worked Example Using the 2026 Sample Paper</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Let&rsquo;s look at the real Question 4 from the AQA 2026 sample paper. The question asks:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><em>&ldquo;Compare how the writers convey their different feelings and perspectives about their experiences of travelling on a train.&rdquo;</em></p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Source A is by Peter Fleming, who describes the end of a long, tedious journey on the Trans-Siberian Railway in 1933. Source B is a letter by Fanny Kemble about her first ride on a steam train in 1830. Here is how a Level 4 paragraph might look:</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n31">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Mistake 1: Writing about each source separately.</strong> This is the most common error. If you write three paragraphs about Source A, then three paragraphs about Source B, you are juxtaposing, not comparing. The examiner will place you in Level 1 or Level 2 at best. Always discuss both texts within the same paragraph.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Mistake 2: Comparing content without methods.</strong> Simply saying &ldquo;Fleming doesn&rsquo;t like the train but Kemble does&rdquo; tells the examiner <em>what</em> the writers think, but not <em>how</em> they convey it. You must comment on language, structure, tone, or form to access the higher levels.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Mistake 3: Not making like-for-like comparisons.</strong> A like-for-like comparison means you compare the same <em>aspect</em> of both texts. For example, comparing Fleming&rsquo;s use of personification with Kemble&rsquo;s use of personification is like-for-like. Comparing Fleming&rsquo;s feelings about bread with Kemble&rsquo;s description of a viaduct is not &mdash; these are unrelated aspects.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Mistake 4: Forgetting about perspective and context.</strong> The mark scheme rewards understanding of <em>why</em> the writers feel differently. Fleming is an experienced traveller nearing the end of a week-long journey &mdash; he is weary. Kemble is experiencing train travel for the very first time in 1830 &mdash; everything is new and astonishing. Acknowledging these contextual differences shows perceptive understanding.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Timing and Planning</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40">You have approximately 45 minutes for the entire reading section. Question 4 is worth the most marks (16 out of 40), so you should spend around <strong>20&ndash;22 minutes</strong> on it. Spend the first 3&ndash;4 minutes planning your comparison points before you start writing. A clear plan with 3&ndash;4 comparison points will keep your response focused and prevent you from drifting into a source-by-source structure.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n41">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A strong Question 4 response typically has 3&ndash;4 well-developed paragraphs, each covering one comparison point. Each paragraph should discuss both sources, use a comparison connective, identify a method, and embed quotations from both texts.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n42">For Question 4, the examiner wants to see you weaving both texts together &mdash; not writing about them separately. Start each paragraph with a comparison point (e.g. &ldquo;Both writers personify the train, but for very different purposes&rdquo;), then discuss Source A and Source B within that same paragraph. Always comment on <em>how</em> the writer conveys their feelings (methods), not just <em>what</em> they feel. The mark scheme specifically states that &ldquo;juxtaposition rather than synthesis/comparison&rdquo; places you in Level 1. Use connectives like &ldquo;In contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;Similarly,&rdquo; and &ldquo;Unlike&rdquo; to signal your comparisons clearly. Finally, remember that you must write about both texts to access Level 3 or above.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n43">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n44">Organise your response by <strong>comparison points</strong>, not by source &mdash; discuss both texts in every paragraph to avoid juxtaposition.</li><li data-narration-id="n45">Always comment on writers&rsquo; <strong>methods</strong> (language, structure, tone, form) as well as their ideas and perspectives &mdash; this is what separates Level 2 from Level 3 and above.</li><li data-narration-id="n46">Use comparison discourse markers (&ldquo;In contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;Similarly,&rdquo; &ldquo;Unlike&rdquo;) and embed short quotations from both texts to support every point you make.</li></ul>
