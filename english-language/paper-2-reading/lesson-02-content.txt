# Inference and Summary Skills

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Inference and Summary?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">On AQA Paper 2, Question 2 tests your ability to read two non-fiction sources and work out the differences between them. This question is worth 8 marks and assesses AO1: <dfn class="term" data-def="Assessment Objective 1 — identify and interpret explicit and implicit information and ideas, and select and synthesise evidence from different texts.">AO1</dfn>. You need two core skills: <dfn class="term" data-def="Reading beyond what is directly stated to work out what a writer is suggesting or implying.">inference</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="Drawing together key information from one or more texts to present a concise overview of similarities or differences.">synthesis</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Inference means reading between the lines. Instead of just repeating what the text says, you explain what the writer is suggesting. Summary means pulling together the key points from both sources to show clear differences. The examiner wants you to do both at the same time &mdash; that is what synthesis means on this paper.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Question 2 is a synthesis question. You must write about <strong>both</strong> sources and address the specific focus of the question. If you only write about one source, you cannot get above Level 2 (4 marks maximum). If you only write about one source at Level 1 quality, the maximum is 1 mark.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Explicit vs. Implicit Information</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Before you can make inferences, you need to understand the difference between <dfn class="term" data-def="Information that is directly and clearly stated in the text, leaving no room for interpretation.">explicit</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="Information that is suggested or hinted at by the writer rather than directly stated. The reader must interpret clues to understand the meaning.">implicit</dfn> information. Explicit information is stated directly. Implicit information is hinted at &mdash; you have to work it out from the clues the writer gives you.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">For example, look at this line from the AQA sample Source A by Peter Fleming: &ldquo;no more of a monk&rsquo;s existence.&rdquo; The explicit information is that the writer compares his train journey to being a monk. The implicit information &mdash; the inference &mdash; is that he has felt isolated and cut off from normal society during his long journey, living a restricted, almost religious life of deprivation.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Another example from Source B by Fanny Kemble: &ldquo;A normal sheet of writing paper is enough for love, but only a large sheet can contain my raptures about my railroad journey.&rdquo; Explicitly, she needs a bigger piece of paper. Implicitly, her excitement about the train is so overwhelming that it surpasses even romantic love &mdash; she is suggesting this experience is the most extraordinary thing she has ever felt.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n9">Ask yourself these questions when reading each source:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>What is the writer&rsquo;s attitude?</strong> Are they positive, negative, bored, excited? Look at their word choices for clues. Peter Fleming uses words like &ldquo;rancid,&rdquo; &ldquo;blank,&rdquo; and &ldquo;monk&rsquo;s existence&rdquo; &mdash; all suggesting negativity and boredom. Fanny Kemble uses &ldquo;raptures,&rdquo; &ldquo;wonderful,&rdquo; and &ldquo;delightful&rdquo; &mdash; all suggesting joy and amazement.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>What does this detail reveal?</strong> When Kemble describes the train as a &ldquo;little engine&rdquo; with just an &ldquo;uncovered carriage&rdquo; and &ldquo;benches,&rdquo; the inference is that train technology is at an early, basic stage. When Fleming mentions a &ldquo;dining-car&rdquo; and &ldquo;five sleeping cars,&rdquo; the inference is that trains have developed significantly and now offer comfort for long journeys.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">The Question 2 Format</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">On the AQA 2026 sample paper, Question 2 reads:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><em>&ldquo;You need to refer to Source A and Source B for this question. The writers in Source A and Source B are travelling on different types of trains. What can you infer about the differences between the two trains?&rdquo;</em> [8 marks]</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Notice the key instruction: &ldquo;What can you <strong>infer</strong>.&rdquo; This means the examiner does not want you to simply list what each text says. You must read between the lines and explain what the details suggest about broader ideas like the stage of development, purpose, or design of the trains.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">The Four Levels &mdash; What the Examiner Wants</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The mark scheme uses four levels. Understanding the difference between them is essential for knowing how to push your answer higher.</p>

<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 1</div>
    <h4>Simple, Limited (1&ndash;2 marks)</h4>
    <p>Paraphrases the texts rather than making inferences. Uses simple references. Shows only simple differences. Example: &ldquo;The train in Source A is big as it has lots of carriages because it says &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo;. This is different to the train in Source B, which is smaller.&rdquo;</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 2</div>
    <h4>Some Attempts (3&ndash;4 marks)</h4>
    <p>Attempts some inferences from one or both texts. Selects some appropriate references. Shows some differences. Example: &ldquo;The train in Source A has a &lsquo;mail van&rsquo;, a &lsquo;dining car&rsquo; and &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo; which shows that the train is used for many reasons. This is different from the train in Source B, which has just a &lsquo;little engine&rsquo; and an &lsquo;uncovered carriage&rsquo;.&rdquo;</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 3</div>
    <h4>Clear, Relevant (5&ndash;6 marks)</h4>
    <p>Makes clear inferences from both texts. Selects clear references. Shows clear differences with like-for-like comparison. The student explains what the details <strong>suggest</strong>.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Level 4</div>
    <h4>Perceptive, Detailed (7&ndash;8 marks)</h4>
    <p>Makes perceptive inferences from both texts. Selects judicious (well-chosen) references. Shows perceptive differences through developed synthesis.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n19">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The biggest jump is from Level 2 to Level 3. At Level 2, students describe what the text says. At Level 3, students explain what the details <strong>suggest</strong> or <strong>reveal</strong>. The secret is using inference words: &ldquo;this suggests,&rdquo; &ldquo;this implies,&rdquo; &ldquo;this reveals,&rdquo; &ldquo;this indicates.&rdquo;</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">How to Structure a Level 4 Answer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Use a <dfn class="term" data-def="A comparison that directly matches a point from one source with a related point from the other source, addressing the same aspect (e.g. both about size, both about facilities).">like-for-like comparison</dfn> structure. This means matching your points across the two sources so they address the same aspect of the question focus.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Follow this pattern for each paragraph:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Make a point about Source A, using a short embedded quotation.<br/><strong>Step 2:</strong> Infer what this suggests beyond the literal meaning.<br/><strong>Step 3:</strong> Link to Source B on the <strong>same</strong> aspect, using a short embedded quotation.<br/><strong>Step 4:</strong> Infer what this suggests, showing the difference clearly.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n24">Here is an example based on the AQA sample sources, written at Level 4 standard:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><em>&ldquo;The train in Source A reflects significant progress in train travel. It is being used to transport mail and people and so has a &lsquo;mail van&rsquo;, &lsquo;a dining car&rsquo; and &lsquo;five sleeping cars&rsquo;, showing both the advancement of train manufacture and the purpose of rail travel to enable long journeys by providing eating and sleeping facilities. This relative luxury is a complete contrast to Source B where the steam engine is an earlier model and is, therefore, much simpler in its design. In Source B, the train is just a functional &lsquo;uncovered carriage&rsquo; with &lsquo;benches&rsquo; as the purpose at this earlier stage of invention is to develop the mechanics of the engine, not provide more luxurious passenger facilities.&rdquo;</em></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26">Notice how this paragraph: (1) addresses both sources, (2) uses embedded quotations, (3) makes inferences about what the details suggest about the stage of development, and (4) makes a direct, like-for-like comparison on the same point &mdash; facilities and purpose.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Mistake 1: Only writing about one source.</strong> You must cover both. If you only write about one source, you are capped at Level 2 at best (maximum 4 marks), and at Level 1 quality it drops to just 1 mark.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Mistake 2: Paraphrasing instead of inferring.</strong> Saying &ldquo;the train has a dining car&rdquo; is paraphrase. Saying &ldquo;the dining car suggests the train is designed for extended journeys, providing passengers with eating facilities because they will be travelling for days&rdquo; is inference.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Mistake 3: Ignoring the focus of the question.</strong> If the question asks about differences between the trains, do not write about the writers&rsquo; feelings or the language techniques. Stay focused on the specific topic you are asked about. You must address the correct focus to reach Level 3 or above.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Mistake 4: Not making like-for-like comparisons.</strong> At Level 1, students just put two bits of information next to each other (<dfn class="term" data-def="Placing two things side by side without explaining the connection or difference between them. In the AQA mark scheme, juxtaposition rather than synthesis keeps answers at Level 1.">juxtaposition</dfn>). At Level 3 and above, students compare the same aspect &mdash; for example, comparing the facilities on both trains, or the size of both trains.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Size</strong> &mdash; Source A has multiple carriages; Source B has one small engine and one carriage.<br/><strong>Number of carriages</strong> &mdash; Source A has at least seven carriages; Source B has one.<br/><strong>Facilities</strong> &mdash; Source A has dining, sleeping and mail carriages; Source B has open benches.<br/><strong>Performance/ability</strong> &mdash; Source A is a fast express; Source B reaches thirty-five miles per hour at most.<br/><strong>Stage in development</strong> &mdash; Source A is an advanced, established technology; Source B is a brand-new invention.<br/><strong>Number of passengers</strong> &mdash; Source A carries many; Source B carries sixteen.<br/><strong>Purpose/design</strong> &mdash; Source A is designed for long-distance travel with comfort; Source B is a demonstration of new technology.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n34">Timing and Approach</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n35">You have approximately 45 minutes for the whole of Section A (Questions 1&ndash;4). Question 2 is worth 8 marks, so aim to spend around <strong>8&ndash;10 minutes</strong> on it. Write two or three well-developed paragraphs rather than five or six short ones. Quality matters more than quantity &mdash; the mark scheme says &ldquo;it is not the number of references, but the quality of the comments that will determine the level.&rdquo;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36">Before you start writing, spend one minute identifying two or three clear differences. Underline a short quotation from each source for each difference. Then write your paragraphs using the four-step structure described above.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n37">Question 2 is an inference and synthesis question &mdash; not a language analysis question. Do <strong>not</strong> comment on metaphors, similes, or other language techniques here. Save that for Question 3. Instead, focus on what the details in each source <strong>suggest</strong> about the topic. Use connectives that signal comparison: &ldquo;in contrast,&rdquo; &ldquo;on the other hand,&rdquo; &ldquo;unlike Source A.&rdquo; Always make like-for-like comparisons, addressing the same aspect from both sources in each paragraph. Remember: both sources, clear inferences, correct focus &mdash; those three things unlock Level 3 and above.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n38">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Inference means reading between the lines</strong> &mdash; explain what the details suggest, not just what they say. Use phrases like &ldquo;this implies,&rdquo; &ldquo;this suggests,&rdquo; &ldquo;this reveals.&rdquo;</li><li data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Synthesis means comparing both sources together</strong> &mdash; you must write about both sources and make like-for-like comparisons to reach Level 3 (5&ndash;6 marks) or above.</li><li data-narration-id="n41"><strong>Stay focused on the question</strong> &mdash; only write about the specific topic the question asks about (e.g. differences between the trains, not the writers&rsquo; feelings). Do not analyse language techniques &mdash; that is for Question 3.</li></ul>
