Customer Needs & Market Research

0:00 / 0:00

Understanding Customer Needs

A business can only succeed if it gives customers what they want. Customer needs are the things people look for when choosing a product or service. Understanding these needs is the starting point for every business decision.

The four main customer needs are:

  • Price — customers want value for money. Some prioritise the cheapest option; others will pay more if they see extra value.
  • Quality — the product must be fit for purpose. A cheap umbrella that breaks in the first storm does not meet the customer’s quality need.
  • Choice — customers want options. Different sizes, colours, flavours, or service levels let them find something that matches their preferences.
  • Convenience — how easy it is to find, buy, and use the product. Online ordering, home delivery, and long opening hours all improve convenience.
Key Fact

Identifying customer needs is critical for two reasons: it generates sales (customers buy products that meet their needs) and it ensures business survival (businesses that ignore customer needs lose customers to competitors). A business that does not understand its customers will fail.

What Is Market Research?

Market research is the process of gathering information about customers, competitors, and the market. It helps a business understand what people want, how much they are willing to pay, and what competitors are already offering.

Why Do Businesses Conduct Market Research?

  • To identify and understand customer needs — what do customers actually want? What features matter most?
  • To identify gaps in the market — is there a product or service that customers want but nobody is currently offering?
  • To reduce risk — launching a product without research is gambling. Research gives evidence that there is demand before investing money.
  • To inform business decisions — research helps with pricing, location, promotion, and product design decisions.

Methods of Market Research

Market research is divided into two main types: primary research (original data you collect yourself) and secondary research (data that already exists).

Primary Research (Field Research)

Primary research is data collected first-hand by the business for a specific purpose. Common methods include:

  • Surveys and questionnaires — a set of questions asked to a sample of potential customers, either online, by phone, or face-to-face. Cheap and can reach many people, but responses may be biased or dishonest.
  • Focus groups — a small group of people discussing a product or idea in depth. Gives detailed, qualitative feedback but is time-consuming and may not represent the wider market.
  • Observation — watching customer behaviour, such as counting footfall past a potential shop location or tracking which products customers pick up. Objective but doesn’t explain why customers behave that way.
Key Fact

Primary research is specific to the business and up to date, but it is expensive and time-consuming to collect. Secondary research is quick and cheap, but it may be outdated or not specific enough to the business’s needs.

Secondary Research (Desk Research)

Secondary research uses data that has already been collected by someone else. Sources include:

  • Internet research — competitor websites, online reviews, social media trends. Free and fast, but reliability varies.
  • Market reports — published by companies like Mintel or Statista, these give detailed analysis of specific industries. Reliable but expensive.
  • Government reports — the Office for National Statistics (ONS) publishes data on population, spending habits, and economic trends. Free and reliable, but very general.

Types of Data

Market research produces two types of data:

  • Quantitative data — numerical data that can be measured and analysed statistically. “72% of customers prefer online ordering” is quantitative. It is objective and easy to compare, but it doesn’t explain the reasons behind the numbers.
  • Qualitative data — descriptive data about opinions, feelings, and experiences. “Customers said the packaging felt cheap” is qualitative. It gives depth and insight, but it is harder to analyse and may be subjective.

The Role of Social Media in Market Research

Social media has transformed market research. Businesses can monitor customer opinions in real time by tracking comments, reviews, likes, and shares. Instagram polls, Twitter threads, and Facebook groups all provide free qualitative and quantitative data. The advantage is speed and cost; the risk is that social media users may not represent the full target market.

Reliability of Market Research Data

Not all data is equally useful. Reliability depends on several factors:

  • Sample size — asking 10 people is less reliable than asking 1,000
  • Bias — leading questions or unrepresentative samples distort results
  • Age of data — consumer trends change quickly; data from two years ago may be outdated
  • Source — government statistics are more reliable than a random blog post

Gymshark was founded by Ben Francis in 2012 from his parents’ garage. Instead of expensive market reports, Francis used social media as his primary research tool. He monitored fitness communities on Instagram and YouTube to understand what gym-goers wanted: functional, well-fitting workout clothes at a mid-range price point.

He sent free products to fitness influencers and tracked engagement — which designs got the most likes, comments, and shares. This real-time qualitative data shaped product development. By 2020, Gymshark was valued at over £1 billion. Francis used social media not just for promotion but as a continuous market research tool, constantly adapting products based on customer feedback.

Exam Tip

Questions about market research often ask you to evaluate a specific method. Always give an advantage AND a disadvantage, and link your answer to the type of business in the question. A multinational can afford Mintel reports; a start-up probably cannot. Context matters.

Conclusion

Understanding customer needs and conducting market research are the foundations of every successful business. Research reduces the risk of failure by providing evidence for decisions, whether that evidence comes from surveys, social media, or government data.

Key Takeaways

  • Four customer needs: price, quality, choice, and convenience. Successful businesses identify which matters most to their target market.
  • Primary vs secondary: primary research is specific but costly; secondary research is cheap but may not fit your exact needs.
  • Data reliability: always consider sample size, bias, age, and source when using market research data to make decisions.
