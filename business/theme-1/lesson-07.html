Break-Even Analysis

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

What Is Break-Even?

The break-even point is the exact level of output where a business’s total revenue equals its total costs. At this point, the business is not making a profit, but it is not making a loss either. Every unit sold above break-even generates profit; every unit below it means a loss.

Key Fact

Break-even level of output = fixed costs ÷ (selling price per unit − variable cost per unit). The bottom part of this formula — selling price minus variable cost — is called the contribution per unit. Each unit sold “contributes” this amount towards paying off fixed costs.

Calculating Break-Even

Tom sells custom phone cases online. His figures are:

  • Selling price: £15 per case
  • Variable cost: £5 per case (materials + printing)
  • Fixed costs: £2,000 per month (website, equipment lease, insurance)

Step 1: Calculate contribution per unit = £15 − £5 = £10

Step 2: Break-even = £2,000 ÷ £10 = 200 cases per month

Tom must sell at least 200 cases each month to cover all his costs. If he sells 250 cases, his profit is 50 × £10 = £500 (the 50 extra units above break-even, each contributing £10).

The Break-Even Chart

A break-even chart is a graph that shows three lines plotted against the number of units produced (output):

  • Fixed costs line — a horizontal line because fixed costs do not change with output
  • Total costs line — starts at the level of fixed costs (when output is zero) and slopes upward as variable costs increase with each unit produced
  • Total revenue line — starts at zero (no sales = no revenue) and slopes upward as more units are sold

The break-even point is where the total revenue line crosses the total costs line. To the left of this point, total costs are higher than revenue (the business makes a loss). To the right, revenue exceeds costs (the business makes a profit).

Key Fact

To read a break-even chart: find where the total revenue and total costs lines intersect. Drop a vertical line down to the x-axis to read the break-even output. The vertical gap between the two lines at any other output level shows the profit or loss at that level of sales.

Margin of Safety

The margin of safety is the gap between the business’s actual (or expected) sales and its break-even point. It tells you how much sales could drop before the business starts making a loss.

Key Fact

Margin of safety = actual (or expected) output − break-even output. Using Tom’s example: if he expects to sell 300 cases and his break-even is 200, his margin of safety is 100 cases. Sales could fall by 100 units before he starts losing money. A large margin of safety means the business is in a secure position; a small one means even a slight drop in sales could cause a loss.

Impact of Changes on Break-Even

Changes in revenue or costs shift the break-even point:

If the Selling Price Increases

Contribution per unit rises, so fewer units need to be sold to cover fixed costs. The break-even point falls and the margin of safety increases. On the chart, the total revenue line becomes steeper.

If Variable Costs Increase

Contribution per unit falls, so more units must be sold to cover fixed costs. The break-even point rises and the margin of safety decreases. On the chart, the total costs line becomes steeper.

If Fixed Costs Increase

There are more costs to cover before profit begins. The break-even point rises. On the chart, both the fixed costs line and the total costs line shift upward.

Tom considers raising his price from £15 to £18 per case. Variable costs stay at £5 and fixed costs stay at £2,000.

New contribution per unit = £18 − £5 = £13

New break-even = £2,000 ÷ £13 = 154 cases (rounded up)

The break-even point drops from 200 to 154 cases. If Tom still expects to sell 300 cases, his margin of safety increases from 100 to 146 — a much more secure position. However, the higher price might reduce demand, so Tom needs to consider whether customers will still buy at £18.

Why Break-Even Analysis Is Useful

  • Planning — helps entrepreneurs set realistic sales targets before launching
  • Securing finance — banks and investors want to see break-even analysis in a business plan
  • Decision-making — shows the impact of changing prices, costs, or output levels
  • Risk assessment — the margin of safety shows how vulnerable the business is to a drop in sales

Limitations of Break-Even Analysis

Break-even analysis is a useful tool, but it has weaknesses:

  • Assumes all output is sold — in reality, some stock may remain unsold
  • Assumes costs are linear — bulk-buying discounts or overtime pay can change costs at different output levels
  • Assumes a single selling price — businesses often sell at different prices (discounts, sales, different products)
  • Static model — costs and prices change over time, so the chart quickly becomes outdated
Exam Tip

Break-even calculations appear on almost every exam paper. Learn the formula by heart: fixed costs ÷ contribution per unit. Always show your working in three steps: (1) write the formula, (2) substitute the numbers, (3) calculate. If asked to “analyse the impact” of a change, calculate the new break-even point and compare it to the original — state whether it has risen or fallen and what this means for the business.

Conclusion

Break-even analysis helps businesses understand the minimum level of sales needed to avoid a loss. It is a vital planning tool, but it relies on assumptions that may not hold true in practice.

Key Takeaways

  • The formula: break-even = fixed costs ÷ (selling price − variable cost per unit). Learn it.
  • Margin of safety shows how far sales can drop before losses begin — the bigger the better.
  • Changes in price or costs shift the break-even point. Higher prices or lower costs reduce it; higher costs or lower prices increase it.
