Revenue, Costs & Profit

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Revenue

Revenue (also called turnover or sales revenue) is the total amount of money a business receives from selling its products or services. It is the starting point for understanding whether a business is financially healthy.

Key Fact

Revenue = price per unit × quantity sold. If a bakery sells 200 loaves at £2.50 each, its revenue is 200 × £2.50 = £500. Revenue is NOT profit — it does not account for costs.

A business can increase revenue by either raising its prices or selling more units. However, raising prices may cause some customers to buy less, so the effect on revenue depends on how sensitive customers are to price changes.

Costs

Every business has costs — the money spent to produce and sell its products. Costs are divided into two categories:

Fixed Costs

Fixed costs do not change with the level of output. Whether a café serves 10 customers or 100, its rent stays the same. Common fixed costs include:

  • Rent — the cost of business premises
  • Insurance — protection against risks
  • Salaries — fixed pay for permanent staff
  • Loan repayments — regular payments on borrowed money

Variable Costs

Variable costs change in direct proportion to the amount produced. The more a business produces, the higher its variable costs. Common variable costs include:

  • Raw materials — ingredients, components, fabric
  • Packaging — boxes, bags, labels
  • Hourly wages — pay for temporary or shift workers
  • Delivery costs — postage and distribution
Key Fact

Total costs = fixed costs + variable costs. If a T-shirt business has fixed costs of £2,000 per month (rent, insurance) and variable costs of £5 per shirt, then producing 500 shirts gives total costs of £2,000 + (500 × £5) = £4,500.

Profit and Loss

Profit is what remains after all costs have been deducted from revenue. It is the ultimate measure of a business’s financial success.

Key Fact

Profit (or loss) = total revenue − total costs. Using the T-shirt example: if 500 shirts sell at £15 each, revenue is £7,500. Total costs are £4,500. Profit is £7,500 − £4,500 = £3,000. If total costs exceeded revenue, the business would make a loss.

Profit matters to different stakeholders for different reasons:

  • Owners — profit is their financial reward for taking the risk of running the business
  • Employees — a profitable business can afford pay rises and is less likely to make redundancies
  • Government — profitable businesses pay corporation tax and create jobs that reduce welfare spending
  • Investors — profit can be reinvested to grow the business or paid out as dividends

Interest

Interest is the cost of borrowing money. When a business takes out a loan, it must pay back more than it borrowed — the extra amount is the interest. Interest is calculated as a percentage of the loan.

Key Fact

Interest = loan amount × interest rate (%) ÷ 100. A £10,000 loan at 5% annual interest costs £500 per year in interest. Interest is a fixed cost — it must be paid regardless of how much the business sells. High interest rates increase costs and reduce profit.

Putting It All Together

Amy runs a small candle-making business from home. Here are her monthly figures:

  • Selling price: £12 per candle
  • Units sold: 150 candles per month
  • Variable cost per candle: £3.50 (wax, wicks, fragrance, packaging)
  • Fixed costs: £400 per month (website, insurance, market stall fee)
  • Loan interest: £50 per month

Revenue = 150 × £12 = £1,800

Variable costs = 150 × £3.50 = £525

Total costs = £525 + £400 + £50 = £975

Profit = £1,800 − £975 = £825 per month

If Amy sold only 80 candles: revenue = £960, variable costs = £280, total costs = £730, profit = £230. Fewer sales mean lower profit because revenue drops while fixed costs stay the same.

Ways to Improve Profit

A business can increase profit in two ways: increase revenue or reduce costs.

  • Increase revenue: raise prices (risk losing customers), sell more units (through better marketing or new products), enter new markets
  • Reduce costs: negotiate cheaper suppliers, cut waste, move to cheaper premises, use technology to reduce labour costs

In practice, most businesses try to do both. However, cutting costs too aggressively can reduce quality, which may drive customers away and reduce revenue — so there is always a balance.

Exam Tip

Calculation questions are common on this topic. Always show your working — even if your final answer is wrong, you can pick up marks for a correct method. Write the formula first, then substitute the numbers, then calculate. And always include the £ sign in your answer.

Conclusion

Revenue, costs, and profit are the financial building blocks of every business. Understanding how they relate to each other allows entrepreneurs to make better decisions about pricing, production levels, and cost control.

Key Takeaways

  • Three key formulas: Revenue = price × quantity; Total costs = fixed + variable; Profit = revenue − total costs.
  • Fixed vs variable: fixed costs stay the same regardless of output; variable costs change with the amount produced.
  • Interest is a cost: borrowing money increases fixed costs, which reduces profit. Higher interest rates make loans more expensive.
