Stakeholders & Technology

What Is a Stakeholder?

A stakeholder is anyone who has an interest in a business and is affected by its decisions. Stakeholders include people inside the business, such as owners and employees, and people outside it, such as customers, suppliers, and the local community.

Every business has multiple stakeholders. Understanding who they are and what they want is important because their support — or opposition — can directly affect whether the business succeeds.

Types of Stakeholders

Stakeholders fall into two broad groups:

  • Internal stakeholders — people within the business, such as owners, employees, and shareholders
  • External stakeholders — people outside the business who are still affected by it, such as customers, suppliers, the local community, and the government

What Each Stakeholder Wants

Owners

Profit & Growth

Owners want the business to make a profit so they earn a return on the money and time they have invested. They also want the business to grow in value over time.

Employees

Fair Pay & Job Security

Employees want fair wages, good working conditions, job security, and opportunities for promotion. A well-treated workforce is more motivated and productive.

Customers

Quality & Value

Customers want high-quality products at fair prices, reliable service, and good after-sales support. Satisfied customers return and recommend the business to others.

Suppliers

Regular Orders & Payment

Suppliers want the business to pay on time and place regular orders. A reliable business relationship benefits both sides.

Local Community

Jobs & Minimal Disruption

The local community wants the business to create jobs, support the area, and avoid causing problems such as pollution, noise, or traffic congestion.

Government

Tax Revenue & Legal Compliance

The government wants businesses to pay taxes, follow employment laws, and comply with regulations on health, safety, and the environment.

Shareholders

Dividends & Rising Share Price

Shareholders (in limited companies) want a good return on their investment through dividends and an increase in the value of their shares.

Key Fact

Internal stakeholders (owners, employees, shareholders) are part of the business. External stakeholders (customers, suppliers, local community, government) are outside it but still affected by its decisions.

Stakeholder Conflict

Stakeholder conflict happens when the interests of different stakeholder groups clash. Because each group wants something different, it is often impossible for a business to keep everyone happy at the same time.

Common examples of stakeholder conflict include:

  • Owners vs employees — owners may want to cut costs to increase profit, but employees want higher wages and better conditions
  • Customers vs owners — customers want low prices and high quality, but owners want to charge higher prices to maximise profit
  • Shareholders vs local community — shareholders may push for expansion that increases profits, but the local community may oppose new factories that cause noise and pollution
  • Suppliers vs customers — suppliers want higher prices for their materials, which pushes up costs and may lead to higher prices for customers

Businesses must manage these conflicts carefully. Ignoring one stakeholder group for too long can cause real damage — unhappy employees leave, dissatisfied customers switch to competitors, and angry communities campaign against the business.

Amazon is one of the world’s most profitable companies, but it has faced repeated criticism over working conditions in its warehouses. Customers benefit from fast, cheap delivery, and shareholders benefit from rising profits. However, employees have reported intense pressure to meet targets, limited break times, and high injury rates. The government has investigated these claims, and local communities have sometimes opposed new warehouse developments due to traffic and environmental concerns. This is a clear example of stakeholder conflict — the interests of customers and shareholders clash with those of employees and the local community.

Technology and Business

Technology has transformed the way businesses operate. From selling products online to communicating with customers on social media, digital tools have created new opportunities — and new challenges — for businesses of every size.

E-Commerce

E-commerce means buying and selling goods or services online. It allows businesses to reach customers far beyond their local area — even internationally — without needing a physical shop.

  • Advantage: Open 24/7, wider customer reach, lower overhead costs than a high-street shop
  • Disadvantage: High competition online, delivery costs, customers cannot see or try products before buying

Social Media Marketing

Social media marketing uses platforms like Instagram, TikTok, and Facebook to promote products, build brand awareness, and interact directly with customers. It is much cheaper than traditional advertising such as TV or newspaper adverts.

  • Advantage: Low cost, highly targeted (businesses can reach specific age groups, locations, or interests), builds customer loyalty through direct engagement
  • Disadvantage: Negative reviews and complaints spread quickly; requires constant content creation to stay visible

Digital Communication

Tools like email, video calls, and instant messaging have made business communication faster and cheaper. Teams can collaborate from different locations, and businesses can respond to customer enquiries almost instantly.

  • Advantage: Reduces travel costs, speeds up decision-making, enables remote working
  • Disadvantage: Can lead to information overload; less personal than face-to-face meetings

Automation

Automation means using machines, robots, or software to carry out tasks that were previously done by people. In manufacturing, robots assemble products faster and more accurately. In offices, software can handle repetitive tasks like invoicing and data entry.

  • Advantage: Reduces labour costs, increases speed and consistency, fewer human errors
  • Disadvantage: High initial cost of equipment; can lead to job losses, creating conflict with employees and the local community
Key Fact

Technology can create stakeholder conflict. For example, automation benefits owners (lower costs, higher profits) but can harm employees (job losses) and the local community (fewer local jobs). Businesses must weigh up these trade-offs when adopting new technology.

Impact of Technology on Business

Technology affects almost every area of business activity:

  • Sales — e-commerce and mobile apps allow businesses to sell to a global market around the clock, increasing revenue potential
  • Costs — automation and cloud computing can reduce long-term costs by replacing manual labour and expensive physical infrastructure
  • Marketing — social media and targeted online advertising are far cheaper and more measurable than traditional methods
  • Communication — email, video conferencing, and collaboration tools allow faster decision-making and remote working

Gymshark started in 2012 as a small e-commerce business selling fitness clothing from a garage in Birmingham. Instead of spending money on traditional advertising, the founder Ben Francis used social media marketing — partnering with fitness influencers on Instagram and YouTube to promote the brand. This approach cost a fraction of what TV advertising would have cost, but it reached millions of young, fitness-focused customers directly. By 2020, Gymshark was valued at over £1 billion. The business shows how e-commerce and social media marketing can allow a start-up to compete with much larger, established brands.

Exam Tip

Stakeholder and technology questions often ask you to analyse conflict or evaluate the impact of a decision on different groups. Always identify at least two stakeholders and explain how they are affected differently. For top marks, make a judgement about which stakeholder is affected most or whether the overall impact is positive or negative — and explain why.

Conclusion

Every business must manage the competing interests of its stakeholders while deciding how to use technology effectively. Getting this balance right is key to long-term success.

Key Takeaways

  • Stakeholders have different interests: owners want profit, employees want fair pay, customers want value, and the community wants jobs without disruption.
  • Conflict is inevitable: satisfying one group often means disappointing another — businesses must find a balance.
  • Technology creates opportunities and tensions: e-commerce, social media, and automation can boost sales and cut costs, but may also cause job losses and require careful stakeholder management.
