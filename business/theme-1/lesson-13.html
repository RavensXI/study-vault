Business Plans

0:00 / 0:00

What Is a Business Plan?

A business plan is a written document that sets out a business idea and explains how it will be put into practice. It covers everything from what the business will sell and who it will sell to, through to how it will be financed and how much profit it expects to make.

Think of it as a roadmap. Just as a journey needs directions, a new business needs a clear plan to guide it from idea to reality. Without one, the entrepreneur is making decisions based on guesswork rather than research.

Why Write a Business Plan?

Business plans are written for several important reasons:

  • To obtain finance — banks and investors will not hand over money without seeing a detailed plan. They need evidence that the idea is viable and that the entrepreneur has thought it through.
  • To guide the business — the plan acts as a reference document that keeps the entrepreneur focused on their objectives, especially in the busy early months.
  • To identify potential problems — writing the plan forces the entrepreneur to research the market and think about risks before they happen, not after.
  • To set targets and measure progress — the plan includes financial forecasts and objectives that the business can check itself against over time.
Key Fact

The most common reason for writing a business plan is to obtain finance. Banks typically require a business plan before approving a loan, and investors want to see one before committing their money.

Who Reads a Business Plan?

Different people read a business plan for different reasons:

  • Banks — to decide whether to approve a loan. They look closely at the financial forecasts to judge whether the business can make the repayments.
  • Investors and venture capitalists — to assess whether the business has strong growth potential and is worth investing in.
  • Business partners — to understand the idea and agree on how the business will be run.
  • The entrepreneur themselves — to organise their thoughts, check their figures, and keep the business on track.

Contents of a Business Plan

There is no single fixed format, but most business plans cover the same core sections:

Business Idea and Objectives

This section explains what the business will do, what product or service it will offer, and what makes it different from competitors. It also sets out the aims and objectives — for example, to achieve £50,000 revenue in the first year or to break even within six months.

Target Market

The plan must identify the target market — the specific group of customers the business aims to sell to. This includes details about customer demographics, the size of the market, and evidence of demand from market research.

Marketing Strategy

This section outlines how the business will attract and keep customers, using the four Ps of the marketing mix: what the product is, what price it will be sold at, how it will be promoted, and where it will be sold (place).

Financial Forecasts

This is often the section that banks and investors look at most closely. It includes a cash-flow forecast showing expected inflows and outflows of cash, plus predictions for revenue, costs, and profit. Together, these are known as financial forecasts.

Sources of Finance

The plan explains how much money is needed to start and run the business, and where that money will come from — for example, personal savings, a bank loan, or investment from a partner.

Location and Staffing

The plan describes where the business will operate and why that location was chosen. It also covers staffing — how many employees are needed, what roles they will fill, and how much they will be paid.

Imagine Zara wants to open a vegan bakery. Her business plan would include: the business idea (freshly baked vegan cakes and pastries), her target market (health-conscious consumers aged 18–35 in her local town), a marketing strategy (Instagram promotion, loyalty cards, competitive pricing), financial forecasts (a 12-month cash-flow forecast showing she expects to break even by month 8), sources of finance (£5,000 personal savings plus a £10,000 bank loan), and location details (a small unit on the high street near a gym and a university).

Limitations of Business Plans

A business plan is a useful tool, but it has significant limitations:

  • Based on estimates — the financial forecasts are predictions, not facts. Revenue may be lower than expected, or costs may be higher. If the figures are wrong, the whole plan becomes unreliable.
  • Market conditions can change — a plan written today may not reflect reality in six months. Consumer tastes shift, new trends emerge, and economic conditions fluctuate.
  • Competitor reactions are unpredictable — existing businesses may lower their prices, launch new products, or increase their marketing in response to a new competitor entering the market.
  • Plans become outdated quickly — the business environment moves fast. A plan that is not regularly updated can become more of a historical document than a useful guide.
Key Fact

Business plans are only as good as the research behind them. If the market research is poor or the financial forecasts are over-optimistic, the plan will give a misleading picture of the business’s chances of success.

In 2020, thousands of businesses had carefully prepared plans for the year ahead. When the COVID-19 pandemic hit, almost every assumption in those plans — about customer footfall, supply chains, staffing, and cash flow — became instantly outdated. Restaurants that had forecast steady growth had to close overnight. Retailers with plans based on high-street footfall saw customers shift entirely to online shopping. This is an extreme example, but it shows that no business plan can predict every change in the external environment.

The Value of Planning

Despite these limitations, writing a business plan is still extremely valuable. The process itself — researching the market, calculating costs, thinking about competitors — forces the entrepreneur to confront difficult questions before spending any money. Even if the plan turns out to be inaccurate, the business is better prepared than one that had no plan at all.

A good business plan is also a living document. Successful entrepreneurs review and update their plan regularly as conditions change, rather than writing it once and forgetting about it.

Exam Tip

When discussing business plans, always consider both sides. Examiners want to see that you understand why a plan is useful (obtaining finance, setting targets, reducing risk) and why it has limitations (based on predictions, can become outdated). A balanced answer that weighs up the value of planning against its limitations will score higher than one that only gives advantages or disadvantages.

Conclusion

A business plan is a vital document for any new enterprise. It turns an idea into a structured proposal that can convince banks and investors, guide decision-making, and highlight risks before they become problems.

Key Takeaways

  • Purpose: business plans are written primarily to obtain finance, but they also guide the business and help identify problems early.
  • Contents: a good plan covers the business idea, target market, marketing strategy, financial forecasts, sources of finance, location, and staffing.
  • Limitations: plans are based on estimates and can quickly become outdated — they need regular updating to stay useful.
