Cash & Cash-Flow Forecasts

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Why Cash Matters

Cash is the money a business has available right now to pay its bills. Without enough cash, a business cannot pay suppliers, cover wages, or keep the lights on — even if it is technically profitable on paper.

A business needs cash to:

  • Pay suppliers — raw materials and stock must be paid for, often before the finished product is sold
  • Cover overheads — rent, electricity, insurance, and other fixed costs are due regularly
  • Pay employees — wages and salaries must be paid on time, or staff will leave
  • Prevent insolvency — a business that cannot pay its debts when they are due is insolvent and may be forced to close
Key Fact

Cash is not the same as profit. A business can be profitable but still run out of cash. For example, if a business sells £50,000 of goods on credit but customers take 90 days to pay, the business has £50,000 in profit on paper — but zero cash in the bank to pay next month’s bills. This timing gap is why cash-flow management is critical.

Cash-Flow Forecasts

A cash-flow forecast is a prediction of the money flowing into and out of a business over a future period, usually month by month. It helps a business plan ahead and spot potential cash shortages before they happen.

Key Components

A cash-flow forecast has four key rows:

  • Cash inflows — all money coming into the business (sales revenue, loans, investments, grants)
  • Cash outflows — all money leaving the business (supplier payments, wages, rent, utilities, loan repayments)
  • Net cash flow — the difference between inflows and outflows in each period (net cash flow = cash inflows − cash outflows)
  • Opening and closing balances — the opening balance is the cash available at the start of the month; the closing balance is the cash left at the end (closing balance = opening balance + net cash flow)

Leila is opening a bakery. Here is her three-month cash-flow forecast:

January: Opening balance £5,000 | Cash inflows £3,000 | Cash outflows £6,500 | Net cash flow −£3,500 | Closing balance £1,500

February: Opening balance £1,500 | Cash inflows £4,500 | Cash outflows £4,000 | Net cash flow +£500 | Closing balance £2,000

March: Opening balance £2,000 | Cash inflows £5,500 | Cash outflows £4,200 | Net cash flow +£1,300 | Closing balance £3,300

January shows a negative net cash flow of −£3,500 because Leila had high start-up costs (equipment, initial stock, deposit on premises). Without the £5,000 opening balance from her savings, she would have run out of cash. By March, the business is generating positive cash flow as sales increase.

Common Cash-Flow Problems

Businesses often face cash-flow difficulties even when they are profitable. Common causes include:

  • Late payment by customers — if customers buy on credit and pay late, the business has sold goods but has no cash to show for it
  • Seasonal demand — an ice-cream shop earns most of its revenue in summer but still has to pay rent in winter
  • Overtrading — growing too fast means buying more stock and hiring more staff before additional revenue arrives
  • Unexpected costs — equipment breakdowns, tax bills, or legal fees can drain cash without warning

Managing Cash-Flow Problems

Businesses can take several actions to improve their cash-flow position:

  • Negotiate shorter payment terms — ask customers to pay within 14 days instead of 60
  • Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers — delay paying suppliers to keep cash in the business longer
  • Reduce stock levels — holding less stock means less cash is tied up in unsold goods
  • Arrange an overdraft — a bank overdraft provides a short-term safety net for months when outflows exceed inflows
  • Cut unnecessary spending — review costs and eliminate non-essential expenses
Key Fact

The cash-flow forecast is a planning tool, not a guarantee. Actual cash flows may differ from the forecast if sales are lower than expected or costs are higher. Businesses should update their forecast regularly and have a contingency plan (like an overdraft facility) for months when cash is tight.

Exam Tip

Cash-flow questions often give you a table and ask you to fill in missing figures or interpret the forecast. Remember: closing balance = opening balance + net cash flow, and next month’s opening balance = this month’s closing balance. A negative closing balance means the business needs additional finance (e.g. an overdraft) to survive that month.

Conclusion

Cash flow is the lifeblood of a business. Even profitable businesses can fail if they run out of cash. A cash-flow forecast helps entrepreneurs plan ahead and avoid nasty surprises.

Key Takeaways

  • Cash ≠ profit. A business can be profitable on paper but run out of cash if customers pay late or costs come early.
  • Four key figures: cash inflows, cash outflows, net cash flow, and opening/closing balances.
  • Prevention over cure: forecasting cash flow helps spot shortages before they become crises.
