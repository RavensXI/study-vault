Sources of Business Finance

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Why Businesses Need Finance

Every business needs money to get started and to keep running. Finance is the money a business uses to pay for its activities — from buying stock and equipment to paying wages and rent.

Businesses need finance for different reasons at different stages:

  • Start-up costs — one-off expenses to launch the business (equipment, stock, deposits, legal fees)
  • Running costs — ongoing day-to-day expenses (wages, rent, utilities, raw materials)
  • Expansion — investing in growth (new premises, extra staff, more stock)

The right source of finance depends on how much money is needed, how quickly it is needed, and whether the business wants to repay a debt or give up a share of ownership.

Short-Term Sources of Finance

Short-term finance covers needs that last less than a year — such as bridging a gap in cash flow or buying seasonal stock.

Overdraft

An overdraft lets a business spend more than it has in its bank account, up to an agreed limit. It is flexible — the business only borrows what it needs and pays interest only on the amount overdrawn.

  • Advantage: Very flexible — ideal for covering short-term cash-flow gaps
  • Disadvantage: Interest rates are usually high, and the bank can withdraw the facility at any time

Trade Credit

Trade credit is when a supplier allows a business to receive goods now and pay later, usually within 30–90 days. This means the business can sell the goods and use the revenue to pay the supplier.

  • Advantage: No interest charged — it is essentially a free short-term loan
  • Disadvantage: Not always available to new businesses, as suppliers may want proof of reliability first

Long-Term Sources of Finance

Long-term finance is used for bigger investments that will benefit the business over several years — such as buying premises, vehicles, or expensive equipment.

Personal Savings

Many sole traders and small business owners use their own savings to start their business. This is the simplest source of finance — there is no interest to pay and no ownership to give away.

  • Advantage: No debt, no interest, and the owner keeps full control
  • Disadvantage: Limited by how much the owner has saved — may not be enough for larger ventures

Venture Capital

Venture capital is investment from specialist firms or wealthy individuals who put large sums into businesses they believe will grow rapidly. In return, they take a share of ownership and often want a say in how the business is run.

  • Advantage: Provides large amounts of funding plus business expertise and contacts
  • Disadvantage: The owner gives up some control and a share of future profits

Share Capital

Share capital is money raised by selling shares in a limited company. Each share represents a small piece of ownership, and shareholders may receive a share of profits called dividends.

  • Advantage: Can raise very large amounts without taking on debt
  • Disadvantage: Only available to limited companies, and the original owner’s share of ownership is diluted

Loans

A loan is a fixed sum borrowed from a bank, repaid in regular instalments with interest over an agreed period (e.g. 3–10 years). The interest rate is usually lower than an overdraft because the repayment terms are fixed.

  • Advantage: Fixed repayments make budgeting easier; the business keeps full ownership
  • Disadvantage: Interest adds to costs, and the bank may require collateral (an asset as security)

Retained Profit

Retained profit is profit the business has already earned and chooses to reinvest rather than pay out to owners. It is the most common source of finance for established businesses.

  • Advantage: No interest, no loss of control — the cheapest source of finance
  • Disadvantage: Only available to businesses that are already making a profit — not useful for start-ups

Crowdfunding

Crowdfunding raises small amounts from a large number of people, usually through online platforms like Kickstarter or Crowdcube. Backers may receive rewards (e.g. early access to the product) or a small share of the business.

  • Advantage: Tests whether customers want the product before it launches; doubles as marketing
  • Disadvantage: No guarantee of reaching the funding target; the business idea is publicly visible to competitors
Key Fact

Finance sources are either internal (from within the business — personal savings, retained profit) or external (from outside — loans, overdrafts, venture capital, share capital, crowdfunding, trade credit). Internal sources avoid debt and interest, but are limited in amount.

Choosing the Right Source

The best source of finance depends on several factors:

  • Amount needed — small amounts suit overdrafts or savings; large amounts may need loans or share capital
  • Time period — short-term needs suit overdrafts and trade credit; long-term needs suit loans and share capital
  • Willingness to give up control — venture capital and share capital mean sharing ownership
  • Cost — interest charges on loans and overdrafts increase overall costs
  • Stage of business — start-ups cannot use retained profit; established businesses have more options

Scottish craft brewery Brewdog needed finance to expand but did not want to borrow from banks or give control to venture capitalists. Instead, they launched “Equity for Punks” — a crowdfunding campaign that sold shares directly to customers. Over several rounds, they raised more than £90 million from over 200,000 small investors. Each investor became a part-owner of the company and received perks like discounts and free beer on their birthday. This approach gave Brewdog the finance it needed while building a loyal community of brand ambassadors.

Exam Tip

Questions about finance sources often ask you to recommend a source for a specific business. Always link your answer to the context — consider whether the business is a start-up or established, how much money is needed, and whether the owner wants to keep full control. A generic answer like “they should get a loan” without explaining why will not reach the top marks.

Conclusion

Businesses can choose from a range of internal and external finance sources. The right choice depends on how much is needed, for how long, and how much control the owner is willing to share.

Key Takeaways

  • Internal vs external: personal savings and retained profit are internal (no debt); loans, overdrafts, venture capital, share capital, and crowdfunding are external.
  • Short-term vs long-term: overdrafts and trade credit cover short-term gaps; loans, share capital, and venture capital fund bigger investments.
  • Trade-offs: every source has a cost — either interest payments or giving up a share of ownership and control.
