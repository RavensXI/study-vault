What Are Business Aims and Objectives?

Every business needs a sense of direction. Without knowing where it is heading, a business cannot make good decisions about how to spend money, hire staff, or develop products. That direction comes from its aims and objectives.

A business aim is a long-term goal that gives the business its overall purpose. It describes what the business wants to achieve in the future, but it does not specify exactly how it will get there. For example, “to become the leading coffee shop chain in the North West” is an aim.

A business objective is a specific, measurable target that helps the business achieve its aim. Objectives break the aim down into smaller, achievable steps. For example, “to increase sales by 10% within the next 12 months” is an objective.

Key Fact

The key difference: aims are broad and long-term, giving the business direction. Objectives are specific and measurable, giving the business clear targets to work towards. Think of the aim as the destination and objectives as the steps on the journey.

SMART Objectives

Good objectives follow the SMART criteria. This means they are:

  • Specific — clearly defined, not vague. “Increase sales” is vague; “increase sales of hot drinks by 15%” is specific.
  • Measurable — you can track progress with numbers. If you cannot measure it, you cannot tell whether you have achieved it.
  • Achievable — realistic given the resources and time available. Setting an impossible target demotivates staff.
  • Relevant — linked to the overall aim of the business. Every objective should move the business closer to its aim.
  • Time-bound — has a clear deadline. “Within the next 12 months” creates urgency and focus.
Key Fact

SMART objectives are powerful because they give a business a clear way to measure success. Without a deadline and a number, it is impossible to know whether the business is on track. Examiners reward students who use the SMART framework when explaining why objectives matter.

Financial Aims and Objectives

Financial aims and objectives focus on the money side of the business. These are especially important for start-ups and small businesses because running out of cash is the most common reason new businesses fail.

Survival

Survival is the most basic financial aim. For a brand-new business, simply staying open and not running out of money is the first priority. In the early months, most start-ups make a loss. The aim is to keep going long enough for revenue to cover costs. Survival is also critical during tough times — a recession, a pandemic, or a sudden loss of a major customer can make survival the top priority even for established businesses.

Profit

Once a business has survived its early stage, the next financial aim is usually profit. Profit is what remains after all costs are subtracted from revenue. It rewards the owner for the risk they have taken, funds future growth, and acts as a safety net for bad times. A profit objective might be “to achieve a net profit of £50,000 by the end of year two.”

Sales and Revenue

Some businesses focus on increasing the total value or volume of sales rather than profit alone. A start-up might aim to sell 500 units in its first quarter. A growing business might target £1 million in annual revenue. High sales figures can attract investors, build brand recognition, and create economies of scale — even if profits are slim in the short term.

Market Share

Market share is the percentage of total sales in a market that belongs to one business. If the UK coffee market is worth £10 billion and a chain sells £500 million, its market share is 5%. Increasing market share means the business is growing faster than its competitors. A high market share gives a business power over suppliers and pricing.

Financial Security

Many small business owners want financial security — a steady, reliable income rather than dramatic growth. A sole trader running a plumbing business may not want to become the biggest plumber in the country. They may simply want enough income to pay the mortgage, save for retirement, and take a holiday. This is a perfectly valid financial aim and is common among lifestyle businesses.

Non-Financial Aims and Objectives

Not every business is motivated purely by money. Many entrepreneurs start businesses for reasons that go beyond profit. These non-financial aims are especially common in start-ups, social enterprises, and small owner-managed businesses.

Social Objectives

Some businesses exist primarily to help society. A social enterprise might aim to reduce food waste, provide jobs for homeless people, or improve access to education. Profit is still needed to keep the business running, but it is not the main purpose. Instead, profit is reinvested to achieve the social mission.

Personal Satisfaction

Many entrepreneurs start businesses doing something they love. A keen baker who opens a cake shop, or a graphic designer who goes freelance, may be motivated by the satisfaction of doing work they enjoy every day. The aim is personal fulfilment rather than maximum profit.

Challenge

Some people start businesses simply because they want to test themselves. The challenge of building something from nothing, solving problems, and competing in a market is a powerful motivator. Many serial entrepreneurs start new businesses even after becoming wealthy because they thrive on the challenge itself.

Independence and Control

Being your own boss is one of the most commonly cited reasons for starting a business. Independence means making your own decisions about working hours, business direction, and strategy without answering to a manager or employer. Control over your own work-life balance is a major non-financial aim for many small business owners.

TOMS Shoes was founded by Blake Mycoskie in 2006 after he visited Argentina and saw children without shoes. His aim was not to maximise profit — it was to improve the lives of children in poverty. He created the “One for One” model: for every pair of shoes sold, TOMS would give a pair to a child in need.

This social objective drove every business decision, from product design to marketing. The company still needed to make a profit to survive and fund its giving programme, but profit was a means to an end rather than the end itself. By 2020, TOMS had given away over 100 million pairs of shoes. The case shows that non-financial aims can be the primary purpose of a business, with financial objectives supporting them rather than the other way around.

Why Aims and Objectives Differ Between Businesses

Not all businesses have the same aims and objectives. Several factors explain why they differ:

Size of the Business

A sole trader with one shop has very different objectives from a multinational corporation. Small businesses often focus on survival and personal satisfaction. Large businesses are more likely to target market share, growth, and shareholder returns. As a business grows, its objectives tend to shift from survival towards expansion and profit maximisation.

Age of the Business

Start-ups almost always prioritise survival in their first year. Once the business is established and generating consistent revenue, objectives shift towards growth, profit, and market share. A business that has been trading for 20 years will have very different targets from one that launched last month.

Market Conditions

During a recession or economic downturn, even large, profitable businesses may shift their objective back to survival. When the economy is booming and consumer confidence is high, businesses are more likely to pursue ambitious growth targets. External factors such as new competitors, changing consumer trends, or new legislation can also force a business to rethink its objectives.

Type of Ownership

The legal structure of a business affects its objectives. A charity or social enterprise will prioritise social aims over profit. A public limited company (PLC) with shareholders will face pressure to maximise profits and dividends. A family-run business may prioritise long-term stability and passing the business to the next generation rather than rapid growth.

Exam Tip

When a question asks about aims and objectives, always make the distinction clear. Aims are broad and long-term; objectives are specific and measurable. If you are asked why objectives change, link your answer to specific factors — the size of the business, how long it has been trading, or what is happening in the wider economy. Generic answers without context will not reach the top marks.

Conclusion

Aims and objectives give a business direction and purpose. Whether a business is chasing profit, survival, or a social mission, having clear targets helps owners make better decisions and measure their progress.

Key Takeaways

  • Aims vs objectives: aims are broad long-term goals; objectives are specific, measurable targets. SMART objectives are the most effective because they are clear, trackable, and time-bound.
  • Financial and non-financial: start-ups often prioritise survival and financial security, while social enterprises focus on social objectives. Most businesses pursue a mix of both.
  • Objectives change: the size, age, ownership, and market conditions of a business all influence which aims and objectives it prioritises at any given time.
