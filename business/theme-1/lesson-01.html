Enterprise & New Business Ideas

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

What Is Enterprise?

Enterprise is the process of identifying a business opportunity and taking the risk to set up a business. It involves spotting a gap in the market or a better way of doing things, then organising the resources needed to make it happen.

Key Fact

An entrepreneur is someone who takes the financial risk of starting and running a business. The UK has over 5.5 million small businesses, and they account for 99.9% of all businesses in the country. Famous UK entrepreneurs include James Dyson (vacuum cleaners), Anita Roddick (The Body Shop), and Alan Sugar (Amstrad).

Why Do People Start Businesses?

People become entrepreneurs for different reasons. Some are driven by financial rewards — the chance to earn more than they would as an employee. Others want independence: being their own boss and making their own decisions. Some spot a gap in the market and are passionate about filling it. Others may have been made redundant and see starting a business as the best alternative.

  • Financial rewards — keeping the profits rather than earning a fixed salary
  • Independence — being your own boss, choosing your own hours
  • Passion — turning a hobby or interest into a career
  • Dissatisfaction — frustration with existing products or a former employer
  • Redundancy — job loss forcing a new direction

Where Do New Business Ideas Come From?

Markets are dynamic — they change constantly. New business ideas come about because of changes in technology, shifts in what consumers want, and existing products or services becoming obsolete.

Changes in Technology

New technology creates entirely new markets. Smartphones created opportunities for app developers. Social media created a whole industry of digital marketing. Streaming services like Netflix and Spotify made DVD rental shops and CD stores obsolete. Businesses that fail to adapt to technological change risk being left behind.

Key Fact

Blockbuster Video had over 9,000 stores worldwide in 2004. By 2013, the company was bankrupt — destroyed by Netflix’s streaming model. This is a textbook example of how changes in technology make existing business models obsolete.

Changes in Consumer Wants

What customers want changes over time. Growing awareness of health and fitness created demand for gym memberships, protein bars, and fitness apps. Concern about the environment led to demand for electric vehicles, reusable packaging, and plant-based food. Businesses that spot these trends early have a first-mover advantage.

Products and Services Becoming Obsolete

When existing products become outdated, new opportunities appear. Film cameras were replaced by digital cameras, which were then largely replaced by smartphone cameras. Typewriters gave way to word processors, then laptops, then tablets. Each shift opened a gap in the market for new businesses.

Original Ideas vs. Adapting Existing Ideas

New business ideas fall into two broad categories:

  • Original ideas — a completely new product or service that did not exist before. These are rare and carry high risk, but the potential rewards are huge. Example: when Apple launched the iPhone in 2007, it created a new category of product.
  • Adapting existing ideas — taking a product, service, or business model that already works and improving it or applying it to a new market. This is far more common and usually less risky. Example: Innocent Drinks did not invent smoothies, but they spotted a gap for convenient, natural fruit drinks and built a brand around it.

In 1999, three Cambridge graduates — Richard Reed, Adam Balon, and Jon Wright — spent £500 on fruit to test their smoothie recipes at a music festival. They put up a sign asking “Should we give up our jobs to make these smoothies?” with bins marked “Yes” and “No.” The “Yes” bin filled up. They quit their jobs the next day.

Innocent did not invent smoothies — they adapted an existing product by focusing on natural ingredients, fun branding, and ethical sourcing. By 2008 they had 71% of the UK smoothie market. Coca-Cola bought a majority stake in 2013 for over £100 million. Their story shows how adapting an existing idea with a clear unique selling point (USP) can be hugely successful.

Risk and Reward

Every business involves risk. Entrepreneurs invest their own money, time, and effort with no guarantee of success. The potential rewards — profit, independence, personal satisfaction, and capital growth — balance out the risk.

Key Fact

The relationship between risk and reward is central to business. Generally, the higher the risk, the higher the potential reward. A safe savings account earns low interest; investing everything in a new business idea could lead to bankruptcy — or millions. Entrepreneurs are people willing to take calculated risks in the hope of earning rewards.

The Purpose of Business Activity

The basic purpose of all business activity is to produce goods or provide services that meet customer needs and wants. Businesses do this to earn revenue and, ultimately, to make a profit.

However, not all businesses aim primarily for profit. Social enterprises like The Big Issue exist to address social problems. They trade like regular businesses but reinvest most of their profits into their cause rather than paying shareholders.

The Role of Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurs play a vital role in the economy. They create jobs, generate tax revenue for the government, and drive innovation. Without entrepreneurs willing to take risks, there would be fewer new products, less competition, and less economic growth.

Key entrepreneurial skills include:

  • Creativity — generating new ideas or finding new approaches
  • Risk-taking — being willing to invest money and time with uncertain outcomes
  • Determination — persisting through setbacks and challenges
  • Decision-making — making quick, informed choices under pressure
  • Communication — persuading investors, customers, and employees

James Dyson spent 15 years and built 5,127 prototypes before perfecting his bagless vacuum cleaner. Every major manufacturer rejected his design, so he set up his own company. Today, Dyson is worth over £5 billion and employs thousands of people globally. His story demonstrates every key entrepreneurial trait: creativity (original cyclone technology), risk-taking (remortgaging his house to fund development), determination (5,127 failed prototypes), and the enormous rewards that can follow.

Exam Tip

In the exam, you will always be given a case study about a real or fictional business. When asked about enterprise or entrepreneurship, always link your answer back to the specific business in the case study — don’t just write general theory. For example, if asked to “discuss whether the entrepreneur took a risk,” explain what they risked and what they stood to gain, using evidence from the source booklet.

Conclusion

Enterprise is about spotting opportunities and taking the risk to turn them into a business. New ideas come from changes in technology, shifts in consumer wants, and products becoming obsolete. Entrepreneurs can create original ideas or adapt existing ones. The key trade-off is always risk versus reward.

Key Takeaways

  • Dynamic markets: Technology and consumer trends constantly create new opportunities and make old products obsolete.
  • Risk and reward: Every business carries financial risk, but the potential rewards — profit, independence, satisfaction — drive entrepreneurs to start businesses.
  • Entrepreneurial skills: Creativity, risk-taking, determination, and strong communication are essential for business success.
