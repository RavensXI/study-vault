9 or 12 marks

“Justify…” / “Evaluate…”

Used in: Theme 1 & Theme 2

What the Examiner Wants

This is the highest-value question on the paper and the one that separates the top grades. The examiner wants you to weigh up options, factors, or strategies and make a supported judgement. “Justify” means recommend and defend a choice. “Evaluate” means judge how important, successful, or effective something is. Both require analysis of multiple sides and a clear final position.

Level Descriptors — 9-Mark Version

Level Marks What You Need
Level 3 7–9 Thorough analysis and evaluation of both sides. A justified conclusion that draws on the arguments and is supported by the context given in the question.
Level 2 4–6 Some analysis of one or both sides. Attempts a conclusion but it may be superficial or not linked to the analysis.
Level 1 1–3 Basic or vague points about the topic. No clear analysis or development.
— 0 Nothing relevant

Level Descriptors — 12-Mark Version

Level Marks What You Need
Level 3 9–12 Thorough and balanced analysis with well-developed chains of reasoning on both sides. A justified, context-linked conclusion.
Level 2 5–8 Some analysis with limited development. Attempts evaluation but lacks balance or a convincing conclusion.
Level 1 1–4 Basic or undeveloped points. No evaluation or conclusion.
— 0 Nothing relevant

Step-by-Step Formula

  1. 1
    Plan (2 minutes) — Read the question and the case study carefully. Identify the options or factors. Jot down one strong argument for each side with a link to the case study.
  2. 2
    Paragraph 1: Analyse Option/Factor A — Build a full chain of reasoning (point → explain → apply to case study → impact). This should be your longest paragraph.
  3. 3
    Paragraph 2: Analyse Option/Factor B — Start with “However…” or “On the other hand…” and build an equally developed counter-argument. Apply to the case study.
  4. 4
    Paragraph 3 (12-mark only): Additional analysis — For 12-mark questions, add a third paragraph that deepens your analysis with another argument, a counter-point, or a “depends on” factor.
  5. 5
    Conclusion: Justify your judgement — State your position clearly. Explain WHY you chose this side, referencing the case study. Acknowledge the other side briefly but explain why your choice is stronger. For “Justify” questions, recommend an option and defend it. For “Evaluate” questions, make a judgement about extent or importance.

Around 14 minutes. This is where you earn the most marks per minute — it’s worth investing time here.

12-mark version

Plan
2 min Para 1
4 min Para 2
4 min Para 3
4 min Conclude
4 min

Around 18 minutes. The extra paragraph is what separates 9-mark from 12-mark structure.

Argument 1

“One [advantage/reason/option] is [point].”

“This is because [explain the reasoning]…”

“For [business name], this would mean [apply to context]…”

“As a result, [impact on business, e.g. profit/sales/reputation]…”

Counter-argument

“However, [the alternative/counter-point] could be argued because…”

“This means that [explain]…”

“For [business name], this could lead to [consequence]…”

Conclusion (Justify)

“I would recommend [option] because [strongest reason linked to context]. Although [other option] has [acknowledge benefit], [chosen option] is more appropriate for [business name] because [justify with case study detail].”

Conclusion (Evaluate)

“Overall, [factor/strategy] is [very/somewhat/not very] important because [reason]. While [other factors] also matter, [this factor] is the most significant for [business name] because [link to context].”

Priya wants to open a café. She is deciding between renting a unit on the high street or a cheaper location on a side street two minutes away. Justify which location Priya should choose. [9 marks]

Argument for the high street

The high street location would give Priya much higher footfall. Customers who are already shopping are more likely to notice a café and walk in without planning to, which means Priya would benefit from impulse purchases. For a new café with no established reputation, being visible is crucial because Priya cannot yet rely on loyal customers or word-of-mouth. Higher footfall would lead to more customers from day one, increasing her revenue and helping the business break even faster.

Argument for the side street

However, the side-street location is cheaper, which would reduce Priya’s fixed costs. Lower rent means her break-even point would be lower — she would need fewer customers to cover her costs. This is important for a new business because cash flow is often tight in the early months, and many start-ups fail because they run out of cash rather than because their product is bad. The money saved on rent could be invested in marketing or better equipment, which might attract customers despite the less visible location.

Justified conclusion

I would recommend Priya chooses the side-street location. Although the high street offers better footfall, Priya is a new business with limited finance, and the risk of high fixed costs is a bigger threat to her survival than lower visibility. She could compensate for the quieter location by investing the money saved on rent into social media marketing and a distinctive shop front to draw customers in. A café two minutes from the high street is still conveniently located, and once Priya builds a reputation through good food and service, customers will seek her out regardless of location. Keeping costs low gives the business the best chance of surviving its first year.

Examiner: 9/9 (Level 3) — both options thoroughly analysed with developed chains of reasoning. Conclusion is clearly justified with reference to Priya’s specific situation and acknowledges the other side.

Evaluate whether social media is the most important factor in the success of a new online clothing business. [12 marks]

Level 1 Answer (1–4 marks)

Lists points, no chain of reasoning

Social media is good because lots of people use it. The business can post pictures of clothes. Customers can share posts. It is cheap compared to TV adverts. But not everyone uses social media. Some older people prefer to shop in stores.

Level 3 Answer (9–12 marks)

Developed chain + context

Social media is arguably the most important factor for a new online clothing business because it directly drives both awareness and sales. Platforms like Instagram and TikTok allow the business to showcase products visually, which is essential for clothing where appearance drives purchasing decisions. Social media advertising can also be precisely targeted to the business’s ideal customer demographic, meaning marketing spend is more efficient than traditional advertising. For a start-up with limited funds, this cost-effective reach could be the difference between attracting enough customers to survive and running out of cash.

A full Level 3 answer would continue with a counter-argument (e.g. product quality, pricing, or website design could matter more) and a conclusion evaluating the relative importance of social media versus other factors.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Ignoring the case study — This is the biggest mistake. The examiner specifically designs these questions around a scenario. Generic answers that never mention the business, its situation, or the figures given cannot reach Level 3. Always use names, details, and numbers from the case study.
  • ❌
    Weak conclusion — “Overall it depends on the situation” is not a conclusion. You must commit to a position and explain why, using evidence from your analysis. The conclusion is where the highest marks live.
  • ❌
    Only analysing one side — A one-sided answer — no matter how well-developed — cannot reach the top of Level 3. You must show the examiner you can see both sides before making a judgement.
  • ❌
    Running out of time — This is the last question and the one worth the most marks. Do NOT let earlier questions eat into this time. If you’re running low, skip back to unfinished short questions afterwards. Always attempt the full Justify/Evaluate.
