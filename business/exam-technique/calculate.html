2–4 marks

“Calculate…”

Used in: Theme 1 & Theme 2

What the Examiner Wants

The examiner wants to see the correct answer and your working. Even if your final answer is wrong, you can still earn marks for using the right formula and substituting the correct values. Never just write a number — always show the calculation.

Marking

Marks available Typical breakdown
2 marks 1 mark for correct working / formula substitution — 1 mark for correct answer
3–4 marks Marks split across multiple steps (e.g. calculate contribution first, then break-even)

Formulae You Must Know

These are not given to you in the exam. Learn them.

What you’re calculating Formula
Revenue Price × Quantity sold
Total costs Fixed costs + Variable costs
Variable costs (total) Variable cost per unit × Quantity
Profit / Loss Revenue − Total costs
Contribution per unit Selling price − Variable cost per unit
Break-even output Fixed costs ÷ Contribution per unit
Margin of safety Actual output − Break-even output
Gross profit margin (%) (Gross profit ÷ Revenue) × 100
Net profit margin (%) (Net profit ÷ Revenue) × 100
Percentage change ((New − Original) ÷ Original) × 100
Average Rate of Return (ARR) (Average annual profit ÷ Cost of investment) × 100

Step-by-Step Approach

  1. 1
    Identify what you’re being asked to calculate — Read the question carefully. Underline the key figures given in the source booklet or question.
  2. 2
    Write the formula — State the formula before substituting values. This earns you the method mark even if you make an arithmetic error later.
  3. 3
    Substitute the numbers — Replace the formula terms with the actual values from the question. Write this out clearly: e.g. £8,000 ÷ £4 = ?
  4. 4
    Calculate and state your answer clearly — Write the final answer with the correct unit (£, %, units). If the question is multi-step, show each step separately.

Timing

Read
30 s Formula
30 s Calculate
1–2 min

2–3 minutes is enough for most calculation questions. If you’re stuck after 2 minutes, write the formula with the values substituted in — you may still pick up a method mark — then move on.

A business sells 500 units at £12 each. Its fixed costs are £3,000 and variable costs are £5 per unit. Calculate the profit. [2 marks]

Step 1 — Revenue

Revenue = Price × Quantity = £12 × 500 = £6,000

Step 2 — Total Costs

Total costs = Fixed costs + (Variable cost per unit × Quantity)
= £3,000 + (£5 × 500) = £3,000 + £2,500 = £5,500

Step 3 — Profit

Profit = Revenue − Total costs = £6,000 − £5,500 = £500

Examiner: 2/2

A business has fixed costs of £8,000. It sells each unit for £20 and the variable cost per unit is £12. Calculate the break-even output. [2 marks]

Step 1 — Contribution per unit

Contribution per unit = Selling price − Variable cost per unit
= £20 − £12 = £8

Step 2 — Break-even output

Break-even output = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution per unit
= £8,000 ÷ £8 = 1,000 units

Examiner: 2/2

A business made a net profit of £45,000 on revenue of £180,000. Calculate the net profit margin. [2 marks]

Formula + substitution

Net profit margin = (Net profit ÷ Revenue) × 100
= (£45,000 ÷ £180,000) × 100 = 25%

Examiner: 2/2

A business’s revenue increased from £120,000 to £150,000. Calculate the percentage change in revenue. [2 marks]

Formula + substitution

Percentage change = ((New − Original) ÷ Original) × 100
= ((£150,000 − £120,000) ÷ £120,000) × 100
= (£30,000 ÷ £120,000) × 100 = 25%

Examiner: 2/2

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Not showing working — If you just write “1,000 units” with no working, you risk getting 0 if it’s wrong. Write the formula, substitute the values, then give the answer. You can earn 1 mark just for the correct method.
  • ❌
    Forgetting units — Always include the unit in your answer: £ for money, % for margins, “units” for break-even. An answer of “25” is incomplete; “25%” is correct.
  • ❌
    Confusing gross and net profit — Gross profit = Revenue − Cost of goods sold. Net profit = Gross profit − Other operating expenses. Read the question carefully to see which figure you’ve been given.
  • ❌
    Using total variable costs instead of per-unit costs in break-even — Contribution per unit uses the per-unit variable cost, not the total. Divide total variable costs by output first if you’re only given totals.
