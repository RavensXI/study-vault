Practising Calculations

Cover the formula and reproduce it

Why Calculations Need Special Practice

Business Studies is different from most GCSE subjects because it has a significant maths component. Calculation questions appear across both papers and can be worth 2–4 marks each. The most common student error isn’t getting the arithmetic wrong — it’s forgetting the formula, or confusing similar formulas under pressure. The solution is formula retrieval practice: cover the formula, write it from memory, then apply it. This is exactly what happens in the exam.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact

The Formula Retrieval Method

  1. 1
    Build your formula inventory — Write out all 13 formulas (see the reference card) on one page. Review this list once to make sure you know each one.
  2. 2
    Cover and recall — Cover the formula sheet. Pick one formula by name (e.g. “Gross Profit Margin”) and write it out from memory. Then uncover and check.
  3. 3
    Apply it to a number problem — Don’t stop at writing the formula. Make up or use a practice question and carry out the full calculation. Always show your working step by step.
  4. 4
    Write the units — After your answer, always state the units: £, %, units, etc. Examiners award a mark for the correct unit in many calculation questions.
  5. 5
    Traffic-light your formulas — Mark each formula Green (instant recall), Amber (hesitant), or Red (forgot). Practise red formulas daily and amber ones every other day.
  6. 6
    Practice under timed conditions — In the final 2 weeks, do full calculation questions against the clock. A 3-mark calculation should take no more than 4–5 minutes including working and units.

Step 1: Write the formula before using it

Break-even output = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution per unit
Contribution per unit = Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit

Step 2: Calculate contribution first

Contribution per unit = £12 − £4 = £8 per candle

Step 3: Calculate break-even output

Break-even output = £3,200 ÷ £8 = 400 candles

Step 4: Calculate margin of safety

Margin of Safety = Actual Output − Break-even Output = 500 − 400 = 100 candles

What the answer means

The business is currently selling 100 candles above its break-even point — this is the “safety zone”. If sales fell by more than 100 units per month, the business would start making a loss. An examiner might ask you to interpret this: a margin of safety of 100 out of 500 units (20%) is reasonably slim and suggests the business is vulnerable to any fall in demand.

Key habits shown here: (1) Wrote the formula before substituting numbers. (2) Calculated contribution as an intermediate step and labelled it. (3) Added the units (candles) to the final answers. (4) Interpreted the result in context — which can earn marks on longer questions.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Re-reading formulas instead of recalling them — Staring at the formula sheet doesn’t build memory. Cover it, try to write the formula, then check. The retrieval attempt is where the learning happens.
  • ❌
    Confusing similar formulas — Gross Profit and Net Profit are different. Contribution per unit and Total Contribution are different. Practise them separately until the distinction is automatic.
  • ❌
    Not showing working — Even if your final answer is wrong, you can still earn method marks if your working is clear. Always write out the formula, then substitute, then calculate.
  • ❌
    Omitting units from the answer — Writing “400” when the answer is “400 units” or “25%” can cost you a mark. Always write the unit alongside your answer.
