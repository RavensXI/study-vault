Summarising

Knowledge Organisers

Condense each topic onto one page

What the Research Says

A knowledge organiser is a single A4 page that contains all the essential information for one topic: key terms and definitions, formulas, real business examples, advantages, and disadvantages. The real value isn’t the finished page — it’s the process of creating it from memory. When you decide what to include, how to organise it, and fill it in without looking at your notes, you’re combining summarising, retrieval practice, and organisation into one activity.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Fiorella & Mayer (2016) Generative learning — students learn better when they create their own summaries rather than receiving pre-made ones Making it yourself matters
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Summarisation alone rated LOW utility, but combined with self-testing it becomes much more effective Must test from it, not just create it
EEF Cognitive Science Review (2021) Recommended structured knowledge organisers as tools for building substantive knowledge and reducing cognitive load Endorsed for GCSE learners broadly

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Read one lesson — Go through the full lesson page once to refresh your memory. Pay attention to the key fact boxes, formulas, and glossary terms.
  2. 2
    Set up your A4 page with sections — Divide the page into a grid with these headings: Key Terms & Definitions, Key Formulas, Real Business Examples, Advantages, Disadvantages. Leave space for each.
  3. 3
    Fill it in from memory first — Close your notes and write what you can remember in each section. This is retrieval practice built into the process.
  4. 4
    Check and complete in a different colour — Open your notes and fill in anything you missed using a different colour pen. The colour difference shows you exactly what you didn’t know.
  5. 5
    Self-test from it — In your next revision session, cover the knowledge organiser and try to recreate it from memory. Alternatively, use it to quiz yourself: cover one column and try to recall what’s there.

Creating one knowledge organiser takes about 35 minutes. Aim to create one per revision session. Over several weeks you can build a complete set of 30 — one per lesson. Then use them as retrieval practice tools: try to recreate each one from memory at spaced intervals.

Key Terms & Definitions

Sole trader — a business owned and run by one person; unlimited liability
Partnership — a business owned by 2–20 people; governed by a partnership agreement
Private limited company (Ltd) — incorporated business with limited liability; shares sold privately
Public limited company (plc) — incorporated; shares sold on the Stock Exchange; minimum £50,000 share capital
Unlimited liability — owner’s personal assets can be used to pay business debts
Limited liability — owner only loses what they invested in the business
Incorporation — the process of creating a separate legal entity

Key Formulas

No standard formulas for this topic — concepts are definitional. Not every knowledge organiser will have a formulas section; leave it brief or note that it doesn’t apply.

Real Business Examples

Sole trader: local plumber, freelance designer
Partnership: a solicitors’ firm, a dental practice
Ltd: Innocent Drinks (before acquisition), Dyson Ltd
plc: Tesco plc, Next plc

Advantages & Disadvantages

Sole trader — easy to set up, owner keeps all profit / unlimited liability, hard to raise finance
Ltd — limited liability, more finance options / more admin, profit shared among shareholders
plc — can raise large amounts of capital on the Stock Exchange / complex to set up, must publish accounts publicly

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Copying from notes instead of recalling — If you fill it in while looking at your notes, you’re just rewriting, not learning. The retrieval step is where the memory is built.
  • ❌
    Making it too detailed — One A4 page is the limit. If you can’t fit it on one page, you’re including too much. The constraint forces you to identify what really matters.
  • ❌
    Creating but never testing from it — A knowledge organiser in a folder is just a piece of paper. You must use it for self-testing in future sessions to get the full benefit.
  • ❌
    Using someone else’s instead of making your own — Pre-made knowledge organisers skip the generative learning process. The act of deciding what to include and organising it is where most of the learning happens.
