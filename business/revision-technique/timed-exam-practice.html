Exam readiness

Timed Exam Practice

Write like it’s the real thing

What the Research Says

Knowing the content is not enough — you also need to practise applying it under exam conditions. Timed practice combines retrieval (recalling knowledge), application (using the right exam structure), and time management. Edexcel examiner reports repeatedly highlight the same weaknesses: spending too long on low-mark questions, not applying knowledge to the business context in the question, and failing to reach a judgement on 6- and 9-mark answers. The only way to fix these is practice.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Practice testing rated HIGH utility — one of the two most effective revision strategies across all research Top-rated technique
Roediger & Karpicke (2006) Transfer-appropriate processing — practising in the same format as the test produces the best results Practice under exam conditions
Edexcel Examiner Reports Recurring weaknesses: students over-describe on low-mark questions, don’t apply to the specific business context, and miss the judgement on extended answers Timed practice directly addresses these

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Choose a practice question — Use the practice questions on each lesson page, or pick from a past paper. Identify how many marks it is so you can use the correct time allocation.
  2. 2
    Set your timer — Use the correct time allocation: 1 mark = 1–2 min, 2 marks = 2–3 min, 3 marks = 4–5 min, 6 marks = 8–10 min, 9 marks = 12–15 min, 12 marks = 15–18 min. Start the timer before you write anything.
  3. 3
    Write without notes — Closed-book only. This is retrieval practice in exam format. If you can’t remember something, leave a gap and move on — don’t look it up.
  4. 4
    Stop when the timer ends — Even if you haven’t finished. Learning to work within the time limit is part of the skill. Note where you ran out.
  5. 5
    Self-mark against the level descriptors — Use the Exam Technique guides for the mark scheme. For 6- and 9-mark questions, check which level your answer reaches. Be honest.
  6. 6
    Note 2–3 specific improvements — Don’t just write “do better”. Identify exactly what would move your answer up a level: more application to the context? A clear two-sided argument? A final judgement?

Practise 2–3 timed answers per week in the final 4 weeks before the exam. Mix the question types — include at least one short-answer (1–3 marks), one Discuss (6 marks), and one Justify or Evaluate (9 or 12 marks) each week. Use the timing guide above and stick to it strictly.

Plan (1 min)

Point for: Franchisees provide capital and take on risk; rapid expansion without huge investment; franchisees motivated as they own their outlet.
Point against: Less control over brand standards; profit shared with franchisee; could damage Gymshark’s premium brand reputation if outlets underperform.
Judgement: Depends on how important brand control is to Gymshark.

Paragraph 1: Point for (3 min)

One reason Gymshark should use franchising is that it would allow rapid expansion with lower financial risk. Franchisees pay an upfront fee and cover their own set-up costs, meaning Gymshark can open new outlets without using its own capital. This is important because Gymshark is still a relatively young business and may not want to take on significant debt. The franchisees are also highly motivated to run their outlets well because they have invested their own money, which can lead to better customer service and higher sales. This suggests franchising could help Gymshark grow quickly while keeping its costs manageable.

Paragraph 2: Point against + judgement (4 min)

However, a major disadvantage is that franchising could damage Gymshark’s brand. Gymshark has built a premium, digitally-native identity that depends on consistent quality and image. If a franchisee delivers poor customer experiences or fails to uphold brand standards, this could harm Gymshark’s reputation with all customers, not just those in that outlet. On balance, I think Gymshark should not franchise at this stage. Its brand is its main competitive advantage — compromising quality control for faster growth could destroy the very thing that makes Gymshark valuable. Direct expansion, even if slower, would protect the brand more effectively.

Self-marking

Level 3 (5–6): Yes — both sides argued with application to Gymshark, and a clear, justified judgement given.
Estimated mark: 5/6
Improvements: (1) Could strengthen the “for” paragraph with a specific example of another brand that franchised successfully. (2) The judgement could link back more explicitly to Gymshark’s specific business model (direct-to-consumer, online-first).

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Spending too long on low-mark questions — A 1-mark Define only needs one sentence. A 2-mark Outline needs two points. Don’t write a paragraph for a question worth 1 or 2 marks — you’re stealing time from the big questions.
  • ❌
    Writing without a timer — Untimed practice doesn’t prepare you for exam conditions. If you always have “as long as you need”, you’ll run out of time on the real thing.
  • ❌
    Not applying to the business in the question — Generic answers about “businesses in general” won’t reach Level 3. You must refer to the specific business named in the question and explain why the point matters in that context.
  • ❌
    Forgetting the judgement on 6- and 9-mark answers — Both sides argued but no conclusion = Level 2 at best. The judgement is what lifts you to Level 3. It doesn’t need to be long — one clear, justified sentence is enough.
  • ❌
    Not using the Exam Technique guides — Each question type has a specific structure that examiners expect. Practising without knowing the formula means you’re guessing at the format instead of nailing it.
