Visual learning

Dual Coding

Combine words and visuals to remember more

What the Research Says

Your brain processes words and images through separate channels. When you encode information in both — reading about a concept AND drawing a diagram of it — you create two memory pathways instead of one. If one pathway fades, the other can still retrieve the information. This is why break-even charts, SWOT matrices, and comparison tables are so effective for Business revision.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Paivio (1971) Dual coding theory — information stored in both verbal and visual memory is recalled better than either alone Foundational theory for visual learning
Mayer (2009) Multimedia learning principles — people learn better from words and pictures together than from words alone Words + visuals outperform words only
Clark & Paivio (1991) Dual coding consistently enhanced learning across different tasks, ages, and content areas Works for all subjects including Business

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Pick a topic — Choose one lesson or a key section within a lesson. Read through it to refresh your memory.
  2. 2
    Identify the relationships — What kind of information is this? Causes and effects → flowchart. Sequence of events → timeline. Two things to compare → Venn diagram or table. Categories of information → grid or mind map. Numbers and thresholds → graph or chart.
  3. 3
    Choose the right visual format — Match the format to the content. Don’t force everything into a mind map. A break-even analysis needs a chart; a SWOT analysis needs a grid; advantages vs. disadvantages needs a table.
  4. 4
    Draw it from memory — Close your notes and create the visual from what you remember. Use short labels (3–5 words max), arrows to show connections, and colour to group related ideas.
  5. 5
    Check and annotate — Open your notes and fill in anything you missed using a different colour. Add brief annotations explaining WHY each connection exists.

One visual takes about 25 minutes to create properly. Create 1–2 per revision session. Your visuals become revision tools themselves — in later sessions, try to recreate them from memory as a retrieval practice exercise.

Step 1: Identify relationships — this is a numbers-to-chart problem

Break-even involves fixed costs, variable costs, revenue, and the point where revenue equals total costs. A line graph is the right format: the horizontal axis shows output (units), the vertical axis shows money (£).

Step 2: Chart drawn from memory

Fixed costs line: horizontal line starting at the fixed cost value on the y-axis (e.g. £10,000) — stays flat regardless of output.
↓
Total costs line: starts at the same point as fixed costs, then slopes upward as variable costs add up.
↓
Revenue line: starts at the origin (zero output = zero revenue) and slopes upward more steeply.
↓
Break-even point: where the revenue line crosses the total costs line — annotated with a dotted vertical line down to the x-axis showing the break-even output.
↓
Margin of safety: the gap between actual (or forecast) output and the break-even point, shaded or labelled.

After checking — additions in a different colour

Missing from first attempt: the profit area (region above total costs to the right of break-even) and the loss area (region between total costs and revenue to the left of break-even). Also added the formula labels: Break-even output = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution per unit.

Why this works: Drawing the chart from memory forces you to understand the relationships between fixed costs, variable costs, and revenue — not just copy a diagram. In the exam, you can mentally picture this chart and use it to answer both calculation questions and written explanation questions.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Too much text on visuals — If your diagram has full sentences, it’s just notes in boxes. Keep labels to 3–5 words and let the visual structure do the work.
  • ❌
    Copying instead of drawing from memory — The retrieval effort is essential. Copying a diagram from your textbook is just re-reading with extra steps.
  • ❌
    Using mind maps for everything — Mind maps are good for categories but poor for numerical relationships or comparisons. A break-even chart must be a chart, not a mind map.
  • ❌
    Making it decorative, not informative — Spending 20 minutes colouring in a title doesn’t help memory. Keep it clean and functional — the information matters, not the aesthetics.
