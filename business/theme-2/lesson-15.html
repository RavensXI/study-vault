Training, Development & Motivation

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Training

Training is the process of teaching employees the skills and knowledge they need to do their job effectively. Every business — from a corner shop to a multinational corporation — depends on its workforce being properly trained.

On-the-Job Training

On-the-job training happens in the workplace while the employee carries out their normal duties. Common methods include shadowing an experienced colleague, being coached by a manager, or simply learning by doing.

  • Advantage: Cheap to deliver because no external courses are needed, and the employee is producing output while they learn
  • Disadvantage: Quality depends on whoever is doing the teaching — they may pass on bad habits or lack patience

Off-the-Job Training

Off-the-job training takes place away from the normal workplace. This includes external courses, college qualifications, conferences, and online learning programmes run by specialist training providers.

  • Advantage: Taught by specialists, often leads to recognised qualifications, and brings in new ideas from outside the business
  • Disadvantage: Expensive (course fees, travel, accommodation) and the employee is not working while they train
Key Fact

Most businesses use a mix of on-the-job and off-the-job training. New starters often receive on-the-job induction training to learn the basics, then attend off-the-job courses later to develop specialist skills or gain qualifications.

Benefits of Training

  • Improved skills and productivity — trained employees work faster and make fewer mistakes
  • Better quality — staff who understand proper techniques produce higher-quality goods and services
  • Staff feel valued — investing in employees shows the business cares about their development, which improves loyalty and reduces staff turnover
  • Legal compliance — some training is legally required, such as health and safety, food hygiene, and data protection
  • Flexibility — multi-skilled staff can cover different roles, making the business more adaptable

Development

Development goes beyond training. While training focuses on the skills needed for an employee’s current job, development is about the long-term growth of an employee’s skills and abilities to prepare them for future roles and responsibilities.

Examples include leadership programmes, mentoring schemes, secondments to other departments, and supporting employees through professional qualifications. Development benefits the business because it creates a pipeline of capable people ready to step into more senior roles, reducing the cost of external recruitment.

What Is Motivation?

Motivation is the desire and willingness of employees to work hard and perform well. Motivated staff are more productive, produce better quality work, take fewer days off sick, and are less likely to leave the business.

Key Fact

Low motivation is expensive. It leads to higher absenteeism, higher staff turnover (which means more spending on recruitment and training), lower productivity, and poorer customer service. Businesses that invest in motivation often find the benefits far outweigh the costs.

Financial Methods of Motivation

Financial methods use money or monetary rewards to encourage employees to work harder:

  • Salary — a fixed annual amount paid monthly, giving employees financial security and stability
  • Wages — paid per hour or per item produced; can motivate employees to work more hours or produce more
  • Bonus — a one-off payment for hitting targets or performing well, rewarding extra effort
  • Commission — a percentage of each sale made, common in retail and estate agency; directly rewards selling effort
  • Profit sharing — employees receive a share of the company’s profits, giving them a direct stake in the business’s success
  • Fringe benefits — non-cash perks such as a company car, private healthcare, gym membership, or staff discounts

Non-Financial Methods of Motivation

Non-financial methods motivate employees without relying on money. They focus on making work more interesting, fulfilling, and rewarding:

  • Job rotation — moving employees between different tasks to reduce boredom and develop a wider range of skills
  • Job enrichment — redesigning a job to include more challenging, meaningful tasks and greater responsibility, making the work more satisfying
  • Empowerment — giving employees the authority to make decisions about their own work, which builds trust and confidence
  • Teamwork — organising employees into teams so they can support each other and share ideas, creating a sense of belonging
  • Recognition — praising good work publicly, “employee of the month” awards, or simply saying thank you — costs nothing but can be very effective

Motivation Theories

Several theorists have tried to explain what motivates people at work. The Edexcel specification focuses on three:

F.W. Taylor — Scientific Management

Taylor believed that workers are primarily motivated by money. He argued that managers should break jobs down into small, repetitive tasks and pay workers per item produced (piece rate). Workers who produced more would earn more, so they would work harder. This approach treats employees as an economic resource — it ignores the fact that people also want interesting work, good relationships, and a sense of purpose.

Abraham Maslow — Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow argued that people have five levels of needs, arranged in a hierarchy. Lower-level needs must be met before higher-level needs become motivating:

  • Physiological — basic survival needs (food, water, shelter) — met by earning a wage
  • Safety — job security, a safe working environment, a contract
  • Social — belonging, teamwork, friendships at work
  • Esteem — recognition, respect, promotion, status
  • Self-actualisation — reaching your full potential, personal growth, creative fulfilment

According to Maslow, once a need is satisfied it no longer motivates. A well-paid employee with a secure contract will not be further motivated by more money — they need social belonging, recognition, or opportunities for growth.

Frederick Herzberg — Two-Factor Theory

Herzberg identified two types of factors that affect motivation:

  • Hygiene factors — things like pay, working conditions, company policy, and job security. If these are poor, employees become dissatisfied. But improving them only removes dissatisfaction — it does not actively motivate.
  • Motivators — things like achievement, recognition, responsibility, and interesting work. These are what truly motivate employees to work harder and perform better.
Key Fact

Herzberg’s key insight is that money alone does not motivate. Pay is a hygiene factor — employees expect fair pay, and they will be unhappy without it, but giving someone a pay rise does not make them love their job. Real motivation comes from the work itself: challenge, responsibility, recognition, and growth.

Google is famous for its approach to employee motivation. Staff receive competitive salaries and generous fringe benefits (free meals, on-site gyms, healthcare) — addressing Herzberg’s hygiene factors and Maslow’s lower-level needs. But Google also empowers employees with “20% time” — allowing them to spend one day a week on personal projects. Gmail and Google Maps both started as 20% time projects. This addresses Maslow’s self-actualisation and Herzberg’s motivators (achievement, creativity). The result is low staff turnover and high innovation.

Exam Tip

When discussing motivation in the exam, always consider both financial and non-financial methods. A common mistake is assuming that money is always the best motivator. Use the theories to support your answer — for example, Herzberg would argue that a pay rise only removes dissatisfaction, while job enrichment creates genuine motivation. Always apply your answer to the specific business in the case study.

Conclusion

Training, development, and motivation are closely linked. A well-trained workforce is more productive, and employees who feel invested in are more motivated. The best businesses combine financial rewards with non-financial methods to get the most from their people.

Key Takeaways

  • Training can be on-the-job (cheap, practical) or off-the-job (specialist, often leads to qualifications). Most businesses use both.
  • Financial methods (salary, bonus, commission, profit sharing) and non-financial methods (job enrichment, empowerment, teamwork) both play a role in motivation.
  • Motivation theories: Taylor said money motivates; Maslow said people have a hierarchy of needs; Herzberg said hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction but only motivators create real drive.
