Globalisation

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

What Is Globalisation?

Globalisation is the increasing interconnection of the world’s economies. It means that businesses, consumers, and governments are more linked across national borders than ever before.

Globalisation has been driven by:

  • Improvements in technology — the internet and digital communication make it easy to buy, sell, and communicate across the world
  • Cheaper transport — container shipping and air freight have made it affordable to move goods long distances
  • Removal of trade barriers — many countries have reduced tariffs and joined trade blocs, making international trade easier
  • Growth of multinational companies — large businesses now operate in dozens of countries, sourcing materials and selling products globally
Key Fact

Globalisation means that a product you buy in the UK may contain raw materials from Africa, be assembled in China, and be designed in the United States. Supply chains now stretch across the entire world.

Imports and Exports

Imports are goods or services bought from another country and brought into the UK. Exports are goods or services produced in the UK and sold to customers abroad.

International trade matters for UK businesses because:

  • Importing allows businesses to source cheaper raw materials or products that are not available domestically
  • Exporting opens up much larger markets — a business that only sells in the UK is limited to around 67 million customers, but exporting gives access to billions
  • The UK imports more than it exports, meaning the country has a trade deficit

Barriers to International Trade

Although globalisation has made trade easier, there are still significant barriers that businesses face when trading internationally.

Tariffs

A tariff is a tax placed on imported goods by a government. Tariffs make imported products more expensive, which protects domestic businesses from cheaper foreign competition. However, they also raise costs for businesses that rely on imported raw materials.

Trade Blocs

A trade bloc is a group of countries that agree to reduce or remove trade barriers between themselves. The European Union (EU) single market is a major example — member countries can trade with each other without tariffs or quotas. However, countries outside the bloc often face higher barriers when trying to sell into it.

Other Barriers

  • Language and cultural differences — marketing messages, packaging, and customer service may need to be adapted for different countries
  • Legal differences — different countries have different laws on product safety, employment, and advertising
  • Logistics — shipping goods internationally is slower and more complex than domestic delivery

Since leaving the EU single market in 2021, UK businesses face new paperwork and checks when exporting to Europe. Some small businesses have found the extra cost and red tape too expensive and have stopped selling to EU customers. At the same time, the UK has signed new trade deals with countries such as Australia and Japan. The impact of Brexit on UK trade is still unfolding, but it is a clear example of how trade barriers affect businesses.

Exchange Rates

The exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. For example, if £1 = $1.25, then one British pound buys 1.25 US dollars.

Exchange rates change constantly. These changes have a direct impact on the cost of imports and exports.

Strong Pound

When the pound is strong (its value rises against other currencies), each pound buys more foreign currency. This means:

  • Imports become cheaper — UK businesses pay less for raw materials and products from abroad
  • Exports become more expensive — UK goods cost more for foreign customers, which can reduce demand

Weak Pound

When the pound is weak (its value falls against other currencies), the opposite happens:

  • Imports become more expensive — UK businesses pay more for foreign supplies, raising costs
  • Exports become cheaper — UK goods are more affordable for foreign buyers, which can boost demand
Key Fact

A simple way to remember: SPICED — Strong Pound, Imports Cheap, Exports Dear. A strong pound helps importers but hurts exporters.

How Businesses Compete Internationally

To succeed in global markets, UK businesses must find ways to stand out against competitors from all over the world. Key strategies include:

  • Product quality — producing high-quality goods builds customer loyalty and justifies premium pricing. “Made in Britain” is associated with quality in many overseas markets.
  • Competitive pricing — keeping costs low through economies of scale or efficient production allows businesses to price competitively against foreign rivals
  • Brand reputation — a strong, trusted brand can attract customers even in crowded international markets. Brands like Burberry and Dyson sell worldwide because of their reputation.
  • Use of technology — e-commerce platforms and social media marketing allow even small UK businesses to reach global customers at low cost

Impact of Globalisation on UK Businesses

Globalisation creates both opportunities and threats for UK businesses.

Wider market opportunities: UK businesses can sell to customers around the world, not just domestically. This is especially valuable for niche products with limited UK demand.

Access to cheaper suppliers: Businesses can source raw materials and components from countries where production costs are lower, reducing their overall costs and improving profit margins.

Economies of scale: Selling to a global market means higher output, which spreads fixed costs over more units and reduces the cost per unit.

Increased competition: UK businesses now compete against companies from all over the world, including those in countries with much lower labour costs. This puts pressure on prices and profit margins.

Risk of losing domestic market share: Foreign competitors can enter the UK market and take customers away from local businesses. For example, many UK high street retailers have lost market share to global online giants.

Exchange rate risk: Businesses that trade internationally are exposed to currency fluctuations that can suddenly increase costs or reduce revenue.

Dyson is a UK-based technology company that designs vacuum cleaners, hand dryers, and haircare products. The company sells in over 80 countries, and around 95% of its revenue comes from outside the UK. Dyson moved its manufacturing to Malaysia to take advantage of lower production costs and to be closer to fast-growing Asian markets. This shows how globalisation allows UK businesses to source, manufacture, and sell internationally — but it also led to criticism about moving jobs abroad.

Exam Tip

In questions about globalisation, always think about both sides. Globalisation creates opportunities (bigger markets, cheaper supplies) but also threats (more competition, exchange rate risk). The best answers explain how these factors affect the specific business in the question — a small local bakery is affected differently from a large multinational like Dyson.

Conclusion

Globalisation means the world’s economies are increasingly connected. UK businesses can sell abroad and source from cheaper suppliers, but they also face tougher competition and exchange rate risks.

Key Takeaways

  • Imports and exports are the foundation of international trade — importing brings in goods from abroad, exporting sends them out.
  • Exchange rates directly affect costs and revenues — remember SPICED: Strong Pound, Imports Cheap, Exports Dear.
  • Globalisation creates opportunities (wider markets, cheaper suppliers) and threats (more competition, risk of losing domestic market share).
