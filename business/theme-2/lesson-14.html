Organisational Structures & Recruitment

Organisational Structure

Every business needs a structure that shows who is in charge, who reports to whom, and how decisions are made. An hierarchy is the system of levels within a business, with the most senior people at the top and the least senior at the bottom. This is usually shown in an organisational chart.

Two key concepts describe how a hierarchy works:

  • Chain of command — the line of authority from the top of the organisation to the bottom. Instructions flow downwards and accountability flows upwards.
  • Span of control — the number of employees a manager is directly responsible for. A manager with eight people reporting to them has a span of control of eight.

Tall vs Flat Structures

Tall Structures

A tall structure has many layers of hierarchy and a narrow span of control. Each manager supervises only a small number of employees. Large organisations like the NHS or the army often use tall structures.

  • Advantage: Clear roles and responsibilities — every employee knows exactly who their line manager is
  • Advantage: Close supervision means tight quality control and fewer mistakes
  • Disadvantage: Slow communication — messages must pass through many levels, which can cause delays and distortion
  • Disadvantage: Expensive — more managers means higher wage costs

Flat Structures

A flat structure has few layers of hierarchy and a wide span of control. Each manager oversees a larger number of employees. Start-ups and small businesses often use flat structures.

  • Advantage: Faster communication — fewer layers mean decisions can be made and shared more quickly
  • Advantage: Employees may feel more empowered and motivated because they have greater responsibility
  • Disadvantage: Managers can become overloaded with too many subordinates to supervise effectively
  • Disadvantage: Fewer promotion opportunities, which may reduce motivation for ambitious employees
Key Fact

Many businesses have been “delayering” — removing layers of middle management to create flatter structures. This cuts costs and speeds up decision-making, but it means remaining managers have a wider span of control.

Centralised vs Decentralised Structures

Centralised

In a centralised structure, all important decisions are made by senior managers at the top of the organisation. Local branches or departments follow the instructions they are given.

  • Advantage: Consistent decisions across the whole business — customers get the same experience everywhere
  • Advantage: Senior managers have an overview of the whole organisation and can coordinate strategy
  • Disadvantage: Slow to respond to local conditions — a branch manager cannot react quickly without head office approval
  • Disadvantage: Lower-level employees may feel demotivated because they have no say in decisions

Decentralised

In a decentralised structure, decision-making is delegated to managers at a local or departmental level. Each branch or team has the authority to make its own choices.

  • Advantage: Faster decisions — local managers can respond quickly to changes in their market without waiting for head office
  • Advantage: Motivating for employees — being trusted with decisions increases job satisfaction
  • Disadvantage: Inconsistency — different branches may make different decisions, leading to an uneven customer experience
  • Disadvantage: Local managers may lack the experience or knowledge to make the best strategic decisions

McDonald’s uses a largely centralised structure — the brand, menu, and operational standards are controlled from head office to ensure consistency worldwide. However, it also allows some decentralisation: franchisees in different countries can adapt the menu to local tastes. In India, the McAloo Tikki burger replaced beef options, while in Japan, the Teriyaki McBurger is a best-seller. This blend of centralised control with decentralised flexibility is common in large multinational businesses.

The Recruitment Process

Recruitment is the process of finding and hiring the right person for a job vacancy. Getting it right is essential — hiring the wrong person wastes time and money, while a strong hire can transform a team.

The typical recruitment process follows these steps:

Step 1

Identify the vacancy

The business recognises that a role needs to be filled — either a new position or a replacement for someone who has left.

Step 2

Write a job description & person specification

The job description lists the duties and responsibilities of the role. The person specification lists the qualifications, skills, and personal qualities the ideal candidate should have.

Step 3

Advertise the position

The vacancy is advertised internally (within the business) or externally (job websites, social media, newspapers, recruitment agencies).

Step 4

Shortlist candidates

Applications are reviewed and the strongest candidates are selected for interview based on the person specification.

Step 5

Interview & select

Shortlisted candidates attend interviews, assessments, or practical tests. The best candidate is chosen.

Step 6

Offer the job

The successful candidate receives a formal job offer, and the employment contract is agreed.

Internal vs External Recruitment

Internal Recruitment

Internal recruitment means filling a vacancy with someone who already works for the business — through promotion or transfer to a new role.

  • Advantage: Cheaper and faster — no need to advertise externally or run a lengthy selection process
  • Advantage: The candidate already knows the business, its culture, and its systems, so they settle in quickly
  • Disadvantage: Limits the pool of candidates — a better-qualified person outside the business may be missed
  • Disadvantage: Creates another vacancy that still needs to be filled

External Recruitment

External recruitment means hiring someone from outside the business. This can involve job adverts, recruitment agencies, social media, or headhunting.

  • Advantage: Wider pool of candidates — more likely to find the best person for the role
  • Advantage: New employees bring fresh ideas, perspectives, and skills that existing staff may not have
  • Disadvantage: More expensive — advertising, agency fees, and a longer selection process all cost money
  • Disadvantage: Higher risk — the business does not know the candidate as well, so they may not fit the culture
Key Fact

In the exam, the best recruitment method depends on the role. Senior or specialist roles often need external recruitment to find the right expertise. Promotion for lower or mid-level roles rewards loyalty and motivates existing staff.

Types of Employment

Businesses use different types of employment contracts depending on the role, the work pattern, and the needs of the business:

  • Full-time — typically 35–40 hours per week. The employee is available throughout the working week, making them ideal for core roles. However, full-time staff are more expensive in terms of salary and benefits.
  • Part-time — fewer hours than full-time (e.g. 15–20 hours per week). Useful for businesses that need extra cover at peak times, such as evenings or weekends. Lower cost, but may cause communication difficulties if part-time staff miss meetings.
  • Permanent — an ongoing contract with no set end date. Gives the employee job security and the business a stable workforce, but makes it harder to reduce staff numbers if demand falls.
  • Temporary — a contract for a fixed period (e.g. six months or to cover maternity leave). Flexible for the business, but temporary workers may be less committed or motivated.
  • Freelance / self-employed — the worker is hired for a specific project or task and is not a permanent employee. The business avoids paying National Insurance and employee benefits, but has less control over when and how the work is done.

The growth of app-based platforms like Uber and Deliveroo has created the “gig economy”, where workers are treated as self-employed and paid per task (or “gig”) rather than receiving a regular salary. This gives businesses flexibility and lower costs, but workers often lack sick pay, holiday pay, and job security. The gig economy has sparked debate about whether these workers should have more employment rights.

Exam Tip

When answering questions about organisational structure, always link your points to the size and type of business. A small start-up benefits from a flat, decentralised structure for speed and flexibility. A large multinational like Tesco needs taller, more centralised structures to maintain control and consistency across hundreds of stores.

Conclusion

Organisational structure determines how a business communicates, makes decisions, and supervises its employees. Recruitment — whether internal or external — shapes the skills and culture of the workforce.

Key Takeaways

  • Tall structures offer clear authority and close supervision but are slow and costly; flat structures are faster and cheaper but risk overloading managers.
  • Centralised structures ensure consistency; decentralised structures allow faster local responses and motivate employees.
  • Recruitment involves identifying the vacancy, writing a job description and person specification, advertising, shortlisting, interviewing, and offering the job — using internal or external methods depending on the role.
