Quality & Customer Service

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Quality Control

Quality control means inspecting products at the end of the production process to find and remove any faulty items before they reach the customer. Inspectors check a sample — or sometimes every single product — against set standards.

  • Advantage: Simple to set up — only needs trained inspectors at the end of the production line
  • Disadvantage: Faults are only spotted after production is complete, so defective items have already used up materials, time, and labour — creating waste and increasing costs

Quality control is reactive rather than preventive. If a whole batch is faulty, the business may have to scrap or rework every item, which is expensive and slows down delivery to customers.

Quality Assurance

Quality assurance takes a different approach. Instead of checking products only at the end, quality checks are built into every stage of production. Each worker or team is responsible for the quality of their own work before passing it to the next stage.

This means defects are caught and fixed early, reducing waste and ensuring a consistently high standard. Workers take more ownership of quality, which can improve motivation.

Total Quality Management (TQM)

Total Quality Management (TQM) is the most thorough form of quality assurance. It means that every employee — from the factory floor to senior management — is responsible for quality. Quality is not just a production task; it becomes part of the business culture.

  • Advantage: Reduces waste, improves consistency, and builds a strong reputation for reliability
  • Disadvantage: Expensive and time-consuming to implement — requires training for all staff and a genuine culture change
Key Fact

Quality control checks products after production (detecting faults). Quality assurance checks during production (preventing faults). TQM makes quality everyone’s responsibility across the whole organisation.

The Cost of Poor Quality

Selling poor-quality products or delivering a bad experience has serious consequences for a business:

  • Returns and refunds — customers send products back, costing the business money and stock
  • Bad reviews — negative online reviews and social media posts can spread quickly, putting off new customers
  • Loss of repeat business — unhappy customers will not buy again, reducing long-term revenue
  • Damage to brand reputation — once a brand is known for poor quality, it is very hard to rebuild trust
  • Legal costs — if products are unsafe or not fit for purpose, the business may face legal action under the Consumer Rights Act 2015

Customer Service

Customer service is the support a business gives to its customers at every stage of the buying process. Good customer service makes people feel valued and builds loyalty.

Before the Sale

Before customers buy, businesses can provide helpful product information, expert advice, and clear pricing. A well-designed website, knowledgeable staff, and prompt responses to enquiries all help customers make confident purchasing decisions.

During the Sale

During the purchase, customers expect helpful, friendly staff, easy payment options, and a smooth buying experience — whether online or in store. Long queues, confusing checkouts, or unhelpful staff can drive customers away.

After the Sale

After-sale service includes handling returns and complaints efficiently, honouring warranties, providing follow-up support, and checking customer satisfaction. A business that resolves complaints well can actually turn a disappointed customer into a loyal one.

Good vs Poor Customer Service

The quality of customer service directly affects a business’s success:

  • Good customer service leads to repeat purchases, positive word-of-mouth recommendations, a strong brand reputation, and higher sales revenue
  • Poor customer service leads to lost customers, negative reviews, declining sales, and difficulty attracting new customers — especially in competitive markets where rivals offer a better experience
Key Fact

It costs a business around five times more to attract a new customer than to keep an existing one. Good customer service is one of the cheapest and most effective ways to build long-term revenue.

The Role of ICT in Customer Service

Technology has given businesses new ways to communicate with customers and resolve issues quickly:

  • Online chat and chatbots — provide instant answers to common questions on the business’s website, 24 hours a day
  • Social media responses — businesses can respond publicly to customer complaints and queries on platforms like X (Twitter), Instagram, and Facebook, showing they care about their customers
  • CRM systems — Customer Relationship Management software stores customer data such as purchase history, preferences, and past complaints, allowing businesses to personalise service and track interactions
  • Automated emails — order confirmations, delivery updates, and follow-up satisfaction surveys keep customers informed without needing staff to send each message manually

Amazon built its brand around customer service. The company makes returns simple and fast — often issuing refunds before the returned item has even arrived back. Its CRM system tracks every purchase and interaction, allowing personalised recommendations. Amazon’s customer reviews system means buyers can make informed decisions, and the company actively removes fake reviews to maintain trust. Prime membership offers fast, free delivery as an additional service. This relentless focus on the customer experience is a major reason Amazon became the world’s largest online retailer — customers trust that problems will be resolved quickly and fairly.

Exam Tip

When answering questions on quality or customer service, always link your answer back to the impact on the business. Do not just describe what quality control or good customer service looks like — explain how it affects sales, reputation, costs, or repeat business. Use the context of the business in the question to make your answer specific.

Conclusion

Quality and customer service work together to shape how customers view a business. Getting them right builds loyalty and reputation; getting them wrong is expensive and hard to recover from.

Key Takeaways

  • Quality control inspects finished products; quality assurance (including TQM) prevents defects by checking at every stage of production.
  • Poor quality leads to returns, bad reviews, and lost customers — damaging both revenue and brand reputation.
  • Good customer service before, during, and after the sale builds loyalty, and ICT tools like CRM systems and social media help businesses deliver it efficiently.
