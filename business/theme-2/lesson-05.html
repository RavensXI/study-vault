Product Design & the Product Life Cycle

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Design Mix

When a business designs a product, it has to consider three factors at the same time. These three factors are known as the design mix: function, aesthetics, and cost. Getting the balance right is essential — a product that looks good but does not work will fail, and a product that works well but costs too much to make will not be profitable.

Function

Function means how well the product does its job. A phone must make calls and run apps reliably. A coat must keep the wearer warm and dry. If a product does not perform its basic function, customers will not buy it again and will leave negative reviews that put off other buyers.

Aesthetics

Aesthetics refers to how the product looks, feels, and appeals to the senses. This includes shape, colour, texture, and packaging. In many markets — especially fashion, technology, and food — aesthetics are a major reason why customers choose one product over another. Apple’s products are a well-known example: they function well, but their sleek, minimalist design is a key part of why customers are willing to pay premium prices.

Cost

Cost refers to how much the product costs to manufacture. Businesses must design products that can be made at a cost low enough to sell at a competitive price while still earning a profit. Using cheaper materials or simpler production methods can reduce costs, but this may compromise function or aesthetics.

Key Fact

The design mix is always a trade-off. A luxury car prioritises aesthetics and function over cost. A budget supermarket’s own-brand products prioritise low cost over aesthetics. The right balance depends on the target market and the business’s positioning.

The Product Life Cycle

Every product goes through a series of stages from the moment it is developed to the point where it is eventually withdrawn from sale. This journey is called the product life cycle. Understanding these stages helps businesses plan their marketing, pricing, and investment decisions.

1. Development

The product is researched, designed, and tested before launch. Sales are zero because the product is not yet on the market. Costs are high due to research and development spending. Many products never make it past this stage — the idea may prove too expensive to produce or market research may show insufficient demand.

2. Introduction

The product is launched onto the market. Sales are low because customers are not yet aware of it. Marketing costs are high as the business promotes the product to build awareness. Cash flow is usually negative at this stage because spending on promotion outweighs the revenue coming in from early sales.

3. Growth

Sales rise rapidly as more customers become aware of and start buying the product. The product begins to generate profit as revenue overtakes costs. Competitors may notice the product’s success and launch rival versions, so the business may need to invest in further marketing or product improvements to maintain its lead.

4. Maturity

Sales reach their peak and then level off. The market is saturated — most potential customers already own the product or a competitor’s version. Profit is at its highest because development costs have been recovered and marketing spending can be reduced. Competition is intense, and businesses may cut prices to hold onto market share.

5. Decline

Sales start to fall. This could happen because customer tastes have changed, newer products have replaced it, or the technology has become outdated. The business must decide whether to try to extend the product’s life, sell off remaining stock cheaply, or withdraw the product entirely.

Apple’s iPod is a textbook example of the product life cycle. It was developed in secret and launched in 2001 (introduction). Sales grew rapidly as digital music replaced CDs (growth), peaking around 2008 with over 50 million units sold per year (maturity). After the iPhone launched in 2007, the iPod entered decline because customers could play music on their phones instead. Apple tried extension strategies — the iPod Touch, iPod Nano, and iPod Shuffle — but eventually discontinued the product in 2022.

Extension Strategies

When a product enters maturity or begins to decline, a business can use extension strategies to boost sales and delay the decline stage. The aim is to keep the product profitable for as long as possible.

Common extension strategies include:

  • New packaging or design — updating the look of the product to make it feel fresh and attract new buyers
  • Targeting new markets — selling the product to a different customer group or in a new geographical area (e.g. launching in another country)
  • Price reductions — lowering the price to attract price-sensitive customers who would not have bought at the original price
  • New advertising campaigns — increasing or changing promotion to remind customers about the product or reach new audiences
  • Product updates or improvements — adding new features, flavours, or variations to give customers a reason to buy again
Key Fact

Coca-Cola is one of the best examples of successful extension strategies. The original recipe has been on sale since 1886, but the company regularly launches new variants (Diet Coke, Coke Zero, Cherry Coke), updates its packaging, and runs major advertising campaigns to keep the brand relevant.

Product Differentiation

Product differentiation means making a product different from those offered by competitors. In a crowded market, businesses need a clear reason for customers to choose their product over a rival’s.

Ways to differentiate a product include:

  • Unique design — a distinctive look or shape that is instantly recognisable (e.g. the Dyson bladeless fan)
  • Superior features — offering functions that competitors do not, such as a longer battery life or extra accessories
  • Higher quality — using better materials or more careful manufacturing to produce a more durable or reliable product
  • Strong branding — building an image, reputation, or emotional connection that makes customers loyal (e.g. Nike’s “Just Do It” identity)

Effective differentiation allows a business to charge higher prices because customers perceive the product as better or more desirable than alternatives. It also helps build brand loyalty, meaning customers come back to buy again rather than switching to a competitor.

Advantages:

  • Allows the business to charge premium prices and earn higher profit margins
  • Builds customer loyalty — buyers are less likely to switch to a competitor
  • Reduces price competition — customers are buying for reasons beyond just price
  • Creates a unique selling point that can be used in marketing

Disadvantages:

  • Research, design, and quality materials increase costs
  • Competitors may copy successful features over time
  • Customers’ tastes change, so differentiation must be maintained and updated
  • Heavy investment in branding and marketing is needed to communicate the differences
Exam Tip

When a question asks about the product life cycle, always consider which stage the product is at before recommending a strategy. A product in growth needs different actions (invest in production, expand distribution) than a product in decline (extension strategies or withdrawal). Use the case study context to identify the stage and justify your answer.

Conclusion

Product design is a balancing act between function, aesthetics, and cost. Every product follows a life cycle from development through to decline, and businesses use extension strategies to keep products profitable for as long as possible.

Key Takeaways

  • The design mix requires businesses to balance function (does it work?), aesthetics (does it look good?), and cost (can we make it affordably?) for their target market.
  • The product life cycle has five stages — development, introduction, growth, maturity, and decline — each with different implications for sales, profit, and marketing.
  • Product differentiation helps a business stand out from competitors and can justify higher prices, but it requires ongoing investment.
