Production Methods

Every business that makes a physical product must decide how to produce it. The method of production affects costs, quality, and how quickly goods can be made.

Job Production

Job production means making one unique item at a time, tailored to a customer’s specific requirements. Examples include wedding cakes, bespoke furniture, and custom-built websites.

  • Advantage: High quality, unique products that customers are willing to pay a premium for
  • Disadvantage: Slow and expensive — labour-intensive and cannot benefit from economies of scale

Batch Production

Batch production means making a set quantity of identical products before switching to a different product. A bakery might produce 200 white loaves, then switch to making 100 brown loaves.

  • Advantage: Flexible — can produce different products using the same equipment; some economies of scale
  • Disadvantage: Downtime when switching between batches; goods must be stored, increasing costs

Flow Production

Flow production (also called mass production) means making identical products continuously on an assembly line. Products move from one stage to the next without stopping. Car factories and bottling plants use flow production.

  • Advantage: Very low cost per unit due to economies of scale; fast and consistent output
  • Disadvantage: Very expensive to set up; products are standardised with little variety; breakdowns stop the entire line
Key Fact

The best production method depends on the product and market. Job production suits unique, high-value products. Batch production suits businesses that make several related products. Flow production suits high-volume, standardised goods where cost per unit must be as low as possible.

Lean Production

Lean production is an approach that aims to reduce waste in all forms — wasted time, wasted materials, and wasted effort. The goal is to produce goods as efficiently as possible.

Just-in-Time (JIT)

Just-in-time (JIT) means ordering materials only when they are needed for production, so the business holds very little stock. This reduces storage costs and waste from unsold stock.

  • Advantage: Saves money on storage; reduces waste from expired or outdated stock
  • Disadvantage: Relies on suppliers being extremely reliable; any delay stops production entirely

Just-in-Case (JIC)

Just-in-case (JIC) is the opposite approach — holding large amounts of stock to ensure the business never runs out. This is more traditional but ties up cash in unsold inventory.

The Role of Technology in Production

Technology has transformed how businesses produce goods:

  • Automation — machines and robots perform repetitive tasks faster and more consistently than humans, reducing labour costs and errors
  • Computer-aided design (CAD) — software that allows designers to create and modify product designs digitally before manufacturing
  • Computer-aided manufacturing (CAM) — using computers to control machinery during production, improving precision and speed
  • 3D printing — creating products layer by layer from a digital design, useful for prototyping and small-batch production

Toyota pioneered lean production with the “Toyota Production System” in the 1950s. The company uses JIT delivery so parts arrive at the assembly line only when needed, dramatically reducing warehouse costs. Workers are empowered to stop the production line if they spot a defect (called “andon”), fixing problems immediately rather than producing faulty cars. Toyota also practises “kaizen” — continuous improvement — where every employee is encouraged to suggest small improvements. This approach helped Toyota become the world’s largest car manufacturer by keeping costs low and quality high.

Exam Tip

When asked to recommend a production method, always consider what the business makes and who it sells to. A jeweller making engagement rings needs job production. A crisp factory needs flow production. Justify your choice by linking it to the product type, demand level, and budget of the business in the question.

Conclusion

Production methods range from job (one-off, bespoke) to batch (groups) to flow (continuous). Lean production and technology help businesses reduce waste and improve efficiency.

Key Takeaways

  • Three production methods: job (unique, expensive), batch (flexible, moderate cost), flow (mass-produced, lowest unit cost).
  • Lean production minimises waste — JIT reduces stock costs but depends on reliable suppliers.
  • Technology (automation, CAD, CAM) improves speed, quality, and consistency but requires significant investment.
