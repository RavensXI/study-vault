Changing Aims & Objectives

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Why Aims and Objectives Change

In Theme 1 you learned that businesses set aims (long-term goals) and objectives (specific, measurable targets). But these do not stay the same forever. As a business grows, its circumstances change — and its aims and objectives need to change with them.

A start-up might aim simply to survive its first year. Once established, the same business might shift its focus to growth, profit maximisation, or expanding into new markets.

Internal Factors That Cause Change

Changes within the business itself can shift its priorities:

  • Business performance — if profits are higher than expected, the business may set more ambitious growth targets. If profits are falling, the focus may shift to cutting costs or improving efficiency
  • Change of ownership or leadership — a new owner or manager may have different priorities. For example, a family-run business sold to a large corporation may shift from community focus to profit maximisation
  • Business size — as a business grows from a sole trader to a limited company, its objectives often become more complex. A larger business may add objectives around market share, international expansion, or corporate social responsibility
  • Workforce changes — recruiting skilled staff or losing key employees can change what the business is capable of achieving

External Factors That Cause Change

Changes in the business environment outside the company’s control can also force a rethink:

  • Market conditions — a recession may force a business to focus on survival rather than growth. A booming economy may encourage expansion
  • Competition — a new competitor entering the market may push a business to focus on customer retention or price competitiveness
  • Technology — new technology can create opportunities (e.g. selling online) or threats (e.g. automation replacing workers), changing what the business aims to do
  • Legislation — new laws (e.g. environmental regulations) may force a business to change its practices and set new objectives around compliance
  • Social trends — changing consumer attitudes (e.g. demand for sustainable products) may shift a business towards ethical or environmental objectives
Key Fact

Aims and objectives are not fixed. They evolve as a business moves through different stages of its life — from start-up (survive) to growth (expand) to maturity (maintain market position) to possible decline (cut costs or reinvent).

How Objectives Differ Between Businesses

Different businesses prioritise different objectives depending on their situation:

  • Start-ups often focus on survival — generating enough cash to cover costs and stay in business
  • Growing businesses often focus on sales growth or market share — building a customer base and competing with rivals
  • Established businesses may focus on profit maximisation, shareholder returns, or diversification
  • Social enterprises may prioritise social or environmental objectives over profit — such as reducing poverty or cutting carbon emissions

Outdoor clothing company Patagonia started with a simple aim: to make the best climbing equipment. Over time, as the business grew and the founder became more aware of environmental damage, the company’s objectives shifted dramatically. Patagonia now prioritises environmental sustainability — donating 1% of sales to environmental causes, using recycled materials, and even telling customers to buy less. In 2022, the founder transferred ownership of the company to a trust dedicated to fighting climate change. This shows how a business’s objectives can change radically as the owner’s values and external pressures evolve.

The Impact of Changing Objectives

When a business changes its objectives, it affects every part of the organisation:

  • Marketing — new objectives may require a new target market, different pricing, or updated branding
  • Finance — growth objectives may need additional investment; survival objectives may mean cutting budgets
  • Operations — expanding into new markets may require new suppliers, larger premises, or more efficient production methods
  • Human resources — new objectives may require recruiting new staff, retraining existing employees, or making redundancies
Exam Tip

Questions about changing objectives often give you a scenario and ask why the business’s priorities have shifted. Always identify what changed (internal or external factor) and how it affected the objective. Link your answer to the specific business in the question — generic answers score lower.

Conclusion

Business aims and objectives are not set in stone. They change in response to internal factors (performance, leadership, size) and external factors (economy, competition, technology, legislation).

Key Takeaways

  • Objectives evolve with the business: start-ups focus on survival, growing businesses on sales, and established firms on profit or market share.
  • Internal and external factors both trigger changes — from new leadership to new competitors to changing laws.
  • Changing objectives affects everything: marketing, finance, operations, and human resources all need to adapt.
