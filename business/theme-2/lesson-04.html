Ethics & the Environment

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

What Does “Ethical” Mean in Business?

Ethics in business means doing what is morally right — not just what is legally required. A business might follow the law but still behave unethically. For example, it is legal to pay workers the minimum wage, but some people would argue it is not ethical if the company is making huge profits while its employees struggle to afford basic living costs.

Ethical behaviour includes treating workers fairly, being honest with customers, sourcing materials responsibly, and considering the impact of business decisions on the wider community. Many growing businesses face difficult choices about whether to prioritise ethics or profits.

Key Fact

Ethical decisions go beyond legal compliance. A business can be fully legal but still face public criticism for unethical behaviour — such as using tax loopholes to avoid paying a fair share, or sourcing products from factories with poor working conditions.

Ethical Trade and Fair Wages

Ethical trade means ensuring that the workers who produce goods are treated fairly. This includes paying fair wages, providing safe working conditions, and not using child labour. The Fairtrade mark is one of the most recognised ethical labels. It guarantees that producers in developing countries receive a minimum price for their goods, even if world market prices fall.

Businesses that adopt ethical trade practices often pay more for their raw materials. A coffee shop using Fairtrade beans will have higher costs than one using the cheapest available supplier. However, many customers are willing to pay a premium for products they know have been sourced responsibly.

The Trade-Off Between Ethics and Profit

Ethical choices often increase costs for a business. Paying higher wages, using sustainable materials, or choosing Fairtrade suppliers all eat into profit margins. This creates a trade-off: businesses must decide whether the long-term benefits of ethical behaviour outweigh the short-term cost.

Arguments that ethics reduce profit:

  • Higher costs — Fairtrade ingredients, better wages, and sustainable packaging all cost more than cheaper alternatives
  • Lower short-term profit — money spent on ethical practices cannot be spent on growth or paid to shareholders
  • Competitive disadvantage — rivals who cut corners on ethics can offer lower prices

Arguments that ethics increase profit in the long run:

  • Brand reputation — customers increasingly prefer businesses they trust to act responsibly
  • Customer loyalty — ethical businesses build stronger relationships with their customers, leading to repeat purchases
  • Attracting talent — skilled employees often prefer working for companies with strong ethical values
  • Premium pricing — customers may pay more for ethically sourced products

The Body Shop was founded by Anita Roddick in 1976 with a clear ethical mission: no animal testing, Fairtrade ingredients, and minimal packaging. At a time when most cosmetics companies tested on animals, The Body Shop stood out. Customers were willing to pay higher prices because they trusted the brand’s values. By 2006, the company had over 2,000 stores worldwide and was sold to L’Oréal for £652 million. The Body Shop showed that ethical behaviour can be a powerful unique selling point that drives long-term growth.

Environmental Considerations

Every business has an impact on the environment. As public awareness of climate change and pollution has grown, businesses face increasing pressure to reduce their environmental footprint. Key environmental issues include:

  • Pollution — factories and transport release harmful emissions into the air and water
  • Waste — manufacturing processes create waste that may end up in landfill or the ocean
  • Carbon footprint — the total greenhouse gas emissions caused by a business’s activities, including production, transport, and energy use
  • Packaging — excessive or non-recyclable packaging contributes to waste and pollution
  • Sustainability — using resources at a rate that can be maintained long-term, without depleting them for future generations
Key Fact

Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is the idea that businesses should consider the interests of society and the environment alongside profit. Many large companies publish annual CSR reports showing how they are reducing waste, cutting emissions, and supporting local communities.

Pressure Groups

A pressure group is an organisation that campaigns to change the behaviour of businesses or governments. Pressure groups focus on specific issues — such as the environment, animal welfare, or workers’ rights — and use various methods to achieve their aims.

Methods used by pressure groups include:

  • Protests and demonstrations — drawing public attention to an issue through marches or blockades
  • Social media campaigns — using platforms like Twitter/X and Instagram to spread awareness and put pressure on brands
  • Boycotts — encouraging consumers to stop buying from a business until it changes its behaviour
  • Petitions — collecting signatures to show widespread public concern
  • Lobbying — directly contacting politicians and decision-makers to push for new laws or regulations

In 1995, Greenpeace campaigned against Shell’s plan to dump the Brent Spar oil platform in the North Sea. Greenpeace activists occupied the platform, and the campaign generated huge media coverage across Europe. Consumers in Germany boycotted Shell petrol stations, causing sales to drop by up to 50% at some locations. Shell eventually reversed its decision and dismantled the platform on land instead. This case showed the power of pressure groups to change the behaviour of even the largest multinational companies.

The Impact on Business Decisions

Ethical and environmental considerations affect almost every area of a growing business:

  • Supply chain — choosing ethical suppliers may cost more but protects the brand from scandals
  • Product design — using recyclable materials or reducing packaging adds cost but appeals to environmentally conscious consumers
  • Marketing — businesses can use their ethical stance as a selling point, but must be genuine — “greenwashing” (making false environmental claims) can backfire badly
  • Location — choosing sites that minimise transport emissions and pollution
  • Human resources — paying fair wages and offering good working conditions reduces staff turnover and boosts productivity
Key Fact

Businesses that ignore ethical and environmental concerns risk losing customers, facing boycotts, and damaging their reputation. In the age of social media, bad practices can go viral in hours. On the other hand, genuinely ethical businesses often find that doing good is also good for business.

Advantages:

  • Improved brand image and customer loyalty
  • Ability to charge premium prices
  • Attracts and retains high-quality employees
  • Reduces risk of boycotts, fines, and negative publicity

Disadvantages:

  • Higher costs of production (Fairtrade materials, better wages, sustainable processes)
  • Lower short-term profits compared to less ethical competitors
  • Difficult to verify ethical claims throughout complex global supply chains
  • Some customers may not be willing to pay higher prices, especially in a recession
Exam Tip

In “Discuss” and “Justify” questions about ethics, always consider both sides of the argument. Acknowledge that ethical behaviour increases costs, then explain how it can also benefit the business through reputation, loyalty, and premium pricing. Use the specific business from the case study — do not just write in general terms. The best answers weigh up the trade-off and reach a reasoned judgement.

Conclusion

Ethics and environmental responsibility are no longer optional for growing businesses. Customers, pressure groups, and governments all expect businesses to act responsibly. While ethical practices increase costs, they can also build brand loyalty, justify premium pricing, and protect against reputational damage.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethics vs. profit: Ethical choices cost more in the short term but can strengthen a brand and increase customer loyalty over time.
  • Environmental impact: Businesses must consider pollution, waste, carbon footprint, and sustainability — or risk backlash from consumers and pressure groups.
  • Pressure groups: Organised campaigns, boycotts, and social media pressure can force even large companies to change their behaviour.
