Business Calculations

Gross Profit

Gross profit is the money left over after a business subtracts the direct cost of making or buying its products from its revenue. It shows whether the core trading activity is profitable before considering other expenses.

Key Fact

Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Sales

Cost of sales includes the direct costs of producing or purchasing the goods sold — raw materials, stock bought for resale, and direct labour on the production line. It does not include rent, marketing, or office salaries.

Worked example: A clothing business has revenue of £250,000 and cost of sales of £100,000.

  • Gross profit = £250,000 − £100,000 = £150,000

This tells us the business earns £150,000 from its core trading activity. However, it still needs to pay rent, wages, utilities, and other running costs out of this amount.

Net Profit

Net profit is the profit remaining after all business costs have been deducted, not just the cost of sales. It is the true “bottom line” — what the business actually keeps.

Key Fact

Net Profit = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses

Operating expenses include rent, staff wages, marketing, insurance, utility bills, and any other day-to-day costs not already counted in cost of sales.

Worked example: The same clothing business has operating expenses of £110,000.

  • Net profit = £150,000 − £110,000 = £40,000

A business can have a healthy gross profit but a low (or even negative) net profit if its operating expenses are too high. This is why both figures matter.

Gross Profit Margin

Gross profit margin expresses gross profit as a percentage of revenue. It measures how efficiently a business converts revenue into profit from its core trading activity.

Key Fact

Gross Profit Margin (%) = (Gross Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100

Worked example: Using the clothing business figures — gross profit of £150,000 and revenue of £250,000:

  • Gross profit margin = (£150,000 ÷ £250,000) × 100 = 60%

This means 60p of every £1 of revenue is left after paying for the goods sold. A high gross profit margin suggests the business is good at controlling its production or purchasing costs. A falling gross profit margin could mean materials have become more expensive or the business has been forced to cut prices.

Net Profit Margin

Net profit margin expresses net profit as a percentage of revenue. It measures the overall profitability of the business — how much of each pound of revenue the business actually keeps as profit after all costs.

Key Fact

Net Profit Margin (%) = (Net Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100

Worked example: Net profit of £40,000 and revenue of £250,000:

  • Net profit margin = (£40,000 ÷ £250,000) × 100 = 16%

The business keeps 16p as profit from every £1 of revenue. If the gross profit margin is healthy but the net profit margin is low, the problem lies in operating expenses rather than production costs. The business might need to cut overheads — for example, moving to cheaper premises or reducing marketing spend.

Average Rate of Return (ARR)

Average rate of return (ARR) helps a business decide whether an investment is worthwhile. It calculates the average annual profit from an investment as a percentage of the initial cost, making it easy to compare different options.

Key Fact

ARR (%) = (Average Annual Profit ÷ Cost of Investment) × 100

To find average annual profit, calculate the total profit over the life of the investment and divide by the number of years.

Worked example: A clothing business invests £80,000 in new machinery. Over 4 years it generates total profit of £48,000.

  • Average annual profit = £48,000 ÷ 4 = £12,000
  • ARR = (£12,000 ÷ £80,000) × 100 = 15%

The higher the ARR, the better the return on investment. A business will usually compare the ARR to other investment options or to the interest rate it could earn by leaving the money in a bank. If the ARR is lower than the bank interest rate, the investment may not be worthwhile.

Interpreting Financial Data

Financial calculations are only useful if a business can interpret what the numbers mean and take action. Here are the key things to consider:

  • Compare over time — is the profit margin improving or declining? A falling margin is a warning sign even if revenue is growing
  • Compare against competitors — a 10% net profit margin is strong in supermarkets (where margins are typically 2–5%) but weak in software (where 20%+ is common)
  • Look at both margins together — a high gross profit margin but low net profit margin suggests operating expenses are the problem, not production costs
  • Consider the context — a new business may have low margins while it builds a customer base; a seasonal business may have very different margins at different times of year

UrbanWear is a high-street retailer with revenue of £500,000, cost of sales of £300,000, and operating expenses of £160,000. Its gross profit is £200,000 (40% margin) and net profit is £40,000 (8% margin).

LuxeThread is a premium online brand with revenue of £200,000, cost of sales of £60,000, and operating expenses of £70,000. Its gross profit is £140,000 (70% margin) and net profit is £70,000 (35% margin).

Although UrbanWear has higher revenue, LuxeThread is far more profitable per pound of sales. LuxeThread’s higher gross margin comes from premium pricing, and its lower operating expenses (no physical shop, fewer staff) give it a much stronger net profit margin. UrbanWear might improve by cutting overheads or negotiating lower costs with suppliers.

Exam Tip

Always show your working in calculation questions — even if your final answer is wrong, you can still pick up marks for the correct method. When interpreting figures, don’t just state the number — explain what it means for the business. For example, “a net profit margin of 5% means the business keeps only 5p from every £1 of sales, which suggests its operating costs are too high.”

Conclusion

Financial calculations allow a business to measure profitability, compare performance over time, and make informed investment decisions. Gross and net profit margins reveal different things about business efficiency, and the ARR helps evaluate whether an investment is worth pursuing.

Key Takeaways

  • Gross profit measures core trading profitability (Revenue − Cost of sales); net profit is the true bottom line after all expenses.
  • Profit margins turn raw figures into percentages for comparison — a high gross but low net margin points to high operating expenses.
  • ARR compares investment options by expressing average annual profit as a percentage of the initial cost.
