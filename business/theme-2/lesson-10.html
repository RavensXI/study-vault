Working with Suppliers

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Procurement

Procurement is the process of finding, buying, and receiving goods or services that a business needs from external suppliers. This could be raw materials for manufacturing, components for assembly, or finished products for resale.

Effective procurement matters because the inputs a business buys directly affect the quality of its outputs, its costs, and how quickly it can fulfil customer orders. A business that gets procurement wrong — paying too much, receiving poor-quality materials, or waiting too long for deliveries — will struggle to compete.

Choosing the Right Supplier

Selecting the right supplier is one of the most important decisions a business makes. There are several key factors to consider:

  • Price — lower prices reduce costs and improve profit margins, but the cheapest supplier is not always the best choice
  • Quality — poor-quality materials lead to poor-quality products, more customer complaints, and damage to the brand’s reputation
  • Reliability — can the supplier consistently deliver the right goods, in the right quantities, on time?
  • Flexibility — can the supplier respond quickly if the business needs to increase an order at short notice or change a specification?
  • Location — a nearby supplier means shorter lead times and lower delivery costs, but a distant supplier may offer better prices
  • Capacity — does the supplier have the ability to meet the business’s needs as it grows?
  • Payment terms — generous trade credit (e.g. 60 days to pay) helps the business manage its cash flow
  • Ethical standards — customers increasingly expect businesses to use suppliers that treat workers fairly and minimise environmental harm
Key Fact

There is often a trade-off between price and quality when choosing suppliers. A business must decide which factors matter most based on its target market and competitive strategy. A luxury brand prioritises quality; a budget retailer prioritises price.

Managing Stock

Once a business has chosen its suppliers, it must decide how much stock to hold. Holding too much stock ties up cash and increases storage costs. Holding too little risks running out and losing sales.

There are two main approaches to stock management:

  • Just-in-time (JIT) — ordering stock only when it is needed for production, keeping stock levels very low. This reduces storage costs and waste but depends on suppliers being extremely reliable.
  • Just-in-case (JIC) — holding large amounts of stock as a buffer so the business never runs out. This is safer but more expensive, as cash is tied up in inventory that may not sell quickly.

Most businesses set a minimum stock level — the lowest amount of stock they are willing to hold before reordering. They also track the reorder level (the point at which a new order is placed) and the lead time (how long the supplier takes to deliver).

The Supply Chain

The supply chain is the entire network of businesses and processes involved in getting a product from raw materials to the final customer. It includes suppliers, manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and the transport links between them.

A reliable supply chain means a business can produce goods consistently and meet customer demand. An unreliable supply chain leads to delays, stock shortages, lost sales, and a damaged reputation.

Key Fact

Supply chain disruptions can have serious consequences. During the COVID-19 pandemic, global supply chains were severely disrupted — businesses faced shortages of components, long delays, and rapidly rising costs. This showed how dependent modern businesses are on complex, international supply chains.

Supplier Relationships

Businesses must decide how to manage their relationships with suppliers. There are two broad approaches:

  • Long-term partnerships — building a close, ongoing relationship with a small number of trusted suppliers. This can lead to better prices through bulk discounts, more flexible terms, priority treatment during shortages, and shared innovation. The supplier benefits from guaranteed, regular orders.
  • Shopping around — regularly switching suppliers to find the cheapest price for each order. This keeps costs down but means the business has no loyalty from suppliers, may receive inconsistent quality, and gets no priority if demand surges.

Many businesses use a mix of both approaches — maintaining long-term partnerships for critical materials while shopping around for less important supplies.

The Role of Logistics

Logistics is about getting the right goods to the right place at the right time. It covers transport, warehousing, and distribution — everything that happens between a product leaving the supplier and reaching the customer.

Good logistics management helps a business:

  • Reduce delivery times and meet customer expectations
  • Minimise transport and storage costs
  • Keep stock levels balanced — enough to meet demand without overstocking
  • Respond quickly to changes in demand or supply chain disruptions

Zara, the Spanish fashion retailer, has built its success on an exceptionally fast supply chain. While most fashion brands take 6–9 months to get a new design from concept to shop floor, Zara can do it in just 2–3 weeks. The company keeps much of its manufacturing close to its headquarters in Spain and Portugal rather than outsourcing to Asia. This means shorter lead times and the ability to respond almost instantly to new fashion trends. Zara deliberately produces small batches and restocks frequently, creating a sense of scarcity that encourages customers to buy quickly. The result is less unsold stock, lower markdowns, and a reputation for always having something new in store.

Exam Tip

When discussing suppliers in an exam, always link back to the type of business in the question. A manufacturer using JIT needs suppliers that are reliable and nearby. A retailer selling luxury goods needs suppliers that deliver high quality. Show the examiner you understand that the “best” supplier depends on the business’s specific needs and strategy.

Conclusion

Working effectively with suppliers is essential for keeping costs down, maintaining quality, and ensuring products reach customers on time. The right supplier relationships and stock management approach depend on what the business sells and how it competes.

Key Takeaways

  • Choosing suppliers involves balancing price, quality, reliability, flexibility, and ethical standards — the cheapest option is not always the best.
  • Stock management is a trade-off between holding too much (costly) and too little (risky) — JIT and JIC represent opposite ends of this spectrum.
  • Strong supplier relationships can provide better prices, priority treatment, and shared innovation, but require commitment from both sides.
