Business Growth

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Why Businesses Want to Grow

Most business owners want their business to grow over time. Business growth means increasing in size — whether that is measured by revenue, profit, number of employees, or market share.

Businesses want to grow because it can lead to:

  • Higher profits — more sales typically means more profit, benefiting owners and shareholders
  • Economies of scale — larger businesses can reduce their cost per unit by buying in bulk or spreading fixed costs over more output
  • Increased market share — a bigger share of the market makes it harder for competitors to challenge the business
  • Greater security — larger businesses are less likely to fail because they have more resources to survive difficult periods

Organic Growth

Organic growth (also called internal growth) happens when a business expands using its own resources. This is the most common way small businesses grow.

Methods of organic growth include:

  • Increasing sales — through better marketing, improved products, or reaching new customers
  • Opening new locations — opening additional shops, branches, or outlets
  • Launching new products — developing products that appeal to new or existing customers
  • Entering new markets — selling to different customer groups or expanding into other countries
Key Fact

Organic growth is slower than external growth but is lower risk. The business retains full control and does not take on the challenges of merging with or buying another company.

External Growth

External growth (also called inorganic growth) happens when a business grows by joining with or buying another business. It is faster than organic growth but carries more risk.

Mergers

A merger is when two businesses agree to combine into a single new company. Both sides choose to join forces, often because they believe the combined business will be more competitive.

Takeovers

A takeover (or acquisition) is when one business buys another by purchasing a controlling share. The buyer gains control of the other company’s assets, staff, and customers.

Types of Integration

  • Horizontal integration — joining with a business at the same stage of production (e.g. two car manufacturers merging). Benefit: increased market share and reduced competition.
  • Vertical integration — joining with a business at a different stage in the supply chain. Backward integration means buying a supplier (e.g. a bakery buying a flour mill). Forward integration means buying a distributor or retailer (e.g. a bakery buying a chain of shops). Benefit: greater control over supply or distribution.

Advantages:

  • Very fast — the business can double in size overnight
  • Gains access to the other company’s customers, brand, technology, and expertise
  • Can eliminate a competitor (horizontal integration)
  • Can secure supply or distribution (vertical integration)

Disadvantages:

  • Very expensive — requires large amounts of finance
  • Culture clashes — two different workforces may struggle to work together
  • Redundancies — duplicate roles often lead to job losses
  • Integration problems — different IT systems, management styles, and processes must be combined

Economies and Diseconomies of Scale

Economies of scale are the cost savings a business gains as it grows. When a business produces more, the cost per unit falls because fixed costs are spread over a larger output.

Types of economies of scale include:

  • Purchasing economies — buying raw materials in bulk at a lower price per unit
  • Technical economies — investing in specialist machinery that produces goods more efficiently
  • Financial economies — larger businesses can borrow money at lower interest rates because they are seen as lower risk
  • Marketing economies — the cost of advertising is spread over more units sold

However, growing too large can lead to diseconomies of scale — where the cost per unit starts to rise. This happens because:

  • Communication problems — messages take longer to reach people in a large organisation
  • Coordination difficulties — managing thousands of employees and multiple locations is complex
  • Low morale — employees may feel like a “small cog in a big machine” and become less motivated

Tesco grew from a single market stall in 1919 to the UK’s largest supermarket. It used organic growth (opening new stores, launching Tesco Express and Tesco Metro formats) and external growth (acquiring competitors like Booker, the UK’s largest wholesaler, in 2018). This horizontal integration gave Tesco access to Booker’s wholesale customers and supply network. However, Tesco also experienced diseconomies of scale — in 2014, an accounting scandal and declining customer satisfaction showed the dangers of growing too fast without maintaining quality and communication.

Exam Tip

When asked to recommend organic or external growth, always consider the context of the business. A small start-up probably cannot afford a takeover, so organic growth is more realistic. A large company with cash reserves might benefit from a merger to quickly gain market share. Always justify your recommendation with reference to the specific business in the question.

Conclusion

Businesses can grow organically (from within) or externally (by merging with or buying another business). Each approach has trade-offs between speed, cost, risk, and control.

Key Takeaways

  • Organic growth is slower but lower risk — the business expands through more sales, new products, or new locations.
  • External growth is fast but expensive and risky — mergers and takeovers can bring culture clashes and integration problems.
  • Economies of scale reduce costs as a business grows, but growing too large can create diseconomies of scale.
