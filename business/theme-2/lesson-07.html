Promotion & Place

0:00 / 0:00

What Is Promotion?

Promotion is how a business communicates with potential customers to make them aware of a product and persuade them to buy it. It is one of the four Ps of the marketing mix, alongside product, price, and place.

The aims of promotion include:

  • Raising awareness — making customers know the product exists
  • Attracting new customers — encouraging people to try the product for the first time
  • Increasing sales — boosting revenue by persuading more people to buy
  • Building brand image — creating a positive reputation in customers’ minds
  • Competing with rivals — standing out in a crowded market

Methods of Promotion

Advertising

Advertising is a paid form of promotion where a business pays to have its message shown to a wide audience. Common advertising channels include:

  • Television — reaches millions of viewers but is very expensive (a 30-second prime-time TV advert can cost over £100,000)
  • Radio — cheaper than TV and good for reaching local audiences
  • Print — newspapers, magazines, leaflets, and posters; useful for targeting specific groups
  • Online — social media ads, search engine ads, and banner ads; highly targeted and measurable

Sales Promotions

Sales promotions are short-term offers designed to give customers an extra reason to buy now. Examples include:

  • BOGOF (buy one get one free) — encourages customers to buy more
  • Discounts and price reductions — a temporary lower price to attract bargain hunters
  • Loyalty cards — reward repeat customers (e.g. Tesco Clubcard, Costa Coffee rewards)
  • Free samples — let customers try before they buy, reducing the risk of purchase
  • Competitions and prize draws — create excitement and engagement around the brand
Key Fact

Sales promotions boost sales in the short term, but they reduce profit margins because the business earns less per unit sold. A business that relies too heavily on promotions risks training customers to only buy when there is a deal on.

Sponsorship

Sponsorship is when a business pays to be associated with an event, sports team, or individual. For example, Nike sponsors many top athletes, and Emirates sponsors Arsenal FC. Sponsorship builds brand awareness and creates a positive image by linking the brand with something the target audience cares about.

Public Relations (PR)

Public relations involves managing the reputation of the business through media coverage, press releases, charity events, and community involvement. Unlike advertising, PR is not directly paid for — a positive news story about a business is free publicity. However, negative PR (such as a product recall or scandal) can damage a brand quickly.

Social Media Marketing

Social media platforms like Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, and Facebook allow businesses to reach millions of customers for relatively low cost. Benefits include:

  • Direct two-way communication with customers
  • Highly targeted advertising based on age, location, and interests
  • Content can go viral, reaching a huge audience at no extra cost
  • Real-time customer feedback and engagement

Direct Marketing

Direct marketing involves communicating with customers individually rather than through mass media. Methods include email newsletters, text messages, catalogues, and personalised offers. It is effective because the message is tailored to the customer, but it can be seen as intrusive if overused.

Factors Affecting the Choice of Promotion

A business must choose the right mix of promotional methods. The choice depends on several factors:

  • Budget — a small start-up cannot afford TV advertising, so social media or leaflets may be more realistic
  • Target audience — the promotion must reach the right people. A business targeting teenagers would focus on TikTok and Instagram, not newspaper adverts
  • Product type — complex or expensive products (like cars) may need personal selling, while everyday items (like snacks) suit TV or social media ads
  • Competition — if a competitor runs a major advertising campaign, the business may need to respond with its own promotion to avoid losing market share

Gymshark was founded in 2012 by 19-year-old Ben Francis in his parents’ garage. Instead of spending on traditional advertising, Gymshark built its brand almost entirely through social media and influencer marketing. The company sent free products to fitness influencers on YouTube and Instagram, who promoted the brand to millions of followers. By 2020, Gymshark was valued at over £1 billion — all without a single TV advert. This shows how social media marketing can be more effective than traditional advertising, especially for brands targeting younger consumers with limited budgets.

What Is Place?

Place refers to how a product gets from the manufacturer to the customer. It is about choosing the right distribution channel so that products are available where and when customers want to buy them.

Types of Distribution

  • Wholesaler — buys in bulk from the manufacturer and sells in smaller quantities to retailers. Useful for small retailers who cannot buy directly from the manufacturer.
  • Retailer — sells products directly to the consumer. Examples include supermarkets, high-street shops, and department stores.
  • E-tailer — a retailer that sells online (e.g. Amazon, ASOS). Growing rapidly as more consumers shop online.
  • Direct selling — the manufacturer sells straight to the consumer with no intermediary. Examples include farm shops, factory outlets, and company websites.

Channels of Distribution

The main channels of distribution are:

Channel 1

Traditional Channel

Manufacturer → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer. The longest channel, involving the most intermediaries. Each intermediary adds a mark-up, increasing the final price.

Channel 2

Modern Channel

Manufacturer → Retailer → Consumer. Common with large retailers like Tesco and Sainsbury’s, which buy directly from manufacturers in bulk, cutting out the wholesaler.

Channel 3

Direct Channel

Manufacturer → Consumer. No intermediaries. The manufacturer keeps the full profit margin. Common with online businesses and company websites (e.g. Apple selling through apple.com).

Key Fact

The fewer intermediaries in the distribution channel, the more profit the manufacturer keeps. However, intermediaries provide value — wholesalers break bulk and retailers provide a convenient location for customers to browse and buy.

E-commerce and M-commerce

E-commerce is the buying and selling of goods and services over the internet. M-commerce (mobile commerce) is e-commerce carried out through smartphones and tablets. Both have grown rapidly — in the UK, online sales now account for over 25% of all retail spending.

Advantages:

  • Open 24/7 — customers can shop at any time, not just during store hours
  • Global reach — a small business can sell to customers anywhere in the world
  • Lower costs — no need to pay rent for a high-street shop, reducing fixed costs
  • Customer data — online businesses can track browsing habits and personalise marketing

Disadvantages:

  • Delivery costs — products must be shipped to customers, which costs money and takes time
  • Returns — customers cannot try products before buying, leading to higher return rates
  • Security risks — customers worry about data theft and online fraud
  • High competition — it is easy for competitors to set up online, making it hard to stand out
  • No physical experience — customers cannot touch, try on, or test products before purchase

The Integrated Marketing Mix

Promotion and place do not work in isolation. They must fit together with product and price to create a consistent marketing mix. A luxury product needs premium promotion (high-quality advertising, exclusive events) and selective distribution (specialist retailers, not discount stores). A budget product needs mass-market promotion (TV ads, social media offers) and wide distribution (supermarkets, online retailers).

Key Fact

If the elements of the marketing mix send conflicting messages, customers become confused. A high-end product sold through a discount retailer undermines its premium image. An exclusive brand running constant BOGOF offers cheapens its reputation. Consistency across all four Ps is essential.

Apple is a textbook example of an integrated marketing mix. The product is premium and innovative. The price is high (price skimming). Promotion uses sleek advertising, high-profile launch events, and word-of-mouth rather than discounts or BOGOF deals. Place is carefully controlled — products are sold through Apple’s own stores, its website, and selected premium retailers. Apple never sells through discount chains. Every element reinforces the same message: this is a premium, desirable brand. The consistency across all four Ps is what makes Apple’s marketing so effective.

Exam Tip

When answering questions about promotion or place, always consider the type of business in the case study. A small local bakery has a very different promotional budget and distribution strategy to a national clothing brand. Link your answer to the size of the business, its target market, and its budget — don’t just list generic methods.

Conclusion

Promotion is how a business communicates with customers, and place is how products reach them. Choosing the right methods for each depends on the business’s budget, target audience, and product type. Both must be consistent with the rest of the marketing mix.

Key Takeaways

  • Promotion methods range from advertising and sales promotions to social media and PR — the right choice depends on budget, audience, and product type.
  • Distribution channels determine how products reach customers — fewer intermediaries mean more profit for the manufacturer but may limit reach.
  • E-commerce offers 24/7 global access and lower costs, but brings challenges like delivery expenses, returns, and online security.
