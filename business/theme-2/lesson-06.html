Price and the Marketing Mix

Price is one of the four elements of the marketing mix. It is the amount a customer pays for a product or service. Getting the price right is critical — too high and customers go elsewhere, too low and the business may not cover its costs or may damage its brand image.

Price must be consistent with the other three elements of the marketing mix:

  • Product — a high-quality, premium product justifies a higher price; a basic product should be priced lower
  • Place — selling in an upmarket department store suggests a higher price than selling through a discount website
  • Promotion — advertising that emphasises luxury and exclusivity must match a premium price, not a budget one
Key Fact

If the elements of the marketing mix do not match, customers become confused. A £5 perfume advertised as “luxury” lacks credibility, and a £200 perfume sold in a pound shop undermines the brand. Price must fit the overall image the business wants to project.

Pricing Strategies

A pricing strategy is the approach a business takes when setting the price of its products. Different strategies suit different situations. The five main pricing strategies for GCSE Business are:

Cost-Plus Pricing

Cost-plus pricing is the simplest method. The business calculates the total cost of making the product, then adds a percentage on top (the markup) to ensure a profit. For example, if a product costs £10 to make and the business applies a 50% markup, the selling price is £15.

  • Advantage: Simple to calculate and guarantees a profit on every item sold
  • Disadvantage: Ignores what competitors charge and what customers are willing to pay — the price may be too high or too low for the market

Competitive Pricing

Competitive pricing means setting the price based on what competitors charge. A business might match the market price or undercut rivals slightly to attract customers. This strategy is common in markets where products are similar, such as petrol stations or supermarkets.

  • Advantage: Keeps the business in line with the market and avoids losing customers to cheaper rivals
  • Disadvantage: Can lead to price wars where all businesses cut prices, reducing profit margins for everyone

Penetration Pricing

Penetration pricing means launching a product at a low price to attract customers quickly and build market share. Once the business has established a loyal customer base, the price is gradually raised. This is often used when entering a competitive market.

  • Advantage: Attracts customers quickly and discourages competitors from entering the market
  • Disadvantage: The business makes little or no profit initially, and customers may leave when the price rises

Price Skimming

Price skimming is the opposite of penetration pricing. The business launches a new or innovative product at a high price to maximise revenue from early adopters who are willing to pay more. Over time, the price is lowered to attract more price-sensitive customers.

  • Advantage: Maximises profit from customers willing to pay a premium, and helps recover research and development costs quickly
  • Disadvantage: The high price may put off many customers and attract competitors who offer cheaper alternatives

Apple is a classic example of price skimming. When a new iPhone launches, it is priced at a premium — often over £1,000. Early adopters and loyal fans buy it immediately. Within a year, the price drops as newer models are announced and competitors release alternatives. Apple recovers its huge development costs from those early, high-price sales, then captures a wider market as the price falls. The strategy works because Apple’s brand loyalty and innovation give it a product that customers feel is worth the premium.

Promotional Pricing

Promotional pricing involves temporarily reducing the price to boost sales in the short term. Common examples include buy-one-get-one-free (BOGOF) offers, seasonal sales, loyalty discounts, and introductory offers for new products.

  • Advantage: Creates urgency and encourages customers to buy now; useful for clearing old stock or attracting new customers
  • Disadvantage: Reduces profit per item and customers may only buy during promotions, waiting for the next sale rather than paying full price
Key Fact

Penetration pricing and price skimming are opposite strategies. Penetration starts low and goes up; skimming starts high and comes down. In the exam, make sure you do not confuse them — think “penetrate” (break into the market with a low price) versus “skim” (skim the cream off the top with a high price).

Factors Affecting Pricing Decisions

A business does not pick a pricing strategy at random. Several factors influence which strategy is most appropriate:

  • Costs of production — the price must cover costs or the business will make a loss. If costs rise (e.g. raw materials become more expensive), prices may need to increase
  • Competition — in a market with many competitors selling similar products, a business may have little choice but to use competitive pricing. In a market with few competitors, it has more freedom to set higher prices
  • Target market — luxury brands targeting wealthy customers can charge premium prices. Budget brands targeting price-conscious customers must keep prices low
  • Product life cycle stage — a brand-new product might use skimming or penetration pricing at launch, then switch to competitive pricing as the market matures
  • Brand strength — strong brands like Nike or Apple can charge higher prices because customers trust the quality and are willing to pay more. Weaker or unknown brands must compete more on price

Pricing often changes as a product moves through its life cycle:

  • Introduction: Price skimming (if the product is innovative) or penetration pricing (if the market is competitive) to build initial sales
  • Growth: Prices may stay stable as demand rises and the business builds brand loyalty
  • Maturity: Competitive pricing becomes important as rivals offer similar products and the market is saturated
  • Decline: Promotional pricing (sales, discounts) to clear remaining stock before the product is discontinued

Price Sensitivity

Not all products are equally affected by price changes. Some products are price sensitive — even a small price increase causes a large drop in demand. Others are price insensitive — customers continue to buy even when the price rises significantly.

  • Necessities (e.g. bread, milk, petrol) tend to be price insensitive — people need them regardless of price
  • Luxuries (e.g. designer clothes, holidays, eating out) tend to be price sensitive — customers can do without them, so they shop around for better deals
  • Products with many substitutes are more price sensitive — if one brand of cola raises its price, customers easily switch to another
  • Strongly branded products are less price sensitive — loyal Apple customers pay a premium because they value the brand
Key Fact

Understanding price sensitivity helps a business decide how much flexibility it has when setting prices. A business selling a necessity with few substitutes can raise prices without losing many customers. A business selling a luxury with many alternatives must be much more careful.

Exam Tip

In longer-answer questions, always link your pricing recommendation to the type of product, the target market, and the competitive environment in the case study. Saying “they should use penetration pricing” without explaining why it suits this business will not reach the top marks. Context is everything.

Conclusion

Pricing is not just about picking a number — it is a strategic decision that must align with the product, the target market, and the competition. Different strategies suit different situations, and the best choice depends on the business’s objectives and market conditions.

Key Takeaways

  • Five key strategies: cost-plus, competitive, penetration, skimming, and promotional pricing each suit different market conditions and business goals.
  • Pricing must fit the marketing mix: the price should be consistent with the product quality, where it is sold, and how it is promoted.
  • Context matters: factors like production costs, competition, brand strength, target market, and product life cycle stage all influence which pricing strategy works best.
