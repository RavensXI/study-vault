01

Elizabeth's Accession, Character, and Early Problems

Study Notes

Elizabeth I is one of England's most famous queens, and her journey to the throne was anything but easy. She became queen in 1558 at the age of 25, but her early life was full of challenges that shaped the smart and strong leader she would become.

Birth and Early Childhood

Elizabeth was born on 7 September 1533 to Henry VIII and Anne Boleyn. Her birth was a big deal at first, but because she was a girl, her father was disappointed. Things got worse when her mother couldn't have a son - Henry had Anne executed when Elizabeth was just two years old. After that, Elizabeth was declared illegitimate and removed from royal favor. Despite this rocky start, these experiences taught Elizabeth how to be tough and independent.

Education and Formation

Elizabeth had an amazing education, especially for a girl in her time. She learned Latin, Greek, French, and Italian, along with history, philosophy, and religion. Her tutors, like Roger Ascham admired her for being so smart and eager to learn. This education helped her become a great ruler who could hold her own in politics and religion.

Religious Turmoil and Political Danger

Elizabeth grew up in a time of religious chaos. When her Protestant half-brother Edward VI was king, she had some peace. But when her Catholic half-sister Mary I took over, things got dangerous. Mary suspected Elizabeth of plotting against her and even locked her in the Tower of London in 1554. Elizabeth's famous "Tide Letter" to Mary, where she defended herself, shows how clever and persuasive she could be.

The Road to the Throne

When Mary died childless in 1558, Elizabeth became queen. The country was a mess: people were divided over religion, the government was broke, and England had few allies. Elizabeth's first job was to calm things down and prove that she could rule as a woman in a man's world.

Elizabeth's Early Policies and Advisors

One of her first moves was appointing William Cecil as her top advisor. He was smart and loyal, and together they laid the groundwork for Elizabeth's long and successful reign. She also surrounded herself with other capable people, but she always made it clear that she was in charge.

Public Perception and Initial Reforms

Elizabeth's charm and intelligence won people over quickly. In her first speeches, she promised to serve her people and follow God's will. She introduced the 1559 Religious Settlement, which aimed to end the fighting between Catholics and Protestants by creating a middle ground. This helped bring some stability to England.

While you won't be directly asked about Elizabeth's early life in the exam (the topic starts from 1558, the year she became queen), understanding her background is still very useful. Adding these details to your answers about her later actions, like the Religious Settlement or her handling of Parliament, shows a strong understanding of wider context.

Conclusion

Elizabeth's early life was filled with hardships, but those challenges shaped her into a strong and smart leader. By the time she became queen, she was ready to handle the pressures of ruling a divided country. Her reign, often called the "Elizabethan Golden Age," was one of the most successful in English history.

The Religious Settlement of 1559

Study Notes

When Elizabeth I became Queen in 1558, England was divided by religion. Her predecessor, Mary I, was a devout Catholic and had persecuted Protestants. Before that, under Edward VI, England had been Protestant. Elizabeth wanted to find a peaceful solution, a 'middle way', that most people could accept. This was known as the Religious Settlement of 1559.

What did the Settlement Say?

The Religious Settlement was made up of two main Acts of Parliament:

  • The Act of Supremacy: This made Elizabeth the Supreme Governor of the Church of England. This was important because it meant she was in charge, not the Pope in Rome.
  • The Act of Uniformity: This set out the rules for religious worship and services. It said that all churches had to use the Book of Common Prayer. This book included a mix of traditional Catholic and newer Protestant ideas.

Challenges to the Settlement

Catholics: Many English Catholics wanted to remain loyal to the Pope and the traditional Catholic faith. Some were willing to attend the new Church of England services to avoid fines (known as recusancy fines), but others refused. The Pope excommunicated Elizabeth in 1570, cutting her off from the Catholic Church and encouraging Catholics to disobey her.

Puritans: These were very strict Protestants who felt the Religious Settlement hadn't gone far enough to remove all traces of Catholicism. They wanted simpler services, plainer churches, and were against things like priests wearing special robes (vestments) or bishops being in charge.

Elizabeth's Approach: Initially, Elizabeth was quite tolerant as long as people outwardly conformed to her settlement. However, as threats from both Catholics and Puritans grew, her government took a harsher line.

When answering questions about the Religious Settlement, make sure you explain why Elizabeth chose a 'middle way'. Consider the pressures she was under from both Catholics and Protestants, and the need for stability in England after years of religious changes.

Conclusion

Elizabeth I's Religious Settlement of 1559 was a clever attempt to bring religious peace to England. While it didn't please everyone and faced significant challenges from both Catholics and Puritans, it largely succeeded in establishing a Church of England that most people could accept.

Elizabeth's Court, Government, and Relations with Parliament

Study Notes

How Elizabeth Ruled England

The Queen: Queen Elizabeth I was the most powerful person in England. She made all the big decisions. This is called the Royal Prerogative.

The Privy Council: This was a group of her most trusted advisors. They met almost every day to help Elizabeth govern the country, manage money, and deal with problems. Famous members included William Cecil (Lord Burghley) and Sir Francis Walsingham.

Parliament: Parliament was made up of the House of Lords (nobles and bishops) and the House of Commons (elected MPs). Parliament's main jobs were to pass laws and agree to taxes. Elizabeth didn't have to call Parliament often, and she could choose to ignore their advice.

Local Government

Lord Lieutenants: These were nobles chosen by the Queen to be in charge of a local area. They had to keep order, raise an army if needed, and report back to the Privy Council.

Justices of the Peace (JPs): JPs were wealthy local landowners who were responsible for making sure laws were obeyed in their area. They weren't paid but it was a position of high status.

Getting the Message Out: Propaganda

Elizabeth I was very clever at controlling her image. This is called propaganda. It was important because she was a woman ruler in a time when men held most of the power, there were religious divisions, and she needed to build loyalty and national pride.

Ways Elizabeth Used Propaganda

  • Portraits: Elizabeth's portraits were carefully designed to show her as powerful, wealthy, and ageless. Symbols were often used. Unflattering portraits were ordered to be destroyed.
  • Progresses: Elizabeth and her court would travel around the country and stay in the homes of nobles, allowing many people to see her.
  • Writings and Performances: Plays, poems, and books were written that praised the Queen, creating the "Cult of Gloriana."
  • Royal Speeches: Elizabeth was a good speaker. Her speeches, like the one at Tilbury before the Spanish Armada, were designed to inspire courage and loyalty.
  • Patronage: Elizabeth gave jobs, land, and titles to people who supported her.

The government also controlled what was printed, stopping the spread of ideas critical of the Queen.

When discussing Elizabeth's propaganda, always link it back to why it was used. Explain how a specific portrait or a royal progress helped Elizabeth overcome challenges like her gender, religious unrest, or threats from abroad.

Conclusion

Elizabeth I's government was a mix of her own power, the advice of her Privy Council, and the laws made by Parliament. To stay in control and keep her people loyal, she used propaganda very effectively through portraits, progresses, and the promotion of a positive image, building a powerful idea of herself as "Gloriana."

The Problem of Marriage and Succession

Study Notes

Why was Marriage a Big Deal?

  • Producing an Heir: It was expected that a monarch would marry and have children to ensure a smooth succession. Without a clear heir, there was a risk of civil war.
  • A Woman Ruler: Many people believed that a woman could not rule on her own and needed a husband.
  • Foreign Policy and Alliances: Marriage could be a powerful diplomatic tool.
  • Domestic Stability: Marrying an English nobleman might create jealousy.

Potential Suitors

1. Robert Dudley, Earl of Leicester (English):

  • Pros: Childhood friend; Elizabeth was very fond of him; kept the crown in English hands.
  • Cons: Already married; his wife died suspiciously; could have created political unrest.

2. King Philip II of Spain (Spanish):

  • Pros: Powerful King of Spain; could create a strong alliance.
  • Cons: Strict Catholic; many English people would have been unhappy.

3. Francis, Duke of Anjou and Alencon (French):

  • Pros: Could create alliance with France against Spain.
  • Cons: French and Catholic; worried English Protestants.

Why did Elizabeth Never Marry?

  • Maintaining Power: By not marrying, Elizabeth kept all the power.
  • Avoiding Unpopularity: Choosing any suitor risked upsetting people.
  • Past Experience: She had seen problems her sister Mary I faced being married to Philip II. Her own mother was executed.
  • The "Virgin Queen" Image: Elizabeth cultivated the image of being married only to her kingdom.

Focus on the different pressures she faced (from Parliament, councillors, societal expectations, and foreign powers). Explain the advantages and disadvantages of potential suitors and why she might have chosen to remain unmarried.

Conclusion

The question of Elizabeth's marriage was a major issue throughout her reign. She faced constant pressure to marry and secure the succession, but each potential match brought significant risks. By choosing to remain single, Elizabeth kept control of her power and built the iconic image of the "Virgin Queen."

The Puritan Challenge

Study Notes

What is Puritanism?

Puritans were very committed Protestants who believed the Church of England hadn't gone far enough to remove all traces of Catholic practices. They wanted a "purer" form of worship, with simpler services and a church structure closer to what the Bible described.

Why did Puritans Challenge Elizabeth?

  • The Religious Settlement (1559): Puritans felt the Settlement kept too many things that looked Catholic, including vestments, bishops, and certain church decorations.
  • Church Hierarchy: Many Puritans disliked the power of bishops, preferring a simpler church structure governed by elders.
  • Services and Practices: They wanted to get rid of practices like kneeling to receive communion, making the sign of the cross, and using rings in marriage.

How did Puritans Express their Opposition?

  • In Parliament: Some MPs tried to pass laws to reform the Church further.
  • Preaching and 'Prophesyings': Clergy meetings to discuss the Bible that sometimes became forums for criticism.
  • Pamphlets and Writings: Published to spread ideas and criticise the Church.
  • Refusal to Conform: Some clergy refused to wear prescribed vestments.

How did Elizabeth Respond?

Elizabeth relied on her Archbishops of Canterbury. Archbishop Parker tried to find a middle way. Archbishop Grindal was more sympathetic to prophesyings, leading to his suspension. Archbishop Whitgift took a much tougher stance, forcing clergy to accept the Settlement.

Prophesyings were eventually banned. Laws were passed to punish those who refused to attend Church of England services. By the end of her reign, organised Puritan opposition had been significantly weakened.

Remember to explain why they were a threat (challenging royal supremacy, creating division) but also why their threat was limited (government action, lack of widespread support for extreme Puritanism).

Conclusion

The Puritans presented a significant and persistent challenge to Elizabeth's religious settlement. However, Elizabeth and her government, particularly under Archbishop Whitgift, took firm measures to enforce conformity. While Puritanism continued as a religious force, its direct political threat was largely contained by the end of the 16th century.

Conflict with Spain – The Road to War

Study Notes

At the start of Elizabeth I's reign in 1558, England and Spain were reasonably friendly. However, relations gradually worsened over the next 30 years, leading to the Spanish Armada in 1588.

Religious Differences

Protestant England vs. Catholic Spain. Philip II saw himself as a defender of Catholicism. In 1570, the Pope excommunicated Elizabeth, encouraging Catholics to plot against her.

Political and Personal Rivalry

Spain was the dominant European power with a vast empire. Philip II had been King of England during his marriage to Mary I and found it hard to accept England becoming a Protestant rival.

English Support for Dutch Rebels

The Netherlands was ruled by Spain. Many Dutch were Protestant and resented Spanish rule. Elizabeth allowed Dutch rebel ships to use English ports. In 1585, Elizabeth signed the Treaty of Nonsuch, openly agreeing to send soldiers and money to help the Dutch - a key turning point.

Rivalry At Sea

English privateers like Drake and Hawkins attacked Spanish treasure ships and ports. Elizabeth secretly encouraged these activities. Drake's circumnavigation (1577-1580) plundered vast amounts of Spanish treasure. The attack at San Juan de Ulua (1568) increased bitterness.

Mary, Queen of Scots

Philip II supported various Catholic plots to overthrow Elizabeth. Mary's execution in 1587 was the final straw for Philip.

Avoid presenting it as a single-cause event. Show the interconnectedness of multiple factors - religious, political, economic, and individual actions - building up tension over time.

Conclusion

Relations between England and Spain soured dramatically. What began as a manageable relationship deteriorated due to deep religious divisions, Philip II's ambition, Elizabeth's support for Dutch rebels, English privateering, and plots surrounding Mary, Queen of Scots. By 1587, direct conflict seemed almost inevitable.

The Spanish Armada – Reasons for English Victory

Study Notes

Why did Philip II Launch the Armada?

  • Religious: Philip wanted to overthrow Protestant Elizabeth and make England Catholic again.
  • Political: Wanted to stop English support for Dutch rebels. The Treaty of Nonsuch (1585) was a direct act of war.
  • Provocation: Drake's raid on Cadiz in 1587 ('singeing of the King of Spain's beard') angered Philip.
  • Mary, Queen of Scots: Her execution in 1587 gave Philip justification to invade.

The English Plan & Strengths

The English navy, commanded by Lord Howard with Drake as vice-admiral, had faster, more manoeuvrable ships (galleons) with longer-range cannons (culverins). English sailors were experienced and fighting in home waters.

Key Events

  • Sighting: Armada sighted off the English coast on 29th July 1588. Beacons lit along the south coast.
  • Early Skirmishes: English engaged Armada as it sailed up the Channel. Spanish maintained defensive crescent formation.
  • The Fireships at Calais (6th-7th August): English sent eight unmanned ships filled with flammable materials into the Spanish fleet at night. This caused panic and broke the Spanish formation.
  • The Battle of Gravelines (8th August): With the fleet scattered, English inflicted significant damage using superior speed and gunnery.
  • The "Protestant Wind": Strong winds forced surviving Spanish ships northwards around Scotland and Ireland.
  • The Journey Home: Many ships wrecked by storms. Only about half the Armada made it back to Spain.

Why was the Armada Defeated?

  • English Tactics and Leadership: Effective leaders. Fireships broke Spanish formation. Better gunnery.
  • Ship Design and Technology: English galleons faster, more manoeuvrable. Better cannons with longer range.
  • Spanish Weaknesses and Mistakes: Medina Sidonia had little naval experience. Plan relied on perfect timing. Poor quality cannonballs. Harder-to-reload cannons.
  • Weather (Luck): The "Protestant Wind" prevented further attempts and storms destroyed many remaining ships.

Aim for a balanced answer. Don't just list reasons; explain how each factor contributed. Show the interplay of different factors.

Conclusion

The defeat of the Spanish Armada was due to English naval skill, innovative tactics like fireships, superior ship design, Spanish weaknesses including flawed planning and communication problems, and terrible weather. The victory boosted English national pride.

The Problem of the Poor and Elizabethan Poor Laws

Study Notes

Growing Poverty in Elizabethan England

During Elizabeth's reign (1558-1603), England saw an increase in poverty and vagabondage.

Reasons for Increased Poverty

  • Growing Population: More competition for jobs and resources.
  • Bad Harvests: Less food, higher prices.
  • Changes in Farming: Landlords enclosed common land for sheep farming, fewer farm jobs.
  • Demobilised Soldiers: Returning from wars with no work.
  • Trade Problems: Issues with the wool trade caused unemployment.
  • Inflation: Prices rose but wages didn't keep up.

Attitudes Towards the Poor

  • The "Deserving Poor": People who couldn't work due to age, sickness, or disability. People were sympathetic.
  • The "Idle Poor" or "Undeserving Poor": People seen as fit to work but didn't. Feared as "vagabonds" or "sturdy beggars."

Government Takes Action: The Poor Laws

Key features:

  • Local Responsibility: Parishes were made responsible for their own poor.
  • Poor Relief: The "poor rate" was collected from property owners.
  • Work for the Able-Bodied: In "Houses of Correction" or workhouses.
  • Punishment for Vagrants: Whipping, branding, or even execution for repeat offenders.

The 1601 Elizabethan Poor Law

This brought together previous laws and created a more complete system. It remained the basis of poor relief for over 200 years.

  • Each parish appointed an "Overseer of the Poor"
  • Provided food or money for those who couldn't work
  • Provided work for the able-bodied in workhouses
  • Harsh punishments for vagrants who refused to work

How Effective Were the Poor Laws

Successes: Established society's responsibility to help the needy; created a national system.

Limitations: Relief was often very small; workhouse conditions were grim; did little to solve root causes.

Don't just list what the laws were. Explain why they were introduced and evaluate how effective they were.

Conclusion

The Elizabethan Poor Laws marked an important change, moving from relying only on charity to government responsibility. The 1601 Poor Law was a landmark piece of legislation that shaped how poverty was handled for centuries.

Elizabethan Exploration – Drake, Hawkins, and Raleigh

Study Notes

Why did English Sailors Explore?

  • Wealth and Trade: New trade routes and goods like spices, silk, precious metals.
  • Adventure and Fame: Looking for adventure and renown.
  • Expanding English Power: Establishing presence in new parts of the world.
  • Privateering: Attacking Spanish ships with the Queen's unofficial approval.

Key Explorers and Their Voyages

1. Sir John Hawkins: One of the first English sailors involved in the transatlantic slave trade. Made voyages in the 1560s. The battle at San Juan de Ulua in 1568 increased tension with Spain.

2. Sir Francis Drake: Famous privateer. Circumnavigated the globe 1577-1580, bringing great wealth back to England.

3. Sir Walter Raleigh: Sponsored voyages to establish a colony at Roanoke (the "Lost Colony"). Also searched for El Dorado.

What did these Voyages Achieve?

  • Increased Wealth
  • New Knowledge: Mapped new lands and seas
  • Foundation of Empire: Laid groundwork for the future British Empire
  • Rivalry with Spain: Challenged Spain's dominance
  • Development of Slave Trade: A darker aspect of these voyages

Link the motives of the sailors to the actual voyages and their outcomes. Mention specific explorers and their achievements.

Conclusion

Elizabethan exploration was driven by economic ambition, adventure, and national pride. While these voyages had significant achievements, it's also important to remember the darker aspects like the slave trade.

A 'Golden Age' – Rise of the Gentry & Theatre

Study Notes

What was Elizabethan Society Like?

Elizabethan society had a clear structure called the 'Great Chain of Being':

  • The Nobility: Richest and most powerful - dukes and earls.
  • The Gentry: Growing in importance. Wealthy landowners but not nobles. Many became MPs or JPs.
  • Yeomen and Tenant Farmers: Owned or rented land.
  • Merchants and Craftsmen: Made and sold goods in towns.
  • Labourers and Servants: Worked for others.
  • The Poor and Unemployed: Many poor people in Elizabethan England.

Growing Prosperity and the Rise of the Gentry

The Gentry benefited from rising prices for wool and crops, buying land that used to belong to the Church, and increased trade opportunities. This new wealth meant grand new houses, expensive fashions, and educated children.

Case Study: Bess of Hardwick

Elizabeth Talbot, known as Bess of Hardwick, was born into the gentry. Through clever marriages (she married four times, each time to a richer man) and good business sense, she became one of the richest women in England. She was involved in mining and money lending. She built magnificent houses, especially Hardwick Hall ("Hardwick Hall, more glass than wall").

Always try to show that life was very different for different groups of people. Avoid saying 'everyone' was rich or 'everyone' was poor.

Conclusion

For the gentry and some merchants, it was a time of increasing wealth. However, the structure of society was rigid, and a large portion of the population did not experience this prosperity.

Elizabeth's Later Reign – The Essex Rebellion

Study Notes

Who Was the Earl of Essex?

Robert Devereux, the Earl of Essex, was a young, charming, and ambitious nobleman who became Elizabeth's favourite in the late 1580s and early 1590s. He was the stepson of Robert Dudley.

Why Did he Rebel?

  • Loss of Royal Favour: His military leadership in Ireland was a disaster (1599). Elizabeth placed him under house arrest, banned him from court, and took away his sweet wine monopoly.
  • Rivalry and Factions: Essex felt rivals, particularly Robert Cecil, were turning the Queen against him.
  • Ambition and Frustration: He found it hard to accept his fall from grace.
  • Financial Problems: Losing his sweet wine monopoly hit him hard financially.
  • Misjudgment: He believed he had enough popular support in London.

What Happened During the Rebellion?

  • Essex gathered around 200-300 followers.
  • His plan was to march into London, capture the Tower, and force the Queen to dismiss his rivals.
  • On 8th February 1601, he and his followers rode into London.
  • The rebellion was poorly planned. Robert Cecil and the Privy Council were prepared.
  • The people of London did not support Essex. Many locked their doors.
  • The Queen's forces quickly surrounded Essex. He was forced to surrender.

Why Did the Rebellion Fail?

  • Lack of Support from Londoners
  • Strong Government Response
  • Poor Planning
  • Loyalty to Elizabeth

Consequences

  • Essex was tried for treason and executed on 25th February 1601.
  • Demonstrated Elizabeth's authority even in old age.
  • Removed a troublesome figure.
  • Strengthened Cecil's position.

Consider what the rebellion reveals about the end of Elizabeth's reign. It showed that while court factions were strong, Elizabeth's ultimate authority remained intact.

Conclusion

The Essex Rebellion of 1601 was the final, dramatic chapter in Essex's life. His ill-fated attempt to force his way back into power failed due to lack of popular support and a strong government response. His execution underlined that even in her final years, Elizabeth's authority was absolute.
