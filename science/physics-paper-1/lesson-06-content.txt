# Density, States of Matter and Changes of State

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Density?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how much mass is contained in a given volume of a substance, calculated using density = mass &divide; volume.">Density</dfn> is a measure of how much mass there is in a given volume. A dense material has its particles packed closely together, so there is a lot of mass in a small space. A less dense material has its particles more spread out.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The equation for density is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Density (&rho;) = mass (m) &divide; volume (V)</strong><br>Units: density in kg/m&sup3; (or g/cm&sup3;), mass in kg (or g), volume in m&sup3; (or cm&sup3;).<br>This equation is on the AQA physics equation sheet.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">You need to be able to rearrange this equation. To find mass: <strong>m = &rho; &times; V</strong>. To find volume: <strong>V = m &divide; &rho;</strong>. For example, if an object has a mass of 500 g and a volume of 50 cm&sup3;, its density is 500 &divide; 50 = 10 g/cm&sup3;.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Measuring Density &mdash; Required Practical</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">AQA requires you to know how to measure the density of both regular and irregular solid objects, and you may be asked about this in the exam. The method depends on the shape of the object.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Regular Solid Objects</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n8">For a <dfn class="term" data-def="An object with a uniform geometric shape (such as a cube or cylinder) whose volume can be calculated from length measurements.">regular solid</dfn> such as a cube or rectangular block:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>1.</strong> Measure the mass using a digital balance.<br><strong>2.</strong> Measure the length, width, and height using a ruler or vernier callipers.<br><strong>3.</strong> Calculate the volume (length &times; width &times; height for a rectangular block).<br><strong>4.</strong> Use the equation &rho; = m &divide; V to calculate density.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Irregular Solid Objects</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n10">For an <dfn class="term" data-def="An object with a non-uniform shape whose volume cannot be calculated from simple measurements &mdash; its volume is found using displacement.">irregular solid</dfn> (e.g. a pebble or a key):</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>1.</strong> Measure the mass using a digital balance.<br><strong>2.</strong> Fill a <dfn class="term" data-def="A container with a spout near the top, used to measure the volume of an irregular object by collecting the water it displaces.">eureka can</dfn> (displacement can) with water until it overflows from the spout.<br><strong>3.</strong> Gently lower the object into the water. The water displaced by the object flows out of the spout and into a measuring cylinder.<br><strong>4.</strong> Read the volume of displaced water from the measuring cylinder &mdash; this equals the volume of the object.<br><strong>5.</strong> Use &rho; = m &divide; V to calculate density.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">The Particle Model of Matter</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">All matter is made up of tiny particles (atoms or molecules). The <dfn class="term" data-def="A model that describes the arrangement, movement, and energy of particles in solids, liquids, and gases.">particle model</dfn> explains the properties of the three states of matter &mdash; solid, liquid, and gas &mdash; in terms of how their particles are arranged and how they move.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Solids:</strong> Particles are held closely together in a fixed, regular arrangement. They vibrate about fixed positions but cannot move freely. Strong forces of attraction between particles. High density.<br><strong>Liquids:</strong> Particles are close together but can slide past each other. Arrangement is irregular. Medium forces of attraction. Medium density.<br><strong>Gases:</strong> Particles are far apart and move randomly at high speeds. Almost no forces of attraction. Very low density. Gases can be compressed because of the large spaces between particles.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n15">The particle model explains why solids have a fixed shape and a fixed volume, liquids have a fixed volume but take the shape of their container, and gases have no fixed shape or volume &mdash; they spread out to fill any space available. It also explains density differences: solids are generally the densest state because their particles are packed most closely, while gases are the least dense because their particles are spread far apart.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Changes of State</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">When a substance is heated or cooled, it can change from one state to another. These changes are called <dfn class="term" data-def="A physical change in which a substance moves between solid, liquid, and gas states without any change in its chemical composition.">changes of state</dfn>. During a change of state, the substance&rsquo;s mass is conserved &mdash; it stays the same because no particles are added or lost.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">The key changes of state are:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Melting</strong> &mdash; solid to liquid. Happens at the substance&rsquo;s melting point. Energy is absorbed to weaken the bonds between particles so they can move past each other.<br><strong>Boiling</strong> &mdash; liquid to gas. Happens at the substance&rsquo;s boiling point. Energy is absorbed to overcome the forces of attraction between particles so they can move freely.<br><strong>Evaporation</strong> &mdash; liquid to gas, but it happens at the surface of a liquid at any temperature below the boiling point. The most energetic particles at the surface escape into the gas phase.<br><strong>Condensation</strong> &mdash; gas to liquid. Happens when a gas is cooled and particles lose energy and move closer together.<br><strong>Freezing</strong> &mdash; liquid to solid. Happens at the freezing point (which is the same temperature as the melting point). Energy is released as particles form a fixed arrangement.<br><strong>Sublimation</strong> &mdash; solid directly to gas, without passing through the liquid state.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A change of state is a <strong>physical change</strong>, not a chemical change. The substance can be reversed back to its original state. No new substance is formed, and the mass is conserved because the total number of particles stays the same.</p>
</div>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n21">When a substance is changing state (e.g. ice melting to water), the temperature remains constant even though energy is still being supplied. This is because the energy is being used to break or weaken the bonds between particles rather than increasing their kinetic energy. The energy absorbed during a change of state is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The energy required to change the state of a substance without changing its temperature &mdash; latent heat of fusion (melting) or latent heat of vaporisation (boiling).">latent heat</dfn>. On a heating curve, the flat sections (where temperature does not change) represent changes of state.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Internal Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Every object or system stores <dfn class="term" data-def="The total energy stored within a system, equal to the sum of the kinetic energy and potential energy of all particles.">internal energy</dfn>. Internal energy is the total kinetic energy and potential energy of all the particles in a system. When a substance is heated, its internal energy increases &mdash; the particles gain kinetic energy and move more vigorously. During a change of state, the potential energy of the particles changes (bonds break or form) but the kinetic energy stays the same, which is why the temperature does not change.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Evaporation vs Boiling</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Evaporation</strong> happens at the surface of a liquid, at any temperature. Only the fastest (most energetic) particles escape from the surface, which is why evaporation causes cooling &mdash; the average kinetic energy of the remaining particles decreases, so the temperature drops.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Boiling</strong> happens throughout the liquid, only at the boiling point. Bubbles of gas form inside the liquid and rise to the surface. Boiling requires a continuous energy input and happens at a fixed temperature for a given substance.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n26">Density calculation questions are common on AQA Physics Paper 1 &mdash; always show your working and include units. If the question asks you to describe the required practical, state the equipment used (balance, ruler, eureka can, measuring cylinder) and explain each step clearly. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about changes of state, always link your answer to particles: describe what happens to the arrangement, movement, and energy of particles. Remember that mass is conserved during a change of state &mdash; this is a common exam trap.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n27">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n28">Density = mass &divide; volume (&rho; = m/V). You must know how to measure density for regular shapes (ruler + balance) and irregular shapes (eureka can + balance).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29">The particle model explains why solids are dense with a fixed shape, liquids flow and take the shape of their container, and gases are easily compressed and spread to fill any space.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30">Changes of state (melting, boiling, evaporation, condensation, freezing, sublimation) are physical changes where mass is conserved, and temperature stays constant during the change because energy goes into breaking or forming bonds, not increasing kinetic energy.</li></ul>
