# Energy Stores, Transfers and Power

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Energy Stores</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In physics, energy is described as being held in different <dfn class="term" data-def="Descriptions of the ways in which energy can be stored &mdash; there are eight energy stores in the AQA specification.">energy stores</dfn>. Energy is never created or destroyed &mdash; it is simply transferred from one store to another. This principle is known as the <dfn class="term" data-def="The principle that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one store to another or converted from one form to another.">conservation of energy</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">AQA requires you to know eight energy stores. Each one describes a different way that energy can be held within a system.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The eight energy stores are: <strong>kinetic</strong>, <strong>thermal</strong>, <strong>chemical</strong>, <strong>gravitational potential</strong>, <strong>elastic potential</strong>, <strong>electrostatic</strong>, <strong>magnetic</strong>, and <strong>nuclear</strong>. You must be able to identify which stores are involved in any energy transfer scenario.</p>
</div>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n5"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy store associated with a moving object &mdash; the faster it moves and the greater its mass, the more kinetic energy it has.">Kinetic energy</dfn></strong> is the energy store of any moving object. A car on a motorway, a sprinter in a race, and air molecules in a warm room all have energy in their kinetic store.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n6"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy store associated with the temperature of an object &mdash; the hotter it is, the more energy is in its thermal store.">Thermal energy</dfn></strong> is associated with the temperature of an object. The hotter something is, the more energy it has in its thermal store. When you heat water in a kettle, energy is transferred to the thermal store of the water.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy stored in the bonds between atoms in fuels, food, and batteries &mdash; released during chemical reactions.">Chemical energy</dfn></strong> is the energy stored in chemical bonds. Food, fuels such as petrol and natural gas, and batteries all store energy chemically. This energy is released during chemical reactions.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy stored by an object raised above ground level &mdash; depends on mass, gravitational field strength, and height.">Gravitational potential energy</dfn></strong> (GPE) is the energy stored by an object that has been raised above the ground. The higher the object, the greater its mass, and the stronger the gravitational field, the more GPE it has.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy stored in a stretched or compressed elastic object such as a spring or rubber band.">Elastic potential energy</dfn></strong> is stored in objects that have been stretched or compressed, such as springs, rubber bands, and trampolines. When the object returns to its original shape, this energy is released.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Electrostatic energy</strong> is stored when electric charges are separated. <strong>Magnetic energy</strong> is stored when magnets are pushed together or pulled apart. <strong>Nuclear energy</strong> is stored in the nuclei of atoms and released during nuclear reactions such as fission and fusion.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Energy Transfers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Energy can be transferred between stores in four main ways, known as <dfn class="term" data-def="The four ways energy moves between stores: mechanically (by a force), electrically (by a current), by heating, or by radiation (light or sound waves).">energy transfer pathways</dfn>. These are: <strong>mechanically</strong> (when a force acts on an object), <strong>electrically</strong> (when charge flows through a circuit), <strong>by heating</strong> (when there is a temperature difference), and <strong>by radiation</strong> (such as light waves or sound waves).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">For example, when a ball is dropped from a height: energy is transferred from the gravitational potential store to the kinetic store as the ball falls (mechanically, by the force of gravity). When the ball hits the ground, some energy is transferred to the thermal store of the ball and the ground (by heating), and some to the surroundings as sound (by radiation).</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Energy is always conserved. In a closed system, the total energy before a transfer equals the total energy after. Energy that seems to &ldquo;disappear&rdquo; has actually been transferred to less useful stores &mdash; usually the thermal store of the surroundings.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n15">In any transfer, some energy is <dfn class="term" data-def="Energy that is transferred to stores that are not useful &mdash; typically the thermal store of the surroundings, often described as wasted energy.">dissipated</dfn> (spread out) into less useful stores, usually as thermal energy in the surroundings. This is why no energy transfer is 100% efficient in practice. A light bulb, for example, transfers energy from the chemical store of the mains supply to light (useful) and thermal energy (wasted).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Calculating Kinetic Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The amount of kinetic energy an object has depends on its mass and speed. The equation is:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Kinetic energy = &frac12; &times; mass &times; speed&sup2;</strong><br>E<sub>k</sub> = &frac12; mv&sup2;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Where E<sub>k</sub> is kinetic energy in joules (J), m is mass in kilograms (kg), and v is speed in metres per second (m/s). Notice that speed is squared &mdash; this means doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Calculating Gravitational Potential Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Gravitational potential energy depends on the mass of the object, the gravitational field strength, and the height above the ground:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>GPE = mass &times; gravitational field strength &times; height</strong><br>E<sub>p</sub> = mgh</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Where E<sub>p</sub> is gravitational potential energy in joules (J), m is mass in kilograms (kg), g is gravitational field strength in newtons per kilogram (N/kg) &mdash; on Earth, g = 9.8 N/kg &mdash; and h is height in metres (m).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Elastic Potential Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">When a spring is stretched or compressed within its limit of proportionality, it stores elastic potential energy. The equation is:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Elastic potential energy = &frac12; &times; spring constant &times; extension&sup2;</strong><br>E<sub>e</sub> = &frac12; ke&sup2;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Where E<sub>e</sub> is elastic potential energy in joules (J), k is the <dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how stiff a spring is &mdash; the force needed to extend or compress it by one metre, measured in newtons per metre (N/m).">spring constant</dfn> in newtons per metre (N/m), and e is the extension in metres (m). Like kinetic energy, extension is squared &mdash; doubling the extension quadruples the energy stored.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Work Done and Energy Transfer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy transferred when a force moves an object through a distance &mdash; measured in joules (J). Work done equals force times distance.">Work done</dfn> is the scientific term for energy transfer. When a force causes an object to move through a distance, work is done on that object. The equation is:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Work done = force &times; distance</strong><br>W = Fs</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35">Where W is work done in joules (J), F is force in newtons (N), and s is distance along the line of action of the force in metres (m). One <dfn class="term" data-def="The SI unit of energy &mdash; one joule is the energy transferred when a force of one newton moves an object one metre.">joule</dfn> of work is done when a force of one newton moves an object one metre.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n36">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Work done = energy transferred. These two quantities are always equal. If 500 J of work is done on an object, then 500 J of energy has been transferred. The unit for both is the joule (J).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n37">Power</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n38"><dfn class="term" data-def="The rate at which energy is transferred or work is done &mdash; measured in watts (W), where 1 watt equals 1 joule per second.">Power</dfn> is the rate at which energy is transferred, or equivalently the rate at which work is done. A more powerful device transfers the same amount of energy in less time. The equations are:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Power = energy transferred &divide; time</strong><br>P = E/t</p>
<p data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Power = work done &divide; time</strong><br>P = W/t</p>
<p data-narration-id="n41">Power is measured in <dfn class="term" data-def="The SI unit of power &mdash; one watt equals one joule of energy transferred per second.">watts</dfn> (W). One watt means one joule of energy is transferred per second. A 60 W light bulb transfers 60 joules of energy every second. For larger values, kilowatts (kW) are used: 1 kW = 1000 W.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n44">In AQA Combined Science, calculation questions on energy are very common. Always show your working clearly: write the equation, substitute values with units, and give your final answer with the correct unit (J for energy, W for power). Watch out for unit conversions &mdash; time must be in seconds for power calculations, and speed must be in m/s for kinetic energy. In &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about energy transfers, name the specific energy stores (not just &ldquo;energy&rdquo;) and state how the energy is transferred between them.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n45">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n46">There are eight energy stores (kinetic, thermal, chemical, gravitational potential, elastic potential, electrostatic, magnetic, nuclear) and four transfer pathways (mechanically, electrically, by heating, by radiation).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n47">Key equations: E<sub>k</sub> = &frac12;mv&sup2;, E<sub>p</sub> = mgh, E<sub>e</sub> = &frac12;ke&sup2;, W = Fs, and P = E/t. You must be able to select and apply the correct equation for each scenario.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n48">Energy is always conserved &mdash; it cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between stores. Dissipated energy (usually thermal) is wasted, not lost.</li></ul>
