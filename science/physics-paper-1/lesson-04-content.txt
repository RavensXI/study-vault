# Series and Parallel Circuits

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Two Types of Circuit</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Every electrical circuit you meet in GCSE Physics falls into one of two categories: <dfn class="term" data-def="A circuit in which all components are connected end-to-end in a single loop, so there is only one path for current to flow.">series</dfn> or <dfn class="term" data-def="A circuit in which components are connected on separate branches, giving the current more than one path to follow.">parallel</dfn>. Understanding how current, voltage, and resistance behave in each type is essential &mdash; the exam frequently tests whether you can apply the rules to unfamiliar circuits and perform calculations.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">In a <strong>series circuit</strong>, all components are connected one after another in a single loop. There is only one path for the current to take. In a <strong>parallel circuit</strong>, the circuit splits into two or more branches, giving the current multiple paths. Most real-world circuits &mdash; including household wiring &mdash; use parallel connections.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">Current in Series Circuits</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">In a series circuit, there is only one path for the <dfn class="term" data-def="The rate of flow of electrical charge, measured in amperes (A).">current</dfn> to flow through. This means the current is the <strong>same</strong> at every point in the circuit. It does not matter where you place an ammeter &mdash; before the first component, between the two, or after the last &mdash; the reading will always be identical.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n6">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In a series circuit, the current is the same everywhere: I<sub>total</sub> = I<sub>1</sub> = I<sub>2</sub> = I<sub>3</sub></p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n7">This happens because charge has no alternative route. Every coulomb of charge that leaves the cell must pass through every component before returning. If one component breaks, the entire circuit stops working because the single loop is broken.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Voltage in Series Circuits</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><dfn class="term" data-def="The potential difference across a component, measured in volts (V) &mdash; it represents the energy transferred per coulomb of charge.">Voltage</dfn> (potential difference) behaves differently from current in a series circuit. The total voltage supplied by the cell is <strong>shared</strong> between the components. Each component uses up some of the energy carried by the charge, so the voltages across all components add up to the supply voltage.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In a series circuit, the voltages across components add up to the total supply voltage: V<sub>total</sub> = V<sub>1</sub> + V<sub>2</sub> + V<sub>3</sub></p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n11">If two identical resistors are connected in series to a 6 V battery, each resistor gets 3 V across it. If the resistors are different, the one with the higher resistance takes a larger share of the voltage.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Resistance in Series Circuits</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">When <dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how much a component opposes the flow of current, measured in ohms (&Omega;).">resistors</dfn> are connected in series, the total resistance is simply the sum of all the individual resistances. Adding more resistors in series increases the total resistance, which reduces the current flowing through the circuit.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Current in Parallel Circuits</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">In a parallel circuit, the current <strong>splits</strong> at each junction (branch point). The total current from the supply equals the sum of the currents through each branch. More current flows through the branch with the lower resistance.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n19">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In a parallel circuit, current is shared between branches: I<sub>total</sub> = I<sub>1</sub> + I<sub>2</sub> + I<sub>3</sub></p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n20">For example, if a parallel circuit draws 3 A from the supply and one branch carries 1.2 A, the other branch must carry 1.8 A. The current recombines after the branches and returns to the cell.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Voltage in Parallel Circuits</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">The voltage across each branch of a parallel circuit is the <strong>same</strong> as the supply voltage. This is because each branch is connected directly to the cell, so every component on every branch receives the full <dfn class="term" data-def="The difference in electrical potential between two points in a circuit, also called voltage, measured in volts (V).">potential difference</dfn> from the supply.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In a parallel circuit, the voltage across each branch equals the supply voltage: V<sub>total</sub> = V<sub>1</sub> = V<sub>2</sub> = V<sub>3</sub></p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n24">This is why household appliances are wired in parallel &mdash; each device receives the full 230 V mains supply regardless of how many other devices are switched on.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Resistance in Parallel Circuits</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Adding resistors in parallel <strong>decreases</strong> the total resistance of the circuit. This seems surprising, but it happens because each new branch provides an additional path for current to flow. The total resistance of a parallel combination is always less than the smallest individual resistor.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n28">Example: Two resistors of 6 &Omega; and 12 &Omega; are connected in parallel. Find the total resistance.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29">1 &divide; R<sub>total</sub> = 1 &divide; 6 + 1 &divide; 12 = 2 &divide; 12 + 1 &divide; 12 = 3 &divide; 12</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30">R<sub>total</sub> = 12 &divide; 3 = <strong>4 &Omega;</strong></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n31">Notice that 4 &Omega; is less than the smallest individual resistor (6 &Omega;). This is always the case with parallel resistors.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Why Does This Matter?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Understanding these rules lets you predict what happens when components are added to or removed from a circuit. In a series circuit, adding more resistors <strong>increases</strong> total resistance and <strong>decreases</strong> current. In a parallel circuit, adding more branches <strong>decreases</strong> total resistance and <strong>increases</strong> total current from the supply.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n38"><dfn class="term" data-def="A set of lights wired in parallel so that if one bulb blows, the rest remain lit.">Christmas tree lights</dfn> are a classic example. Old-fashioned sets used series wiring &mdash; if one bulb blew, the whole string went dark. Modern sets use parallel wiring, so a single blown bulb does not affect the rest.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n39">In exam questions, you may be given a circuit diagram and asked to identify whether components are in series or parallel, then apply the correct rules. Always start by tracing the path of the current: if there is only one loop, it is series; if the current splits, it is parallel.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n40">When a question asks you to <strong>calculate</strong> total resistance, show your working clearly &mdash; write out the formula first, then substitute the values. For parallel resistance, remember to find 1 &divide; R<sub>total</sub> first, then flip it to get R<sub>total</sub>. A common mistake is forgetting the final step and leaving the answer as 1 &divide; R<sub>total</sub>. For <strong>compare and explain</strong> questions about series vs parallel, always state the rule (e.g. &ldquo;current is the same in series&rdquo;) and then explain <em>why</em> (e.g. &ldquo;because there is only one path for the charge to flow through&rdquo;).</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n41">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n42">In a series circuit, current is the same everywhere and voltage is shared between components. Total resistance = R<sub>1</sub> + R<sub>2</sub> + R<sub>3</sub>.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n43">In a parallel circuit, voltage is the same across each branch and current splits between branches. Total resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistor.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44">Adding resistors in series increases total resistance and decreases current; adding branches in parallel decreases total resistance and increases total current.</li></ul>
