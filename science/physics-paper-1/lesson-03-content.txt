# Circuits: Current, Resistance and Potential Difference

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Current, Resistance and Potential Difference?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Every electrical circuit relies on three linked quantities. <dfn class="term" data-def="The flow of electrical charge around a circuit, measured in amperes (A) using an ammeter.">Current</dfn> is the flow of electrical charge around a circuit. It is measured in <dfn class="term" data-def="The unit of electric current, symbol A.">amperes</dfn> (A) using an <dfn class="term" data-def="An instrument used to measure electric current, connected in series with the component being tested.">ammeter</dfn>, which is always connected in <strong>series</strong> &mdash; in line with the component being tested.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy transferred per unit of charge passed, measured in volts (V) using a voltmeter. Also called voltage.">Potential difference</dfn> (p.d.) is the energy transferred per unit of charge that passes between two points. It is measured in <dfn class="term" data-def="The unit of potential difference, symbol V.">volts</dfn> (V) using a <dfn class="term" data-def="An instrument used to measure potential difference, connected in parallel across the component being tested.">voltmeter</dfn>, which is always connected in <strong>parallel</strong> &mdash; across the component.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4"><dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how much a component opposes the flow of current through it, measured in ohms (&Omega;).">Resistance</dfn> is a measure of how much a component opposes the flow of current. It is measured in <dfn class="term" data-def="The unit of electrical resistance, symbol &Omega;.">ohms</dfn> (&Omega;). The higher the resistance, the harder it is for current to flow.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In most circuits the charge carriers are <strong>electrons</strong>. Conventional current flows from positive to negative, but electrons actually move from negative to positive.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Ohm&rsquo;s Law</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The relationship between potential difference, current and resistance is given by the equation:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>potential difference = current &times; resistance</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>V = I &times; R</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">where V is potential difference in volts, I is current in amperes, and R is resistance in ohms. You need to be able to rearrange this equation: <strong>I = V &divide; R</strong> and <strong>R = V &divide; I</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">This equation applies to all components, but <dfn class="term" data-def="Describes a component where the current is directly proportional to the potential difference at a constant temperature, giving a straight-line I-V graph through the origin.">Ohm&rsquo;s law</dfn> specifically states that for some conductors, the current is <strong>directly proportional</strong> to the potential difference &mdash; provided the <strong>temperature remains constant</strong>. A component that behaves this way is called an <strong>ohmic conductor</strong> because its resistance stays the same regardless of the current flowing through it. A fixed resistor at constant temperature is a good example.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>V = IR is the equation linking potential difference, current and resistance. <strong>Ohm&rsquo;s law</strong> is the statement that current is directly proportional to p.d. at constant temperature &mdash; this only applies to ohmic conductors.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Investigating I&ndash;V Characteristics</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">AQA Required Practical 4 asks you to investigate the I&ndash;V characteristics of different circuit components. The basic circuit uses a <strong>variable power supply</strong> (or a battery with a <strong>variable resistor</strong>), an ammeter in series, a voltmeter in parallel across the component, and the component itself.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">You change the potential difference using the variable supply, record the ammeter and voltmeter readings at each setting, then reverse the connections and repeat for negative values. Plotting current (y-axis) against potential difference (x-axis) gives an <dfn class="term" data-def="A graph of current against potential difference for a component, showing how the component behaves electrically.">I&ndash;V characteristic</dfn> graph. The shape of this graph tells you about the component&rsquo;s behaviour.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">I&ndash;V Graphs for Key Components</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Ohmic conductor (fixed resistor at constant temperature):</strong> The graph is a <strong>straight line through the origin</strong>. This means current is directly proportional to potential difference &mdash; doubling the p.d. doubles the current. The resistance is constant and can be calculated from the gradient.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Filament lamp:</strong> The graph is a <strong>curve</strong> that starts steep and flattens out. As current increases, the filament heats up and its resistance increases. At higher p.d. values, the same increase in voltage produces a smaller increase in current. The graph is symmetrical &mdash; reversing the p.d. gives the same shape in the opposite direction.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Diode:</strong> A <dfn class="term" data-def="A component that allows current to flow in one direction only (forward bias) and blocks it in the reverse direction.">diode</dfn> allows current to flow in only one direction. In the <strong>forward direction</strong>, current flows once the p.d. reaches about 0.7 V (the threshold voltage), and then rises steeply. In the <strong>reverse direction</strong>, the resistance is very high and essentially no current flows. The I&ndash;V graph shows current only on one side of the origin.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Special Resistors: LDRs and Thermistors</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Some components have a resistance that changes in response to the environment. These are useful in <strong>sensing circuits</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A light-dependent resistor &mdash; its resistance decreases as the light intensity increases.">light-dependent resistor (LDR)</dfn> has a resistance that depends on light intensity. In <strong>bright light</strong>, the resistance of an LDR is <strong>low</strong>. In <strong>darkness</strong>, the resistance is <strong>high</strong>. LDRs are used in circuits that detect light levels &mdash; for example, automatic street lights that switch on when it gets dark.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A resistor whose resistance decreases as the temperature increases.">thermistor</dfn> (in GCSE, this means an NTC thermistor) has a resistance that depends on temperature. As the <strong>temperature increases</strong>, the resistance of a thermistor <strong>decreases</strong>. As the temperature decreases, the resistance increases. Thermistors are used in temperature-sensing circuits &mdash; for example, thermostats that control central heating.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n32">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>LDR:</strong> more light &rarr; lower resistance. <strong>Thermistor:</strong> higher temperature &rarr; lower resistance. Both are used in sensing circuits. Remember: for both components, the resistance goes <strong>down</strong> when the stimulus (light or heat) goes <strong>up</strong>.</p>
</div>

    <p data-narration-id="n34">In a <strong>thermistor</strong>, the semiconductor material releases more charge carriers as it heats up, making it easier for current to flow &mdash; so resistance decreases with temperature.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n35">In an <strong>LDR</strong>, photons of light release charge carriers in the semiconductor &mdash; more light means more carriers and lower resistance.</p>
  </div></div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n36">Charge Flow and Current</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n37">Current is linked to charge flow by the equation:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>charge flow = current &times; time</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Q = I &times; t</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n40">where Q is charge in <dfn class="term" data-def="The unit of electric charge, symbol C. One coulomb is the charge transferred by a current of one ampere in one second.">coulombs</dfn> (C), I is current in amperes (A), and t is time in seconds (s). One ampere means one coulomb of charge passes a point in the circuit every second.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n41">In calculation questions, always write the equation first, substitute the values with units, and show your working clearly. If asked to &ldquo;explain&rdquo; how the resistance of a filament lamp changes, link your answer to temperature: as current increases, the filament heats up, metal ions vibrate more, so resistance increases. For the required practical, be ready to describe the circuit, name the measuring instruments (ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel), and explain how you would obtain negative readings by reversing the connections.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n42">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n43">V = IR links potential difference (V), current (I) and resistance (R). Ohm&rsquo;s law states current is directly proportional to p.d. at constant temperature &mdash; true for ohmic conductors only.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44">I&ndash;V graphs show how components behave: straight line (resistor), curve (filament lamp), one-way flow (diode). Know how to sketch and identify each.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n45">LDR resistance falls in bright light; thermistor resistance falls as temperature rises. Both are used in sensing circuits.</li></ul>
