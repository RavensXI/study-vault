# Domestic Electricity, Power and the National Grid

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">AC and DC &mdash; Two Types of Current</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All electrical circuits carry <dfn class="term" data-def="The flow of electrical charge around a circuit, measured in amperes (A).">current</dfn>, but current can flow in two different ways. Understanding the difference between them is essential for this topic.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><dfn class="term" data-def="Direct current &mdash; electric current that flows in one direction only, as supplied by cells and batteries.">Direct current (d.c.)</dfn> flows in one direction only. The current is constant in size and does not change direction. Batteries, cells, and solar cells all supply d.c. If you plotted d.c. on a voltage&ndash;time graph, you would see a flat horizontal line above the axis.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4"><dfn class="term" data-def="Alternating current &mdash; electric current that repeatedly reverses direction, as supplied by the mains.">Alternating current (a.c.)</dfn> repeatedly changes direction. The current flows back and forth, and the <dfn class="term" data-def="The difference in electrical energy carried by the charge between two points in a circuit, measured in volts (V).">potential difference</dfn> alternates between positive and negative values. The UK <dfn class="term" data-def="The electricity supply to homes and buildings in the UK, providing a.c. at approximately 230 V and a frequency of 50 Hz.">mains electricity</dfn> supply is a.c. with a frequency of 50&thinsp;Hz and a peak voltage of about 230&thinsp;V. This means the current changes direction 50 times every second.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>UK mains supply: <strong>a.c., 230&thinsp;V, 50&thinsp;Hz</strong>. Batteries supply: <strong>d.c.</strong> These three facts come up repeatedly in exam questions.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">The Three-Pin Plug</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Most electrical appliances in the UK connect to the mains through a <dfn class="term" data-def="A UK mains plug containing three wires (live, neutral, earth), a fuse, and a cable grip, designed for safe connection to the 230 V supply.">three-pin plug</dfn>. Inside the plug there are three colour-coded wires, a fuse, and a cable grip. You need to know what each part does.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Live wire (brown):</strong> The <dfn class="term" data-def="The brown wire in a plug that carries the alternating potential difference from the supply, alternating between about +230 V and &minus;230 V.">live wire</dfn> carries the alternating potential difference from the supply. It alternates between approximately +230&thinsp;V and &minus;230&thinsp;V. This is the dangerous wire &mdash; even when an appliance is switched off, the live wire can still be at 230&thinsp;V.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Neutral wire (blue):</strong> The <dfn class="term" data-def="The blue wire in a plug that completes the circuit and is at, or close to, 0 V.">neutral wire</dfn> completes the circuit. It is at, or close to, 0&thinsp;V (earth potential). Current flows in through the live wire and out through the neutral wire.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Earth wire (green and yellow):</strong> The <dfn class="term" data-def="The green-and-yellow striped wire in a plug that provides a safe path for current to flow to earth if a fault occurs, preventing electrocution.">earth wire</dfn> is a safety wire. It does not carry current during normal operation. If a fault causes the metal casing of an appliance to become live, the earth wire provides a low-resistance path for current to flow safely to earth, which causes the fuse to blow and breaks the circuit.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Live = brown (dangerous, ~230 V). Neutral = blue (0 V, completes circuit). Earth = green/yellow (safety, connected to metal casing).</strong> A common exam mistake is mixing up the wire colours &mdash; remember: <strong>B</strong>rown = <strong>L</strong>ive (Bottom Right), <strong>B</strong>lue = <strong>N</strong>eutral (Bottom Left).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Electrical Safety &mdash; Fuses and Circuit Breakers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Electrical faults can be dangerous. If the current flowing through an appliance becomes too large, wires overheat, which can cause fires. Two devices protect against this: fuses and circuit breakers.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A safety device containing a thin wire that melts and breaks the circuit when the current exceeds its rated value.">fuse</dfn> is a thin piece of wire inside a ceramic or glass casing. When the current exceeds the fuse&rsquo;s rated value, the wire heats up, melts, and breaks the circuit. Fuses are fitted inside the plug, on the live wire side. Common fuse ratings are 3&thinsp;A, 5&thinsp;A, and 13&thinsp;A. Once a fuse has &ldquo;blown&rdquo; it must be replaced &mdash; it cannot be reused.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">To select the correct fuse, calculate the normal operating current of the appliance using P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;V, then choose a fuse with a rating just above that value. For example, a 700&thinsp;W microwave on a 230&thinsp;V supply draws about 3.04&thinsp;A, so a 5&thinsp;A fuse is correct. A 3&thinsp;A fuse would blow during normal use; a 13&thinsp;A fuse would allow a dangerously large current before breaking the circuit.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n17">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An automatic safety switch that breaks a circuit when the current exceeds a safe level; unlike a fuse, it can be reset and reused.">circuit breaker</dfn> does the same job as a fuse but works differently. It is an electromagnetic switch that trips (opens) when the current is too large. Unlike a fuse, a circuit breaker can be reset by flipping it back &mdash; there is no wire to replace. Circuit breakers also react faster than fuses, which makes them safer. Modern consumer units (fuse boxes) in homes use circuit breakers rather than fuses.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Key difference for the exam:</strong> Fuses melt and must be replaced. Circuit breakers trip and can be reset. Both break the circuit when current is too high. Neither protects against electrocution on its own &mdash; that requires an RCD (residual current device), which detects a difference in current between the live and neutral wires.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n19">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Fuses prevent <strong>fires</strong>, not electrocution. It only takes about 0.1&thinsp;A to stop a human heart, but a 3&thinsp;A fuse would not blow at such a low current. The earth wire works with the fuse &mdash; a fault causes a surge of current through the earth wire, which blows the fuse and disconnects the supply.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Power Equations</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><dfn class="term" data-def="The rate at which energy is transferred or the rate at which work is done, measured in watts (W).">Power</dfn> is the rate at which energy is transferred. It is measured in watts (W), where 1&thinsp;W means 1 joule of energy transferred per second. Two key equations link power to other electrical quantities:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Equation 1:</strong> P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;V &mdash; Power (W) equals current (A) multiplied by potential difference (V).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Equation 2:</strong> P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&sup2;&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;R &mdash; Power (W) equals current squared (A&sup2;) multiplied by resistance (&Omega;). This version is useful when you do not know the voltage.</p>

A 3&thinsp;A fuse would be appropriate.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Energy Transferred</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The amount of energy transferred by an appliance depends on its power and how long it is used for. The equation is:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>E&thinsp;=&thinsp;P&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;t</strong> &mdash; Energy transferred (J) equals power (W) multiplied by time (s). This can also be written as E&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;V&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;t by substituting P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;V.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">For example, a 2000&thinsp;W heater left on for 5 minutes transfers E&thinsp;=&thinsp;2000&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;300&thinsp;=&thinsp;600,000&thinsp;J (600&thinsp;kJ). Always convert minutes to seconds before substituting into the equation.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">The National Grid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A nationwide network of cables and transformers that distributes electricity from power stations to homes and businesses across the UK.">National Grid</dfn> is a system of cables and <dfn class="term" data-def="A device that changes the potential difference of an alternating current supply; step-up transformers increase voltage, step-down transformers decrease it.">transformers</dfn> that links power stations to consumers across the UK. Electricity is generated at power stations, transmitted along high-voltage cables supported by pylons, and then delivered to homes and businesses.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32">The key challenge is efficiency. When current flows through cables, some energy is wasted as heat due to the resistance of the wires. The power lost as heat is given by P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&sup2;&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;R. Because current is squared in this equation, even a small reduction in current leads to a large reduction in energy wasted.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Step-Up and Step-Down Transformers</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><dfn class="term" data-def="A transformer that increases the potential difference and decreases the current, used between power stations and transmission cables.">Step-up transformers</dfn> at power stations increase the potential difference from about 25,000&thinsp;V to 400,000&thinsp;V for transmission. Increasing the voltage means the current decreases (since power&thinsp;=&thinsp;voltage&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;current, and power stays roughly constant). Lower current means less energy is wasted heating the cables.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34"><dfn class="term" data-def="A transformer that decreases the potential difference and increases the current, used to reduce voltage to safe levels for homes (230 V).">Step-down transformers</dfn> near homes and businesses reduce the potential difference back down to 230&thinsp;V, which is safe for domestic use. Without transformers, either the cables would need to be impossibly thick (to reduce resistance), or enormous amounts of energy would be wasted as heat.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n35">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The National Grid uses <strong>high voltage and low current</strong> for transmission to reduce energy losses. Power lost in cables&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&sup2;&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;R &mdash; halving the current reduces power loss to a quarter.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n36">In calculation questions, always show your working: write the equation, substitute the values, then give the answer with the correct unit. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about the National Grid, link your answer to the equation P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&sup2;&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;R &mdash; state that step-up transformers increase voltage, which decreases current, which reduces energy lost as heat in the cables. Many students lose marks by saying &ldquo;reduces energy loss&rdquo; without explaining <em>why</em> (the I&sup2; relationship).</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n37">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n38">UK mains supply is a.c. at 230&thinsp;V, 50&thinsp;Hz. Batteries supply d.c. The live wire carries the alternating p.d., the neutral is at 0&thinsp;V, and the earth wire is a safety connection.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">Fuses and circuit breakers protect against fires caused by excess current. Choose a fuse with a rating just above the normal operating current (calculated using P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;V).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40">The National Grid transmits electricity at high voltage and low current to reduce energy losses (P&thinsp;=&thinsp;I&sup2;&thinsp;&times;&thinsp;R). Step-up transformers increase voltage for transmission; step-down transformers reduce it to 230&thinsp;V for homes.</li></ul>
