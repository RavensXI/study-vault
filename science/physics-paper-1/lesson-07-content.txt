# Internal Energy, Specific Heat Capacity and Latent Heat

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Internal Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Every substance is made up of particles (atoms or molecules) that are constantly moving. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The total kinetic energy and potential energy of all the particles in a system.">internal energy</dfn> of a system is the total kinetic energy and potential energy of all the particles within it. When you heat a substance, you transfer energy to its particles, increasing the internal energy of the system.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The energy a particle has due to its motion &mdash; faster-moving particles have more kinetic energy.">kinetic energy</dfn> of particles is related to their movement. In a solid, particles vibrate about fixed positions. In a liquid, they move more freely. In a gas, they move rapidly in all directions. Heating a substance makes the particles move faster, increasing their kinetic energy and raising the temperature.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Internal energy = total kinetic energy + total potential energy of all the particles. A rise in temperature means the particles have gained kinetic energy. A change of state means the particles have gained potential energy (their spacing changes) but the temperature stays the same.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Changes of State and Internal Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">When a substance changes <dfn class="term" data-def="The physical form a substance takes &mdash; solid, liquid, or gas &mdash; determined by the arrangement and energy of its particles.">state</dfn> (for example, from solid to liquid or liquid to gas), the energy being supplied does not increase the temperature. Instead, it is used to break or weaken the bonds between particles, increasing their potential energy. The particles move further apart, but their speed does not change, so the temperature remains constant during a change of state.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">This is a crucial point for the exam: <strong>during a change of state, the temperature does not change</strong> even though energy is still being transferred. Once the change of state is complete, the temperature begins to rise again as kinetic energy increases.</p>

It has a distinctive shape with five regions:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Region 1 &mdash; Solid heating:</strong> Temperature rises as the particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate faster. <strong>Region 2 &mdash; Melting (fusion):</strong> Temperature stays constant at the melting point while the solid changes to a liquid. Energy is used to overcome forces between particles (potential energy increases). <strong>Region 3 &mdash; Liquid heating:</strong> Temperature rises again as liquid particles gain kinetic energy. <strong>Region 4 &mdash; Boiling (vaporisation):</strong> Temperature stays constant at the boiling point while the liquid changes to a gas. <strong>Region 5 &mdash; Gas heating:</strong> Temperature rises as gas particles gain kinetic energy.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10">The flat sections on a heating curve are where changes of state happen. During these flat sections, the substance exists as a mixture of two states (e.g. solid and liquid during melting). All the energy supplied goes into changing the arrangement of particles, not into raising the temperature.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Specific Heat Capacity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 &deg;C. Units: J/kg &deg;C.">Specific heat capacity</dfn> (SHC) tells you how much energy is needed to raise the temperature of 1&thinsp;kg of a substance by 1&thinsp;&deg;C. Different materials have different specific heat capacities. Water has a high SHC of 4,200&thinsp;J/kg&thinsp;&deg;C, meaning it takes a lot of energy to heat up. Aluminium has an SHC of about 900&thinsp;J/kg&thinsp;&deg;C, so it heats up much more quickly.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The equation linking energy, mass, specific heat capacity, and temperature change is:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>&Delta;E = m &times; c &times; &Delta;&theta;</strong> &mdash; where &Delta;E is the change in thermal energy (J), m is the mass (kg), c is the specific heat capacity (J/kg&thinsp;&deg;C), and &Delta;&theta; is the temperature change (&deg;C). This equation is given on the physics equation sheet in the exam.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n15">To use this equation, you need to be comfortable rearranging it. For example, to find the specific heat capacity: c = &Delta;E &divide; (m &times; &Delta;&theta;). To find the temperature change: &Delta;&theta; = &Delta;E &divide; (m &times; c). Always check your units &mdash; mass must be in kilograms, not grams.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Question:</strong> A 1&thinsp;kg aluminium block is heated by a 50&thinsp;W heater for 5 minutes. The temperature rises by 12.5&thinsp;&deg;C. Calculate the energy supplied and the specific heat capacity of aluminium.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Step 1 &mdash; Energy supplied:</strong> Power = 50&thinsp;W, Time = 5 &times; 60 = 300&thinsp;s. Energy = Power &times; Time = 50 &times; 300 = 15,000&thinsp;J.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Step 2 &mdash; Specific heat capacity:</strong> &Delta;E = m &times; c &times; &Delta;&theta;, so c = &Delta;E &divide; (m &times; &Delta;&theta;) = 15,000 &divide; (1 &times; 12.5) = 1,200&thinsp;J/kg&thinsp;&deg;C.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Note:</strong> The accepted value for aluminium is about 900&thinsp;J/kg&thinsp;&deg;C. The experimental value is higher because some energy is lost to the surroundings rather than heating the block. Insulating the block with a lagging jacket would improve accuracy.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">The SHC Required Practical</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">In the required practical, you determine the specific heat capacity of a metal block or water using an electrical heater. Key steps include: measuring the mass of the block (in kg), recording the starting temperature, switching on the heater for a set time, recording the final temperature, and calculating the energy supplied using E = P &times; t (where P is the power in watts and t is the time in seconds).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Common improvements to make the experiment more accurate include: wrapping the block in insulation to reduce thermal energy losses, waiting for the temperature to peak after switching off the heater before taking the final reading, and using a digital thermometer for more precise temperature measurements. The experimental value is usually too high because not all the electrical energy heats the block &mdash; some is lost to the surroundings.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Specific Latent Heat</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">When a substance changes state, the energy needed is described by the <dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of energy required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance with no change in temperature. Units: J/kg.">specific latent heat</dfn>. There are two types:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy required to change 1 kg of a substance from solid to liquid (or liquid to solid) at its melting point, with no temperature change.">Specific latent heat of fusion</dfn></strong> (L<sub>f</sub>) is the energy required to change 1&thinsp;kg of a substance from a solid to a liquid (or liquid to solid) at its melting point, without any change in temperature. For water, L<sub>f</sub> = 334,000&thinsp;J/kg.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The energy required to change 1 kg of a substance from liquid to gas (or gas to liquid) at its boiling point, with no temperature change.">Specific latent heat of vaporisation</dfn></strong> (L<sub>v</sub>) is the energy required to change 1&thinsp;kg of a substance from a liquid to a gas (or gas to liquid) at its boiling point, without any change in temperature. For water, L<sub>v</sub> = 2,260,000&thinsp;J/kg. Vaporisation requires much more energy than fusion because the particles must completely overcome the intermolecular forces to become a gas.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n27">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>E = m &times; L</strong> &mdash; where E is the energy (J), m is the mass (kg), and L is the specific latent heat (J/kg). This equation is given on the physics equation sheet. Use L<sub>f</sub> for melting/freezing and L<sub>v</sub> for boiling/condensing.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Linking It All Together</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">When you heat a substance from one state to another, you may need to use both equations in sequence. For example, to heat ice from &minus;10&thinsp;&deg;C to steam at 100&thinsp;&deg;C, you would calculate: (1) the energy to heat the ice from &minus;10&thinsp;&deg;C to 0&thinsp;&deg;C using &Delta;E = mc&Delta;&theta;, (2) the energy to melt the ice at 0&thinsp;&deg;C using E = mL<sub>f</sub>, (3) the energy to heat the water from 0&thinsp;&deg;C to 100&thinsp;&deg;C using &Delta;E = mc&Delta;&theta;, and (4) the energy to boil the water at 100&thinsp;&deg;C using E = mL<sub>v</sub>. Each stage requires a separate calculation, and the total energy is the sum of all four.</p>

This requires much more energy. For water, L<sub>v</sub> is roughly seven times larger than L<sub>f</sub>, reflecting the much greater separation of particles when forming a gas.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n35">In calculation questions on SHC and latent heat, always show your working clearly &mdash; you can pick up method marks even if your final answer is wrong. Remember that mass must be in kg (divide grams by 1,000) and that both equations (&Delta;E = mc&Delta;&theta; and E = mL) are given on the equation sheet. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about heating curves, state clearly that temperature stays constant during a change of state because energy is being used to overcome forces between particles (increasing potential energy) rather than increasing kinetic energy.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n36">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n37">Internal energy is the total kinetic and potential energy of all particles. Heating increases internal energy &mdash; either raising temperature (kinetic energy) or causing a change of state (potential energy).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Use &Delta;E = mc&Delta;&theta; for temperature changes and E = mL for changes of state. Both equations are provided in the exam &mdash; practise rearranging them and converting units.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">During a change of state the temperature remains constant because energy goes into breaking bonds between particles, not into making them move faster.</li></ul>
