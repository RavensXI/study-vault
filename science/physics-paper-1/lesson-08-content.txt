# Atomic Structure, Radioactivity and Half-Life

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Structure of the Atom</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Every atom has the same basic structure: a tiny, dense <dfn class="term" data-def="The central part of an atom, containing protons and neutrons, where almost all the atom&rsquo;s mass is concentrated.">nucleus</dfn> at the centre, surrounded by <dfn class="term" data-def="Negatively charged subatomic particles that orbit the nucleus in energy levels (shells).">electrons</dfn> orbiting in energy levels (shells). The nucleus contains two types of subatomic particle &mdash; <strong>protons</strong> (positive charge) and <strong>neutrons</strong> (no charge). Electrons are negatively charged and orbit at relatively large distances from the nucleus.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">An atom has a radius of about 1 &times; 10<sup>&minus;10</sup> m. The nucleus is around 10,000 times smaller than the atom itself, with a radius of about 1 &times; 10<sup>&minus;14</sup> m. Despite its tiny size, the nucleus contains almost all the atom&rsquo;s mass. Most of the atom is empty space.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>The <strong>atomic number</strong> (bottom number on the periodic table) tells you the number of protons. The <strong>mass number</strong> (top number) tells you the total number of protons and neutrons. Number of neutrons = mass number &minus; atomic number.</p>
</div>
<h3 data-narration-id="n5">Isotopes</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><dfn class="term" data-def="Atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons, giving them different mass numbers.">Isotopes</dfn> are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons. For example, carbon-12 and carbon-14 both have 6 protons, but carbon-12 has 6 neutrons while carbon-14 has 8. Some isotopes have unstable nuclei &mdash; these are <dfn class="term" data-def="An unstable isotope that decays over time by emitting radiation from its nucleus.">radioactive isotopes</dfn>. They decay by emitting radiation to become more stable.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n7">Types of Nuclear Radiation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">When an unstable nucleus decays, it can emit one or more types of <dfn class="term" data-def="Energy or particles released from the nucleus of an unstable atom during radioactive decay.">nuclear radiation</dfn>. There are three main types you need to know for combined science: alpha (&alpha;), beta (&beta;) and gamma (&gamma;).</p>

<p data-narration-id="n10">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of nuclear radiation consisting of a high-speed electron emitted from the nucleus when a neutron turns into a proton.">beta particle</dfn> is a high-speed electron emitted from the nucleus. It forms when a neutron splits into a proton and an electron &mdash; the electron is ejected at high speed. Beta particles are moderately ionising and moderately penetrating &mdash; they pass through paper but are stopped by a thin sheet of aluminium. When beta decay occurs, the mass number stays the same but the atomic number increases by 1.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n11"><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of electromagnetic radiation emitted from the nucleus; the most penetrating but least ionising form of nuclear radiation.">Gamma radiation</dfn> is an electromagnetic wave, not a particle. It has no mass and no charge. Gamma rays are the most penetrating type of radiation &mdash; they can only be reduced (not fully stopped) by several centimetres of lead or metres of concrete. However, they are the least ionising. Gamma emission does not change the mass number or atomic number of the nucleus.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>There is a trade-off between ionising power and penetration: <strong>alpha</strong> is the most ionising but least penetrating, <strong>gamma</strong> is the most penetrating but least ionising, and <strong>beta</strong> sits in between for both properties.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Radioactive Decay Equations</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which an unstable nucleus loses energy by emitting radiation, changing into a different atom or a more stable form.">Radioactive decay</dfn> is a random process &mdash; you cannot predict exactly when a particular nucleus will decay. However, you can represent what happens using nuclear equations. In every nuclear equation, both the mass numbers and atomic numbers must balance on each side.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Alpha decay example:</strong> When uranium-238 undergoes alpha decay, it loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons. The uranium atom becomes thorium-234:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16" style="text-align:center; font-size:1.1em;"><sup>238</sup><sub>92</sub>U &rarr; <sup>234</sup><sub>90</sub>Th + <sup>4</sup><sub>2</sub>He</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Beta decay example:</strong> When carbon-14 undergoes beta decay, a neutron in the nucleus turns into a proton and emits an electron. Carbon-14 becomes nitrogen-14:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18" style="text-align:center; font-size:1.1em;"><sup>14</sup><sub>6</sub>C &rarr; <sup>14</sup><sub>7</sub>N + <sup>0</sup><sub>&minus;1</sub>e</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n19">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>In alpha decay, mass number drops by 4 and atomic number drops by 2. In beta decay, mass number stays the same and atomic number increases by 1. In gamma emission, neither number changes &mdash; the nucleus just loses energy.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Half-Life</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The time it takes for the number of unstable nuclei in a radioactive sample to halve, or for the count rate to fall to half its initial value.">half-life</dfn> of a radioactive isotope is the time it takes for the number of unstable nuclei in a sample to halve. It can also be defined as the time for the count rate (or <dfn class="term" data-def="The number of radioactive decays detected per second, measured using a Geiger-M&uuml;ller tube.">activity</dfn>) to fall to half its original value. Activity is measured in <dfn class="term" data-def="The unit of radioactive activity, equal to one decay per second. Symbol: Bq.">becquerels</dfn> (Bq), where 1 Bq = 1 decay per second.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Different isotopes have very different half-lives. Some, like uranium-238, have half-lives of billions of years. Others decay in fractions of a second. The key skill for exams is using half-life to calculate how activity changes over time.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n23">A sample has an activity of 8000 Bq. After 30 days, the activity has fallen to 1000 Bq. What is the half-life?</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Contamination and Irradiation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><dfn class="term" data-def="The unwanted presence of radioactive atoms on or inside a person, object or material.">Contamination</dfn> occurs when radioactive material gets onto or inside a person or object. This is dangerous because the radioactive atoms remain in contact with the body, continuing to emit radiation. <dfn class="term" data-def="Exposure to radiation from an external source, without the radioactive material being in direct contact.">Irradiation</dfn> is exposure to radiation from an external source &mdash; the person does not become radioactive themselves.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">The key difference matters for safety: contamination is more dangerous because you cannot move away from the source, whereas with irradiation the risk drops as soon as the source is removed or you move further away. Workers who handle radioactive materials take precautions such as wearing protective clothing, using tongs to increase distance from sources, and keeping exposure time as short as possible.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p><strong>Contamination</strong> = radioactive material is on you or inside you (ongoing exposure). <strong>Irradiation</strong> = you are exposed to radiation from a distance (exposure stops when source is removed). A person who has been irradiated does <strong>not</strong> become radioactive.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<div class="exam-tip-label">Exam Tip</div>
<p data-narration-id="n30">When writing nuclear decay equations, always check that both the mass numbers (top) and atomic numbers (bottom) balance on each side. For half-life calculations, the easiest method is to keep halving the activity until you reach the target value, then count the number of halvings. Remember the difference between contamination and irradiation &mdash; this is a very common exam question and students often mix them up.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n31">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n32">Atoms have a small, dense nucleus of protons and neutrons, with electrons in shells. <strong>Isotopes</strong> have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons &mdash; some are unstable and radioactive.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">The three types of nuclear radiation &mdash; <strong>alpha</strong>, <strong>beta</strong> and <strong>gamma</strong> &mdash; differ in their composition, ionising power, penetrating ability and effect on the nucleus.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Half-life</strong> is the time for the number of unstable nuclei (or count rate) to halve. <strong>Contamination</strong> means radioactive material is on or inside you; <strong>irradiation</strong> means exposure from an external source.</li></ul>
