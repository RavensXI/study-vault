# Conservation of Energy, Efficiency and Energy Resources

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Principle of Conservation of Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">One of the most important ideas in physics is the <dfn class="term" data-def="The principle that energy cannot be created or destroyed &mdash; only transferred between stores or dissipated to the surroundings.">conservation of energy</dfn>. This principle states that energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred from one store to another, or dissipated to the surroundings. The total amount of energy in a <dfn class="term" data-def="A collection of objects where no energy can enter or leave &mdash; the total energy remains constant.">closed system</dfn> always stays the same.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">A closed system is one where energy cannot enter or leave. An insulated flask of hot water is a good example &mdash; the thermal energy stays inside the flask because the insulation prevents transfer to the surroundings. In a closed system, any energy transferred away from one store must appear in another store.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Energy cannot be created or destroyed &mdash; only transferred between stores. In a closed system, the total energy before a transfer is always equal to the total energy afterwards.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Energy Transfers and Dissipation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">When energy is transferred, some of it usually ends up in stores that are not useful. This energy is said to be <dfn class="term" data-def="Energy that has spread out to the thermal energy store of the surroundings and is no longer useful.">dissipated</dfn> (or &ldquo;wasted&rdquo;). Dissipated energy is transferred to the thermal energy store of the surroundings, where it spreads out and becomes increasingly difficult to use.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Consider a light bulb. Electrical energy is transferred to the light&rsquo;s thermal and light energy stores. The light is useful, but the heat given off is <dfn class="term" data-def="Energy transferred during a process that is not part of the intended output &mdash; it reduces the efficiency of the device.">wasted energy</dfn>. The wasted thermal energy heats the surroundings and cannot easily be recovered. This is why no device is ever 100% efficient in practice.</p>

In a house, the biggest heat losses occur through the roof and walls, so insulating these areas has the greatest impact.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Calculating Efficiency</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how much of the input energy is usefully transferred &mdash; calculated as useful output divided by total input, expressed as a decimal or percentage.">Efficiency</dfn> tells us how good a device is at transferring energy usefully. It compares the useful output energy (or power) to the total input energy (or power).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The equation is:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Efficiency = useful output energy transfer &divide; total input energy transfer</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">This gives a decimal between 0 and 1. Multiply by 100 to get a percentage. You can also use power instead of energy:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Efficiency = useful output power &divide; total input power</strong></p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Efficiency = useful output energy &divide; total input energy. It can never be greater than 1 (100%) because energy is always dissipated. A higher efficiency means less energy is wasted.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Energy Resources &mdash; Renewable and Non-Renewable</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">The world relies on a mix of <dfn class="term" data-def="An energy source that can be replenished at the same rate it is used and will not run out &mdash; examples include solar, wind, and tidal.">renewable</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="An energy source that will eventually be used up because it is consumed faster than it can be replaced &mdash; examples include fossil fuels and nuclear fuel.">non-renewable</dfn> energy resources to generate electricity, heat buildings, and power transport. Understanding the advantages and disadvantages of each type is a key part of the AQA spec (4.1.3).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Non-Renewable Resources</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><dfn class="term" data-def="Coal, oil, and natural gas &mdash; formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient organisms, releasing carbon dioxide when burned.">Fossil fuels</dfn> (coal, oil, and natural gas) are the most widely used non-renewable resources. They were formed over millions of years from the remains of dead organisms. Burning them releases carbon dioxide (a greenhouse gas that contributes to climate change) and sulphur dioxide (which causes acid rain).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><dfn class="term" data-def="Energy released by splitting atoms of uranium or plutonium in a controlled chain reaction &mdash; produces no greenhouse gases during operation but generates radioactive waste.">Nuclear energy</dfn> uses uranium or plutonium as fuel. Nuclear power stations produce no greenhouse gases during operation, and a small amount of fuel produces a large amount of energy. However, they are expensive to build and decommission, and used fuel rods produce <strong>radioactive waste</strong> that must be stored safely for thousands of years. The 1986 Chernobyl disaster highlighted the dangers of nuclear accidents.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Non-renewable resources will run out. The three fossil fuels are coal, oil, and natural gas. Nuclear fuel (uranium/plutonium) is also non-renewable. Fossil fuels release CO<sub>2</sub> (global warming) and SO<sub>2</sub> (acid rain).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Renewable Resources</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Renewable resources will not run out because they are replenished naturally. Most produce no greenhouse gases during operation, but they each have limitations.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Wind, Solar, and Hydroelectric Power</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Wind power:</strong> Wind turns turbine blades, transferring kinetic energy to electrical energy via a generator. Wind power produces no CO<sub>2</sub>, but turbines only generate electricity when the wind blows &mdash; making them <strong>unreliable</strong>. Some people also consider them unsightly or noisy.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Solar power:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="A device that converts light energy directly into electrical energy using semiconductor materials.">Solar cells</dfn> convert sunlight directly into electricity. Solar panels are renewable and cost nothing to run once installed, but they depend on sunlight and produce no electricity at night or on very cloudy days.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Hydroelectric power:</strong> Rainwater is collected in a reservoir and released downhill through turbines to generate electricity. It produces no CO<sub>2</sub> during operation and can respond quickly to changes in demand. However, building the dam requires flooding a valley, which destroys habitats.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Tidal power:</strong> Water trapped at high tide is released through turbines to generate electricity. Tidal power is highly reliable because tides are predictable, but barrages can harm marine wildlife and are expensive to build. Tidal power is the only major energy resource whose original source is <strong>not</strong> the Sun &mdash; it is driven by the gravitational pull of the Moon.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Energy generated from the heat stored deep within the Earth &mdash; water is pumped underground into hot rocks and returns as steam to drive turbines.">Geothermal</dfn> power:</strong> Water is pumped down into hot rocks deep underground and returns as steam to turn turbines. It is only available in volcanic areas or places with hot rocks near the surface, which limits where it can be used.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Biomass:</strong> Fuel made from living or recently living organisms (such as wood, animal waste, or crops). Biomass is considered <dfn class="term" data-def="A process where the CO2 released by burning a fuel is equal to the CO2 absorbed during growth of the source organism &mdash; the net addition of CO2 is zero.">carbon neutral</dfn> because the CO<sub>2</sub> released when burned equals the CO<sub>2</sub> the organisms absorbed during growth. However, growing and transporting biomass does have a carbon footprint.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n33">Trends in Energy Use</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n34">The UK is shifting from fossil fuels towards renewable sources. Demand for electricity varies throughout the day and across seasons &mdash; it peaks in the evening and in winter. <strong>Fossil fuel and nuclear</strong> power stations provide reliable <dfn class="term" data-def="The minimum level of electricity demand that is always needed, typically supplied by power stations that run continuously.">base load</dfn> supply. Hydroelectric and gas-fired power stations can start up quickly to meet sudden increases in demand. Renewable sources like wind and solar contribute a growing share but cannot always guarantee supply because they depend on weather conditions.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n35">In energy questions, always state which energy <strong>store</strong> energy is being transferred from and to (e.g. &ldquo;from the kinetic store to the thermal store of the surroundings&rdquo;). For efficiency calculations, show every step: write the equation, substitute values, then give your answer as a decimal <strong>and</strong> a percentage. When comparing energy resources, structure your answer with clear advantages <strong>and</strong> disadvantages of each &mdash; and always link your points to reliability, environmental impact, or cost.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n36">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n37">Energy is always conserved &mdash; it cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred between stores. Dissipated energy spreads to the thermal store of the surroundings and is no longer useful.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Efficiency = useful output energy &divide; total input energy. No device can be 100% efficient because some energy is always dissipated. Lubrication and insulation reduce unwanted transfers.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">Non-renewable resources (fossil fuels, nuclear) will run out and fossil fuels release greenhouse gases. Renewable resources (wind, solar, tidal, hydroelectric, geothermal, biomass) won&rsquo;t run out but many are weather-dependent and unreliable.</li></ul>
