# Energy Changes in Chemical Reactions

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Energy Changes in Reactions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Every chemical reaction involves a transfer of energy between the reacting substances and their surroundings. Some reactions release energy &mdash; making the surroundings hotter &mdash; while others absorb energy, making the surroundings cooler. Understanding these <dfn class="term" data-def="The transfer of energy into or out of a reaction system, measured as a temperature change in the surroundings.">energy changes</dfn> is essential for explaining everyday processes, from hand warmers to cold packs, and for predicting whether a reaction will happen on its own.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n3">Exothermic Reactions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n4">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction that transfers energy to the surroundings, causing a temperature increase.">exothermic reaction</dfn> is one that transfers energy to the surroundings. The surroundings get hotter, so a thermometer placed in the reaction mixture would show a temperature rise. The energy is usually transferred as heat, but sometimes as light, sound or electrical energy.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Common examples of exothermic reactions include:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n6">
<li><strong>Combustion</strong> &mdash; burning fuels like methane or wood releases heat and light energy.</li>
<li><strong>Neutralisation</strong> &mdash; when an acid reacts with an alkali, the solution warms up.</li>
<li><strong>Oxidation reactions</strong> &mdash; such as respiration in cells or the rusting of iron.</li>
<li><strong>Hand warmers</strong> &mdash; disposable hand warmers use an exothermic reaction (often iron oxidation) to release heat slowly.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>In an exothermic reaction, the products have <strong>less</strong> energy than the reactants. The &ldquo;missing&rdquo; energy has been transferred to the surroundings, which is why the temperature rises.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Endothermic Reactions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction that takes in energy from the surroundings, causing a temperature decrease.">endothermic reaction</dfn> is the opposite &mdash; it takes in energy from the surroundings. The surroundings get cooler, so a thermometer would show a temperature drop.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">Common examples of endothermic reactions include:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n11">
<li><strong>Thermal decomposition</strong> &mdash; calcium carbonate must be heated continuously to break down into calcium oxide and carbon dioxide.</li>
<li><strong>Citric acid and sodium hydrogen carbonate</strong> &mdash; dissolving these together in water causes the temperature to fall.</li>
<li><strong>Sports cold packs</strong> &mdash; instant cold packs contain ammonium nitrate and water, separated by a barrier. When the barrier is broken, the endothermic dissolving process absorbs heat from the injured area.</li>
<li><strong>Photosynthesis</strong> &mdash; plants absorb light energy from the sun to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>In an endothermic reaction, the products have <strong>more</strong> energy than the reactants. Energy has been taken in from the surroundings, which is why the temperature falls.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Reaction Profiles</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A diagram showing the relative energies of reactants and products in a chemical reaction, including the activation energy.">reaction profile</dfn> is a diagram that shows how the energy of the reactants and products compares, and the energy route the reaction takes. The y-axis shows energy and the x-axis shows the progress of the reaction.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<h3 data-narration-id="n18">Exothermic Reaction Profile</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n19">On an exothermic reaction profile, the reactants start at a <strong>higher</strong> energy level than the products. The difference between the two levels is the <strong>overall energy change</strong> &mdash; this energy is released to the surroundings. There is always a &ldquo;hill&rdquo; that the reaction must climb first. The height of this hill above the reactants is the <dfn class="term" data-def="The minimum amount of energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur.">activation energy</dfn> (E<sub>a</sub>) &mdash; the minimum energy particles need to collide successfully and start the reaction.</p>

<h3 data-narration-id="n20">Endothermic Reaction Profile</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n21">On an endothermic reaction profile, the products are at a <strong>higher</strong> energy level than the reactants. The overall energy change is taken in from the surroundings. The activation energy hill is still present &mdash; endothermic reactions still need an initial energy input to get started, and the overall energy absorbed is shown by the gap between the product and reactant levels.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>A catalyst provides an <strong>alternative reaction pathway</strong> with a lower activation energy. On a reaction profile, a catalyst is shown as a second, lower curve &mdash; the reactant and product energy levels stay the same, but the hill is shorter.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n23">When reading a reaction profile in the exam, you should be able to identify: (1) whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic by comparing the energy levels of reactants and products, (2) the activation energy as the height from the reactants to the top of the curve, and (3) the overall energy change as the difference between reactant and product energy levels.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24">When drawing a reaction profile, always label both axes (energy on the y-axis, progress of reaction on the x-axis), mark the reactants and products at the correct levels, draw a smooth curve over the top (not a pointed peak), and label the activation energy and overall energy change with arrows.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Bond Energy Calculations (Higher Tier)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">During a chemical reaction, bonds in the reactants must be <strong>broken</strong> and new bonds in the products must be <strong>made</strong>. Breaking bonds requires energy (it is endothermic), and making bonds releases energy (it is exothermic). The overall energy change of a reaction depends on the balance between these two processes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of energy needed to break one mole of a particular covalent bond, measured in kJ/mol.">bond energy</dfn> is the amount of energy needed to break one mole of a particular type of bond (measured in kJ/mol). The same amount of energy is released when that bond forms. Bond energies are given in data tables in the exam &mdash; you do not need to memorise them.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n28">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Overall energy change = <strong>energy to break bonds (in reactants) &minus; energy released making bonds (in products)</strong>. If the answer is positive, the reaction is endothermic. If the answer is negative, the reaction is exothermic.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n29">Calculate the overall energy change for the reaction: H<sub>2</sub> + Cl<sub>2</sub> &rarr; 2HCl</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Step 1 &mdash; identify bonds broken:</strong> One H&ndash;H bond (436 kJ/mol) and one Cl&ndash;Cl bond (242 kJ/mol). Total energy to break bonds = 436 + 242 = <strong>678 kJ/mol</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Step 2 &mdash; identify bonds made:</strong> Two H&ndash;Cl bonds (431 kJ/mol each). Total energy released making bonds = 2 &times; 431 = <strong>862 kJ/mol</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Step 3 &mdash; calculate overall energy change:</strong> 678 &minus; 862 = <strong>&minus;184 kJ/mol</strong>. The negative sign means the reaction is <strong>exothermic</strong> &mdash; more energy is released making bonds than is needed to break them.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">When asked to explain whether a reaction is exothermic or endothermic, always link the direction of energy transfer to the temperature change in the surroundings &mdash; saying &ldquo;energy is released&rdquo; is not enough on its own. For bond energy calculations, show every step: list bonds broken, list bonds made, then subtract. The examiner awards method marks, so even if your final answer is wrong, you can still pick up marks for a clear working method.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n36">Exothermic reactions transfer energy to the surroundings (temperature rises); endothermic reactions take in energy from the surroundings (temperature falls).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Reaction profiles show the relative energy of reactants and products, and the activation energy needed to start the reaction.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Overall energy change = energy to break bonds &minus; energy released making bonds. A negative value is exothermic; a positive value is endothermic.</li>
</ul>
