# Reactivity of Metals and Metal Extraction

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Metal Oxides</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Metals react with oxygen to form <dfn class="term" data-def="A compound formed when a metal reacts with oxygen.">metal oxides</dfn>. For example, magnesium burns brightly in air to form magnesium oxide:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><strong>2Mg + O&#8322; &rarr; 2MgO</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n4">Most metal oxides are <strong>bases</strong>. This means they react with acids to form a salt and water. Some metal oxides dissolve in water to form alkaline solutions &mdash; for example, sodium oxide dissolves in water to produce sodium hydroxide. However, not all metal oxides are basic: <strong>aluminium oxide</strong> and <strong>zinc oxide</strong> are <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that can act as both an acid and a base, reacting with acids and alkalis.">amphoteric</dfn> &mdash; they can react with both acids and alkalis.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Most metal oxides are bases. Some, like sodium oxide and potassium oxide, dissolve in water to form alkaline solutions. Aluminium oxide and zinc oxide are amphoteric &mdash; they react with both acids and bases.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n6">The Reactivity Series</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The <dfn class="term" data-def="An arrangement of metals in order of their reactivity, from most reactive to least reactive.">reactivity series</dfn> ranks metals from most reactive to least reactive. The more reactive a metal is, the more easily it loses electrons to form positive ions.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">From most reactive to least reactive, the series runs: <strong>potassium, sodium, lithium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium, carbon, zinc, iron, tin, lead, hydrogen, copper, silver, gold, platinum</strong>. Carbon and hydrogen are included as reference points even though they are not metals.</p>

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n9">The reactivity series is established by observing how metals react with water and dilute acids. Metals like potassium and sodium react violently with cold water. Magnesium reacts slowly with cold water but vigorously with steam. Metals like iron and zinc only react with steam. Copper, silver and gold do not react with water or dilute acids at all &mdash; they are the least reactive.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Displacement Reactions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">A more reactive metal can <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction in which a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in a compound.">displace</dfn> a less reactive metal from its compound. For example, if iron is added to copper sulfate solution, the iron displaces the copper:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Fe + CuSO&#8324; &rarr; FeSO&#8324; + Cu</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">You would observe the blue copper sulfate solution turning <strong>pale green</strong> (iron sulfate) and an orange-brown coating of copper appearing on the iron. This confirms that iron is more reactive than copper.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>A more reactive metal will displace a less reactive metal from a solution of its salt. This is the basis of displacement reactions, and it is how the reactivity series is confirmed experimentally.</p>
</div>
<div class="collapsible">

<span>The Thermite Reaction</span>

</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n15">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A vigorous displacement reaction between aluminium powder and iron oxide, producing molten iron and aluminium oxide.">thermite reaction</dfn> is a dramatic displacement reaction. Aluminium powder reacts with iron(III) oxide to produce molten iron and aluminium oxide: <strong>2Al + Fe&#8322;O&#8323; &rarr; Al&#8322;O&#8323; + 2Fe</strong>. This works because aluminium is more reactive than iron. The reaction is so exothermic that the iron produced is molten. It is used to weld railway tracks together.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Extraction of Metals</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Metals are found in the Earth&rsquo;s crust, mostly as <dfn class="term" data-def="A rock containing enough metal or metal compound to make it economically worthwhile to extract the metal.">ores</dfn> &mdash; rocks that contain enough metal compound to make extraction worthwhile. The method used to extract a metal depends on its position in the reactivity series.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Unreactive metals like <strong>gold</strong> and <strong>platinum</strong> exist naturally as pure elements because they do not easily react with other substances. Most other metals must be chemically separated from their ores.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
<button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
<span>Extraction methods by reactivity</span>
<svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n19">Metals <strong>below carbon</strong> in the reactivity series (zinc, iron, tin, lead, copper) can be extracted by <dfn class="term" data-def="The removal of oxygen from a compound, or the gain of electrons by a substance.">reduction</dfn> with carbon. Carbon is more reactive than these metals, so it displaces them from their oxides. Metals <strong>above carbon</strong> in the reactivity series (potassium, sodium, lithium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium) are too reactive to be extracted by carbon &mdash; they must be extracted using <strong>electrolysis</strong>, which is more expensive because it uses large amounts of electricity.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h3 data-narration-id="n20">Reduction with Carbon</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Iron is extracted from its ore, <strong>iron oxide</strong> (Fe&#8322;O&#8323;), in a <dfn class="term" data-def="A tall industrial furnace used to extract iron from iron ore by reduction with carbon (coke).">blast furnace</dfn>. Coke (a form of carbon) is burned with hot air to produce carbon monoxide, which then reduces the iron oxide:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Fe&#8322;O&#8323; + 3CO &rarr; 2Fe + 3CO&#8322;</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">The iron oxide has been <strong>reduced</strong> because it has lost oxygen. The carbon monoxide has been <strong>oxidised</strong> because it has gained oxygen. This is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A chemical reaction in which both oxidation and reduction occur at the same time.">redox reaction</dfn> &mdash; reduction and oxidation happen simultaneously.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Metals less reactive than carbon are extracted by reduction with carbon. Metals more reactive than carbon must be extracted by electrolysis. The position of carbon in the reactivity series is the dividing line.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Oxidation and Reduction in Terms of Electrons (Higher Tier)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><dfn class="term" data-def="The loss of electrons by a substance during a chemical reaction.">Oxidation</dfn> is the <strong>loss</strong> of electrons. <dfn class="term" data-def="The gain of electrons by a substance during a chemical reaction.">Reduction</dfn> is the <strong>gain</strong> of electrons. The memory aid <strong>OIL RIG</strong> helps: <strong>O</strong>xidation <strong>I</strong>s <strong>L</strong>oss, <strong>R</strong>eduction <strong>I</strong>s <strong>G</strong>ain (of electrons).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">In the reaction between iron and copper sulfate:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Fe &rarr; Fe&#178;&#8314; + 2e&#8315;</strong> (iron is oxidised &mdash; it loses electrons)</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Cu&#178;&#8314; + 2e&#8315; &rarr; Cu</strong> (copper is reduced &mdash; it gains electrons)</p>
<div class="collapsible">
<button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
<span>Why do more reactive metals lose electrons more easily?</span>
<svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n30">More reactive metals have a stronger tendency to lose their outer electrons and form positive ions. This is because their outer electrons are further from the nucleus and are more easily removed. Potassium, for example, loses its single outer electron very easily, which is why it reacts so vigorously. Gold holds onto its electrons tightly, which is why it barely reacts at all &mdash; and why it is valued for jewellery and electronics.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n31">When explaining why a displacement reaction occurs, always refer to the reactivity series: state which metal is <strong>more reactive</strong> and explain that it displaces the <strong>less reactive</strong> metal. For higher tier, use the terms oxidation and reduction in terms of electron transfer &mdash; remember <strong>OIL RIG</strong>. In extended response questions on metal extraction, link the extraction method (carbon reduction or electrolysis) to the metal&rsquo;s position in the reactivity series relative to carbon.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n32">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n33">The reactivity series ranks metals by how readily they react &mdash; more reactive metals lose electrons more easily and can displace less reactive metals from their compounds.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n34">Metals below carbon in the reactivity series are extracted by reduction with carbon (e.g. iron in the blast furnace). Metals above carbon require electrolysis.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n35">Oxidation is the loss of electrons (OIL); reduction is the gain of electrons (RIG). A reaction where both occur is called a redox reaction.</li>
</ul>
