# Atoms, Elements, Compounds and the Model of the Atom

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Atoms, Elements and Compounds</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All substances are made of <dfn class="term" data-def="The smallest part of an element that can exist, consisting of a nucleus surrounded by electrons.">atoms</dfn>. An atom is the smallest part of an element that can exist. There are about 100 different types of atom, and each type is a different <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance made up of only one type of atom.">element</dfn>. Elements are shown in the periodic table. Each element has its own chemical symbol &mdash; for example, O for oxygen, Na for sodium, and Fe for iron.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>An <strong>element</strong> contains only one type of atom. A <strong>compound</strong> contains two or more different elements chemically bonded together. A <strong>mixture</strong> contains two or more substances that are not chemically bonded.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n4">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance formed when two or more elements are chemically bonded together.">compound</dfn> is formed when atoms of two or more different elements are chemically bonded together. For example, water (H&#8322;O) is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen. Carbon dioxide (CO&#8322;) is a compound of carbon and oxygen. The properties of a compound are different from the properties of the elements it contains &mdash; for example, hydrogen and oxygen are both gases at room temperature, but water is a liquid.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Mixtures</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A <dfn class="term" data-def="Two or more substances that are not chemically bonded together and can be separated by physical methods.">mixture</dfn> consists of two or more elements or compounds not chemically combined together. The chemical properties of each substance in the mixture are unchanged. Mixtures can be separated by physical processes such as filtration, distillation, and chromatography &mdash; these do not involve chemical reactions, so no new substances are made.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Filtration</strong> separates an insoluble solid from a liquid, for example sand from water. <strong>Simple distillation</strong> separates a solvent from a solution, for example water from salt water. <strong>Fractional distillation</strong> separates a mixture of liquids with different boiling points, such as crude oil. <strong>Chromatography</strong> separates dissolved substances based on how they move through a stationary phase &mdash; useful for identifying unknown substances such as food colourings.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n8">The Structure of the Atom</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Atoms consist of a small, dense <dfn class="term" data-def="The central part of an atom, containing protons and neutrons.">nucleus</dfn> surrounded by electrons. The nucleus contains two types of particle: <dfn class="term" data-def="A positively charged subatomic particle found in the nucleus of an atom.">protons</dfn> (which carry a positive charge) and <dfn class="term" data-def="A subatomic particle with no charge found in the nucleus of an atom.">neutrons</dfn> (which carry no charge). <dfn class="term" data-def="A negatively charged subatomic particle that orbits the nucleus in energy levels.">Electrons</dfn> orbit the nucleus in energy levels (shells) and carry a negative charge.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>In an atom, the number of protons always equals the number of electrons, so atoms have <strong>no overall electrical charge</strong>. The relative masses and charges are: proton (mass 1, charge +1), neutron (mass 1, charge 0), electron (mass very nearly 0, charge &minus;1).</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n11">The radius of an atom is about 1 &times; 10&#8315;&sup1;&#8304; m. The radius of the nucleus is less than 1/10,000 of the radius of the atom. Most of the mass of an atom is concentrated in the nucleus, while most of the atom is empty space.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Atomic Number, Mass Number and Isotopes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom, which defines the element.">atomic number</dfn> (also called the proton number) is the number of protons in the nucleus. It defines what element an atom is &mdash; all atoms of the same element have the same atomic number. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.">mass number</dfn> is the total number of protons plus neutrons in the nucleus.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">To find the number of neutrons: subtract the atomic number from the mass number. For example, sodium has an atomic number of 11 and a mass number of 23, so it has 23 &minus; 11 = 12 neutrons.</p>
<!-- DIAGRAM -->
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.">Isotopes</dfn> are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. This gives them different mass numbers. For example, carbon-12 has 6 protons and 6 neutrons, while carbon-14 has 6 protons and 8 neutrons. Both are carbon because they have the same atomic number (6).</p>

<p data-narration-id="n16">Because isotopes have different masses, the <strong>relative atomic mass</strong> (A&#7523;) of an element is an average value that takes into account the abundance of each isotope. It is calculated as a weighted mean: A&#7523; = &Sigma;(isotope mass &times; abundance) &divide; &Sigma;(abundance). The A&#7523; shown on the periodic table is not always a whole number because it is an average. For example, chlorine has two isotopes: chlorine-35 (75%) and chlorine-37 (25%), giving A&#7523; = (35 &times; 75 + 37 &times; 25) &divide; 100 = 35.5.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n17">The Development of the Atomic Model</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Our understanding of the atom has changed over time as new evidence became available. Before the discovery of the electron, atoms were thought to be tiny, solid, indivisible spheres &mdash; this was John Dalton&rsquo;s model from the early 1800s.</p>
<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n19">
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">1897</div>
<h4>Discovery of the Electron</h4><p>J.J. Thomson discovered that atoms contain smaller, negatively charged particles called electrons. He proposed the <dfn class="term" data-def="A model of the atom where negative electrons are embedded in a ball of positive charge, like plums in a pudding.">plum pudding model</dfn> &mdash; a ball of positive charge with negative electrons embedded in it, like plums in a pudding.</p>
</div>
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">1909</div>
<h4>Alpha Scattering Experiment</h4><p>Rutherford, Geiger and Marsden fired alpha particles at thin gold foil. Most passed straight through, but a small number were deflected at large angles and some bounced straight back. This could not be explained by the plum pudding model.</p>
</div>
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">1911</div>
<h4>The Nuclear Model</h4><p>Rutherford concluded that the atom must have a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at its centre, with electrons orbiting around it. Most of the atom is empty space.</p>
</div>
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">1913</div>
<h4>Bohr&rsquo;s Model</h4><p>Niels <dfn class="term" data-def="Danish physicist who proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus at specific distances in fixed energy levels.">Bohr</dfn> adapted Rutherford&rsquo;s model by suggesting that electrons orbit the nucleus at specific distances in fixed energy levels (shells), rather than orbiting randomly. Later experiments confirmed the existence of these energy levels.</p>
</div>
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">1932</div>
<h4>Discovery of the Neutron</h4><p>James Chadwick discovered a neutral particle inside the nucleus &mdash; the neutron. This completed the modern model of the atom with protons, neutrons and electrons.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>The alpha scattering experiment disproved the plum pudding model. Most alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil (atom is mostly empty space), but some bounced back (nucleus is small, dense and positive).</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Electronic Structure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Electrons occupy energy levels (shells) around the nucleus. The first shell (closest to the nucleus) holds a maximum of 2 electrons. The second and third shells each hold a maximum of 8 electrons. Electrons fill the lowest energy level first before moving to the next.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
<button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
<span>Working Out Electronic Structure</span>
<svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n23">To work out the <dfn class="term" data-def="The arrangement of electrons in the energy levels (shells) of an atom, written as numbers separated by commas.">electronic structure</dfn> of an atom, find the element on the periodic table and note its atomic number (the number of protons, which equals the number of electrons). Fill the first shell with up to 2 electrons, the second with up to 8, and the third with up to 8. For example: sodium has 11 electrons, so its electronic structure is 2, 8, 1. Chlorine has 17 electrons, so its electronic structure is 2, 8, 7. Calcium has 20 electrons: 2, 8, 8, 2.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n24">The electronic structure of an atom determines its chemical properties. Elements in the same group of the periodic table have the same number of electrons in their outer shell, which is why they react in similar ways. The group number tells you how many outer-shell electrons an atom has (for Groups 1&ndash;7).</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n25">For &ldquo;describe&rdquo; questions about the development of the atomic model, make sure you name the scientist AND explain what their evidence showed &mdash; not just what their model looked like. For calculate questions on subatomic particles, always show your working: number of neutrons = mass number &minus; atomic number. Examiners award marks for the method even if your final answer is wrong.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n26">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n27">Atoms have a small, dense nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons in energy levels. The number of protons defines the element and equals the number of electrons in a neutral atom.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n28">The atomic model evolved from Dalton&rsquo;s solid sphere, through Thomson&rsquo;s plum pudding model, to Rutherford&rsquo;s nuclear model, Bohr&rsquo;s energy levels, and Chadwick&rsquo;s discovery of the neutron.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29">Electronic structures are written as numbers separated by commas (e.g. 2, 8, 1 for sodium) and determine an element&rsquo;s chemical properties and position in the periodic table.</li></ul>
