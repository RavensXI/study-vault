# Structure, Properties and Carbon Allotropes

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">States of Matter</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All substances exist in one of three <dfn class="term" data-def="The three physical forms in which matter can exist: solid, liquid and gas.">states of matter</dfn>: solid, liquid or gas. The state a substance is in at a given temperature depends on the strength of the forces between its particles. In a <strong>solid</strong>, particles are held in fixed positions by strong forces and can only vibrate. In a <strong>liquid</strong>, particles are close together but can move around each other. In a <strong>gas</strong>, particles are far apart and move quickly in all directions.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>You can predict the state of a substance at any temperature using its melting and boiling points. Below its melting point &rarr; solid. Between its melting and boiling points &rarr; liquid. Above its boiling point &rarr; gas.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n4">When a substance changes state, the particles themselves do not change &mdash; only the forces between them are overcome. <strong>Melting</strong> is the change from solid to liquid, <strong>boiling</strong> (or evaporating) is liquid to gas, <strong>freezing</strong> is liquid to solid, and <strong>condensing</strong> is gas to liquid. <dfn class="term" data-def="The change of state directly from a solid to a gas without passing through the liquid state.">Sublimation</dfn> is the direct change from solid to gas without passing through the liquid state.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n5">How Bonding Affects Properties</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The properties of a substance depend on its type of bonding and its structure. There are four main types of structure you need to know: <strong>giant ionic</strong>, <strong>simple covalent</strong> (small molecules), <strong>giant covalent</strong> and <strong>giant metallic</strong>. Each structure gives a substance a distinctive set of physical properties.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n7"><dfn class="term" data-def="A regular, repeating three-dimensional arrangement of ions held together by strong electrostatic forces in all directions.">Giant ionic lattices</dfn> (such as sodium chloride) have <strong>high melting and boiling points</strong> because a large amount of energy is needed to overcome the many strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions. They do <strong>not conduct electricity when solid</strong> because the ions are held in fixed positions and cannot move. However, they <strong>do conduct when molten or dissolved</strong> in water because the ions become free to move and carry charge.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n8"><dfn class="term" data-def="Weak attractive forces that exist between molecules, much weaker than covalent bonds within the molecules themselves.">Intermolecular forces</dfn> are the weak forces of attraction between simple covalent molecules. Within each molecule, the covalent bonds are strong, but the forces <strong>between</strong> molecules are weak. This means simple covalent substances have <strong>low melting and boiling points</strong> &mdash; only a small amount of energy is needed to overcome the intermolecular forces. When a simple covalent substance melts or boils, it is the intermolecular forces that are overcome, <strong>not</strong> the covalent bonds. Simple covalent substances do <strong>not conduct electricity</strong> because they have no free electrons or ions to carry charge.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n9">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>A common exam mistake is saying that covalent bonds break when a simple substance melts or boils. They do not &mdash; it is the <strong>intermolecular forces</strong> between molecules that are overcome, not the strong covalent bonds within the molecules.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Polymers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><dfn class="term" data-def="Very large molecules made by joining many small monomer molecules together in long chains.">Polymers</dfn> are very large molecules made from many small repeating units called <dfn class="term" data-def="A small molecule that can join with many others to form a polymer chain.">monomers</dfn> joined together in long chains. The intermolecular forces between polymer chains are stronger than those between small molecules because the chains are so long and have many points of contact. This means polymers are generally <strong>solid at room temperature</strong>. The properties of a polymer depend on the monomers used and the conditions of the reaction.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Giant Covalent Structures</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><dfn class="term" data-def="A structure where millions of atoms are bonded to each other by strong covalent bonds in a continuous network.">Giant covalent structures</dfn> contain millions of atoms all bonded to each other by strong covalent bonds in a continuous network. Because of the large number of strong bonds that must be broken, these substances have <strong>very high melting and boiling points</strong>. They are sometimes called macromolecular structures.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Alloys</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Pure metals are relatively soft because the layers of identical atoms can slide over each other easily. An <dfn class="term" data-def="A mixture of two or more metals, or a metal mixed with a non-metal, where atoms of different sizes disrupt the regular arrangement and prevent layers from sliding.">alloy</dfn> is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (usually other metals or carbon). In an alloy, the atoms of different elements are <strong>different sizes</strong>. This distorts the regular arrangement of metal atoms, <strong>preventing the layers from sliding</strong> over each other. This is why alloys are <strong>harder</strong> and <strong>stronger</strong> than pure metals.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Diamond</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><dfn class="term" data-def="A giant covalent structure of carbon where each carbon atom is bonded to four others in a rigid tetrahedral arrangement.">Diamond</dfn> is a form of carbon with a giant covalent structure. Each carbon atom forms <strong>four strong covalent bonds</strong> with other carbon atoms in a rigid <dfn class="term" data-def="A three-dimensional arrangement where each atom is bonded to four others at angles of 109.5 degrees.">tetrahedral</dfn> arrangement. This makes diamond extremely hard &mdash; in fact, it is the hardest known natural substance. Diamond has a <strong>very high melting point</strong> because many strong covalent bonds must be broken. It does <strong>not conduct electricity</strong> because all four outer electrons on each carbon atom are used in bonding &mdash; there are no free electrons or delocalised electrons to carry charge.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Graphite</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><dfn class="term" data-def="A giant covalent structure of carbon where each carbon atom bonds to three others in flat layers, with one delocalised electron per atom between the layers.">Graphite</dfn> is another form of carbon with a giant covalent structure, but its structure is very different from diamond. In graphite, each carbon atom bonds to only <strong>three</strong> other carbon atoms, forming flat hexagonal <dfn class="term" data-def="Flat sheets of atoms arranged in a regular pattern, stacked on top of each other.">layers</dfn>. This leaves one electron on each carbon atom free to become <strong>delocalised</strong> between the layers.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Graphite is unusual for a non-metal because it <strong>conducts electricity</strong>. Each carbon atom has one delocalised electron that is free to move along the layers and carry charge. The layers are held together by weak intermolecular forces, so they can <strong>slide over each other</strong> easily &mdash; this is why graphite is soft and slippery, and is used in pencils and as a lubricant.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Graphene and Fullerenes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><dfn class="term" data-def="A single layer of graphite just one atom thick, with exceptional strength and electrical conductivity.">Graphene</dfn> is a single layer of graphite &mdash; a sheet of carbon atoms arranged in hexagons, just one atom thick. Despite being so thin, graphene is incredibly strong and is an excellent conductor of electricity and heat. It has potential applications in electronics, composite materials and energy storage.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n23"><dfn class="term" data-def="Hollow carbon molecules made of hexagonal and pentagonal rings of carbon atoms, including buckminsterfullerene (C60) and carbon nanotubes.">Fullerenes</dfn> are molecules of carbon atoms arranged in hollow shapes. The most well-known fullerene is <strong>buckminsterfullerene</strong> (C&#8326;&#8320;), which has 60 carbon atoms arranged in a sphere made of hexagons and pentagons &mdash; like a football. <strong>Carbon nanotubes</strong> are cylindrical fullerenes with very high length-to-diameter ratios. They have remarkable properties: they are very strong, are excellent conductors of electricity and heat, and can be used to strengthen composite materials, in electronics and in drug delivery systems.</p>

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n24"><dfn class="term" data-def="Different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state.">Allotropes</dfn> are different structural forms of the same element. Diamond, graphite, graphene and fullerenes are all allotropes of carbon &mdash; they are all made entirely of carbon atoms, but arranged in different ways. This is why they have such different properties:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n25">
<li><strong>Diamond:</strong> each carbon bonded to 4 others &rarr; very hard, does not conduct electricity.</li>
<li><strong>Graphite:</strong> each carbon bonded to 3 others in layers &rarr; soft, slippery, conducts electricity.</li>
<li><strong>Graphene:</strong> single layer of graphite &rarr; strong, flexible, conducts electricity and heat.</li>
<li><strong>Fullerenes:</strong> hollow spheres or tubes &rarr; can trap molecules inside, used in drug delivery and composites.</li>
</ul>
</div></div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n26">When explaining properties of giant covalent substances, always state that there are <strong>many strong covalent bonds</strong> that require a <strong>large amount of energy to break</strong>. For graphite, you must explain that it conducts electricity because of <strong>delocalised electrons</strong> between the layers &mdash; not because it contains &ldquo;free ions.&rdquo; In compare questions about diamond and graphite, structure your answer around the <strong>number of bonds per carbon atom</strong> (4 vs 3) and whether delocalised electrons are present.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n27">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n28">The properties of a substance depend on its type of bonding and structure. Simple covalent substances have low melting points (weak intermolecular forces), while giant covalent and ionic structures have very high melting points (many strong bonds or forces to overcome).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29">Diamond has four covalent bonds per carbon atom making it very hard and non-conducting, while graphite has three bonds per carbon with one delocalised electron per atom, making it soft, slippery and electrically conductive.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30">Graphene is a single layer of graphite with exceptional strength and conductivity. Fullerenes are hollow carbon molecules with applications in drug delivery, electronics and composite materials.</li></ul>
