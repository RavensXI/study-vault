# Reactions of Acids, pH and Neutralisation

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Reactions of Acids with Metals</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">When an <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that produces hydrogen ions (H&plus;) in aqueous solution.">acid</dfn> reacts with a metal, the products are a <dfn class="term" data-def="A compound formed when the hydrogen in an acid is replaced by a metal.">salt</dfn> and hydrogen gas. The general word equation is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>acid + metal &rarr; salt + hydrogen</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n4">For example, magnesium reacts with hydrochloric acid to produce magnesium chloride and hydrogen: Mg + 2HCl &rarr; MgCl&#8322; + H&#8322;. The hydrogen gas can be tested with a burning splint &mdash; it burns with a squeaky pop.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Not all metals react with acids. Only metals above hydrogen in the <dfn class="term" data-def="A list of metals arranged in order of their reactivity, from most reactive to least reactive.">reactivity series</dfn> will displace hydrogen from an acid. Unreactive metals such as copper and gold do not react with dilute acids.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Neutralisation Reactions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction between an acid and a base that produces a salt and water.">Neutralisation</dfn> occurs when an acid reacts with a <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that neutralises an acid, producing a salt and water. If soluble, it is called an alkali.">base</dfn>. The general equation is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n8">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>acid + base &rarr; salt + water</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n9">A base is any substance that reacts with an acid to form a salt and water. If a base dissolves in water, it is called an <dfn class="term" data-def="A soluble base that produces hydroxide ions (OH&minus;) in aqueous solution.">alkali</dfn>. The ionic equation for all neutralisation reactions is: H&plus;(aq) + OH&minus;(aq) &rarr; H&#8322;O(l).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">When acids react with metal carbonates, three products form: a salt, water and carbon dioxide gas. The general equation is: acid + metal carbonate &rarr; salt + water + carbon dioxide. For instance, calcium carbonate reacts with hydrochloric acid: CaCO&#8323; + 2HCl &rarr; CaCl&#8322; + H&#8322;O + CO&#8322;.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Making Soluble Salts</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">In the required practical, you make a pure, dry sample of a soluble salt by reacting an insoluble base or carbonate with an acid. The steps are:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">1. Warm the acid gently in a beaker. 2. Add the insoluble base (e.g. copper oxide) in small amounts, stirring after each addition, until it is in excess &mdash; you know it is in excess when unreacted solid remains at the bottom. 3. Filter the mixture to remove the excess solid. 4. Pour the filtrate into an evaporating basin and heat gently until crystals begin to form at the edges. 5. Leave the solution to cool and allow crystals to form by slow evaporation. 6. Pat the crystals dry with filter paper.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">The pH Scale and Indicators</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A scale from 0 to 14 that measures how acidic or alkaline a solution is.">pH scale</dfn> runs from 0 to 14. A pH below 7 is acidic, pH 7 is neutral, and a pH above 7 is alkaline. The lower the pH, the more acidic the solution; the higher the pH, the more alkaline.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that changes colour depending on the pH of the solution it is added to.">indicator</dfn> is used to measure pH. Litmus paper gives a simple acid/alkali test (red = acidic, blue = alkaline). <dfn class="term" data-def="A mixture of indicators that produces a range of colours across the full pH scale.">Universal indicator</dfn> gives a colour for every pH value, providing more detail. A pH meter gives the most precise reading.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>During neutralisation, hydrogen ions (H&plus;) from the acid react with hydroxide ions (OH&minus;) from the alkali to produce water. As the acid is neutralised, the pH rises towards 7.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Strong and Weak Acids (Higher Tier)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An acid that fully ionises (dissociates) in aqueous solution, releasing all its hydrogen ions.">strong acid</dfn> is one that completely ionises in water &mdash; every molecule splits into ions. Examples include hydrochloric acid (HCl), sulfuric acid (H&#8322;SO&#8324;) and nitric acid (HNO&#8323;).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An acid that only partially ionises in aqueous solution, so an equilibrium is established.">weak acid</dfn> only partially ionises in water. The ionisation is reversible and an equilibrium is established, meaning only a small fraction of the acid molecules release hydrogen ions at any given time. Examples include ethanoic acid (vinegar) and citric acid (in citrus fruits).</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n24">Students often confuse concentration and strength. <strong>Concentration</strong> is the amount of acid dissolved in a given volume of water (measured in mol/dm&sup3;). <strong>Strength</strong> is about how much the acid ionises. A weak acid can be concentrated (lots of it dissolved) but still weak (only partially ionised). A strong acid can be dilute (small amount dissolved) but still strong (fully ionised).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25">As a strong acid is diluted, its pH increases towards 7 but it remains a strong acid. The pH of a strong acid is always lower than a weak acid of the same concentration, because the strong acid releases more H&plus; ions.</p>

</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n27">When answering questions about acid reactions, always name the salt produced &mdash; examiners award marks for correctly identifying it from the acid and metal/base used. For higher tier, make sure you can clearly explain the difference between strong and weak acids using the terms &lsquo;fully ionised&rsquo; and &lsquo;partially ionised&rsquo;, and never confuse strength with concentration.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n28">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n29">Acids react with metals to produce a salt and hydrogen, with bases to produce a salt and water, and with carbonates to produce a salt, water and carbon dioxide.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n30">The pH scale runs from 0 (strongly acidic) to 14 (strongly alkaline), with 7 being neutral. Neutralisation increases the pH of an acid towards 7.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n31">Strong acids fully ionise in water; weak acids only partially ionise. Strength and concentration are different concepts.</li>
</ul>
