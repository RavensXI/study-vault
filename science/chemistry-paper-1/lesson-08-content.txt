# Electrolysis

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Electrolysis?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="The process of using an electric current to decompose (break down) an ionic compound into its elements.">electrolysis</dfn> is the process of using an electric current to decompose an ionic compound into its elements. It only works when the compound is either molten (melted) or dissolved in water, because the <dfn class="term" data-def="Charged particles (atoms or groups of atoms) that have gained or lost electrons &mdash; positive ions are cations, negative ions are anions.">ions</dfn> must be free to move and carry the current. In a solid ionic compound the ions are locked in a fixed lattice and cannot move, so no current flows.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The substance being broken down is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="A molten or dissolved ionic compound that conducts electricity and is decomposed during electrolysis.">electrolyte</dfn>. During electrolysis, the electrolyte is connected to a direct current (d.c.) power supply via two <dfn class="term" data-def="Solid conductors through which the electric current enters and leaves the electrolyte during electrolysis.">electrodes</dfn> &mdash; usually made of inert materials such as graphite or platinum so they do not react with the products.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Electrolysis requires a d.c. supply. The positive electrode is called the <strong>anode</strong> and the negative electrode is called the <strong>cathode</strong>. Positive ions (cations) migrate to the cathode; negative ions (anions) migrate to the anode.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">What Happens at the Electrodes?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">During electrolysis, positive ions are attracted to the negative electrode (<dfn class="term" data-def="The negative electrode in electrolysis &mdash; attracts positive ions (cations), where reduction occurs.">cathode</dfn>) and negative ions are attracted to the positive electrode (<dfn class="term" data-def="The positive electrode in electrolysis &mdash; attracts negative ions (anions), where oxidation occurs.">anode</dfn>). When the ions reach the electrodes, they gain or lose electrons and are discharged as atoms or molecules.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">At the cathode, positive ions <strong>gain</strong> electrons &mdash; this is <dfn class="term" data-def="The gain of electrons by a substance during a chemical reaction.">reduction</dfn>. At the anode, negative ions <strong>lose</strong> electrons &mdash; this is <dfn class="term" data-def="The loss of electrons by a substance during a chemical reaction.">oxidation</dfn>. A useful mnemonic is <strong>OIL RIG</strong>: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Electrolysis of Molten Ionic Compounds</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">When an ionic compound is melted, only two types of ion are present &mdash; the metal ion and the non-metal ion. This makes predicting products straightforward: the metal always forms at the cathode and the non-metal always forms at the anode.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">For example, molten sodium chloride (NaCl) contains Na&#8314; and Cl&#8315; ions. Sodium metal forms at the cathode (Na&#8314; + e&#8315; &rarr; Na) and chlorine gas forms at the anode (2Cl&#8315; &rarr; Cl&#8322; + 2e&#8315;). Molten aluminium oxide (Al&#8322;O&#8323;) produces aluminium at the cathode and oxygen at the anode.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Extracting Aluminium by Electrolysis</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Aluminium is too reactive to be extracted from its ore by carbon reduction, so electrolysis is used instead. The ore is <dfn class="term" data-def="An impure form of aluminium oxide (Al&#8322;O&#8323;) that is the main source of aluminium.">bauxite</dfn>, which is purified to give aluminium oxide (Al&#8322;O&#8323;). Pure aluminium oxide has a very high melting point (over 2000&thinsp;&deg;C), which would be extremely expensive to maintain.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">To solve this, aluminium oxide is dissolved in molten <dfn class="term" data-def="A mineral (Na&#8323;AlF&#8326;) mixed with aluminium oxide to lower the melting point from over 2000&deg;C to about 950&deg;C, reducing energy costs.">cryolite</dfn> (Na&#8323;AlF&#8326;), which lowers the melting point to about 950&thinsp;&deg;C and reduces energy costs significantly.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In aluminium extraction: at the cathode, Al&sup3;&#8314; + 3e&#8315; &rarr; Al (reduction). At the anode, 2O&sup2;&#8315; &rarr; O&#8322; + 4e&#8315; (oxidation). The graphite anodes gradually burn away because they react with the oxygen produced (C + O&#8322; &rarr; CO&#8322;), so they must be replaced regularly.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n18">Aluminium extraction is expensive because it requires a continuous supply of electricity to keep the mixture molten and to drive the electrolysis. This is why aluminium recycling is so important &mdash; recycling uses only about 5% of the energy needed to extract aluminium from bauxite.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">When an ionic compound is dissolved in water, the situation is more complicated. Water itself breaks down into H&#8314; and OH&#8315; ions, so the solution contains <strong>four</strong> types of ion: the metal ion and the non-metal ion from the compound, plus H&#8314; and OH&#8315; from water. This means there is a competition at each electrode to decide which ion is discharged.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Rules for Predicting Products at Each Electrode</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>At the cathode (negative electrode):</strong> If the metal is <em>less reactive</em> than hydrogen (e.g. copper, silver, gold, platinum), the metal is deposited. If the metal is <em>more reactive</em> than hydrogen (e.g. sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium, zinc), hydrogen gas is produced instead.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>At the anode (positive electrode):</strong> If the solution contains <dfn class="term" data-def="Ions of halogens (Group 7 elements): Cl&#8315;, Br&#8315;, and I&#8315; &mdash; when present in solution, halide ions are discharged at the anode in preference to hydroxide ions.">halide ions</dfn> (Cl&#8315;, Br&#8315;, or I&#8315;), the halogen is produced. If no halide ions are present, oxygen gas is produced (from the discharge of OH&#8315; ions).</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n23">For example, electrolysis of copper sulfate solution (CuSO&#8324;(aq)) produces copper at the cathode (because copper is less reactive than hydrogen) and oxygen at the anode (because sulfate ions are not halide ions, so OH&#8315; ions are discharged instead).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Electrolysis of Brine (Sodium Chloride Solution)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">The electrolysis of <dfn class="term" data-def="A concentrated solution of sodium chloride (NaCl) in water &mdash; electrolysed industrially to produce chlorine, hydrogen, and sodium hydroxide.">brine</dfn> is one of the most important industrial applications of electrolysis. Brine contains Na&#8314;, H&#8314;, Cl&#8315;, and OH&#8315; ions. Sodium is more reactive than hydrogen, so <strong>hydrogen gas</strong> is produced at the cathode. Chloride is a halide ion, so <strong>chlorine gas</strong> is produced at the anode. The ions left in solution &mdash; Na&#8314; and OH&#8315; &mdash; form <strong>sodium hydroxide solution</strong>.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The three products of brine electrolysis all have important industrial uses. <strong>Chlorine:</strong> making PVC, bleach, and disinfecting water. <strong>Hydrogen:</strong> making margarine and as a fuel. <strong>Sodium hydroxide:</strong> making soap, paper, and bleach.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Half Equations (Higher Tier)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><dfn class="term" data-def="Equations showing what happens at one electrode during electrolysis &mdash; they show the gain or loss of electrons by the ions being discharged.">half equations</dfn> show the reaction at each individual electrode. At the cathode, ions gain electrons (reduction); at the anode, ions lose electrons (oxidation). The number of electrons must balance the charge on the ion.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n35">For questions on electrolysis, always state which ion goes to which electrode <em>and why</em> (opposite charges attract). If asked to write half equations, check the total charge balances on both sides. Remember that &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions need a reason, not just a fact &mdash; for example, &ldquo;hydrogen is produced at the cathode <em>because</em> sodium is more reactive than hydrogen, so H&#8314; ions are discharged instead.&rdquo;</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n36">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n37">Electrolysis decomposes ionic compounds using electricity &mdash; the electrolyte must be molten or dissolved so ions can move. Reduction (gain of electrons) occurs at the cathode; oxidation (loss of electrons) occurs at the anode.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">For molten compounds, the metal forms at the cathode and the non-metal at the anode. For aqueous solutions, reactivity determines whether the metal or hydrogen is produced at the cathode, and halide ions determine whether a halogen or oxygen forms at the anode.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">Brine electrolysis produces three useful products: chlorine (anode), hydrogen (cathode), and sodium hydroxide (left in solution). Aluminium is extracted from bauxite dissolved in cryolite to lower the melting point.</li></ul>
