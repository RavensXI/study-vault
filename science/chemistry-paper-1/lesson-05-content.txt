# Quantitative Chemistry: Mass, Moles and Concentrations

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Conservation of Mass</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In every chemical reaction, the total mass of the <dfn class="term" data-def="The substances that react together at the start of a chemical reaction.">reactants</dfn> equals the total mass of the <dfn class="term" data-def="The new substances formed during a chemical reaction.">products</dfn>. This is the <dfn class="term" data-def="The principle that no atoms are created or destroyed in a chemical reaction &mdash; the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products.">law of conservation of mass</dfn>. It holds because no atoms are created or destroyed during a chemical reaction &mdash; they are simply rearranged into new substances.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">A balanced symbol equation proves this: the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides. For example, in 2H<sub>2</sub>&nbsp;+&nbsp;O<sub>2</sub>&nbsp;&rarr;&nbsp;2H<sub>2</sub>O there are four hydrogen atoms and two oxygen atoms on each side. If the total <dfn class="term" data-def="The mass of an atom relative to one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom &mdash; symbol A_r, no units.">relative atomic mass</dfn> of the reactant atoms is calculated, it will match the total for the product atoms exactly.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>No atoms are created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. The total mass of reactants always equals the total mass of products. Any apparent change in mass on a balance is caused by a gas entering or leaving the system.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">If a reaction takes place in an <strong>open container</strong>, the mass reading on a balance may appear to change. When a gas is produced &mdash; for example, carbon dioxide from calcium carbonate reacting with hydrochloric acid &mdash; the gas escapes and the balance reading <strong>decreases</strong>. When a metal burns in air &mdash; for example, magnesium combining with oxygen &mdash; the product (magnesium oxide) is heavier than the original metal because oxygen atoms from the air have been added, so the balance reading <strong>increases</strong>. In both cases mass is still conserved when the entire system (including any gas that escaped or entered) is considered.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">Relative Formula Mass (M<sub>r</sub>)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The sum of the relative atomic masses (A_r) of all the atoms shown in a chemical formula &mdash; symbol M_r, no units.">relative formula mass</dfn> (M<sub>r</sub>) of a compound is calculated by adding up the relative atomic masses (A<sub>r</sub>) of every atom in its formula. A<sub>r</sub> values are found on the periodic table. M<sub>r</sub> has no units because it is a ratio compared to one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Worked example &mdash; sodium hydroxide (NaOH):</strong> A<sub>r</sub> values: Na&nbsp;=&nbsp;23, O&nbsp;=&nbsp;16, H&nbsp;=&nbsp;1. M<sub>r</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;23&nbsp;+&nbsp;16&nbsp;+&nbsp;1&nbsp;=&nbsp;40.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Worked example &mdash; calcium carbonate (CaCO<sub>3</sub>):</strong> A<sub>r</sub> values: Ca&nbsp;=&nbsp;40, C&nbsp;=&nbsp;12, O&nbsp;=&nbsp;16. M<sub>r</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;40&nbsp;+&nbsp;12&nbsp;+&nbsp;(3&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;16)&nbsp;=&nbsp;100.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Handling Brackets in Formulae &mdash; Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub></span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n11">When a formula contains brackets, the subscript outside the bracket multiplies every atom inside. In Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>: there is 1&nbsp;Mg, 2&nbsp;O and 2&nbsp;H. A<sub>r</sub> values: Mg&nbsp;=&nbsp;24, O&nbsp;=&nbsp;16, H&nbsp;=&nbsp;1. M<sub>r</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;24&nbsp;+&nbsp;2&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;(16&nbsp;+&nbsp;1)&nbsp;=&nbsp;24&nbsp;+&nbsp;34&nbsp;=&nbsp;58. Another example: Ca(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> has 1&nbsp;Ca, 2&nbsp;N and 6&nbsp;O. M<sub>r</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;40&nbsp;+&nbsp;(2&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;14)&nbsp;+&nbsp;(6&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;16)&nbsp;=&nbsp;40&nbsp;+&nbsp;28&nbsp;+&nbsp;96&nbsp;=&nbsp;164.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n12">The Mole and Avogadro&rsquo;s Constant</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The unit for amount of substance &mdash; one mole contains exactly 6.02 &times; 10^23 particles (atoms, molecules or ions). Symbol: mol.">mole</dfn> (mol) is the unit chemists use to count particles. One mole of any substance contains exactly 6.02&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;10<sup>23</sup> particles &mdash; this number is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The number of particles in one mole of a substance: 6.02 &times; 10^23.">Avogadro&rsquo;s constant</dfn>. Whether counting atoms, molecules or ions, the number is always the same.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">The mass of one mole of a substance (in grams) equals its M<sub>r</sub>. For instance, M<sub>r</sub> of water (H<sub>2</sub>O) is 18, so one mole of water has a mass of 18&nbsp;g. The key equation linking mass and moles is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>moles = mass (g) &divide; M<sub>r</sub></strong>. Rearranged: mass = moles &times; M<sub>r</sub>, or M<sub>r</sub> = mass &divide; moles. This equation is on the AQA equation sheet &mdash; practise rearranging it.</p>
</div>

    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Example 2:</strong> What is the mass of 3&nbsp;mol of NaOH (M<sub>r</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;40)? Mass&nbsp;=&nbsp;3&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;40&nbsp;=&nbsp;120&nbsp;g.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Example 3:</strong> How many moles in 5.6&nbsp;g of nitrogen gas (N<sub>2</sub>, M<sub>r</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;28)? Moles&nbsp;=&nbsp;5.6&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;28&nbsp;=&nbsp;0.2&nbsp;mol. Always use M<sub>r</sub> of the molecule, not A<sub>r</sub> of a single atom.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Reacting Masses from Balanced Equations</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A chemical equation where the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides, showing the mole ratios of reactants and products.">balanced equation</dfn> tells you the mole ratio of reactants and products. This ratio lets you calculate the mass of any substance in the reaction if you know the mass of another. Follow these four steps:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Write the balanced equation. <strong>Step 2:</strong> Calculate moles of the substance you know (moles&nbsp;=&nbsp;mass&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;M<sub>r</sub>). <strong>Step 3:</strong> Use the mole ratio from the equation to find moles of the substance you want. <strong>Step 4:</strong> Convert moles back to mass (mass&nbsp;=&nbsp;moles&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;M<sub>r</sub>).</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Worked Example: Reacting Mass Calculation</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Question:</strong> What mass of iron is produced when 480&nbsp;g of iron oxide (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) reacts with carbon? The equation is: 2Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>&nbsp;+&nbsp;3C&nbsp;&rarr;&nbsp;4Fe&nbsp;+&nbsp;3CO<sub>2</sub>.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Equation already balanced. <strong>Step 2:</strong> M<sub>r</sub> of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;(2&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;56)&nbsp;+&nbsp;(3&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;16)&nbsp;=&nbsp;160. Moles of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>&nbsp;=&nbsp;480&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;160&nbsp;=&nbsp;3&nbsp;mol. <strong>Step 3:</strong> Ratio is 2&nbsp;mol Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>&nbsp;:&nbsp;4&nbsp;mol Fe. So 3&nbsp;mol Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> produces 6&nbsp;mol Fe. <strong>Step 4:</strong> Mass of Fe&nbsp;=&nbsp;6&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;56&nbsp;=&nbsp;336&nbsp;g.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Limiting Reactants and Excess</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">In most practical reactions, one reactant is used up before the other. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The reactant that is completely used up first in a reaction, determining the maximum amount of product formed.">limiting reactant</dfn> is the one completely used up first &mdash; it determines how much product can be formed. The other reactant is in <dfn class="term" data-def="The reactant that remains left over after the limiting reactant has been completely used up.">excess</dfn> because some of it is left unreacted.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">To identify the limiting reactant: calculate the moles of each reactant, then compare these to the mole ratio in the balanced equation. The reactant that provides fewer moles relative to its required ratio is the limiting reactant. Adding more of the limiting reactant increases the amount of product; adding more of the excess reactant makes no difference.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n27">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The <strong>limiting reactant</strong> controls how much product is made. Once it is used up, the reaction stops &mdash; even if there is plenty of the other reactant remaining. To make more product, add more of the limiting reactant.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Concentration of Solutions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A mixture formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent.">solution</dfn> forms when a <dfn class="term" data-def="The substance that dissolves in a solvent to form a solution.">solute</dfn> dissolves in a solvent. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how much solute is dissolved in a given volume of solution, measured in g/dm&sup3; or mol/dm&sup3;.">concentration</dfn> tells you how much solute is dissolved per unit volume of solution.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">At GCSE you need to calculate concentration in grams per decimetre cubed: <strong>concentration (g/dm&sup3;)&nbsp;=&nbsp;mass of solute (g)&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;volume of solution (dm&sup3;)</strong>. Remember that 1&nbsp;dm&sup3;&nbsp;=&nbsp;1000&nbsp;cm&sup3;. Always convert cm&sup3; to dm&sup3; by dividing by 1000 before substituting into the equation.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Worked Examples: Concentration Calculations</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Example 1:</strong> 10&nbsp;g of sodium chloride is dissolved in 250&nbsp;cm&sup3; of water. What is the concentration? Volume&nbsp;=&nbsp;250&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;1000&nbsp;=&nbsp;0.25&nbsp;dm&sup3;. Concentration&nbsp;=&nbsp;10&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;0.25&nbsp;=&nbsp;40&nbsp;g/dm&sup3;.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Example 2:</strong> A solution has a concentration of 80&nbsp;g/dm&sup3; and a volume of 500&nbsp;cm&sup3;. What mass of solute does it contain? Volume&nbsp;=&nbsp;0.5&nbsp;dm&sup3;. Mass&nbsp;=&nbsp;concentration&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;volume&nbsp;=&nbsp;80&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;0.5&nbsp;=&nbsp;40&nbsp;g.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Increasing the mass of solute in the same volume makes the solution <strong>more concentrated</strong>. Adding more solvent without adding more solute makes it <strong>more dilute</strong>. Concentration links directly to rates of reaction: a higher concentration means more solute particles per unit volume, leading to more frequent collisions between reactant particles and therefore a faster reaction rate.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">Calculation questions on quantitative chemistry are worth 2&ndash;3 marks each. <strong>Always show your working</strong> &mdash; even if your final answer is wrong, you can earn method marks. Write the equation you are using, substitute values with units, then state your answer with correct units. For M<sub>r</sub> questions, write out each A<sub>r</sub> value and show the multiplication. For reacting mass problems, follow the four-step method: balanced equation &rarr; moles of known &rarr; mole ratio &rarr; mass of unknown. For concentration, check whether the volume is in cm&sup3; or dm&sup3; before you begin.</p>

<li data-narration-id="n38">The limiting reactant is used up first and controls how much product forms. Concentration (g/dm&sup3;)&nbsp;=&nbsp;mass of solute&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;volume of solution.</li>
