# Ionic, Covalent and Metallic Bonding

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Why Do Atoms Bond?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Atoms are most stable when they have a full outer shell of electrons. For most atoms, this means having eight electrons in their outer shell &mdash; a pattern sometimes called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The tendency of atoms to achieve a full outer shell of electrons, typically eight, through bonding.">octet rule</dfn>. Noble gases already have full outer shells, which is why they are unreactive. All other atoms achieve stability by <dfn class="term" data-def="The formation of chemical bonds between atoms through the transfer or sharing of electrons.">bonding</dfn> with other atoms &mdash; either by transferring electrons or sharing them.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">There are three main types of bonding you need to know for your GCSE: <strong>ionic bonding</strong> (transferring electrons), <strong>covalent bonding</strong> (sharing electrons), and <strong>metallic bonding</strong> (sharing delocalised electrons). Each type produces substances with very different properties.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">Ionic Bonding</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5"><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of chemical bond formed when one atom transfers one or more electrons to another atom, creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other.">ionic bonding</dfn> occurs between a <strong>metal</strong> and a <strong>non-metal</strong>. The metal atom <dfn class="term" data-def="The movement of one or more electrons from one atom to another during ionic bond formation.">transfers</dfn> one or more electrons from its outer shell to the non-metal atom. This is not sharing &mdash; the electrons physically move from one atom to the other.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">When a metal atom loses electrons, it becomes a positively charged <dfn class="term" data-def="An atom that has gained or lost electrons, giving it a positive or negative electrical charge.">ion</dfn> (because it now has more protons than electrons). When a non-metal atom gains electrons, it becomes a negatively charged ion. The strong <dfn class="term" data-def="The force of attraction between particles with opposite electrical charges.">electrostatic attraction</dfn> between these oppositely charged ions is what holds the compound together.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In ionic bonding, metals <strong>lose</strong> electrons to form <strong>positive ions</strong>, and non-metals <strong>gain</strong> electrons to form <strong>negative ions</strong>. The strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions hold the <dfn class="term" data-def="A compound made of positive and negative ions held together by strong electrostatic forces in a regular arrangement.">ionic compound</dfn> together.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Dot and Cross Diagrams for Ionic Bonds</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A diagram that uses dots and crosses to show how electrons are arranged in chemical bonds, with dots representing electrons from one atom and crosses from the other.">dot and cross diagram</dfn> shows which electrons belong to which atom. Electrons from one atom are drawn as dots and electrons from the other as crosses. In an ionic bond, the transferred electrons move completely into the receiving atom&rsquo;s shell. The ions are shown in square brackets with the charge written as a superscript outside.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">When drawing dot and cross diagrams for ionic compounds, remember: only show the <strong>outer shell electrons</strong> (this is what the exam expects), draw square brackets around each ion, and always include the ion charge. The exam may ask you to draw these for compounds like NaCl, MgO, or Na&#8322;O.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Covalent Bonding</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of chemical bond formed when two non-metal atoms share one or more pairs of electrons.">covalent bonding</dfn> occurs between two <strong>non-metal</strong> atoms. Instead of transferring electrons, the atoms <strong>share</strong> pairs of electrons. Each shared pair of electrons forms one covalent bond. The atoms are held together because the shared electrons are attracted to the nuclei of both atoms.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">A single covalent bond involves one shared pair of electrons. Some atoms need to share more than one pair: a <strong>double bond</strong> involves two shared pairs, and a <strong>triple bond</strong> involves three shared pairs. The number of bonds an atom forms depends on how many more electrons it needs to fill its outer shell.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In covalent bonding, atoms <strong>share</strong> pairs of electrons. Each atom counts the shared electrons as part of its own outer shell, so both atoms end up with full outer shells. Covalent bonds form between <strong>non-metals only</strong>.</p>
</div>

    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Hydrogen (H&#8322;):</strong> Each hydrogen atom has one electron and needs two for a full first shell. Two hydrogen atoms share one pair of electrons &mdash; a single bond.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Water (H&#8322;O):</strong> Oxygen has six outer electrons and needs two more. It shares one pair with each of two hydrogen atoms, forming two single bonds. Oxygen also has two <dfn class="term" data-def="A pair of electrons in the outer shell of an atom that is not involved in bonding.">lone pairs</dfn> of electrons that are not shared.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Oxygen (O&#8322;):</strong> Each oxygen atom needs two more electrons, so they share two pairs &mdash; forming a double bond (O=O).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Methane (CH&#8324;):</strong> Carbon has four outer electrons and needs four more. It shares one pair with each of four hydrogen atoms, forming four single bonds.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Nitrogen (N&#8322;):</strong> Each nitrogen atom needs three more electrons, so they share three pairs &mdash; forming a triple bond. This makes nitrogen gas very stable and unreactive.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Metallic Bonding</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><dfn class="term" data-def="The strong electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions arranged in a regular lattice and a sea of delocalised electrons that surround them.">metallic bonding</dfn> is the type of bonding found in metals and alloys. In a metal structure, the atoms are arranged in a regular pattern. The outer shell electrons detach from their atoms and become <dfn class="term" data-def="Electrons that are free to move throughout the entire metal structure rather than being associated with a single atom.">delocalised electrons</dfn> &mdash; free to move throughout the whole structure. This leaves behind a lattice of <strong>positive metal ions</strong> surrounded by a &ldquo;sea&rdquo; of delocalised electrons.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24">The strong electrostatic attraction between the positive ions and the negative delocalised electrons holds the metal together. This is why metals generally have <strong>high melting and boiling points</strong> &mdash; a lot of energy is needed to overcome these strong forces.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n25">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Metallic bonding involves a <strong>regular lattice of positive metal ions</strong> surrounded by a &ldquo;sea&rdquo; of <strong>delocalised electrons</strong>. The strong electrostatic attraction between the ions and the delocalised electrons explains why metals have high melting points, can conduct electricity, and are malleable.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Comparing the Three Types of Bonding</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The type of bonding in a substance depends on the types of atoms involved. Metals bonding with non-metals produce ionic bonds. Non-metals bonding with non-metals produce covalent bonds. Metals bonding with metals produce metallic bonds. Each type involves electrons in a different way, and this directly determines the substance&rsquo;s properties.</p>

Ionic compounds have high melting points because of the strong forces between ions in the <dfn class="term" data-def="A three-dimensional regular arrangement of ions in an ionic compound, held together by strong electrostatic forces in all directions.">giant ionic lattice</dfn>. They conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (because the ions are free to move and carry charge) but not when solid (because the ions are fixed in position). Metals conduct electricity because their delocalised electrons can flow through the structure. Simple covalent molecules generally have low melting points because the forces <em>between</em> molecules are weak, even though the covalent bonds within each molecule are strong.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n30">When asked to &ldquo;explain&rdquo; ionic bonding, always state three things: electrons are <em>transferred</em> (not shared), this forms <em>oppositely charged ions</em>, and there is a strong <em>electrostatic attraction</em> between them. For metallic bonding questions, use the phrase &ldquo;sea of delocalised electrons&rdquo; &mdash; examiners specifically look for this. If you are asked to draw dot and cross diagrams, only show outer shell electrons, use square brackets around ions, and include the charge.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n31">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Ionic bonding</strong> &mdash; metals transfer electrons to non-metals, forming positive and negative ions held together by strong electrostatic attraction in a giant lattice.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Covalent bonding</strong> &mdash; non-metal atoms share pairs of electrons so both atoms achieve full outer shells. Single, double and triple bonds are all possible.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Metallic bonding</strong> &mdash; positive metal ions are held in a regular lattice by a sea of delocalised electrons, explaining metals&rsquo; high melting points and electrical conductivity.</li>
</ul>
