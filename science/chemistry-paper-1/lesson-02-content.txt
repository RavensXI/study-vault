# The Periodic Table and Its Groups

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">How the Periodic Table Is Organised</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A table of all known elements arranged in order of increasing atomic number, with elements that have similar properties placed in the same vertical column (group).">periodic table</dfn> arranges all known elements in order of increasing <dfn class="term" data-def="The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom &mdash; this defines which element it is and determines its position in the periodic table.">atomic number</dfn> (the number of protons in the nucleus). Elements with similar chemical properties are placed in the same vertical column, called a <dfn class="term" data-def="A vertical column in the periodic table &mdash; elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outer shell and therefore similar chemical properties.">group</dfn>. The horizontal rows are called <dfn class="term" data-def="A horizontal row in the periodic table &mdash; the period number tells you how many occupied electron shells each element has.">periods</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The group number tells you how many electrons an element has in its outer shell. For example, all Group 1 elements have one electron in their outer shell, all Group 7 elements have seven, and Group 0 elements have a full outer shell. This is why elements in the same group behave in similar ways &mdash; their chemical reactions depend on the arrangement of their outer electrons.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outer shell. This is why they have similar chemical properties. The group number equals the number of outer-shell electrons (except Group 0, which has a full outer shell of 2 or 8 electrons).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Development of the Periodic Table</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">In the early 1800s, scientists tried to arrange the known elements by <dfn class="term" data-def="The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.">atomic mass</dfn>. However, this produced tables where elements with very different properties ended up in the same group. The breakthrough came from the Russian chemist <dfn class="term" data-def="Russian chemist (1834&ndash;1907) who arranged the elements by atomic mass but left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties &mdash; his predictions were later confirmed.">Mendeleev</dfn> in 1869.</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Mendeleev&rsquo;s Periodic Table</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7">Mendeleev arranged the elements by atomic mass, but he did two things that made his table far more successful than earlier attempts. First, he <strong>left gaps</strong> where he believed undiscovered elements should fit. Second, he <strong>predicted the properties</strong> of these missing elements based on the trends he observed in surrounding elements.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8">When elements like germanium (which Mendeleev called &ldquo;eka-silicon&rdquo;) were later discovered, their properties closely matched his predictions. This confirmed the usefulness of his table and convinced other scientists it was correct. Mendeleev also <strong>swapped the order</strong> of some elements (e.g. tellurium and iodine) so that they sat in groups with elements that had similar properties, even though this meant they were not strictly in atomic mass order.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n9">The modern periodic table is arranged by <strong>atomic number</strong> rather than atomic mass. This resolved the problems Mendeleev encountered when swapping element positions. Knowledge of <dfn class="term" data-def="Negatively charged subatomic particles that orbit the nucleus in shells &mdash; the arrangement of electrons determines an element&rsquo;s chemical properties.">electrons</dfn> and their arrangement in shells (thanks to scientists like Niels Bohr) explained why elements in the same group have similar properties &mdash; they have the same number of outer-shell electrons.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Mendeleev left gaps in his periodic table for undiscovered elements and predicted their properties. When these elements were later found (e.g. germanium), his predictions were confirmed &mdash; proving the table&rsquo;s validity.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Metals and Non-Metals</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The periodic table is divided into <dfn class="term" data-def="Elements on the left and centre of the periodic table that tend to be shiny, conduct electricity and heat, are malleable and ductile, and form positive ions by losing electrons.">metals</dfn> (on the left and centre) and <dfn class="term" data-def="Elements on the right of the periodic table that tend to be dull, poor conductors, brittle (if solid), and form negative ions by gaining electrons.">non-metals</dfn> (on the right). Metals make up the majority of elements.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Metals typically have high melting and boiling points, are good conductors of heat and electricity, and are malleable (can be hammered into shape) and ductile (can be drawn into wires). When metals react, they <strong>lose</strong> electrons to form <strong>positive ions</strong>. Non-metals are typically poor conductors, have lower melting points, and are brittle when solid. When non-metals react, they <strong>gain</strong> electrons to form <strong>negative ions</strong>.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Transition Metals</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n14">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A block of metallic elements in the centre of the periodic table (e.g. iron, copper, gold) that have high densities, high melting points, form coloured compounds, and can have variable oxidation states.">transition metals</dfn> sit in the central block of the periodic table, between Group 2 and Group 3. They include familiar metals like iron, copper, and gold. Compared to Group 1 metals, transition metals have higher melting points, higher densities, and are much stronger and harder.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15">Key properties of transition metals include: they can have <strong>variable oxidation states</strong> (charges), they often form <strong>coloured compounds</strong> (e.g. copper sulfate is blue), and they are useful as <strong>catalysts</strong> (e.g. iron in the Haber process). Roman numerals in compound names (such as copper(II) sulfate) indicate which oxidation state the transition metal is using.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Group 1 &mdash; The Alkali Metals</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Group 1 elements are lithium (Li), sodium (Na), potassium (K), rubidium (Rb), caesium (Cs), and francium (Fr). They are called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The elements in Group 1 of the periodic table (lithium, sodium, potassium, rubidium, caesium, francium) &mdash; they are soft, reactive metals that produce alkaline solutions when they react with water.">alkali metals</dfn> because they react with water to produce alkaline solutions (metal hydroxides).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">All Group 1 metals have <strong>one electron in their outer shell</strong>, which they lose easily during reactions to form <strong>+1 ions</strong>. This makes them very reactive. They are soft enough to cut with a knife, have relatively low melting points for metals, and have low densities &mdash; lithium, sodium, and potassium all float on water.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Reactions of Group 1 Metals with Water</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n19">When alkali metals react with water, they produce a metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>lithium + water &rarr; lithium hydroxide + hydrogen</strong><br><strong>sodium + water &rarr; sodium hydroxide + hydrogen</strong><br><strong>potassium + water &rarr; potassium hydroxide + hydrogen</strong></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n21">The metal hydroxides formed are alkalis &mdash; this is why universal indicator turns purple when potassium reacts with water (KOH is strongly alkaline). Reactivity <strong>increases down the group</strong> because the outer electron is further from the nucleus and more easily lost. Lithium fizzes gently, sodium fizzes vigorously and melts into a ball, and potassium reacts so violently that the hydrogen produced ignites with a lilac flame.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact &mdash; Group 1 Trends</div>
  <p>Going down Group 1: reactivity <strong>increases</strong>, melting point <strong>decreases</strong>, and density generally <strong>increases</strong>. Reactivity increases because the outer electron is further from the nucleus, so it is lost more easily.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Group 7 &mdash; The Halogens</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">Group 7 elements are fluorine (F), chlorine (Cl), bromine (Br), iodine (I), and astatine (At). They are called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The elements in Group 7 of the periodic table (fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine, astatine) &mdash; reactive non-metals that exist as diatomic molecules and gain one electron in reactions to form &minus;1 ions.">halogens</dfn>. They exist as <dfn class="term" data-def="Molecules made of two atoms bonded together, such as Cl&#8322;, Br&#8322;, and H&#8322;.">diatomic molecules</dfn> (e.g. Cl&#8322;, Br&#8322;). All Group 7 elements have <strong>seven electrons in their outer shell</strong> and gain one electron during reactions to form <strong>&minus;1 ions</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Going down the group, the halogens change state at room temperature: fluorine is a pale yellow gas, chlorine is a green gas, bromine is a red-brown liquid, and iodine is a grey solid that sublimes to a purple vapour. Melting and boiling points <strong>increase</strong> down the group because the molecules get larger, so the <dfn class="term" data-def="Weak forces of attraction between molecules &mdash; they increase as molecules get larger, which is why boiling points increase down Group 7.">intermolecular forces</dfn> between them become stronger.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Halogen Displacement Reactions</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n26">A more reactive halogen can <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction where a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in a compound.">displace</dfn> a less reactive halogen from an aqueous solution of its salt. For example:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>chlorine + sodium bromide &rarr; sodium chloride + bromine</strong></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28">Chlorine displaces bromine because chlorine is more reactive (it is higher in the group). Reactivity <strong>decreases down Group 7</strong> because the outer shell is further from the nucleus, so the attraction for an incoming electron is weaker. There is also more <dfn class="term" data-def="The repulsion between inner-shell electrons and outer-shell electrons &mdash; it reduces the effective pull of the nucleus on the outermost electrons.">electron shielding</dfn> from inner electron shells.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact &mdash; Group 7 Trends</div>
  <p>Going down Group 7: reactivity <strong>decreases</strong>, melting and boiling points <strong>increase</strong>. This is the opposite trend to Group 1. Reactivity decreases because the outer shell is further from the nucleus, so gaining an electron becomes harder.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Group 0 &mdash; The Noble Gases</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Group 0 elements are helium (He), neon (Ne), argon (Ar), krypton (Kr), xenon (Xe), and radon (Rn). They are called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The elements in Group 0 of the periodic table (helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, radon) &mdash; unreactive gases with full outer electron shells.">noble gases</dfn>. They are <strong>unreactive</strong> (inert) because they have a full outer shell of electrons &mdash; helium has 2, and the others have 8. Since they do not need to gain, lose, or share electrons, they rarely form compounds.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32">Noble gases are colourless, odourless, and exist as <strong>monatomic</strong> (single atom) gases. Their boiling points are very low but <strong>increase down the group</strong> as the atoms get larger and intermolecular forces increase. Uses include: helium in balloons and airships (low density, non-flammable), neon in illuminated signs (glows red-orange), argon in filament lamps and welding (provides an inert atmosphere to prevent oxidation).</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Why Are Noble Gases Unreactive?</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n33">Chemical reactions involve the transfer or sharing of electrons in the outer shell. Noble gases already have a <strong>stable electron configuration</strong> (a full outer shell), so they have no tendency to react. This is the same electron arrangement that other elements try to achieve when they form ions or covalent bonds. Helium&rsquo;s outer shell holds a maximum of 2 electrons (it only has one shell), while all other noble gases have 8 electrons in their outer shell.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">When answering questions on Group 1 and Group 7 trends, always link the trend to <strong>electron structure</strong>. Saying &ldquo;reactivity increases down Group 1&rdquo; alone is not enough for full marks &mdash; you need to explain <em>why</em> (the outer electron is further from the nucleus, so it is more easily lost). For compare questions, make sure you state the trend for <strong>both</strong> groups and explain the reason for each, linking to whether electrons are lost or gained.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">The periodic table is arranged by <strong>atomic number</strong>; elements in the same group have the same number of outer-shell electrons and similar chemical properties. Mendeleev&rsquo;s key insight was leaving gaps and predicting undiscovered elements.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Group 1</strong> (alkali metals) have 1 outer electron, react with water to form alkaline hydroxides, and get <strong>more reactive</strong> going down the group. <strong>Group 7</strong> (halogens) have 7 outer electrons, gain 1 electron in reactions, and get <strong>less reactive</strong> going down. <strong>Group 0</strong> (noble gases) have full outer shells and are unreactive.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Metals (left/centre) lose electrons to form positive ions; non-metals (right) gain electrons to form negative ions. Transition metals have high melting points, variable oxidation states, and form coloured compounds.</li></ul>
