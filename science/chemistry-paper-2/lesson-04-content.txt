# Chemical Analysis: Purity, Chromatography and Gas Tests

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Pure Substances in Chemistry</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In everyday language, &ldquo;pure&rdquo; means natural or uncontaminated &mdash; like &ldquo;pure orange juice.&rdquo; But in chemistry, the word has a very specific meaning. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that contains only one type of element or one type of compound.">pure substance</dfn> contains only a single element or a single compound. Pure water, for example, contains nothing but H&sub2;O molecules. Crude oil is <strong>not</strong> pure because it is a mixture of many different hydrocarbons.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A pure substance has a <strong>sharp melting point</strong> and a <strong>sharp boiling point</strong>. An impure substance melts and boils over a <strong>range</strong> of temperatures. Impurities lower the melting point and raise the boiling point. For example, pure water melts at exactly 0&deg;C and boils at exactly 100&deg;C, but seawater (which contains dissolved salt) melts below 0&deg;C.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n4">This means that if you measure the melting point of an unknown white powder and find it melts sharply at one temperature, it is likely pure. If it melts gradually over a range of temperatures, it is a mixture.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Formulations</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A mixture that has been carefully designed to have specific properties by mixing components in precise quantities.">formulation</dfn> is a special type of mixture. The components are mixed in carefully measured quantities so that the final product has exactly the right properties for its purpose. Unlike a random mixture, each ingredient in a formulation serves a specific function.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Formulations are everywhere in daily life. Medicines contain an active ingredient plus binders, coatings, and flavourings. Paints are formulations of pigment, solvent, binder, and additives. Other examples include cleaning products, fuels, cosmetics, fertilisers, and alloys. Each component is present in a precise proportion &mdash; too much or too little of any ingredient would change the product&rsquo;s properties.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Chromatography</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><dfn class="term" data-def="A technique used to separate and identify the substances in a mixture based on how soluble they are in a particular solvent.">Chromatography</dfn> is a powerful analytical technique used to separate mixtures of soluble substances. It can reveal whether a substance is pure or impure, and it can identify the individual components of a mixture.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Paper chromatography involves two phases. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The phase in chromatography that moves &mdash; the solvent that carries dissolved substances through the stationary phase.">mobile phase</dfn> is the solvent (often water) that moves up through the paper. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The phase in chromatography that does not move &mdash; the absorbent paper through which the mobile phase travels.">stationary phase</dfn> is the paper itself. Different substances in the mixture are carried different distances by the solvent, depending on how soluble they are. More soluble substances travel further up the paper; less soluble ones stay closer to the start line.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Required Practical: Paper Chromatography Method</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Draw a pencil line 1 cm from the bottom of the chromatography paper. Use pencil, not pen, because pencil ink is insoluble and will not dissolve in the solvent.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Step 2:</strong> Place small spots of each substance onto the pencil line, leaving gaps between them so they do not merge.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Step 3:</strong> Pour a shallow layer of solvent into a beaker. The solvent level must be below the pencil line so that the spots do not dissolve directly into the solvent.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Step 4:</strong> Lower the paper into the solvent and leave it undisturbed. The solvent will travel up the paper by capillary action, carrying the dissolved substances with it.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Step 5:</strong> Remove the paper just before the solvent front reaches the top. Mark the solvent front with a pencil and allow the chromatogram to dry.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Interpreting a Chromatogram</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">The pattern of spots on a finished <dfn class="term" data-def="The result of a chromatography experiment, showing the separated substances as distinct spots on the paper.">chromatogram</dfn> tells you important information. A <strong>pure substance</strong> produces just <strong>one spot</strong>. An <strong>impure substance</strong> (a mixture) produces <strong>two or more spots</strong>, one for each component in the mixture.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">You can identify unknown substances by comparing their spots to known reference substances run on the same chromatogram. If an unknown spot travels the same distance as a known substance, they are likely the same chemical.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Calculating R<sub>f</sub> Values</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The ratio of the distance moved by a substance to the distance moved by the solvent front in chromatography. R_f = distance moved by substance &divide; distance moved by solvent.">R<sub>f</sub> value</dfn> (retention factor) gives each substance a numerical identity in a particular solvent. It is calculated using:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>R<sub>f</sub> = distance moved by substance &divide; distance moved by solvent</strong>. The R<sub>f</sub> value is always between 0 and 1. Each substance has a unique R<sub>f</sub> value in a given solvent, so R<sub>f</sub> values can be compared to reference data to identify unknown chemicals.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Worked example:</strong> A spot of dye travels 30 mm up the paper. The solvent front travels 52 mm. R<sub>f</sub> = 30 &divide; 52 = 0.58 (to 2 significant figures). Different compounds have different R<sub>f</sub> values in different solvents, so the solvent used and the conditions must always be recorded.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Tests for Common Gases</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">The AQA specification requires you to know the laboratory tests for four gases: hydrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and chlorine. Each test has a specific method and a clear positive result that you must be able to recall.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Four Gas Tests in Detail</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Hydrogen:</strong> Hold a burning splint at the mouth of a test tube containing the gas. If hydrogen is present, it burns rapidly with a <dfn class="term" data-def="The characteristic sound made when a burning splint ignites hydrogen gas, confirming its presence.">squeaky pop</dfn> sound. Hydrogen is produced when metals react with acids (e.g. magnesium + hydrochloric acid).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Oxygen:</strong> Insert a <dfn class="term" data-def="A wooden splint that has been lit and then blown out so that it is glowing but not flaming, used to test for oxygen gas.">glowing splint</dfn> into a test tube of the gas. If oxygen is present, the splint <strong>relights</strong>. Oxygen can be produced by decomposing hydrogen peroxide with a manganese dioxide catalyst.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Carbon dioxide:</strong> Bubble the gas through <dfn class="term" data-def="A solution of calcium hydroxide in water, used as the standard test for carbon dioxide gas.">limewater</dfn> (calcium hydroxide solution). If carbon dioxide is present, the limewater turns <strong>cloudy</strong> (milky white). This happens because calcium carbonate, an insoluble white solid, is formed. Carbon dioxide is produced when carbonates react with acids.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Chlorine:</strong> Hold a piece of <strong>damp litmus paper</strong> near the gas. If <dfn class="term" data-def="A toxic, pale green gas with a strong smell. It bleaches damp litmus paper white.">chlorine</dfn> is present, the litmus paper is <strong>bleached white</strong>. This happens because chlorine dissolves in the water on the damp paper and acts as a bleaching agent.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n31">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Remember the four gas tests: <strong>Hydrogen</strong> &rarr; squeaky pop with a burning splint. <strong>Oxygen</strong> &rarr; relights a glowing splint. <strong>Carbon dioxide</strong> &rarr; turns limewater cloudy. <strong>Chlorine</strong> &rarr; bleaches damp litmus paper white.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Common Exam Mistakes with Gas Tests</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n32">Students often confuse the hydrogen and oxygen tests. For hydrogen, the splint must be <strong>burning</strong> (lit). For oxygen, the splint must be <strong>glowing</strong> (no flame, just an ember). Saying &ldquo;the splint relights&rdquo; when testing for hydrogen, or &ldquo;a squeaky pop&rdquo; when testing for oxygen, will lose you marks.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33">Another common error is stating that carbon dioxide &ldquo;extinguishes a flame.&rdquo; While this is true, it is <strong>not</strong> the correct test &mdash; many gases will extinguish a flame. The specific test is the limewater test. Similarly, for chlorine, the litmus paper must be <strong>damp</strong>, not dry.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">In chromatography questions, always show your R<sub>f</sub> calculation with the formula and your working &mdash; the method mark is often awarded even if your final answer is wrong. For gas test questions, be precise: state the exact test method <strong>and</strong> the positive result. &ldquo;Use a burning splint&rdquo; alone is not enough for hydrogen &mdash; you must also state &ldquo;a squeaky pop is heard.&rdquo; For extended response questions on purity, link your answer to melting and boiling point data.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">A chemically pure substance contains only one element or compound and has a sharp, specific melting and boiling point. A formulation is a mixture designed with precise proportions for a specific purpose.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Chromatography separates soluble substances using a mobile phase (solvent) and a stationary phase (paper). R<sub>f</sub> = distance moved by substance &divide; distance moved by solvent. Pure substances produce one spot; mixtures produce multiple spots.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Know the four gas tests: hydrogen (squeaky pop), oxygen (relights glowing splint), carbon dioxide (turns limewater cloudy), chlorine (bleaches damp litmus paper white).</li></ul>
