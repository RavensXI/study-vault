# Atmospheric Pollutants and Their Effects

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Atmospheric Pollutants?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">When we burn <dfn class="term" data-def="Fuels formed over millions of years from the remains of dead organisms, including coal, oil and natural gas.">fossil fuels</dfn> such as coal, oil and natural gas, they don&rsquo;t just produce carbon dioxide and water. Depending on the conditions and the fuel itself, a range of harmful pollutants can be released into the atmosphere. These pollutants cause serious problems for human health and the environment.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The main atmospheric pollutants you need to know are <strong>carbon monoxide</strong>, <strong>sulfur dioxide</strong>, <strong>nitrogen oxides</strong> and <strong>particulates</strong>. Each one is produced in a different way and causes different types of damage.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The four atmospheric pollutants you must know for AQA spec 4.9.3 are: carbon monoxide (CO), sulfur dioxide (SO&lt;sub&gt;2&lt;/sub&gt;), nitrogen oxides (NO&lt;sub&gt;x&lt;/sub&gt;) and particulates (soot and unburnt hydrocarbons).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Complete and Incomplete Combustion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">To understand where pollutants come from, you first need to understand the difference between <dfn class="term" data-def="Burning a fuel in a plentiful supply of oxygen, producing only carbon dioxide and water.">complete combustion</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="Burning a fuel in a limited supply of oxygen, producing carbon monoxide, particulates (soot) and water instead of just carbon dioxide and water.">incomplete combustion</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">In <strong>complete combustion</strong>, a hydrocarbon fuel burns in a plentiful supply of oxygen. The only products are carbon dioxide and water. For example, methane burning completely:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>methane + oxygen &rarr; carbon dioxide + water</strong><br>CH<sub>4</sub> + 2O<sub>2</sub> &rarr; CO<sub>2</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9">In <strong>incomplete combustion</strong>, there is not enough oxygen for the fuel to burn fully. Instead of carbon dioxide, the products include carbon monoxide (CO) and particulate carbon (soot). This is what happens inside a Bunsen burner when the air hole is closed &mdash; you see a yellow, smoky flame because carbon particles glow in the heat.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>methane + oxygen &rarr; carbon monoxide + water</strong><br>2CH<sub>4</sub> + 3O<sub>2</sub> &rarr; 2CO + 4H<sub>2</sub>O</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">If even less oxygen is available, solid carbon (soot) is produced as well:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>methane + oxygen &rarr; carbon + water</strong><br>CH<sub>4</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> &rarr; C + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</p>

  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13">Incomplete combustion occurs when there is a limited supply of oxygen. This commonly happens in car engines (especially diesel engines), gas boilers that are poorly maintained, and any enclosed space where air flow is restricted. The larger the hydrocarbon molecule, the more oxygen it needs to burn completely &mdash; which is why diesel fuel (with bigger molecules than petrol) is more likely to undergo incomplete combustion and produce particulates.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Carbon Monoxide (CO)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="A colourless, odourless, toxic gas produced by incomplete combustion of hydrocarbon fuels.">carbon monoxide</dfn> is a colourless, odourless gas, which makes it extremely dangerous because you cannot detect it without a special alarm. It is produced by the incomplete combustion of any fuel containing carbon.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Carbon monoxide is toxic because it binds to the <dfn class="term" data-def="The protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen around the body.">haemoglobin</dfn> in red blood cells. Haemoglobin normally carries oxygen from the lungs to the body&rsquo;s tissues. Carbon monoxide binds to haemoglobin more strongly than oxygen does, so it takes the place of oxygen molecules. This means less oxygen is carried around the body, which can lead to dizziness, unconsciousness and even death at high concentrations.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Carbon monoxide is dangerous because it is colourless and odourless, and it binds to haemoglobin in red blood cells, reducing the amount of oxygen the blood can carry.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Sulfur Dioxide (SO<sub>2</sub>)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><dfn class="term" data-def="A toxic gas produced when fossil fuels containing sulfur impurities are burned; it dissolves in rainwater to form acid rain.">sulfur dioxide</dfn> is produced when fossil fuels that contain sulfur impurities are burned. Coal is particularly high in sulfur. The sulfur reacts with oxygen during combustion:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>sulfur + oxygen &rarr; sulfur dioxide</strong><br>S + O<sub>2</sub> &rarr; SO<sub>2</sub></p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Sulfur dioxide can also be released naturally by volcanic eruptions. Once in the atmosphere, it dissolves in rainwater to form <dfn class="term" data-def="Rain that has become acidic due to dissolved sulfur dioxide or nitrogen oxides, damaging buildings, vegetation and aquatic life.">acid rain</dfn> (sulfurous acid or sulfuric acid):</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>SO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O &rarr; H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub></strong> (sulfurous acid)</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Effects of Acid Rain</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n23">Acid rain causes widespread environmental damage. It corrodes limestone and marble buildings and statues because these are made of calcium carbonate, which reacts with acid. It kills trees by damaging their leaves and leaching minerals from the soil. It also lowers the pH of lakes and rivers, killing fish and other aquatic organisms. Acid rain can travel hundreds of kilometres from the source of the pollution, meaning one country&rsquo;s emissions can damage another country&rsquo;s environment.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Nitrogen Oxides (NO<sub>x</sub>)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><dfn class="term" data-def="Gases formed when nitrogen and oxygen in the air react at very high temperatures, such as inside car engines; they contribute to acid rain and smog.">nitrogen oxides</dfn> (written as NO<sub>x</sub>) are formed when the temperature inside an engine or furnace is high enough for nitrogen and oxygen in the air to react together. This does not come from the fuel itself &mdash; it comes from the atmosphere. Car engines, aeroplane engines and power stations all produce nitrogen oxides.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Nitrogen oxides also occur naturally during lightning strikes, where the extreme heat forces nitrogen and oxygen molecules to combine. However, the vast majority of nitrogen oxides in the atmosphere come from vehicle engines.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">Nitrogen oxides cause several problems. Like sulfur dioxide, they dissolve in rainwater to form acid rain. They can also trigger <dfn class="term" data-def="A respiratory condition in which the airways narrow and swell, making breathing difficult.">asthma</dfn> attacks and other respiratory problems. In sunlight, nitrogen oxides react with other pollutants to form photochemical smog, which is a serious health hazard in cities.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n28">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Nitrogen oxides are not produced from the fuel &mdash; they form when nitrogen and oxygen from the air react at the very high temperatures inside engines. They cause acid rain, trigger asthma and contribute to smog.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n29">Particulates</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><dfn class="term" data-def="Tiny solid particles of soot (carbon) and unburnt hydrocarbons released during incomplete combustion, especially from diesel engines.">particulates</dfn> are tiny solid particles released during incomplete combustion. They consist of carbon (soot) and unburnt hydrocarbons. Diesel engines are a major source because diesel molecules are larger than petrol molecules and need more oxygen to burn completely.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Particulates cause two main types of harm:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Health effects:</strong> Particulates are so small that they can penetrate deep into the lungs and even pass across cell membranes into the bloodstream. They worsen respiratory conditions like asthma and bronchitis, and long-term exposure may increase the risk of lung cancer and heart disease.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Environmental effects:</strong> When particulates travel high into the atmosphere, they reflect sunlight back into space before it reaches the Earth&rsquo;s surface. This effect is called <dfn class="term" data-def="A reduction in the amount of sunlight reaching the Earth&rsquo;s surface, caused by particulates in the atmosphere reflecting sunlight back into space.">global dimming</dfn>. Some scientists have suggested that global dimming may partially offset the warming effect of greenhouse gases, but it also reduces the light available for photosynthesis and can disrupt weather patterns.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n34">Several methods can reduce the release of atmospheric pollutants. Catalytic converters in car exhausts convert carbon monoxide and nitrogen oxides into less harmful carbon dioxide and nitrogen. Flue gas desulfurisation in power stations removes sulfur dioxide from waste gases before they leave the chimney. Using cleaner fuels (such as natural gas instead of coal) or renewable energy sources avoids producing these pollutants altogether. Regular maintenance of boilers and engines ensures more complete combustion, reducing carbon monoxide and particulate emissions.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n39">In questions about atmospheric pollutants, always be specific about which pollutant causes which problem &mdash; examiners will not give marks for vague answers like &ldquo;pollution damages the environment.&rdquo; State the pollutant by name (e.g. sulfur dioxide), explain how it is produced (burning fuels containing sulfur), and describe its specific effect (dissolves in rainwater to form acid rain, which damages limestone buildings and kills aquatic life). For 6-mark extended response questions, link the source, the pollutant and the effect in a clear chain of reasoning.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n40">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n41">Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen) produces carbon monoxide and particulates, while complete combustion produces only carbon dioxide and water.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42">Carbon monoxide is toxic because it binds to haemoglobin, reducing the blood&rsquo;s ability to carry oxygen.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n43">Sulfur dioxide (from sulfur impurities in fuel) and nitrogen oxides (from high-temperature reactions in engines) both cause acid rain, while particulates cause global dimming and respiratory health problems.</li></ul>
