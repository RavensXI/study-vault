# Crude Oil, Hydrocarbons and Cracking

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Crude Oil?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A thick, dark mixture of hydrocarbons formed over millions of years from the fossilised remains of ancient marine organisms buried under layers of rock.">Crude oil</dfn> is a finite resource found trapped in rocks beneath the Earth&rsquo;s surface. It formed over millions of years from the remains of ancient plants and tiny sea creatures that were buried under layers of sediment. Heat and pressure gradually transformed this biological material into a complex mixture of <dfn class="term" data-def="Compounds made up of hydrogen and carbon atoms only.">hydrocarbons</dfn> &mdash; compounds containing only hydrogen and carbon atoms.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Crude oil is not a single substance. It is a <dfn class="term" data-def="Two or more substances mixed together that are not chemically bonded and can be separated by physical methods.">mixture</dfn> of many different hydrocarbons, each with a different chain length and boiling point. Because they are not chemically bonded together, they can be separated using physical methods. This is what makes crude oil so useful &mdash; it can be split into groups of similar molecules called <dfn class="term" data-def="A group of hydrocarbons with similar chain lengths and boiling points, separated from crude oil by fractional distillation.">fractions</dfn>, each with different properties and uses.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Crude oil is a <strong>finite</strong> (non-renewable) resource. It is a <strong>mixture</strong> of hydrocarbons &mdash; compounds containing <strong>only hydrogen and carbon atoms</strong>. These hydrocarbons can be separated because they have different <strong>boiling points</strong>.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Alkanes &mdash; The Simplest Hydrocarbons</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The main type of hydrocarbon found in crude oil is the <dfn class="term" data-def="A family of saturated hydrocarbons with single bonds only, with the general formula CnH2n+2.">alkane</dfn>. Alkanes are called <dfn class="term" data-def="A molecule that contains only single covalent bonds between its carbon atoms, with no double bonds.">saturated</dfn> hydrocarbons because they contain only single covalent bonds between carbon atoms &mdash; every carbon atom forms as many bonds to hydrogen as possible.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Alkanes follow a general formula: <strong>C<sub>n</sub>H<sub>2n+2</sub></strong>. This means if you know how many carbon atoms are in the chain, you can work out the number of hydrogen atoms. The first four alkanes are:</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">How Chain Length Affects Properties</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">The length of a hydrocarbon chain determines its physical properties. As the number of carbon atoms increases, the <dfn class="term" data-def="Weak attractions between molecules that increase in strength with molecular size, affecting boiling point and viscosity.">intermolecular forces</dfn> between molecules get stronger. This means longer chains require more energy to separate, which directly affects boiling point, viscosity, and flammability.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n11">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>As hydrocarbon chain length <strong>increases</strong>: boiling point <strong>increases</strong>, <dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how thick and resistant to flow a liquid is &mdash; longer hydrocarbon chains are more viscous.">viscosity</dfn> <strong>increases</strong> (thicker, harder to pour), and <dfn class="term" data-def="How easily a substance catches fire and burns &mdash; shorter hydrocarbon chains are more flammable.">flammability</dfn> <strong>decreases</strong> (harder to ignite). Short-chain hydrocarbons are runny, easy to light, and have low boiling points.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n12">Think of it this way: petrol (short chains) flows easily and catches fire with a spark, while bitumen (very long chains) is thick like tar, does not flow, and is difficult to ignite. This pattern of properties determines what each fraction is used for.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Fractional Distillation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><dfn class="term" data-def="The industrial process used to separate crude oil into fractions based on their different boiling points, using a fractionating column.">Fractional distillation</dfn> is the industrial process used to separate crude oil into useful fractions. It works because the different hydrocarbons in crude oil have different boiling points, and a mixture of liquids can be separated if they boil at different temperatures.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n15">The process follows a clear sequence. First, crude oil is heated in a furnace until it vaporises. The hot vapour is then fed into the bottom of a tall <dfn class="term" data-def="A tall tower used in fractional distillation where temperature decreases from bottom to top, allowing different fractions to condense at different heights.">fractionating column</dfn>. The column is very hot at the bottom (around 350&deg;C) and cooler at the top (around 25&deg;C).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16">As the vapour rises up the column, it gradually cools. When a hydrocarbon vapour reaches the height where the temperature matches its boiling point, it <strong>condenses</strong> back into a liquid and is collected. Short-chain hydrocarbons have low boiling points, so they rise to the top before condensing. Long-chain hydrocarbons have high boiling points, so they condense near the bottom.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17">The result is that crude oil is separated into several fractions: gases at the top (used as fuels like LPG), then petrol, kerosene (jet fuel), diesel, fuel oil, and finally bitumen at the very bottom (used for road surfaces). Each fraction is a mixture of hydrocarbons with similar boiling points and chain lengths.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Combustion of Hydrocarbons</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Hydrocarbons are extremely useful as fuels because they release large amounts of energy when burned. When a hydrocarbon burns completely in plenty of oxygen, the products are <strong>carbon dioxide</strong> and <strong>water</strong>. This is called <strong>complete combustion</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">For example, propane burns completely: <strong>C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>8</sub> + 5O<sub>2</sub> &rarr; 3CO<sub>2</sub> + 4H<sub>2</sub>O</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">However, if there is not enough oxygen, <strong>incomplete combustion</strong> occurs. This produces toxic <strong>carbon monoxide</strong> (CO) instead of carbon dioxide, and may also produce <strong>carbon particulates</strong> (soot). Carbon monoxide is a colourless, odourless gas that is extremely dangerous because it binds to haemoglobin in the blood, preventing oxygen transport.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Complete combustion</strong> (plenty of O<sub>2</sub>): hydrocarbon + oxygen &rarr; carbon dioxide + water. <strong>Incomplete combustion</strong> (limited O<sub>2</sub>): produces carbon monoxide (toxic) and/or carbon particulates (soot), which cause <strong>global dimming</strong>.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Why Is Cracking Needed?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><dfn class="term" data-def="The process of breaking down long-chain hydrocarbons into shorter, more useful molecules by heating them and passing over a catalyst or mixing with steam.">Cracking</dfn> is a thermal decomposition reaction that breaks long-chain hydrocarbons into shorter, more useful ones. The demand for different fractions is not equal &mdash; there is far greater demand for short-chain hydrocarbons (like petrol and gases) than for the long-chain fractions (like fuel oil and bitumen) that make up a large proportion of crude oil.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Cracking solves this supply-and-demand problem. It converts the excess long-chain fractions into the shorter-chain molecules that are in high demand, making the refining process much more economically efficient.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Catalytic cracking:</strong> Long-chain hydrocarbons are heated until they vaporise, then passed over a hot <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up itself.">catalyst</dfn> (typically a zeolite catalyst at about 600&ndash;700&deg;C). This breaks the molecules into shorter alkanes and <dfn class="term" data-def="A family of unsaturated hydrocarbons containing a carbon-carbon double bond, with the general formula CnH2n.">alkenes</dfn>.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Steam cracking:</strong> Long-chain hydrocarbons are mixed with steam and heated to very high temperatures (over 800&deg;C). This also produces shorter alkanes and alkenes. Steam cracking tends to produce a higher proportion of alkenes.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28">In both methods, the key products are shorter-chain alkanes (useful as fuels) and alkenes (useful as <dfn class="term" data-def="Chemicals derived from crude oil that are used as raw materials (feedstocks) to make plastics, solvents, detergents, and many other products.">petrochemicals</dfn> &mdash; the starting materials for making plastics, solvents, and detergents).</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n29">Cracking produces alkenes as well as alkanes. Alkenes are <dfn class="term" data-def="A molecule that contains at least one carbon-carbon double bond and can potentially bond with more atoms.">unsaturated</dfn> hydrocarbons &mdash; they contain a <strong>carbon&ndash;carbon double bond</strong> (C=C). Their general formula is <strong>C<sub>n</sub>H<sub>2n</sub></strong>. The simplest alkene is ethene (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>), which is the starting material for making poly(ethene), the most common plastic.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30">You can distinguish alkenes from alkanes using the <strong>bromine water test</strong>. When bromine water (which is orange) is added to an alkene, it <strong>decolourises</strong> (turns colourless) because the bromine reacts with the double bond. With an alkane, bromine water stays orange because there is no double bond to react with.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n31">When answering questions about fractional distillation, always link the <strong>chain length</strong> to the <strong>property</strong> being asked about &mdash; explain <em>why</em> boiling point increases (stronger intermolecular forces between longer chains) rather than just stating the trend. For cracking questions, make sure you state that cracking produces <strong>alkenes</strong> as well as shorter alkanes &mdash; a common mistake is forgetting the alkenes. If asked to compare alkanes and alkenes, mention that alkenes are <strong>unsaturated</strong> (contain a C=C double bond) and can be tested with <strong>bromine water</strong>.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n32">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n33">Crude oil is a finite mixture of hydrocarbons separated by <strong>fractional distillation</strong> based on different boiling points &mdash; short chains rise to the top, long chains stay at the bottom.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n34">As chain length increases, boiling point and viscosity increase while flammability decreases &mdash; these trends are caused by stronger intermolecular forces.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Cracking</strong> breaks long-chain hydrocarbons into shorter alkanes (fuels) and alkenes (for making plastics), solving the mismatch between supply and demand.</li></ul>
