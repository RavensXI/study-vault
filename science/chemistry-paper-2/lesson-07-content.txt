# Using Earth's Resources and Sustainability

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Using Earth&rsquo;s Natural Resources</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Humans rely on the Earth&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="Materials that occur naturally and can be used by humans, including water, wood, metals, and fossil fuels.">natural resources</dfn> to survive and develop. Some of these resources are <dfn class="term" data-def="A resource that is being used up faster than it can be replaced, such as fossil fuels and metal ores.">finite</dfn> &mdash; they will eventually run out because they are being used faster than they can be replaced. Fossil fuels and metal ores are finite resources. Others are <dfn class="term" data-def="A resource that can be replaced at the same rate it is used, such as wood from managed forests or crops.">renewable</dfn>, meaning they can be replaced at the same rate they are used, such as timber from managed forests.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Chemistry plays a key role in improving the way we use natural resources. For example, agriculture uses <dfn class="term" data-def="An artificially produced chemical containing nitrogen, phosphorus, or potassium, added to soil to promote plant growth.">synthetic fertilisers</dfn> to improve crop yields, and industry develops new materials like polymers and alloys from Earth&rsquo;s raw materials. The challenge is to do this <dfn class="term" data-def="Meeting the needs of the current generation without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.">sustainably</dfn> &mdash; meeting our needs today without preventing future generations from meeting theirs.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Natural resources can be classified as <strong>finite</strong> (non-renewable &mdash; will run out) or <strong>renewable</strong> (can be replaced). Sustainable development means using resources at a rate that does not compromise future availability.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Potable Water</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><dfn class="term" data-def="Water that is safe to drink, with sufficiently low levels of dissolved salts and microbes.">Potable water</dfn> is water that is safe to drink. It is not the same as pure water &mdash; potable water still contains dissolved substances, but at levels that are not harmful. In the UK, most potable water comes from surface water sources such as rivers and reservoirs.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Making water potable in the UK involves three main stages:</p>
<div class="collapsible">
<button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
<span>Steps to make potable water from surface water</span>
<svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>1. Filtration</strong> &mdash; Water passes through filter beds made of sand and gravel. This removes large particles such as leaves, soil, and debris.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>2. Sedimentation</strong> &mdash; Chemicals are added that cause fine particles to clump together and sink to the bottom, forming a sediment that can be removed.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>3. Sterilisation</strong> &mdash; The water is treated with chlorine, ozone, or ultraviolet light to kill harmful bacteria and other <dfn class="term" data-def="A tiny organism, such as a bacterium, virus, or fungus, that can cause disease.">microbes</dfn>. This makes the water safe to drink.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n11">In some countries where fresh water is scarce, <dfn class="term" data-def="The process of removing salt and other dissolved minerals from seawater to produce fresh water.">desalination</dfn> is used to produce potable water from seawater. This can be done by distillation (boiling and condensing) or by reverse osmosis (forcing water through membranes that filter out salt ions). Both methods require large amounts of energy, making desalination expensive.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Waste Water Treatment</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Water that has been used in homes, agriculture, and industry becomes <dfn class="term" data-def="Used water from homes, industry, and agriculture that contains dissolved and suspended impurities and must be treated before release into the environment.">waste water</dfn>. Domestic waste water includes <dfn class="term" data-def="Waste water from toilets, sinks, and drains in homes and buildings, containing human waste, detergents, and food particles.">sewage</dfn> from toilets, sinks, and showers. Industrial and agricultural waste water may contain harmful chemicals, heavy metals, or organic waste.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Waste water must be treated before being released back into the environment to prevent pollution and the spread of disease. Treatment involves several stages:</p>
<div class="collapsible">
<button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
<span>Stages of waste water treatment</span>
<svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Screening</strong> &mdash; Large objects such as rags and plastic are removed using metal grilles.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Sedimentation</strong> &mdash; The sewage is held in large tanks where solid material settles as <dfn class="term" data-def="The solid organic matter that settles at the bottom of sewage treatment tanks, which can be broken down by anaerobic digestion.">sludge</dfn>. The liquid effluent is separated off.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Anaerobic digestion of sludge</strong> &mdash; The sludge is broken down by bacteria in the absence of oxygen. This produces biogas (mainly methane), which can be used as a fuel, and a solid residue that can be used as fertiliser.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Aerobic treatment of effluent</strong> &mdash; The liquid effluent is treated with aerobic bacteria (in the presence of oxygen) to break down remaining organic matter. The treated water is then safe to release into rivers or the sea.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Life Cycle Assessments</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An analysis of the environmental impact of a product at every stage of its existence, from extracting raw materials to disposal.">life cycle assessment</dfn> (LCA) examines the environmental impact of a product at every stage of its life &mdash; from &ldquo;cradle to grave.&rdquo; This helps companies and consumers make informed choices about which products are more environmentally friendly.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">The four stages of an LCA are:</p>
<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n22">
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">Stage 1</div>
<h4>Extracting Raw Materials</h4><p>Mining ores, drilling for oil, or harvesting crops. Considers energy used, land damage, and water consumption.</p>
</div>
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">Stage 2</div>
<h4>Manufacturing &amp; Processing</h4><p>Turning raw materials into the product. Considers energy input, waste produced, and pollutants released.</p>
</div>
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">Stage 3</div>
<h4>Use &amp; Distribution</h4><p>Transporting the product to consumers and energy used during its lifetime (e.g. fuel for a car, electricity for a phone).</p>
</div>
<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">Stage 4</div>
<h4>Disposal</h4><p>What happens at end of life &mdash; landfill, incineration, or recycling. Each has a different environmental cost.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>LCAs can be <strong>subjective</strong> &mdash; some environmental impacts (like the effect on local wildlife) are difficult to measure and involve value judgements. This means different LCAs of the same product can reach different conclusions depending on what is prioritised.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Reducing Our Impact &mdash; The Three Rs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">The simplest way to reduce the environmental impact of products is to follow the three Rs: <strong>reduce, reuse, recycle</strong>. Reducing means using fewer resources in the first place. Reusing means finding new purposes for products instead of discarding them. <dfn class="term" data-def="The process of converting waste materials into new materials or products, reducing the need to extract raw resources.">Recycling</dfn> means processing waste materials into new products.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Recycling metals is particularly important. Extracting metals from ores requires large amounts of energy (usually from fossil fuels) and produces waste rock and toxic gases. Recycling aluminium, for example, uses only about <strong>5%</strong> of the energy needed to extract it from bauxite ore.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Extracting Metals from Low-Grade Ores</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28">As high-grade metal ores become scarce, scientists have developed new methods to extract metals from low-grade ores and even from waste. Two key biological methods are <dfn class="term" data-def="A process that uses plants to absorb metal compounds from the ground; the plants are then burned and metal is extracted from the ash.">phytomining</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="A process that uses bacteria to extract metals from low-grade ores; the bacteria produce acidic solutions (leachate) that contain dissolved metal compounds.">bioleaching</dfn>.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
<button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
<span>How phytomining and bioleaching work</span>
<svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Phytomining:</strong> Certain plants are grown on land that contains low concentrations of metal compounds. The plants absorb the metal ions through their roots and accumulate them in their tissues. The plants are then harvested and burned. The ash contains a higher concentration of metal, which can be extracted by smelting or electrolysis.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Bioleaching:</strong> Bacteria are used to break down low-grade ores. The bacteria produce an acidic solution called a <dfn class="term" data-def="The solution produced by bacteria during bioleaching, containing dissolved metal compounds that can be processed to extract pure metal.">leachate</dfn>, which contains dissolved metal compounds. The metal can then be extracted from this solution, often by displacement using a more reactive metal or by electrolysis.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Both methods are slower than traditional mining but are more environmentally friendly because they do not require huge amounts of energy or produce large volumes of waste rock.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n32">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Phytomining uses <strong>plants</strong> to absorb metals from soil; bioleaching uses <strong>bacteria</strong> to dissolve metals from low-grade ores. Both are sustainable alternatives to traditional mining for extracting copper and other metals.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n33">Questions on this topic often ask you to <strong>compare</strong> methods (e.g. desalination vs. filtration, phytomining vs. bioleaching) or <strong>evaluate</strong> the use of LCAs. Remember that LCAs involve subjective judgements &mdash; always explain <em>why</em> some data is hard to quantify. For 6-mark extended responses, use a clear structure: state a point, explain the science, then link it to the environmental or economic impact.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n34">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n35">Natural resources are either finite (e.g. fossil fuels, ores) or renewable (e.g. timber). Sustainable development means using resources without compromising future supply.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n36">Potable water is made safe by filtration, sedimentation, and sterilisation. In water-scarce areas, desalination of seawater is used but requires significant energy.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Life cycle assessments analyse a product&rsquo;s environmental impact from cradle to grave, but some impacts are difficult to measure objectively. Reduce, reuse, and recycle are the simplest ways to lower environmental impact.</li>
</ul>
