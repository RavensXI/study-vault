# The Earth's Atmosphere and Greenhouse Effect

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Earth&rsquo;s Early Atmosphere</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Scientists believe that for the first billion years of Earth&rsquo;s existence, the atmosphere was very different from what it is today. Evidence suggests that the <dfn class="term" data-def="The layer of gases surrounding the Earth, currently made up of roughly 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and small amounts of other gases.">atmosphere</dfn> was formed by <dfn class="term" data-def="The release of gases from the interior of the Earth through volcanic eruptions. Volcanic degassing released carbon dioxide, water vapour, nitrogen, and other gases that formed the early atmosphere.">volcanic degassing</dfn> &mdash; the release of gases from volcanoes during intense volcanic activity. The early atmosphere was probably similar to that of Mars and Venus today.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The early atmosphere likely contained:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n4">A large amount of <strong>carbon dioxide</strong> (CO&#8322;)</li>
<li data-narration-id="n5"><strong>Water vapour</strong>, which later condensed to form the oceans</li>
<li data-narration-id="n6">Small amounts of <strong>methane</strong> (CH&#8324;) and <strong>ammonia</strong> (NH&#8323;)</li>
<li data-narration-id="n7">Little or <strong>no oxygen</strong></li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n8">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The early atmosphere was mainly <strong>carbon dioxide</strong> with little or no oxygen. Volcanoes released carbon dioxide, water vapour, nitrogen, and small amounts of methane and ammonia. This is supported by evidence from the atmospheres of Mars and Venus, which are still mostly CO&#8322; today.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">How Did Oxygen Appear?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">Around 2.7 billion years ago, the first organisms capable of <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which plants and algae use sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water to produce glucose and oxygen. Photosynthesis is responsible for the increase in atmospheric oxygen over billions of years.">photosynthesis</dfn> evolved &mdash; simple algae and <dfn class="term" data-def="Microscopic photosynthetic bacteria that were among the first organisms to produce oxygen. Cyanobacteria are also known as blue-green algae.">cyanobacteria</dfn>. These organisms used carbon dioxide and water in the presence of sunlight to produce glucose and release oxygen as a waste product.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Over hundreds of millions of years, photosynthesising organisms gradually increased the proportion of oxygen in the atmosphere. As oxygen levels rose, more complex life forms could evolve. Eventually, atmospheric oxygen reached today&rsquo;s level of approximately <strong>21%</strong>.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n12">Carbon dioxide was removed from the early atmosphere by three main processes:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>1. Photosynthesis:</strong> Algae and plants absorbed CO&#8322; from the atmosphere and released O&#8322;. This was the single biggest factor in reducing atmospheric carbon dioxide.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>2. Dissolving in the oceans:</strong> When the water vapour in the atmosphere condensed to form the oceans, CO&#8322; dissolved in the water. Some of this dissolved CO&#8322; formed insoluble <dfn class="term" data-def="Solid calcium carbonate deposits formed from carbon dioxide that dissolved in the oceans and reacted with calcium compounds. Limestone and chalk are examples of carbonate sedimentary rocks.">carbonate precipitates</dfn> that fell to the ocean floor and became sedimentary rocks such as limestone (CaCO&#8323;).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>3. Formation of fossil fuels:</strong> The remains of organisms that could not decay were buried under layers of sediment and, over millions of years, became <dfn class="term" data-def="Non-renewable energy resources formed from the remains of ancient organisms buried under sediment for millions of years. They include coal, oil, and natural gas, and they store carbon originally removed from the atmosphere.">fossil fuels</dfn> &mdash; coal, oil, and natural gas. The carbon in these organisms originally came from atmospheric CO&#8322; absorbed during photosynthesis.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Today&rsquo;s Atmosphere</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The proportions of gases in today&rsquo;s atmosphere have been broadly stable for around 200 million years. The current composition is approximately:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Nitrogen</strong> &mdash; about 78%</li>
<li data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Oxygen</strong> &mdash; about 21%</li>
<li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Argon</strong> &mdash; about 0.9%</li>
<li data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Carbon dioxide</strong> &mdash; about 0.04%</li>
<li data-narration-id="n22">Small amounts of <strong>water vapour</strong> and other gases</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Today&rsquo;s atmosphere is approximately <strong>78% nitrogen</strong>, <strong>21% oxygen</strong>, and <strong>0.9% argon</strong>, with about 0.04% carbon dioxide. These proportions have been roughly stable for the last 200 million years.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">The Greenhouse Effect</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The natural process in which certain gases in the atmosphere absorb and re-emit infrared radiation from the Earth's surface, keeping the planet warm enough to support life.">greenhouse effect</dfn> is a natural process that keeps the Earth warm enough to support life. Without it, the average surface temperature would be about &minus;18&deg;C instead of the current average of approximately +15&deg;C.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">The process works as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Short-wavelength radiation from the Sun passes through the atmosphere and warms the Earth&rsquo;s surface.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Step 2:</strong> The warm surface re-emits energy as long-wavelength <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of electromagnetic radiation with a wavelength longer than visible light. Warm objects emit infrared radiation, and greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit it.">infrared radiation</dfn>.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Step 3:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Gases in the atmosphere that absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, contributing to the greenhouse effect. The main greenhouse gases are carbon dioxide, methane, and water vapour.">Greenhouse gases</dfn> in the atmosphere absorb some of this infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back towards the Earth&rsquo;s surface.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Step 4:</strong> This keeps the Earth warmer than it would otherwise be.</li>
</ul>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Carbon dioxide (CO&#8322;):</strong> Released by burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and respiration. It is the greenhouse gas most affected by human activity.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Methane (CH&#8324;):</strong> Released by livestock farming (digestion in cattle), rice paddy fields, decomposition of waste in landfill sites, and extraction of fossil fuels. Methane is a more potent greenhouse gas than CO&#8322; but is present in much smaller quantities.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Water vapour (H&#8322;O):</strong> The most abundant greenhouse gas. As temperatures rise, more water evaporates, which can amplify the greenhouse effect further.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n34">Human Activities and Climate Change</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n35">Since the Industrial Revolution (from around 1750), human activities have released increasing amounts of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. The concentration of CO&#8322; has risen from about 280 parts per million (ppm) to over 420 ppm today. Most scientists agree that this rise is the main cause of recent <dfn class="term" data-def="A large-scale and long-term change in global or regional climate patterns. Current climate change is linked to increased levels of greenhouse gases from human activities.">climate change</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36">The main human activities responsible include:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Burning fossil fuels</strong> &mdash; for transport, electricity generation, heating, and industry. This releases CO&#8322; that was locked away millions of years ago.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Deforestation</strong> &mdash; cutting down forests removes trees that would absorb CO&#8322; through photosynthesis. Burning or decay of the wood releases the stored carbon back into the atmosphere.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Agriculture</strong> &mdash; rice paddies and livestock produce methane. Using nitrogen-based fertilisers releases nitrous oxide, another greenhouse gas.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40"><strong>Landfill</strong> &mdash; decomposing organic waste in landfill produces methane.</li>
</ul>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n41">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Since the Industrial Revolution, atmospheric CO&#8322; has risen from about <strong>280 ppm to over 420 ppm</strong>. The main causes are burning fossil fuels, deforestation, agriculture, and landfill. Most scientists attribute recent climate change to this increase in greenhouse gas concentrations.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n42">Consequences of Climate Change</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n43">Increasing global temperatures have a range of potential consequences. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Rising sea levels</strong> caused by thermal expansion of water and melting of polar ice caps and glaciers, leading to flooding of low-lying areas.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n45"><strong>More extreme weather events</strong> &mdash; more frequent and severe storms, droughts, and heatwaves.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n46"><strong>Changes to ecosystems</strong> &mdash; shifts in habitats and migration patterns, loss of biodiversity, and coral bleaching in warmer oceans.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n47"><strong>Food security</strong> &mdash; changing rainfall patterns and higher temperatures can reduce crop yields in some regions.</li>
</ul>

    <span>Why Is Climate Change a Controversial Topic?</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n48">The vast majority of climate scientists agree that human activities are the main cause of recent climate change. However, some people question the extent of the human contribution. This is partly because:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n49">&bull; The climate has changed naturally throughout Earth&rsquo;s history due to volcanic eruptions, changes in the Sun&rsquo;s output, and orbital variations.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n50">&bull; Climate models involve complex calculations with many variables, and predictions for the future carry a degree of uncertainty.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n51">&bull; Economic interests &mdash; industries that depend on fossil fuels may resist policy changes that could affect profits.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n52">Despite these debates, the scientific consensus, supported by bodies such as the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), is very strong: human activity is the dominant cause of the warming observed since the mid-20th century.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n53">Carbon Footprint</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n54">A <dfn class="term" data-def="The total amount of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases emitted over the full life cycle of a product, service, or event. It is usually measured in kilograms or tonnes of CO2 equivalent.">carbon footprint</dfn> is the total amount of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases emitted over the full life cycle of a product, service, or event. It is usually expressed as <strong>tonnes of CO&#8322; equivalent</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n55">The carbon footprint can be reduced by actions such as:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n56">Using <strong>renewable energy sources</strong> (wind, solar, hydroelectric) instead of fossil fuels.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n57">Improving <strong>energy efficiency</strong> in homes and industry &mdash; insulation, LED lighting, efficient boilers.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n58">Reducing, reusing, and <strong>recycling</strong> materials to cut the energy used in manufacturing.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n59">Using public transport, cycling, or electric vehicles instead of petrol or diesel cars.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n60"><strong>Carbon capture and storage</strong> (CCS) &mdash; capturing CO&#8322; from power stations before it enters the atmosphere and storing it underground.</li>
</ul>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Challenges of Reducing Carbon Footprints</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n61">Reducing carbon footprints is not straightforward. There are several challenges:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n62">&bull; <strong>Cost:</strong> Renewable energy infrastructure and carbon capture technology require significant investment.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n63">&bull; <strong>International cooperation:</strong> Climate change is a global issue, so action by one country alone has limited effect. Agreements like the Paris Agreement (2015) aim to coordinate international efforts, but compliance varies.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n64">&bull; <strong>Lifestyle changes:</strong> Many people are unwilling or unable to change habits such as driving, flying, or consuming energy-intensive products.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n65">&bull; <strong>Developing nations:</strong> Countries experiencing industrial growth may prioritise economic development over emission reductions, arguing that wealthier nations should lead.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n66">In 6-mark extended response questions on this topic, make sure you write about specific gases (<strong>carbon dioxide</strong>, <strong>methane</strong>, <strong>water vapour</strong>) rather than just saying &ldquo;greenhouse gases.&rdquo; Always link human activities to specific consequences &mdash; for example, &ldquo;burning fossil fuels releases CO&#8322;, which increases the greenhouse effect, leading to rising global temperatures.&rdquo; For questions about reducing carbon footprints, discuss both <strong>individual actions</strong> and <strong>government or industrial strategies</strong>, and evaluate their effectiveness.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n67">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n68">The early atmosphere was mainly CO&#8322; with little oxygen. Photosynthesis by algae and plants increased oxygen levels and reduced CO&#8322; over billions of years. Today&rsquo;s atmosphere is approximately 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 0.04% CO&#8322;.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n69">The greenhouse effect is a natural process where greenhouse gases (CO&#8322;, methane, water vapour) absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, keeping the Earth warm. Human activities have increased greenhouse gas levels, causing enhanced global warming and climate change.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n70">Carbon footprints can be reduced through renewable energy, improved efficiency, recycling, and carbon capture &mdash; but cost, international cooperation, and lifestyle changes remain significant challenges.</li></ul>
