# Rates of Reaction and Collision Theory

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Rate of Reaction?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of how quickly reactants are turned into products in a chemical reaction.">rate of reaction</dfn> tells us how fast a chemical reaction happens. Some reactions are very fast &mdash; like an explosion or the neutralisation of an acid. Others are very slow, such as rusting or the weathering of rocks. In GCSE Chemistry, you need to know how to measure, calculate, and explain what controls the rate of a reaction.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Rate of reaction can be defined as the amount of product formed, or the amount of reactant used up, per unit of time. The equation is:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Rate of reaction = amount of product formed &divide; time</strong> &mdash; or &mdash; <strong>amount of reactant used up &divide; time</strong>. The units depend on what you measure: g/s if you measure mass, or cm&sup3;/s if you measure the volume of gas produced.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Measuring Rate of Reaction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">There are two main practical methods for measuring rate of reaction at GCSE. Both appear in the AQA required practicals and you need to know the advantages and limitations of each.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Method 1: Measuring the Volume of Gas Produced</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7">When a reaction produces a gas, you can collect and measure the gas using a <dfn class="term" data-def="A piece of laboratory equipment used to collect and measure the volume of gas produced during a chemical reaction.">gas syringe</dfn> or by collecting the gas over water using an inverted measuring cylinder. You record the volume of gas at regular time intervals (e.g. every 10 seconds) and plot a graph of volume against time.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Example:</strong> Marble chips (calcium carbonate) reacting with hydrochloric acid: CaCO&sub3; + 2HCl &rarr; CaCl&sub2; + H&sub2;O + CO&sub2;. The carbon dioxide gas is collected in the gas syringe. The faster the syringe plunger moves out, the faster the reaction.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Method 2: Measuring the Change in Mass</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n9">Place the reaction flask on a balance and record the mass at regular time intervals. As gas escapes from the flask, the mass decreases. You plot a graph of mass lost against time. This method works well for reactions that produce carbon dioxide or hydrogen gas.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Advantage:</strong> Easy to set up and gives continuous data. <strong>Disadvantage:</strong> Gas escapes into the room, so the mass readings are slightly less accurate than collecting the gas directly. Very light gases like hydrogen are hard to detect by mass loss.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Reading Rate from a Graph</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">When you plot a graph of product formed (or reactant used up) against time, the <strong>gradient</strong> (steepness) of the line tells you the rate. A steep line means a fast reaction. A shallow line means a slow reaction. When the line goes flat, the reaction has finished &mdash; one of the reactants has been completely used up.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">To calculate the rate at a specific point on a curved graph, draw a <dfn class="term" data-def="A straight line that touches a curve at exactly one point, used to find the gradient (and therefore the rate) at that point on a graph.">tangent</dfn> to the curve at that point and find the gradient of the tangent. The gradient equals the change in the y-axis divided by the change in the x-axis.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Gradient = change in y &divide; change in x.</strong> On a volume&ndash;time graph, this gives rate in cm&sup3;/s. On a mass-loss graph, it gives rate in g/s. The <strong>mean rate</strong> for the whole reaction is the total product formed divided by the total time taken.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Collision Theory</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><dfn class="term" data-def="The theory that reactions can only happen when particles collide with enough energy and in the correct orientation.">Collision theory</dfn> explains why reactions happen at different rates. For a reaction to occur, the reacting particles must collide with each other. But not every collision leads to a reaction &mdash; two conditions must be met:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>1. The particles must collide with enough energy.</strong> The minimum energy needed for a collision to result in a reaction is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The minimum amount of energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to take place.">activation energy</dfn>. If particles collide with less energy than this, they just bounce apart. <strong>2. The particles must collide in the correct orientation</strong> &mdash; they need to hit each other the right way round so that bonds can break and new bonds can form.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n18">Anything that increases the <dfn class="term" data-def="The number of successful collisions between reacting particles per second.">frequency of successful collisions</dfn> will increase the rate of reaction. The four main factors that do this are temperature, concentration, surface area, and catalysts.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Factors Affecting Rate</h2>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Temperature</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Increasing the <dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance.">temperature</dfn> makes particles move faster because they have more kinetic energy. This has two effects: particles collide more often (higher collision frequency), and a greater proportion of collisions have energy equal to or greater than the activation energy. Both effects increase the rate. This is why reactions are usually faster when heated.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Concentration (and Pressure for Gases)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Increasing the <dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of solute dissolved in a given volume of solution, usually measured in mol/dm&sup3; or g/dm&sup3;.">concentration</dfn> of a solution means there are more reactant particles in the same volume. This increases the frequency of collisions, so the rate increases. For gaseous reactions, increasing the pressure has the same effect &mdash; it forces particles closer together so they collide more often.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Surface Area</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Breaking a solid into smaller pieces increases its <dfn class="term" data-def="The total area of the outer surface of a solid &mdash; smaller pieces have a larger total surface area exposed to the other reactant.">surface area</dfn>. More surface is exposed to the other reactant, so more collisions can happen at the same time. For example, powdered <dfn class="term" data-def="Small pieces of calcium carbonate (CaCO&sub3;) used in the required practical to investigate rate of reaction with hydrochloric acid.">marble chips</dfn> react much faster with hydrochloric acid than large lumps because the powder has a much greater surface area.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>All four factors &mdash; temperature, concentration, surface area, and catalysts &mdash; affect rate by changing the frequency or energy of collisions. In every exam answer, link back to collision theory: more frequent collisions or more energetic collisions means a faster rate.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Catalysts</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up itself &mdash; it provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy.">catalyst</dfn> is a substance that increases the rate of a reaction without being used up. It works by providing an alternative reaction pathway that has a lower activation energy. This means a greater proportion of collisions now have enough energy to react, so the rate increases. The catalyst is chemically unchanged at the end &mdash; it can be reused.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Enzymes</strong> are biological catalysts that control reactions in living organisms. In industry, catalysts are used to reduce energy costs &mdash; reactions can run at lower temperatures, saving fuel and money. Different reactions require different catalysts.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">When answering questions about rate of reaction, always link your explanation to collision theory. Simply saying &ldquo;the reaction is faster because there are more collisions&rdquo; is not enough &mdash; you must explain <strong>why</strong> collisions are more frequent or more energetic. For example: &ldquo;Increasing temperature gives particles more kinetic energy, so they move faster, collide more frequently, and a greater proportion of collisions exceed the activation energy.&rdquo; In calculate questions, remember to include units (g/s or cm&sup3;/s) and show your working clearly.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">Rate of reaction = amount of product formed &divide; time. Measure it using a gas syringe (volume of gas) or a balance (mass lost).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Collision theory: reactions only happen when particles collide with sufficient energy (the activation energy) and the correct orientation.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Four factors increase rate &mdash; higher temperature, higher concentration (or pressure), greater surface area, and catalysts &mdash; all by increasing the frequency or energy of collisions.</li></ul>
