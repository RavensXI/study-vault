# Reversible Reactions and Equilibrium

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is a Reversible Reaction?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Most chemical reactions you have studied so far go in one direction &mdash; reactants turn into products and the reaction stops. But some reactions can go in both directions. In a <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction that can proceed in both the forward and reverse directions, shown by the equilibrium symbol &#8652;.">reversible reaction</dfn>, the products can react to reform the original reactants. We show this using the equilibrium symbol &#8652; instead of a normal arrow.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">A classic example is the thermal decomposition of <dfn class="term" data-def="A white solid that breaks down on heating into ammonia gas and hydrogen chloride gas, and reforms when the gases cool.">ammonium chloride</dfn>. When heated, ammonium chloride (a white solid) breaks down into ammonia and hydrogen chloride &mdash; two colourless gases. When these gases cool and meet, they recombine to form ammonium chloride again:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4"><strong>ammonium chloride &#8652; ammonia + hydrogen chloride</strong><br>NH<sub>4</sub>Cl(s) &#8652; NH<sub>3</sub>(g) + HCl(g)</p>
<p data-narration-id="n5">You can see this in action: when you heat ammonium chloride in a test tube, a white solid forms higher up in the tube where the gases have cooled and recombined. This proves the reaction is reversible.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n6">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The &#8652; symbol tells you a reaction is reversible. A normal arrow (&rarr;) means the reaction only goes one way. Never use a normal arrow for a reversible reaction in the exam.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">Hydrated Copper Sulfate &mdash; Another Reversible Reaction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Another important example is the hydration of copper sulfate. <dfn class="term" data-def="Copper sulfate crystals that contain water molecules locked into their crystal structure, giving them a blue colour (CuSO&#8324;&middot;5H&#8322;O).">Hydrated copper sulfate</dfn> is a blue crystalline solid. When you heat it, it loses its water of crystallisation and turns into a white powder called <dfn class="term" data-def="Copper sulfate that has had its water of crystallisation removed by heating, leaving a white powder (CuSO&#8324;).">anhydrous copper sulfate</dfn>:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>hydrated copper sulfate (blue) &#8652; anhydrous copper sulfate (white) + water</strong><br>CuSO<sub>4</sub>&middot;5H<sub>2</sub>O(s) &#8652; CuSO<sub>4</sub>(s) + 5H<sub>2</sub>O(l)</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">If you add water back to the white anhydrous powder, it turns blue again and gets hot &mdash; the reverse reaction is exothermic. This colour change is so reliable that anhydrous copper sulfate is used as a chemical test for the presence of water.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Energy Changes in Reversible Reactions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">In any reversible reaction, if the forward reaction is <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction that transfers energy to the surroundings, causing the temperature to increase.">exothermic</dfn> (gives out energy), then the reverse reaction is <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction that takes in energy from the surroundings, causing the temperature to decrease.">endothermic</dfn> (takes in energy) &mdash; and vice versa. The amount of energy transferred in each direction is exactly the same.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">For the copper sulfate example: the forward reaction (heating blue copper sulfate to drive off water) is endothermic &mdash; you have to supply heat energy. The reverse reaction (adding water to the white powder) is exothermic &mdash; the mixture gets hot. The energy released going one way equals the energy absorbed going the other way.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>If a reversible reaction is exothermic in the forward direction, it is endothermic in the reverse direction &mdash; and the energy change is the same in both directions. This is a common exam question.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Dynamic Equilibrium</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">When a reversible reaction takes place in a <dfn class="term" data-def="A sealed container from which no substances can enter or leave &mdash; required for equilibrium to be reached.">closed system</dfn> (where nothing can get in or out), something interesting happens. At first the forward reaction is fast because there are lots of reactants. As products build up, the reverse reaction speeds up. Eventually, the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. This is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The state in a reversible reaction where the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction, so the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant.">dynamic equilibrium</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">&ldquo;Dynamic&rdquo; means that both reactions are still happening &mdash; molecules are constantly reacting in both directions. &ldquo;Equilibrium&rdquo; means the overall concentrations of reactants and products stay the same because the two rates are balanced. It is not a static situation where everything has stopped.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n19">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Dynamic equilibrium requires a closed system. The forward and reverse reactions continue at equal rates, so concentrations of reactants and products do not change &mdash; but the reaction has not stopped.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Le Ch&acirc;telier&rsquo;s Principle</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><dfn class="term" data-def="The principle that if conditions affecting an equilibrium are changed, the position of equilibrium shifts to partially oppose the change.">Le Ch&acirc;telier&rsquo;s principle</dfn> states that if a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in conditions, the position of equilibrium shifts to partially oppose that change. In other words, the system tries to &ldquo;undo&rdquo; whatever you did to it. This principle lets you predict which way the equilibrium will shift when you change temperature, pressure, or concentration.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Effect of Changing Temperature</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n22">If you <strong>increase the temperature</strong>, the equilibrium shifts in the endothermic direction to absorb the extra heat. If you <strong>decrease the temperature</strong>, the equilibrium shifts in the exothermic direction to release heat and restore the temperature. Changing temperature changes both the rate and the position of equilibrium &mdash; and therefore changes the yield of products.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Example:</strong> In a reversible reaction where the forward reaction is exothermic, increasing the temperature will favour the reverse (endothermic) reaction, producing more reactants and fewer products. The yield of products decreases.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n24">For reactions involving gases, changing the pressure affects the equilibrium position. If you <strong>increase the pressure</strong>, the equilibrium shifts towards the side with fewer moles of gas &mdash; this reduces the pressure. If you <strong>decrease the pressure</strong>, the equilibrium shifts towards the side with more moles of gas.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Example:</strong> Consider N<sub>2</sub>(g) + 3H<sub>2</sub>(g) &#8652; 2NH<sub>3</sub>(g). The left side has 4 moles of gas (1 + 3) and the right side has 2. Increasing the pressure shifts the equilibrium to the right, producing more ammonia.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Effect of Changing Concentration</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n26">If you <strong>increase the concentration</strong> of a reactant, the equilibrium shifts to the right (forward direction) to use up the extra reactant, producing more products. If you <strong>increase the concentration</strong> of a product, the equilibrium shifts to the left (reverse direction) to use up the extra product.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Example:</strong> In the reaction A + B &#8652; C + D, adding more of substance A shifts the equilibrium to the right, increasing the yield of C and D until a new equilibrium is established.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Catalysts and Equilibrium</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up, by providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.">catalyst</dfn> speeds up both the forward and reverse reactions equally. This means a catalyst helps the system reach equilibrium faster, but it does not change the position of equilibrium or the yield of products. The same amounts of reactants and products are present at equilibrium whether a catalyst is used or not &mdash; you just get there more quickly.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Applying Equilibrium: The Haber Process</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The industrial process for making ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen: N&#8322; + 3H&#8322; &#8652; 2NH&#8323;, using high pressure, moderate temperature, and an iron catalyst.">Haber process</dfn> is a real-world application of equilibrium principles. Nitrogen from the air reacts with hydrogen (from natural gas) to produce ammonia:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>N<sub>2</sub>(g) + 3H<sub>2</sub>(g) &#8652; 2NH<sub>3</sub>(g)</strong> &mdash; the forward reaction is exothermic.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Using Le Ch&acirc;telier&rsquo;s principle: a low temperature and high pressure would give the best yield of ammonia. However, low temperatures make the reaction too slow, so a compromise temperature of about 450&thinsp;&deg;C is used. High pressure (around 200 atmospheres) favours ammonia production. An iron catalyst is used to speed up the reaction without changing the yield. Any unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen are recycled back through the reactor.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n34">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The Haber process uses a compromise temperature (about 450&thinsp;&deg;C), high pressure (200 atm), and an iron catalyst. High pressure increases yield; the moderate temperature balances acceptable yield with a reasonable rate of reaction.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n35">For questions on equilibrium, always state the direction of the shift AND explain why using Le Ch&acirc;telier&rsquo;s principle. For example: &ldquo;Increasing temperature shifts the equilibrium in the endothermic direction because the system acts to absorb the extra heat energy.&rdquo; Simply saying &ldquo;it shifts right&rdquo; without the reasoning will not earn full marks. In 6-mark extended response questions, use a specific example like the Haber process to illustrate your points.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n36">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n37">Reversible reactions go in both directions (shown by &#8652;). If the forward reaction is exothermic, the reverse is endothermic by the same amount of energy.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Dynamic equilibrium occurs in a closed system when the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal &mdash; concentrations stay constant but the reaction has not stopped.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">Le Ch&acirc;telier&rsquo;s principle: changing temperature, pressure, or concentration shifts the equilibrium to partially oppose the change. Catalysts speed up both directions equally and do not change the position of equilibrium.</li></ul>
