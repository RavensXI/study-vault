# Speed, Velocity and Acceleration

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Distance and Displacement</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Before you can understand motion properly, you need to know the difference between <dfn class="term" data-def="The total length of the path travelled by an object, measured in metres (m). Distance is a scalar quantity.">distance</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="The straight-line distance from the start point to the end point in a given direction. Displacement is a vector quantity.">displacement</dfn>. Distance is the total length of the path an object travels &mdash; it doesn&rsquo;t matter which direction. If you walk 3&thinsp;m north and then 3&thinsp;m south, you&rsquo;ve covered a distance of 6&thinsp;m. Displacement, however, measures the straight-line distance from start to finish <em>in a given direction</em>. In the same example, your displacement is 0&thinsp;m because you ended up back where you started.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Distance is a <strong>scalar</strong> quantity (magnitude only). Displacement is a <strong>vector</strong> quantity (magnitude <em>and</em> direction). The AQA spec requires you to know this distinction for spec ref 4.5.6.1.1.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n4">This difference between <dfn class="term" data-def="A quantity that has magnitude (size) only, such as distance, speed or energy.">scalar</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="A quantity that has both magnitude (size) and direction, such as displacement, velocity or force.">vector</dfn> quantities runs through the whole of this topic. Scalars have size only; vectors have both size and direction. You will meet this again with speed and velocity.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Speed and Velocity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The quantity known as <dfn class="term" data-def="The distance travelled per unit of time, measured in metres per second (m/s). Speed is a scalar quantity.">speed</dfn> tells you how fast an object is moving. It is calculated using the equation:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p><strong>speed = distance &divide; time</strong> &emsp; or &emsp; <strong>v = s &divide; t</strong><br>where v = speed in m/s, s = distance in m, t = time in s.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Speed is a scalar &mdash; it has no direction. <dfn class="term" data-def="The speed of an object in a given direction. Velocity is a vector quantity, measured in metres per second (m/s).">velocity</dfn> on the other hand, is the speed in a stated direction, making it a vector. An object moving at 5&thinsp;m/s to the east has a velocity of 5&thinsp;m/s east. If it turns around and moves at the same speed to the west, its speed is still 5&thinsp;m/s but its velocity has changed to 5&thinsp;m/s west.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Distance&ndash;Time Graphs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A graph with time on the x-axis and distance on the y-axis. The gradient of the line equals the speed of the object.">distance&ndash;time graph</dfn> plots distance on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. The key rule is simple: the <dfn class="term" data-def="The steepness of a line on a graph. On a distance-time graph, gradient equals speed; on a velocity-time graph, gradient equals acceleration.">gradient</dfn> of the line equals the speed.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>On a distance&ndash;time graph: a <strong>horizontal line</strong> means the object is stationary, a <strong>straight diagonal line</strong> means constant speed, and a <strong>curve</strong> means the speed is changing (accelerating or decelerating).</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n14">To find the speed at any point on a curve, draw a <dfn class="term" data-def="A straight line that touches a curve at exactly one point. The gradient of the tangent gives the instantaneous speed or velocity at that point.">tangent</dfn> to the curve at that point and calculate its gradient. The gradient is calculated as the change in distance divided by the change in time (rise &divide; run).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Velocity&ndash;Time Graphs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A graph with time on the x-axis and velocity on the y-axis. The gradient gives acceleration; the area under the line gives the distance travelled.">velocity&ndash;time graph</dfn> plots velocity on the y-axis against time on the x-axis. These graphs tell you two things:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n17"><strong>The gradient</strong> of the line gives the <strong>acceleration</strong> of the object.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n18"><strong>The area under the line</strong> gives the <strong>distance travelled</strong> (or displacement).</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n19">A horizontal line on a velocity&ndash;time graph means constant velocity (zero acceleration). A line sloping upwards means the object is accelerating. A line sloping downwards means <dfn class="term" data-def="A decrease in velocity over time. Sometimes called negative acceleration. Measured in m/s&sup2;.">deceleration</dfn> (negative acceleration). To calculate the area under a straight-line graph, split it into rectangles and triangles and add the areas together.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n20">A car accelerates from rest to 20&thinsp;m/s in 10 seconds, then travels at a constant 20&thinsp;m/s for 5 seconds.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Acceleration phase:</strong> gradient = (20 &minus; 0) &divide; (10 &minus; 0) = 2&thinsp;m/s&sup2;. The area under this section is a triangle: &frac12; &times; 10 &times; 20 = 100&thinsp;m.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Constant speed phase:</strong> gradient = 0 (no acceleration). The area under this section is a rectangle: 5 &times; 20 = 100&thinsp;m.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Total distance:</strong> 100 + 100 = 200&thinsp;m.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Acceleration</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">The quantity <dfn class="term" data-def="The rate of change of velocity, measured in metres per second squared (m/s&sup2;). Acceleration is a vector quantity.">acceleration</dfn> is the rate at which velocity changes. It is calculated using:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p><strong>acceleration = change in velocity &divide; time taken</strong> &emsp; or &emsp; <strong>a = (v &minus; u) &divide; t</strong><br>where a = acceleration in m/s&sup2;, v = final velocity in m/s, u = initial velocity in m/s, t = time in s.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n27">A positive value for acceleration means the object is speeding up. A negative value means it is slowing down (decelerating). Near the surface of the Earth, an object in free fall accelerates at approximately 9.8&thinsp;m/s&sup2; due to gravity (often rounded to 10&thinsp;m/s&sup2; in calculations).</p>

<p data-narration-id="n28">For objects undergoing <dfn class="term" data-def="Acceleration that stays the same over time. The velocity changes by equal amounts in equal time intervals.">uniform acceleration</dfn> (constant acceleration), AQA requires you to use this equation (spec ref 4.5.6.1.5):</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>v&sup2; = u&sup2; + 2as</strong><br>where v = final velocity (m/s), u = initial velocity (m/s), a = acceleration (m/s&sup2;), s = distance (m).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">This equation is particularly useful when you are not given a time value. For example, a cyclist accelerates uniformly from 4&thinsp;m/s at 0.5&thinsp;m/s&sup2; over a distance of 50&thinsp;m. To find the final velocity: v&sup2; = 4&sup2; + 2(0.5)(50) = 16 + 50 = 66. So v = &radic;66 &asymp; 8.1&thinsp;m/s.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n33">In calculation questions, always <strong>write the equation first</strong>, then <strong>substitute your values</strong>, then <strong>calculate</strong>. You earn marks for each step even if you get the final answer wrong. For graph questions, draw clear construction lines when finding gradients or areas &mdash; the examiner needs to see how you read values from the graph. Remember: gradient of a distance&ndash;time graph = speed, gradient of a velocity&ndash;time graph = acceleration, area under a velocity&ndash;time graph = distance.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n34">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n35">Distance is scalar (no direction); displacement is vector (has direction). Speed is scalar; velocity is vector. Use v = s &divide; t for speed and a = (v &minus; u) &divide; t for acceleration.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n36">On a distance&ndash;time graph, the gradient gives speed. On a velocity&ndash;time graph, the gradient gives acceleration and the area under the line gives distance travelled.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">For uniform acceleration without a time value, use v&sup2; = u&sup2; + 2as. Always show your working in calculation questions for full marks.</li></ul>
