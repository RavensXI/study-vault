# Magnets, Electromagnetism and the Motor Effect

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Magnetism as a Non-Contact Force</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A force that acts at a distance without physical contact between objects.">magnetism</dfn> is a non-contact force &mdash; it acts at a distance without objects touching. Unlike gravity, which acts between all masses, magnetism only acts between three types of object: <strong>permanent magnets</strong>, <strong>induced magnets</strong>, and <strong>electromagnets</strong>. Only certain materials are magnetic: iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Every magnet has two poles &mdash; a <strong>north pole</strong> and a <strong>south pole</strong>. The poles are where the <dfn class="term" data-def="The region around a magnet where a magnetic material experiences a force.">magnetic field</dfn> is strongest. When two magnets are brought together, like poles repel and unlike poles attract. If you hang a bar magnet on a string so it can spin freely, its north pole will point towards geographic north &mdash; this is how a compass works.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Like poles <strong>repel</strong>. Unlike poles <strong>attract</strong>. The poles are where the magnetic forces are strongest.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Permanent and Induced Magnets</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A magnet that produces its own magnetic field without needing an external source of magnetism.">permanent magnet</dfn> produces its own magnetic field all the time. It does not need electricity or another magnet to work. Bar magnets, horseshoe magnets, and fridge magnets are all permanent magnets. Steel is used to make permanent magnets because it does not lose its magnetism easily.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A material that becomes a temporary magnet when placed in a magnetic field, losing its magnetism when removed.">induced magnet</dfn> is a magnetic material that becomes magnetised only when it is placed inside a magnetic field. For example, bringing a permanent magnet close to an iron nail turns the nail into a temporary magnet. The induced magnet&rsquo;s poles always line up so that the nearest pole is opposite to the permanent magnet&rsquo;s pole &mdash; this means induced magnets are always <strong>attracted</strong> to the permanent magnet, never repelled.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n8">Steel retains its magnetism when removed from a magnetic field, making it ideal for permanent magnets. Iron magnetises more strongly than steel but loses its magnetism quickly once removed from the field &mdash; this makes iron better for temporary or induced magnets. Electromagnets use an iron core precisely because the magnetism must switch on and off with the current.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">Magnetic Field Lines</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where a magnetic force acts. We represent it using <strong>field lines</strong>. These lines always run from the <strong>north pole</strong> to the <strong>south pole</strong> outside the magnet. Where the lines are close together the field is strong; where they spread apart the field is weak.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">You can reveal field lines by sprinkling iron filings around a bar magnet or by tracing the direction a plotting compass needle points as you move it around the magnet. Two unlike poles placed near each other produce field lines that curve between them (attraction). Two like poles produce lines that push away from each other (repulsion).</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Magnetic field lines always point from <strong>north to south</strong>. Closer lines mean a stronger field. The field is strongest at the poles.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Electromagnets</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">When an electric current flows through a wire, it produces a magnetic field around the wire. The field lines form concentric circles centred on the wire. If you increase the current, the field gets stronger. Reversing the current reverses the direction of the field.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Winding the wire into a coil called a <dfn class="term" data-def="A coil of wire that produces a magnetic field when an electric current flows through it.">solenoid</dfn> concentrates the magnetic field. Inside a solenoid, the field lines are parallel and evenly spaced &mdash; this is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A magnetic field in which the field lines are parallel and equally spaced, meaning the field strength is the same at every point.">uniform magnetic field</dfn>. The field pattern around a solenoid looks very similar to the field around a bar magnet, with a north pole at one end and a south pole at the other.</p>
<!-- DIAGRAM -->
<p data-narration-id="n16">Adding an <strong>iron core</strong> inside a solenoid creates an <dfn class="term" data-def="A solenoid with a soft iron core that behaves as a magnet only when an electric current flows through the coil.">electromagnet</dfn>. The iron core becomes an induced magnet and greatly increases the field strength. Three ways to make an electromagnet stronger are: increase the current, add more turns of wire, or use a softer iron core. When the current is switched off, the iron core loses its magnetism almost immediately &mdash; this is what makes electromagnets so useful.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n17">Electromagnets are used in <strong>scrapyard cranes</strong> (pick up and drop steel cars by switching the current on and off), <strong>circuit breakers</strong> (an excessive current magnetises a coil which trips a switch to break the circuit), <strong>electric bells</strong> (the electromagnet attracts an iron armature to strike the bell, then the circuit breaks and the process repeats), and <strong>relays</strong> (a small current in one circuit activates an electromagnet that closes a switch in a separate, higher-power circuit).</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n18">The Motor Effect</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">When a conductor carrying a current is placed inside a magnetic field, it experiences a force. This is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The force experienced by a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field.">motor effect</dfn>. The force acts at right angles to both the direction of the current and the direction of the magnetic field. If either the current or the magnetic field is reversed, the direction of the force reverses too.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">The size of the force depends on three factors: the <strong>magnetic flux density</strong> (how strong the field is, measured in tesla, T), the <strong>current</strong> through the conductor (in amps, A), and the <strong>length</strong> of the conductor inside the field (in metres, m). The equation is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n21">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p><strong>Force = magnetic flux density &times; current &times; length</strong><br>F = B &times; I &times; l<br>where F is in newtons (N), B is in tesla (T), I is in amps (A), and l is in metres (m).</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Fleming&rsquo;s Left-Hand Rule</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><dfn class="term" data-def="A method for finding the direction of force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field using the thumb, first finger, and second finger of the left hand.">Fleming&rsquo;s left-hand rule</dfn> tells you the direction of the force (and therefore the direction of motion). Hold your left hand with the thumb, first finger, and second finger all at right angles to each other:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>First finger</strong> &mdash; direction of the magnetic <strong>F</strong>ield (north to south). <strong>Second finger</strong> &mdash; direction of the <strong>C</strong>urrent (positive to negative). <strong>Thumb</strong> &mdash; direction of <strong>M</strong>otion (the force on the conductor).</p>

Step 3: Point your left hand&rsquo;s <strong>first finger</strong> in the field direction and your <strong>second finger</strong> in the current direction. <h2 data-narration-id="n26">Electric Motors</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A device that converts electrical energy into rotational kinetic energy using the motor effect.">electric motor</dfn> uses the motor effect to produce continuous rotation. A rectangular coil of wire sits between the poles of two permanent magnets. When current flows through the coil, the two sides of the coil experience forces in opposite directions (one side is pushed up, the other is pushed down), creating a turning effect.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">The coil would stop after half a turn because the forces would then act to push it back the other way. To prevent this, a <dfn class="term" data-def="A device in a DC motor that reverses the direction of current through the coil every half turn so the coil continues to spin in the same direction.">split-ring commutator</dfn> reverses the direction of the current every half turn. This means the forces always push the same side of the coil in the same direction, keeping it spinning continuously. Carbon brushes maintain electrical contact with the spinning commutator.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Three ways to make an electric motor spin faster: increase the <strong>current</strong>, use a <strong>stronger magnet</strong> (greater magnetic flux density), or add <strong>more turns</strong> of wire to the coil.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n30">The key components are: <strong>a rectangular coil</strong> of insulated wire mounted on an axle so it can rotate, <strong>two permanent magnets</strong> with opposite poles facing each other to create a uniform field, a <strong>split-ring commutator</strong> attached to the coil that reverses the current direction every half turn, and <strong>carbon brushes</strong> that press against the commutator to connect the coil to the power supply. The coil rotates because the two sides experience forces in opposite directions due to the motor effect.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n31">For calculation questions using F&nbsp;=&nbsp;B&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;I&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;l, always show your working: write out the formula, substitute values with units, and calculate. If you need to rearrange, use the formula triangle. For &ldquo;Explain&rdquo; questions about the motor effect or electric motors, state the physics rule first (e.g. &ldquo;a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force&rdquo;), then explain each step of the process in order. When asked about Fleming&rsquo;s left-hand rule, always state which finger represents which quantity &mdash; field, current, and motion.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n32">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n33">Permanent magnets produce their own field; induced magnets only become magnetic inside an external field and are always attracted to the permanent magnet.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n34">An electromagnet is a solenoid with an iron core &mdash; its strength depends on current, number of turns, and the core material. It can be switched on and off.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n35">The motor effect (F = B &times; I &times; l) describes the force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field, and Fleming&rsquo;s left-hand rule gives the direction of that force. A split-ring commutator keeps a DC motor spinning continuously.</li></ul>
