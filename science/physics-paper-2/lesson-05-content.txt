# Stopping Distances, Braking and Momentum

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Stopping Distance</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Every time a driver spots a hazard and brings a vehicle to a halt, the car travels a certain distance before it stops completely. This total distance is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The total distance a vehicle travels from the moment a hazard is seen to the moment the vehicle comes to a complete stop.">stopping distance</dfn>. It is made up of two parts: the thinking distance and the braking distance.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Stopping distance = thinking distance + braking distance. You must learn this equation &mdash; it appears frequently in AQA exams.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n4">Thinking Distance</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5"><dfn class="term" data-def="The distance a vehicle travels during the driver&rsquo;s reaction time, before the brakes are applied.">Thinking distance</dfn> is the distance the car travels during the driver&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="The time between a stimulus being detected and a response being made.">reaction time</dfn> &mdash; the time between seeing the hazard and pressing the brake pedal. A typical human reaction time is between 0.2 and 0.9 seconds.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Several factors can increase thinking distance by increasing reaction time:</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Factors That Increase Thinking Distance</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Tiredness</strong> &mdash; a tired driver reacts more slowly because the nervous system processes signals less efficiently.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Alcohol and drugs</strong> &mdash; these substances slow down the transmission of nerve impulses, increasing reaction time significantly.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Distractions</strong> &mdash; using a mobile phone or adjusting the radio diverts attention, delaying the moment the driver notices a hazard.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Speed</strong> &mdash; thinking distance is directly proportional to speed. At higher speeds, the car covers more ground during the same reaction time.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Braking Distance</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><dfn class="term" data-def="The distance a vehicle travels from the moment the brakes are applied to the moment it comes to a complete stop.">Braking distance</dfn> is the distance the vehicle travels after the brakes are applied until it comes to rest. When the brakes are pressed, a <dfn class="term" data-def="The force applied by the braking system to slow a vehicle down, which causes work to be done against kinetic energy.">braking force</dfn> acts on the wheels, doing work to transfer kinetic energy into heat energy in the brakes.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Factors That Increase Braking Distance</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Wet or icy roads</strong> &mdash; reduced friction between the tyres and the road surface means less braking force can be applied.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Worn tyres or brakes</strong> &mdash; worn <dfn class="term" data-def="The pattern of grooves cut into a tyre surface, designed to channel water away and maintain grip on the road.">tread</dfn> on tyres reduces grip. Worn brake pads reduce the braking force.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Higher speed</strong> &mdash; a faster vehicle has more kinetic energy, so more work must be done by the braking force to bring it to rest.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Greater mass</strong> &mdash; a heavier vehicle has more kinetic energy at the same speed, so the braking distance increases.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>When a vehicle brakes, the brakes do work to transfer kinetic energy to thermal energy in the brakes. In an emergency stop at high speed, a very large braking force can cause the brakes to overheat and the vehicle to skid.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Measuring Reaction Time</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Reaction time can be measured using a simple ruler-drop experiment. One person holds a ruler vertically and drops it without warning, while the other person catches it as quickly as possible. The distance the ruler falls before being caught is used to calculate the reaction time using the equation for acceleration due to gravity.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">In the exam, you may be asked to describe this required practical and explain how to improve accuracy &mdash; for example, by repeating the test and calculating a mean, or by making sure the catcher&rsquo;s hand is level with the zero mark at the start.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Momentum</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><dfn class="term" data-def="A property of a moving object equal to its mass multiplied by its velocity; it is a vector quantity measured in kg m/s.">Momentum</dfn> is a property of every moving object. It depends on both the mass and the velocity of the object. The equation is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>p = m &times; v &mdash; where p is momentum (kg m/s), m is mass (kg), and v is velocity (m/s). Momentum is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A quantity that has both magnitude and direction.">vector</dfn> quantity, so its direction matters.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n24">For example, a car of mass 1200 kg travelling at 15 m/s has a momentum of 1200 &times; 15 = 18,000 kg m/s. If the same car were stationary, its momentum would be zero because its velocity is zero.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Conservation of Momentum</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">In a <dfn class="term" data-def="A system where no external forces act, so no energy or momentum is transferred in or out.">closed system</dfn>, the total momentum before an event is always equal to the total momentum after the event. This is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The principle that in a closed system the total momentum before a collision equals the total momentum after.">conservation of momentum</dfn>.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Worked Example: Conservation of Momentum</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n27">A 2 kg trolley moving at 3 m/s collides with a stationary 1 kg trolley. After the collision they stick together and move as one object. What is their velocity after the collision?</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Total momentum before = (2 &times; 3) + (1 &times; 0) = 6 kg m/s.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Step 2:</strong> Total momentum after = total mass &times; velocity = (2 + 1) &times; v = 3v.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Step 3:</strong> By conservation of momentum, 3v = 6, so v = 2 m/s.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Conservation of momentum applies to all collisions and explosions in a closed system. In an explosion, the total momentum before is zero (the objects are stationary), so the total momentum after must also be zero &mdash; the objects move apart with equal and opposite momenta.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Linking Momentum, Force and Braking</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n32">When a force acts on an object, it causes a change in momentum. The relationship is: force = change in momentum &divide; time taken, or F = &Delta;p &divide; &Delta;t. A larger braking force produces a greater rate of change of momentum, bringing the vehicle to a stop in a shorter time (and shorter distance).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33">This also explains why safety features such as crumple zones and airbags work. They increase the time over which the change in momentum happens, which reduces the force on the occupants and lowers the risk of injury.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n34">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Safety features like crumple zones, seat belts, and airbags increase the time over which momentum changes during a collision. By increasing the time, the force on passengers is reduced (F = &Delta;p &divide; &Delta;t).</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n35">Stopping distance questions often ask you to explain factors &mdash; always link each factor back to either thinking distance or braking distance, never just &ldquo;stopping distance&rdquo; on its own. For momentum calculations, show every step clearly: write the equation, substitute values, and give the unit (kg m/s). If asked to &ldquo;explain&rdquo; conservation of momentum, state that total momentum before equals total momentum after in a closed system, then prove it with numbers.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n36">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n37">Stopping distance = thinking distance + braking distance. Thinking distance depends on reaction time and speed; braking distance depends on speed, road conditions, tyre condition, and vehicle mass.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Momentum (p = mv) is a vector quantity measured in kg m/s. In a closed system, total momentum is always conserved.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">Safety features work by increasing the time over which momentum changes, reducing the force on passengers (F = &Delta;p &divide; &Delta;t).</li></ul>
