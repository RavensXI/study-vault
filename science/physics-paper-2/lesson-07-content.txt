# The Electromagnetic Spectrum

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Electromagnetic Waves?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="Transverse waves that transfer energy from one place to another and can travel through a vacuum at the speed of light.">Electromagnetic waves</dfn> (EM waves) are <dfn class="term" data-def="A wave in which the oscillations are perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction of energy transfer.">transverse waves</dfn> that transfer energy from one place to another. Unlike sound waves, they do not need a medium to travel through &mdash; they can pass through a vacuum (empty space). All electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed through a vacuum: 3 &times; 10<sup>8</sup>&thinsp;m/s, the <dfn class="term" data-def="The speed at which all electromagnetic waves travel through a vacuum: 300,000,000 m/s (3 &times; 10 to the power 8 m/s).">speed of light</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">EM waves are produced when charged particles oscillate (vibrate). They consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that are perpendicular to each other and to the direction the wave travels. The key equation linking wave speed, frequency, and wavelength is:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>wave speed = frequency &times; wavelength</strong> &nbsp;&nbsp;(v = f &times; &lambda;). For all EM waves in a vacuum, v = 3 &times; 10<sup>8</sup>&thinsp;m/s. This means waves with a higher frequency have a shorter wavelength, and waves with a lower frequency have a longer wavelength.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Electromagnetic Spectrum</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The continuous range of electromagnetic waves ordered by wavelength and frequency, from radio waves (longest wavelength) to gamma rays (shortest wavelength).">electromagnetic spectrum</dfn> is the entire range of EM waves, arranged in order of wavelength and frequency. There are seven main types, and you must learn them in order from longest wavelength to shortest:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Radio waves &rarr; Microwaves &rarr; Infrared &rarr; Visible light &rarr; Ultraviolet &rarr; X-rays &rarr; Gamma rays</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">As you move from radio waves to gamma rays, the <dfn class="term" data-def="The number of waves passing a point per second, measured in hertz (Hz).">frequency</dfn> increases and the <dfn class="term" data-def="The distance from one point on a wave to the same point on the next wave, measured in metres.">wavelength</dfn> decreases. The boundaries between different types are not sharp &mdash; the spectrum is continuous, and one type blends gradually into the next.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Radio Waves and Microwaves</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><dfn class="term" data-def="Electromagnetic waves with the longest wavelength and lowest frequency, used for television, radio broadcasts, and communication.">Radio waves</dfn> have the longest wavelength and lowest frequency of all EM waves. Wavelengths range from about 1&thinsp;mm to several kilometres. They are used for television and radio broadcasts, Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, and communication with satellites. Radio waves can diffract (bend) around hills and buildings, which is why you can receive radio signals even when you are not in direct line of sight of the transmitter.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><dfn class="term" data-def="Electromagnetic waves with wavelengths between about 1 mm and 30 cm, used in satellite communication, mobile phones, and microwave ovens.">Microwaves</dfn> have shorter wavelengths than radio waves (roughly 1&thinsp;mm to 30&thinsp;cm). They are used in satellite communications, mobile phone signals, and microwave ovens. In a microwave oven, the waves are absorbed by water molecules in food, causing them to vibrate and heat up. Microwaves can penetrate through the atmosphere and clouds, making them ideal for satellite transmissions.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Infrared Radiation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><dfn class="term" data-def="Electromagnetic waves between microwaves and visible light, emitted by all warm objects and used in heaters, thermal imaging, and remote controls.">Infrared radiation</dfn> (IR) is emitted by all objects that have a temperature above absolute zero. The hotter an object is, the more infrared it emits. This is the basis of <dfn class="term" data-def="A camera or device that detects infrared radiation to produce images showing heat patterns.">thermal imaging cameras</dfn>, which detect temperature differences and display them as colour maps &mdash; used by firefighters to find people in smoke-filled buildings, and by police helicopters to track suspects at night.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Other uses include electric heaters and toasters (which emit infrared to warm food and rooms), TV remote controls, optical fibre communication, and cooking. When infrared is absorbed by the skin, it is felt as warmth. Excessive exposure can cause skin burns.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Visible Light</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><dfn class="term" data-def="The narrow band of the electromagnetic spectrum that can be detected by the human eye, ranging from red (longest wavelength) to violet (shortest).">Visible light</dfn> is the only part of the EM spectrum that the human eye can detect. It occupies a very narrow band between infrared and ultraviolet. White light is made up of a continuous spectrum of colours &mdash; red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet &mdash; which can be separated using a prism. Red light has the longest wavelength within this band, and violet has the shortest.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Visible light is used for vision (obviously), photography, fibre optic communication, and illumination. Different surfaces absorb, reflect, or transmit different wavelengths, which is why objects appear as different colours. A red shirt, for example, reflects red wavelengths and absorbs most other colours.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Ultraviolet Radiation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><dfn class="term" data-def="Electromagnetic waves with shorter wavelengths than visible light, produced by the Sun and UV lamps, responsible for tanning, sunburn, and fluorescence.">Ultraviolet radiation</dfn> (UV) has a shorter wavelength and higher frequency than visible light. The Sun is the main natural source of UV. It is also produced by sun lamps and tanning beds. UV radiation causes the skin to produce vitamin D, which is essential for healthy bones.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">However, overexposure to UV can cause <strong>sunburn</strong>, <strong>premature skin ageing</strong>, and increases the risk of <strong>skin cancer</strong>. UV can also damage the eyes, potentially leading to cataracts. Protective measures include wearing sunscreen, sunglasses, and limiting time in direct sunlight. UV is used to detect forged banknotes (special inks fluoresce under UV), in sterilisation of water and medical equipment, and in energy-efficient fluorescent lamps.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>UV, X-rays, and gamma rays have enough energy to cause <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which radiation removes electrons from atoms, creating charged ions &mdash; this can damage living cells and DNA.">ionisation</dfn> &mdash; they can knock electrons out of atoms, creating ions. This is why they are called <strong>ionising radiation</strong>. Ionising radiation can damage cells and DNA, increasing the risk of cancer.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">X-rays</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><dfn class="term" data-def="High-frequency electromagnetic waves that can pass through soft tissue but are absorbed by dense materials like bone and metal.">X-rays</dfn> have very short wavelengths and very high frequencies. They can pass through soft body tissue but are absorbed by dense materials such as bone and metal. This property makes them invaluable in medicine for producing images of bones to check for fractures, and in airport security scanners to inspect luggage.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n25">Medical X-rays work because bone absorbs X-rays more than soft tissue. A photographic plate or digital detector is placed behind the patient, and an X-ray beam is passed through the body. Bones appear white on the image (they block the X-rays), while soft tissue appears darker (X-rays pass through). CT scans use X-rays taken from multiple angles to build a detailed 3D image of internal organs and structures.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26">Because X-rays are ionising, radiographers take precautions: they stand behind lead-lined screens, patients wear lead aprons to protect areas not being imaged, and exposure times are kept as short as possible. The benefits of diagnosis must outweigh the small risk of cell damage from the radiation.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Gamma Rays</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><dfn class="term" data-def="Electromagnetic waves with the shortest wavelength and highest frequency, originating from changes in the nucleus of an atom.">Gamma rays</dfn> have the shortest wavelength and highest frequency of all EM waves. They originate from changes in the nucleus of an atom &mdash; typically from radioactive decay. Gamma rays are the most penetrating type of EM radiation, passing through most materials including body tissue and thin metal sheets. Only thick lead or several metres of concrete can significantly reduce their intensity.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Uses of gamma rays include: sterilising medical instruments and food (killing bacteria without heat), treating cancer through <dfn class="term" data-def="A cancer treatment that uses targeted beams of gamma rays to destroy tumour cells.">radiotherapy</dfn> (focused beams destroy tumour cells), and as a medical tracer when a patient is injected with a gamma-emitting substance so doctors can track its path through the body. Because gamma rays are strongly ionising, overexposure can cause cell damage, mutations, radiation sickness, and cancer.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n30">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>All EM waves travel at the same speed in a vacuum (3 &times; 10<sup>8</sup>&thinsp;m/s), but they differ in wavelength and frequency. The higher the frequency, the more energy the wave carries &mdash; this is why gamma rays are more dangerous than radio waves.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n38">In AQA Physics Paper 2, you will often be asked to compare different EM waves or link a specific wave type to its uses and dangers. Always state whether the wave is ionising or non-ionising, name a specific use with a brief explanation of how it works, and identify the hazard. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions, make sure you connect the property (e.g. short wavelength, high penetration) to the use or danger &mdash; do not just list facts. For calculation questions using v = f &times; &lambda;, remember that all EM waves in a vacuum travel at 3 &times; 10<sup>8</sup>&thinsp;m/s.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n39">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n40">All EM waves are transverse, travel at 3 &times; 10<sup>8</sup>&thinsp;m/s in a vacuum, and differ only in wavelength and frequency. The spectrum runs: radio &rarr; microwave &rarr; infrared &rarr; visible &rarr; ultraviolet &rarr; X-ray &rarr; gamma.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Each type has specific uses linked to its properties &mdash; e.g. X-rays penetrate soft tissue (medical imaging), infrared is emitted by warm objects (thermal cameras), radio waves diffract around obstacles (broadcasting).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42">UV, X-rays, and gamma rays are ionising &mdash; they can damage cells and DNA, increasing cancer risk. Know the precautions for each.</li></ul>
