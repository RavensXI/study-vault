# Waves: Properties and Behaviour

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Waves?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter.">wave</dfn> is a disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter. When you drop a stone into a pond, ripples spread outward &mdash; the water itself does not travel across the pond, but the energy does. This is the key idea behind all waves: energy is transferred, but the particles of the medium return to their original position after the wave passes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Waves can travel through a medium (such as water, air, or a solid) or, in the case of electromagnetic waves, through a vacuum. In this lesson we focus on <dfn class="term" data-def="A physical substance through which a wave can travel, such as air, water, or a solid.">mechanical waves</dfn> &mdash; waves that require a medium to travel through.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Waves transfer energy without transferring matter. The particles of the medium oscillate (vibrate) about a fixed point but do not travel with the wave.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Transverse Waves</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">In a <dfn class="term" data-def="A wave in which the oscillations of the particles are perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction the wave travels.">transverse wave</dfn>, the oscillations of the particles are perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction in which the wave travels. Think of a rope: if you flick one end up and down, the wave moves horizontally along the rope, but each point on the rope moves up and down.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The highest point of a transverse wave is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The highest point on a transverse wave, above the rest position.">crest</dfn> (or peak), and the lowest point is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The lowest point on a transverse wave, below the rest position.">trough</dfn>. Examples of transverse waves include water surface waves, light waves, and all other electromagnetic waves. S-waves produced by earthquakes are also transverse.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Longitudinal Waves</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">In a <dfn class="term" data-def="A wave in which the oscillations of the particles are parallel to the direction the wave travels, producing compressions and rarefactions.">longitudinal wave</dfn>, the oscillations of the particles are parallel to the direction in which the wave travels. A slinky spring demonstrates this well: if you push and pull one end back and forth, regions where the coils bunch together (<dfn class="term" data-def="A region in a longitudinal wave where particles are pushed close together.">compressions</dfn>) and regions where they spread apart (<dfn class="term" data-def="A region in a longitudinal wave where particles are spread further apart.">rarefactions</dfn>) move along the spring.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">Sound is the most common example of a longitudinal wave. When a speaker vibrates, it pushes air molecules together and pulls them apart, creating compressions and rarefactions that travel through the air to your ear. P-waves from earthquakes are also longitudinal.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n11">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Transverse:</strong> oscillations perpendicular to direction of travel (e.g. light, water surface waves). <strong>Longitudinal:</strong> oscillations parallel to direction of travel (e.g. sound). Both types transfer energy, not matter.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Wave Properties</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">All waves &mdash; whether transverse or longitudinal &mdash; can be described using four key properties:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Amplitude</strong> &mdash; the <dfn class="term" data-def="The maximum displacement of a point on a wave from its undisturbed (rest) position, measured in metres.">amplitude</dfn> is the maximum displacement of a point on the wave from its undisturbed (rest) position. For a transverse wave, it is the distance from the rest position to the top of a crest (or bottom of a trough). A larger amplitude means the wave carries more energy. Amplitude is measured in metres (m).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Wavelength</strong> &mdash; the <dfn class="term" data-def="The distance from one point on a wave to the equivalent point on the next wave, such as crest to crest, measured in metres.">wavelength</dfn> (&lambda;, the Greek letter lambda) is the distance from one point on a wave to the equivalent point on the next wave &mdash; for example, from crest to crest or trough to trough. Wavelength is measured in metres (m).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Frequency</strong> &mdash; the <dfn class="term" data-def="The number of complete waves passing a fixed point per second, measured in hertz (Hz).">frequency</dfn> (f) is the number of complete waves that pass a fixed point each second. It is measured in <dfn class="term" data-def="The unit of frequency, equal to one wave per second. Symbol: Hz.">hertz</dfn> (Hz). A frequency of 100 Hz means 100 complete waves pass every second.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Period</strong> &mdash; the <dfn class="term" data-def="The time taken for one complete wave to pass a fixed point, measured in seconds. Period = 1 &divide; frequency.">period</dfn> (T) is the time taken for one complete wave to pass a point. It is the inverse of frequency: T = 1 &divide; f. If a wave has a frequency of 50 Hz, its period is 1 &divide; 50 = 0.02 seconds.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Wave Speed and the Wave Equation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><dfn class="term" data-def="The speed at which a wave front travels through a medium, measured in metres per second (m/s).">Wave speed</dfn> (v) is the speed at which the wave front moves through the medium. It is measured in metres per second (m/s). Wave speed, frequency, and wavelength are linked by a simple equation that you must learn for the exam:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Wave equation:</strong> wave speed = frequency &times; wavelength, or <strong>v = f &times; &lambda;</strong>. Units: v in m/s, f in Hz, &lambda; in m. You must be able to rearrange this to find any of the three quantities.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n24">To rearrange: <strong>f = v &divide; &lambda;</strong> and <strong>&lambda; = v &divide; f</strong>. Always check your units before substituting &mdash; convert centimetres to metres (divide by 100) and kilometres to metres (multiply by 1000).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Measuring Waves: The Ripple Tank</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A shallow tray of water used to study wave behaviour by observing the pattern of ripples created by a vibrating dipper.">ripple tank</dfn> is a piece of apparatus used in required practical work to investigate the properties of waves in water. A motor-driven dipper creates regular waves on the surface of shallow water. A lamp above the tank projects the wave pattern onto a white card below, making the crests and troughs visible as bright and dark lines.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">To measure the wavelength, you can use a ruler to measure the distance across several wave crests and then divide by the number of waves. For example, if 10 crests span 30 cm, the wavelength is 30 &divide; 10 = 3 cm (0.03 m). Measuring multiple wavelengths and dividing gives a more accurate result than measuring a single wave.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">To measure frequency, count the number of waves passing a fixed point in a known time using a stopwatch. For example, if 20 waves pass in 10 seconds, the frequency is 20 &divide; 10 = 2 Hz. Once you know the wavelength and frequency, you can calculate the wave speed using v = f &times; &lambda;.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n34">Common Exam Pitfalls</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n35">Students often confuse amplitude with wavelength. Remember: <strong>amplitude</strong> is measured vertically (rest position to crest), while <strong>wavelength</strong> is measured horizontally (crest to crest). Another common error is forgetting to convert units &mdash; wavelengths given in cm or nm must be converted to metres before using the wave equation. Finally, remember that increasing the amplitude does <strong>not</strong> change the frequency or wavelength; it only changes the energy carried by the wave.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n36">In &ldquo;calculate&rdquo; questions on waves, always write out the equation first (v = f &times; &lambda;), show your substitution with correct units, then give the answer with the right unit. Examiners award marks for each step. When asked to &ldquo;compare&rdquo; transverse and longitudinal waves, structure your answer with one similarity and one difference &mdash; and always refer to the direction of oscillation relative to the direction of energy transfer.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n37">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n38">Waves transfer energy without transferring matter. Transverse waves oscillate perpendicular to the direction of travel (e.g. light); longitudinal waves oscillate parallel (e.g. sound).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">The four key wave properties are amplitude, wavelength, frequency, and period. Amplitude is vertical (rest to crest); wavelength is horizontal (crest to crest).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40">The wave equation v = f &times; &lambda; links wave speed, frequency, and wavelength &mdash; learn it, practise rearranging it, and always convert units to SI before calculating.</li></ul>
