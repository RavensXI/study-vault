# Newton's Laws of Motion

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Newton&rsquo;s First Law &mdash; The Law of Inertia</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">An object at rest will stay at rest, and an object in motion will continue moving at a constant velocity, unless acted upon by a <dfn class="term" data-def="The single overall force acting on an object when all individual forces are combined.">resultant force</dfn>. This is Newton&rsquo;s first law of motion. It tells us that forces are not needed to keep something moving &mdash; they are only needed to change the way it moves.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">If the resultant force on an object is zero, the object is in <dfn class="term" data-def="A state where all forces acting on an object are balanced, so there is no change in motion.">equilibrium</dfn>. A book resting on a table has weight pulling it down and a normal contact force pushing it up. These two forces are equal and opposite, so the resultant force is zero and the book stays still.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Newton&rsquo;s first law: If the resultant force on an object is zero, it remains stationary or continues to move at a constant velocity in a straight line. No resultant force means no change in motion.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">A moving object also obeys this law. A spacecraft in deep space, far from any gravitational pull, will keep travelling at the same speed in the same direction forever because there is no resultant force to slow it down or change its direction. On Earth, friction and air resistance act as forces that slow things down, which is why objects eventually stop &mdash; not because they &ldquo;run out&rdquo; of motion.</p>

The more massive an object is, the greater its inertia. A heavy shopping trolley is harder to start moving and harder to stop than an empty one &mdash; it has more inertia because it has more mass.</p>

It can be found using the equation m&nbsp;=&nbsp;F&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;a. A larger inertial mass means a greater force is needed to produce the same acceleration. This is a Higher Tier concept that links Newton&rsquo;s first and second laws together.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Newton&rsquo;s Second Law &mdash; Force, Mass and Acceleration</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Newton&rsquo;s second law states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the resultant force acting on it, and inversely proportional to its mass. The equation is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>F = m &times; a &mdash; Resultant force (N) = mass (kg) &times; acceleration (m/s&sup2;). This equation is on the AQA Physics equation sheet, but you must be able to rearrange it confidently.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n11">This law explains why a greater force causes a greater acceleration, and why a heavier object accelerates less for the same force. If you push an empty shopping trolley and a full one with the same force, the empty trolley accelerates more because it has less mass.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">To rearrange the equation: a&nbsp;=&nbsp;F&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;m (to find acceleration) or m&nbsp;=&nbsp;F&nbsp;&divide;&nbsp;a (to find mass). Always check your units &mdash; force in newtons (N), mass in kilograms (kg), and acceleration in metres per second squared (m/s&sup2;).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Weight, Mass and Gravitational Field Strength</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Weight is the force acting on an object due to gravity. It is calculated using W&nbsp;=&nbsp;m&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;g, where W is weight in newtons (N), m is mass in kilograms (kg), and g is the <dfn class="term" data-def="The force of gravity per unit mass at a point in a gravitational field, measured in N/kg. On Earth, g is approximately 9.8 N/kg.">gravitational field strength</dfn> in N/kg. On Earth, g&nbsp;=&nbsp;9.8&nbsp;N/kg.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Mass is a scalar quantity &mdash; it stays the same wherever you are. Weight is a vector quantity &mdash; it changes depending on the gravitational field strength. An astronaut on the Moon has the same mass as on Earth, but their weight is about one-sixth because the Moon&rsquo;s gravitational field strength is lower.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Terminal Velocity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">When an object falls through a fluid (such as air or water), it initially accelerates because the force of gravity (weight) is greater than the <dfn class="term" data-def="A force that opposes the motion of an object through a fluid, such as air resistance or water resistance.">drag force</dfn>. As the object speeds up, the drag force increases.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Eventually, the drag force becomes equal to the weight. The resultant force is now zero, so the object stops accelerating and falls at a constant speed. This maximum constant speed is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The constant speed reached by a falling object when the drag force equals its weight, so the resultant force is zero.">terminal velocity</dfn>.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>At terminal velocity, the resultant force on the object is zero. Weight acts downwards and drag acts upwards &mdash; they are balanced. The object has zero acceleration but is still moving.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n24">A skydiver initially accelerates at about 9.8&nbsp;m/s&sup2; (free fall). As their speed increases, air resistance builds. Terminal velocity is reached after about 10&ndash;15 seconds of freefall. When they open a parachute, the surface area increases dramatically, drag force exceeds weight, and they decelerate until a new, much lower terminal velocity is reached &mdash; slow enough to land safely.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Newton&rsquo;s Third Law &mdash; Action and Reaction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Newton&rsquo;s third law states: whenever two objects interact, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other. These are called <dfn class="term" data-def="A pair of forces that are equal in size, opposite in direction, and act on two different objects as described by Newton's third law.">action-reaction pairs</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">When you push against a wall, the wall pushes back on you with the same force. When a swimmer pushes water backwards with their hands, the water pushes the swimmer forwards. The two forces in a pair always act on different objects, are the same type of force, and are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.</p>

A book on a table has weight acting downwards and a normal contact force acting upwards &mdash; these are <strong>not</strong> a third law pair because they both act on the same object (the book). A true third law pair involves two different objects: the book pulls the Earth towards it (gravitational attraction), and the Earth pulls the book downward (weight). These act on different objects and are the same type of force (gravitational).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29">Key test for a third law pair: the two forces must be the same type, equal in size, opposite in direction, and act on two different objects.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n33">In calculation questions using F&nbsp;=&nbsp;m&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;a, always show your working clearly: write the equation, substitute the values with units, then calculate. If the question asks you to &ldquo;explain&rdquo; Newton&rsquo;s third law, make sure you identify both objects, state the forces are equal in size and opposite in direction, and emphasise they act on <em>different</em> objects. A common mistake is describing balanced forces on one object instead of an action-reaction pair on two objects.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n34">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n35">Newton&rsquo;s first law: an object remains at rest or at constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant force. Inertia is an object&rsquo;s resistance to change in motion and depends on mass.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n36">Newton&rsquo;s second law: F&nbsp;=&nbsp;m&nbsp;&times;&nbsp;a. Greater force means greater acceleration; greater mass means less acceleration for the same force. At terminal velocity, the resultant force is zero.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n37">Newton&rsquo;s third law: every action has an equal and opposite reaction. The two forces in a pair always act on different objects and are the same type of force.</li>
</ul>
