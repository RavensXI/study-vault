# Work Done, Elasticity and Hooke's Law

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Work Done</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">When a force causes an object to move through a distance, <dfn class="term" data-def="The energy transferred when a force moves an object through a distance, measured in joules (J).">work is done</dfn> on the object. Work done is simply another way of saying energy has been transferred. The force must act in the direction of movement (or have a component in that direction) for work to be done.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Work done (J) = force (N) &times; distance (m), or <strong>W = Fs</strong>. One joule of work is done when a force of one newton moves an object through a distance of one metre.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n4">For example, if a person pushes a box with a force of 50 N over a distance of 3 m, the work done is 50 &times; 3 = 150 J. This 150 J of energy has been transferred from the person&rsquo;s chemical energy store to the kinetic energy store of the box (and some to thermal energy through friction).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Work done against <dfn class="term" data-def="A contact force that opposes the motion of an object moving through or over a surface.">friction</dfn> causes a rise in the temperature of the object and its surroundings. This is because kinetic energy is being transferred to the thermal energy stores of the surfaces in contact. You can feel this by rubbing your hands together quickly &mdash; the friction between your palms transfers energy to heat.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">Elastic and Inelastic Deformation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">When a force is applied to an object and it changes shape, we say the object has been <dfn class="term" data-def="A change in the shape of an object caused by an applied force.">deformed</dfn>. There are two types of deformation that you need to know:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Elastic deformation</strong> occurs when an object returns to its original shape and length after the force is removed. A stretched spring that bounces back to its normal length is an example of <dfn class="term" data-def="Deformation where the object returns to its original shape when the force is removed.">elastic deformation</dfn>. The energy stored during elastic deformation is recoverable.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Inelastic deformation</strong> occurs when an object does <em>not</em> return to its original shape after the force is removed. It has been permanently deformed. A crushed drinks can is an example of <dfn class="term" data-def="Deformation where the object does not return to its original shape when the force is removed.">inelastic deformation</dfn>. The energy transferred during inelastic deformation is not fully recoverable &mdash; some is dissipated as heat.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p><strong>Elastic deformation</strong> &mdash; the object returns to its original shape (energy is recoverable). <strong>Inelastic deformation</strong> &mdash; the object is permanently changed (energy is not fully recoverable). A spring can undergo either type depending on how far it is stretched.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Hooke&rsquo;s Law and the Spring Constant</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="The extension of an elastic object is directly proportional to the force applied, provided the limit of proportionality is not exceeded.">Hooke&rsquo;s law</dfn> states that the extension of an elastic object (such as a spring) is directly proportional to the force applied to it, as long as the <dfn class="term" data-def="The point beyond which the extension of a spring is no longer directly proportional to the applied force.">limit of proportionality</dfn> is not exceeded. This means that doubling the force doubles the extension.</p>
<!-- DIAGRAM -->
<p data-narration-id="n16">The equation for Hooke&rsquo;s law is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Force (N) = spring constant (N/m) &times; extension (m), or <strong>F = ke</strong>. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of the stiffness of a spring, in newtons per metre (N/m). A higher value means a stiffer spring.">spring constant</dfn> (k) tells you how stiff the spring is &mdash; a higher spring constant means a stiffer spring that requires more force to extend it by a given amount.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n18">For example, if a spring has a spring constant of 25 N/m and is extended by 0.2 m, the force required is F = 25 &times; 0.2 = 5 N. The spring constant is measured in newtons per metre (N/m) and the <dfn class="term" data-def="The increase in length of a spring or elastic object beyond its natural (unstretched) length.">extension</dfn> must be measured in metres for the equation to work correctly.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n19">In this practical, you investigate the relationship between force and extension for a spring. Set up a clamp stand with a spring hanging from a clamp, and a ruler positioned vertically beside it. Measure the natural (unloaded) length of the spring. Then add known weights (e.g. 1 N at a time) and measure the new length each time. The extension equals the new length minus the original length.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Plot a graph of force (y-axis) against extension (x-axis). The graph should show a straight line through the origin up to the limit of proportionality, confirming Hooke&rsquo;s law. The gradient of the straight-line section gives the spring constant (k). Beyond the limit of proportionality, the line curves &mdash; the spring no longer obeys Hooke&rsquo;s law and may be permanently deformed.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Force&ndash;Extension Graphs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">A force&ndash;extension graph for a spring that obeys Hooke&rsquo;s law shows a straight line through the origin. The gradient of this straight line is equal to the spring constant (k). Beyond the limit of proportionality, the graph curves upwards &mdash; the spring extends more for each additional unit of force, and it will not return to its original length when the force is removed.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The maximum force (or extension) beyond which a material will not return to its original length; it is permanently deformed.">elastic limit</dfn> is closely related to the limit of proportionality. Beyond the elastic limit, the spring undergoes inelastic (permanent) deformation. In many exam questions, the limit of proportionality and elastic limit are treated as essentially the same point on the graph.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n24">When you see a force&ndash;extension graph in the exam: the <strong>straight-line section</strong> shows the region where Hooke&rsquo;s law applies (force is proportional to extension). The <strong>point where the line begins to curve</strong> is the limit of proportionality. If asked to find the spring constant from a graph, pick any point on the straight section and divide force by extension: k = F &divide; e. Remember that the area under a force&ndash;extension graph represents the energy stored in the spring (elastic potential energy).</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Elastic Potential Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">When a spring is stretched or compressed within its limit of proportionality, energy is stored in its <dfn class="term" data-def="The energy stored in a stretched or compressed elastic object, calculated using E = &frac12;ke&sup2;.">elastic potential energy</dfn> store. This energy can be recovered when the spring is released &mdash; for example, a catapult transfers elastic potential energy into kinetic energy when it fires a stone.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n27">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Elastic potential energy (J) = &frac12; &times; spring constant (N/m) &times; extension&sup2; (m&sup2;), or <strong>E<sub>e</sub> = &frac12;ke&sup2;</strong>. This equation is given on the equation sheet in the exam, but you must be able to use and rearrange it.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n28">For example, a spring with k = 25 N/m extended by 0.2 m stores: E<sub>e</sub> = &frac12; &times; 25 &times; 0.2&sup2; = &frac12; &times; 25 &times; 0.04 = 0.5 J. Notice that because extension is squared, doubling the extension quadruples the energy stored. This equation only applies when the spring has not been stretched beyond its limit of proportionality.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n29">In &ldquo;calculate&rdquo; questions, always write the equation first, then substitute values, then calculate &mdash; you earn marks for each step even if your final answer is wrong. Watch the units: extension must be in metres (not cm) when using F = ke or E<sub>e</sub> = &frac12;ke&sup2;. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about elastic vs inelastic deformation, make sure you state what happens to the shape AND what happens to the energy. In graph questions, practise identifying the limit of proportionality and calculating the spring constant from the gradient.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n30">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n31">Work done (W = Fs) measures the energy transferred when a force moves an object. Work done against friction causes a temperature rise.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Elastic deformation means the object returns to its original shape; inelastic deformation means it is permanently changed. Hooke&rsquo;s law (F = ke) applies up to the limit of proportionality.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">Elastic potential energy (E<sub>e</sub> = &frac12;ke&sup2;) is stored in a stretched or compressed spring. The energy stored is proportional to the square of the extension.</li></ul>
