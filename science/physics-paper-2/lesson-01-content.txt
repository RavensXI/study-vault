# Forces, Gravity and Resultant Forces

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Scalars and Vectors</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In physics, every quantity you measure falls into one of two categories. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A quantity that has magnitude (size) only, with no direction &mdash; for example, mass, temperature, time, speed, and distance.">scalar</dfn> quantity has magnitude (size) only. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A quantity that has both magnitude and direction &mdash; for example, force, velocity, displacement, and acceleration.">vector</dfn> quantity has both magnitude and direction. Getting this distinction right is essential for the exam &mdash; AQA regularly tests whether students can classify quantities correctly.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><strong>Scalar examples:</strong> mass (measured in kg), temperature (&deg;C), time (s), speed (m/s), distance (m), and energy (J). Notice that speed tells you how fast something is going but not which way. <strong>Vector examples:</strong> force (N), velocity (m/s in a stated direction), displacement (m in a stated direction), and acceleration (m/s&sup2; in a stated direction). Velocity is the vector version of speed &mdash; it includes direction.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Scalar = magnitude only.</strong> <strong>Vector = magnitude + direction.</strong> A common exam mistake is confusing speed (scalar) with velocity (vector), or distance (scalar) with displacement (vector). Always check whether the question asks for a scalar or vector answer.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">What Is a Force?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A push or pull acting on an object, measured in newtons (N). Forces are vectors &mdash; they have both size and direction.">force</dfn> is a push or a pull that acts on an object. Forces are measured in newtons (N) using a Newton meter. Because forces have both magnitude and direction, they are vector quantities. Force arrows are used in diagrams to show the size and direction of a force &mdash; the longer the arrow, the bigger the force, and the arrow points in the direction the force acts.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Forces can change an object&rsquo;s speed, direction of travel, or shape. A force does not always cause movement &mdash; it can also keep an object stationary by balancing another force. For GCSE, you need to understand two main categories of force: contact forces and non-contact forces.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Contact and Non-Contact Forces</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A force that acts between objects that are physically touching &mdash; examples include friction, air resistance, tension, normal contact force, and upthrust.">contact force</dfn> acts when two objects are physically touching. Examples include friction (which opposes sliding motion between surfaces), air resistance (which slows objects moving through air), tension (the pulling force in a stretched rope or cable), the normal contact force (the support force a surface pushes back with), and <dfn class="term" data-def="The upward force exerted by a fluid on an object that is submerged or floating in it.">upthrust</dfn> (the upward force from a fluid on a submerged or floating object).</p>

Friction generates thermal energy &mdash; rub your hands together and they warm up. <strong>Air resistance:</strong> A skydiver falling through the air experiences air resistance pushing upwards against their motion. <strong>Normal contact force:</strong> A book resting on a table is pushed upward by the table &mdash; this support force acts at right angles to the surface. <strong>Tension:</strong> When you pull a dog on a lead, the lead is under tension. <strong>Upthrust:</strong> A boat floats because the upthrust from the water equals its weight.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n11">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A force that acts between objects that are not touching &mdash; examples include gravitational force, electrostatic force, and magnetic force.">non-contact force</dfn> acts at a distance &mdash; the objects do not need to be touching. The three non-contact forces you need to know are gravitational force (the attraction between any two masses), electrostatic force (the attraction or repulsion between electric charges), and magnetic force (the attraction or repulsion between magnets or magnetic materials).</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Contact forces</strong> require objects to touch: friction, air resistance, tension, normal contact force, upthrust. <strong>Non-contact forces</strong> act at a distance: gravitational, electrostatic, magnetic. The exam may give you a scenario and ask you to identify which type of force is acting.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Gravity and Gravitational Fields</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Gravity is a non-contact force of attraction between any two objects that have mass. The more mass an object has, the stronger its gravitational pull. The Earth has a large mass, so it exerts a significant gravitational force on everything near it &mdash; this is what keeps us on the ground, pulls objects downward when dropped, and holds the Moon in orbit.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">A <dfn class="term" data-def="The region around a mass where another mass would experience a gravitational force. The gravitational field strength (g) on Earth&rsquo;s surface is approximately 9.8 N/kg.">gravitational field</dfn> is the region around a mass where another mass would experience a gravitational force. The strength of this field is measured by the <dfn class="term" data-def="The force of gravity per kilogram of mass, measured in N/kg. On Earth&rsquo;s surface, g is approximately 9.8 N/kg.">gravitational field strength</dfn> (symbol: g), measured in newtons per kilogram (N/kg). On the surface of the Earth, g is approximately 9.8 N/kg. This means every kilogram of mass experiences a gravitational force of 9.8 N pulling it downward.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Gravitational field strength varies depending on the mass of the object creating the field and the distance from it. On the Moon, g is only about 1.6 N/kg (roughly one-sixth of Earth&rsquo;s value), so objects weigh much less on the Moon even though their mass stays the same.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Weight, Mass, and the Equation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of matter in an object, measured in kilograms (kg). Mass does not change with location.">Mass</dfn> is the amount of matter in an object. It is measured in kilograms (kg) and does not change wherever you go &mdash; your mass on Earth is the same as your mass on the Moon or in deep space.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><dfn class="term" data-def="The force acting on an object due to gravity, measured in newtons (N). Weight depends on mass and gravitational field strength: W = m &times; g.">Weight</dfn> is the force acting on an object due to gravity. It is measured in newtons (N) and depends on where you are. Weight is calculated using the equation:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Weight = mass &times; gravitational field strength</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>W = m &times; g</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">where W is weight in newtons (N), m is mass in kilograms (kg), and g is gravitational field strength in N/kg. For example, a person with a mass of 70 kg on Earth weighs: W = 70 &times; 9.8 = 686 N. The same person on the Moon would weigh: W = 70 &times; 1.6 = 112 N. Their mass stays at 70 kg in both cases.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>W = m &times; g.</strong> Weight is measured in newtons (N), mass in kilograms (kg), and gravitational field strength in N/kg. On Earth, g &asymp; 9.8 N/kg. Weight changes with location; mass does not. Weight acts downward toward the centre of the Earth and is measured using a calibrated spring balance (Newton meter).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Resultant Forces</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Most objects have more than one force acting on them at the same time. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A single force that has the same effect as all the individual forces acting on an object combined. It is found by adding forces in the same direction and subtracting forces in opposite directions.">resultant force</dfn> is a single force that has the same effect as all the individual forces acting on an object combined. To find the resultant force when forces act along a straight line, add forces acting in the same direction and subtract forces acting in opposite directions.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Example 1:</strong> A car has an engine force of 3000 N driving it forward and air resistance of 1200 N acting backward. The resultant force = 3000 &minus; 1200 = 1800 N forward. The car accelerates. <strong>Example 2:</strong> A book sits on a table. Its weight pushes down at 5 N and the normal contact force pushes up at 5 N. The resultant force = 5 &minus; 5 = 0 N. The book is stationary (or moving at constant speed).</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Resultant force = 0 (balanced forces):</strong> The object is either stationary or moving at a constant speed in a straight line. It does NOT necessarily mean the object is still &mdash; an aircraft flying at constant speed has balanced forces (thrust equals drag, lift equals weight). <strong>Resultant force &ne; 0 (unbalanced forces):</strong> The object accelerates in the direction of the resultant force. It could speed up, slow down, or change direction. <strong>Key point:</strong> A moving object does not need a force to keep moving at constant speed &mdash; it only needs a force to change its speed or direction.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n29">Free Body Diagrams</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n30">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A diagram showing all the forces acting on a single object, represented as labelled arrows pointing in the direction each force acts. The length of each arrow represents the size of the force.">free body diagram</dfn> is a simple sketch showing all the forces acting on a single object. Each force is drawn as a labelled arrow pointing in the direction it acts. The length of the arrow represents the relative size of the force &mdash; a bigger force gets a longer arrow.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">To draw a free body diagram: (1) identify the object you are analysing, (2) draw it as a simple shape (usually a box or dot), (3) identify every force acting on the object, (4) draw each force as a labelled arrow from the object, pointing in the correct direction, (5) make the arrow lengths proportional to the force sizes. Free body diagrams help you work out the resultant force and predict how the object will move.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n33">When the exam asks you to calculate weight, always write out W = m &times; g, substitute the values, then calculate the answer with the correct unit (N). For &ldquo;describe&rdquo; questions about forces, always state the type of force, its direction, and whether it is contact or non-contact. In resultant force questions, show your working clearly &mdash; state which forces act in which direction before adding or subtracting. A common 1-mark question is &ldquo;What is meant by the term resultant force?&rdquo; &mdash; the answer is: a single force that has the same effect as all the forces combined.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n34">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n35">Scalars have magnitude only (speed, mass, distance); vectors have magnitude and direction (force, velocity, displacement). Force is always a vector.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n36">Contact forces need objects to touch (friction, air resistance, tension, normal contact force, upthrust); non-contact forces act at a distance (gravity, electrostatic, magnetic).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Weight = mass &times; gravitational field strength (W = m &times; g). Mass is in kg and doesn&rsquo;t change; weight is in N and depends on g. On Earth, g &asymp; 9.8 N/kg.</li></ul>
