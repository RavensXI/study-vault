# Reproduction, Meiosis and DNA

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Two Types of Reproduction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All living organisms reproduce to pass on their genes to the next generation. There are two fundamentally different ways this can happen: <dfn class="term" data-def="Reproduction involving the fusion of male and female gametes, producing offspring that are genetically different from each parent.">sexual reproduction</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="Reproduction involving only one parent, with no fusion of gametes &mdash; offspring are genetically identical clones of the parent.">asexual reproduction</dfn>. Understanding the differences between them is essential for AQA Biology Paper 2.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><strong>Sexual reproduction</strong> involves the fusion (joining together) of two special sex cells called <dfn class="term" data-def="Sex cells used in reproduction &mdash; sperm and egg cells in animals, pollen and egg cells in plants. Each contains half the normal number of chromosomes.">gametes</dfn>. In animals, the male gamete is the sperm cell and the female gamete is the egg cell (ovum). In plants, pollen contains the male gamete and the ovule contains the female gamete. When two gametes fuse at <dfn class="term" data-def="The fusion of a male and female gamete to form a zygote, restoring the full chromosome number.">fertilisation</dfn>, they form a single cell called a zygote, which then divides to form an embryo. Because each parent contributes different genetic information, the offspring show <dfn class="term" data-def="Differences between individuals of the same species, caused by genetic differences, environmental factors, or both.">genetic variation</dfn> &mdash; they are genetically unique.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Sexual reproduction produces offspring that are genetically different from each other and from both parents. This genetic variation is important because it helps populations adapt to changing environments through natural selection.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n5"><strong>Asexual reproduction</strong> involves only one parent. There is no fusion of gametes. The offspring are produced by <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of cell division that produces two genetically identical daughter cells, used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.">mitosis</dfn> and are genetically identical to the parent &mdash; they are <dfn class="term" data-def="Genetically identical organisms produced from a single parent through asexual reproduction.">clones</dfn>. Many organisms use asexual reproduction: bacteria divide by binary fission, plants can reproduce by runners or bulbs, and some animals such as starfish can regenerate from fragments.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Asexual reproduction:</strong> One parent only. No gamete fusion. Offspring are clones (genetically identical). Involves mitosis. Faster process. Advantages &mdash; only one parent needed, rapid population growth when conditions are favourable.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8">Some organisms, such as fungi, malarial parasites, and many plants, can switch between both types depending on conditions. <h2 data-narration-id="n9">Meiosis &mdash; Making Gametes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of cell division that produces four genetically different daughter cells, each with half the normal chromosome number &mdash; used to make gametes.">Meiosis</dfn> is the special type of cell division that produces gametes. It takes place in the reproductive organs &mdash; the ovaries and testes in animals, and the flower in plants. Meiosis is different from mitosis in several important ways.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Human body cells contain 46 chromosomes, arranged in 23 pairs. These are called <dfn class="term" data-def="A cell that has two sets of chromosomes &mdash; one from each parent. In humans, diploid cells have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).">diploid</dfn> cells. Before meiosis begins, the DNA replicates so that each chromosome is copied. The cell then divides twice: the first division separates the pairs of chromosomes, and the second division separates the copies. The result is four daughter cells, each with half the normal chromosome number &mdash; 23 chromosomes in humans. These cells are called <dfn class="term" data-def="A cell that has one set of chromosomes &mdash; half the diploid number. In humans, haploid cells (gametes) have 23 chromosomes.">haploid</dfn>.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Meiosis produces four haploid daughter cells that are all genetically different. Mitosis produces two diploid daughter cells that are genetically identical. The AQA exam frequently asks you to compare these two types of cell division.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n13">Meiosis introduces genetic variation in two ways. First, when chromosome pairs line up before the first division, they do so randomly &mdash; each gamete gets a different combination of maternal and paternal chromosomes. Second, sections of DNA can be swapped between paired chromosomes in a process called crossing over. These two mechanisms mean that every gamete produced by meiosis is genetically unique.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n14">When a sperm cell (23 chromosomes) fuses with an egg cell (23 chromosomes) at fertilisation, the full diploid number is restored &mdash; the zygote has 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). This is why gametes must be haploid: if they were diploid, the chromosome number would double with every generation.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">DNA &mdash; The Molecule of Life</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><dfn class="term" data-def="Deoxyribonucleic acid &mdash; a large molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the development, functioning, and reproduction of all living organisms.">DNA</dfn> (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a polymer made of two strands twisted into a double helix shape. Each strand is made of repeating units called nucleotides. A nucleotide consists of a sugar (deoxyribose), a phosphate group, and one of four bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), cytosine (C), and guanine (G).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">The two strands of DNA are held together by complementary base pairing: A always pairs with T, and C always pairs with G. This is why DNA can replicate accurately &mdash; each strand acts as a template for making a new complementary strand. The order of bases along a strand forms the genetic code that carries the instructions for building proteins.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n20">Different versions of the same gene are called <dfn class="term" data-def="Different versions of the same gene &mdash; for example, a gene for eye colour may have a brown allele and a blue allele.">alleles</dfn>. For example, the gene for eye colour has alleles for brown, blue, and green. Because chromosomes come in pairs, you inherit two alleles for every gene &mdash; one from each parent. Whether you display the characteristic depends on which alleles you have and whether they are dominant or recessive.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">The Human Genome</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The entire set of genetic material (DNA) in an organism, including all of its genes and non-coding sequences.">genome</dfn> is the entire set of genetic material in an organism. The <dfn class="term" data-def="An international research project completed in 2003 that mapped the entire sequence of human DNA &mdash; approximately 3 billion base pairs and around 20,000&ndash;25,000 genes.">Human Genome Project</dfn> was a major international research effort completed in 2003 that mapped the entire sequence of human DNA. Scientists identified approximately 20,000&ndash;25,000 genes across 3 billion base pairs of DNA.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Understanding the human genome has enormous benefits for science and medicine. It allows scientists to search for genes linked to specific diseases, develop personalised medicines that target an individual&rsquo;s genetic makeup, trace human migration patterns across history, and identify how closely related different species are through evolutionary biology.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The Human Genome Project (completed 2003) identified all the genes in human DNA. Key benefits include: identifying genes linked to disease, developing targeted medicines, understanding evolutionary relationships, and advancing forensic science.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n29">When comparing sexual and asexual reproduction, always address both types &mdash; don&rsquo;t just describe one. For 6-mark extended response questions on meiosis, structure your answer as a sequence: DNA replication &rarr; first division (pairs separate) &rarr; second division (copies separate) &rarr; four haploid cells. Always state the chromosome numbers (46 &rarr; 23 in humans). If asked about the importance of the genome, give specific examples of applications rather than vague statements like &ldquo;it helps scientists.&rdquo;</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n30">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n31">Sexual reproduction involves gamete fusion and produces genetically varied offspring; asexual reproduction uses mitosis and produces genetically identical clones.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Meiosis produces four haploid gametes (23 chromosomes each in humans) that are all genetically different &mdash; variation comes from random chromosome assortment and crossing over.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">DNA is a double helix of nucleotides with complementary base pairs (A&ndash;T, C&ndash;G). Genes are sections of DNA on chromosomes, and different versions of genes are called alleles. The Human Genome Project mapped the entire human DNA sequence, enabling advances in medicine, forensics, and evolutionary biology.</li></ul>
