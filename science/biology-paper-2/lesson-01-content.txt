# Homeostasis and the Nervous System

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Homeostasis?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="The regulation of internal conditions of a cell or organism to maintain optimum conditions for function, in response to internal and external changes.">Homeostasis</dfn> is the regulation of the internal conditions of a cell or organism. The goal is to maintain optimum conditions for function &mdash; the best possible environment for enzyme action and all cell processes &mdash; despite constant changes happening both inside and outside the body.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The human body must keep several internal conditions within narrow limits. These include <strong>blood glucose concentration</strong>, <strong>body temperature</strong>, and <strong>water levels</strong>. If any of these drift too far from the optimum, enzymes cannot work efficiently, chemical reactions slow down or stop, and cells can be damaged or destroyed.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Homeostasis maintains optimal conditions for <strong>enzyme action</strong> and all cell functions. Without it, enzymes would denature and metabolic reactions would fail.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Control Systems</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">All homeostatic control systems &mdash; whether nervous or chemical &mdash; share three essential components. You must know these for the exam:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>1. Receptors</strong> &mdash; cells that detect <dfn class="term" data-def="A change in the environment (internal or external) that is detected by a receptor.">stimuli</dfn> (changes in the environment). For example, temperature receptors in the skin detect when you touch something hot. <strong>2. Coordination centres</strong> &mdash; such as the brain, spinal cord, and pancreas &mdash; that receive and process information from receptors. <strong>3. <dfn class="term" data-def="Muscles or glands that carry out a response to a stimulus, restoring optimum conditions.">Effectors</dfn></strong> &mdash; muscles or glands that bring about responses to restore optimum levels.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n8">The body uses two types of automatic control system. <strong>Nervous responses</strong> are fast, short-lived, and travel as electrical impulses along neurones (e.g. pulling your hand away from a flame). <strong>Chemical (hormonal) responses</strong> are slower, longer-lasting, and travel in the blood (e.g. insulin controlling blood glucose). Both work through the same receptor &rarr; coordinator &rarr; effector pathway, but the speed and duration differ.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">The Nervous System</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The organ system that enables humans to react to their surroundings and coordinate their behaviour, consisting of the brain, spinal cord, and nerves.">nervous system</dfn> enables humans to react to their surroundings and to coordinate their behaviour. It detects changes in the environment using receptors, processes the information, and triggers appropriate responses.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Information from receptors passes along specialised cells called <dfn class="term" data-def="Nerve cells that carry electrical impulses around the body. Types include sensory, relay, and motor neurones.">neurones</dfn> as electrical impulses. These impulses travel to the <dfn class="term" data-def="The central nervous system, consisting of the brain and spinal cord, which coordinates responses.">central nervous system (CNS)</dfn>, which is made up of the brain and spinal cord. The CNS acts as the coordination centre &mdash; it processes the information and coordinates the response of effectors, which may be muscles contracting or glands secreting hormones.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The nervous pathway follows this sequence: <strong>stimulus &rarr; receptor &rarr; coordinator (CNS) &rarr; effector &rarr; response</strong>. Learn this order &mdash; it appears in almost every exam question on this topic.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Types of Neurone</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">There are three types of neurone, each with a specific role in the nervous pathway:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A neurone that carries electrical impulses from receptors to the central nervous system.">Sensory neurones</dfn></strong> carry impulses from receptors to the CNS. They have a long axon and a cell body branching off to one side. <strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A neurone found within the CNS that connects sensory neurones to motor neurones.">Relay neurones</dfn></strong> are found entirely within the CNS. They connect sensory neurones to motor neurones and allow the brain or spinal cord to process information. <strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A neurone that carries electrical impulses from the CNS to effectors (muscles or glands).">Motor neurones</dfn></strong> carry impulses from the CNS to effectors. They have a long axon and a cell body at one end with branching dendrites.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Synapses</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Neurones do not physically touch each other. There is a tiny gap between them called a <dfn class="term" data-def="The junction between two neurones where a signal is transmitted chemically across a small gap by neurotransmitters.">synapse</dfn>. When an electrical impulse reaches the end of one neurone, it triggers the release of chemical <dfn class="term" data-def="Chemical molecules released at a synapse that diffuse across the gap and trigger an electrical impulse in the next neurone.">neurotransmitters</dfn>. These chemicals diffuse across the gap and bind to receptors on the next neurone, triggering a new electrical impulse. This process converts an electrical signal into a chemical signal and back again.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n18">Neurotransmitters are only released from one side of the synapse (the <strong>pre-synaptic neurone</strong>) and can only bind to receptors on the other side (the <strong>post-synaptic neurone</strong>). This means impulses can only travel in one direction, ensuring signals reach the correct destination. Some drugs and toxins work by mimicking or blocking neurotransmitters at synapses.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Reflex Actions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An automatic, rapid response to a stimulus that does not involve the conscious part of the brain, protecting the body from harm.">reflex action</dfn> is an automatic and rapid response to a stimulus. Reflex actions do <strong>not</strong> involve the conscious part of the brain &mdash; they are coordinated by the spinal cord. This makes them much faster than voluntary responses, which is essential for protecting the body from harm.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Examples include pulling your hand away from a hot plate, your pupil constricting in bright light, and blinking when an object moves towards your eye.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The <strong>reflex arc</strong> pathway is: receptor &rarr; sensory neurone &rarr; relay neurone (in spinal cord) &rarr; motor neurone &rarr; effector. Reflex actions bypass the conscious brain, making them automatic and fast enough to prevent injury.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Reaction Time</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><dfn class="term" data-def="The time taken for a person to respond to a stimulus, typically measured by catching a dropped ruler.">Reaction time</dfn> is the time it takes for a person to respond to a stimulus. In humans, typical reaction times are between 0.2 and 0.9 seconds. Reaction time can be measured using a simple ruler-drop test &mdash; this is <strong>Required Practical 7</strong> in the AQA specification.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Factors that can affect reaction time include <strong>age</strong>, <strong>gender</strong>, <strong>caffeine</strong>, <strong>tiredness</strong>, <strong>alcohol</strong>, <strong>drugs</strong>, and <strong>distractions</strong>. In the required practical, students investigate the effect of one factor (such as practice or distraction) on human reaction time using a ruler-drop method, then calculate the mean to improve reliability.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Method:</strong> One person holds a ruler vertically at the 0&thinsp;cm mark. A second person places their thumb and finger around the ruler at the 0 mark without touching it. The ruler is released without warning, and the catcher grabs it as quickly as possible. The distance the ruler falls (in cm) is recorded. This is repeated at least 5 times and the mean distance is calculated.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Key points:</strong> The further the ruler falls, the slower the reaction time. To make it a fair test, the same ruler and same hand position should be used each time. Anomalous results (e.g. if the person anticipates the drop) should be identified and excluded before calculating the mean.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n29">For AQA Trilogy questions on this topic, always use the correct sequence: stimulus &rarr; receptor &rarr; coordinator &rarr; effector &rarr; response. In &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about reflex arcs, name each type of neurone and describe what happens at each synapse. For &ldquo;describe&rdquo; questions about reaction time practicals, remember to mention calculating the mean and identifying anomalous results &mdash; examiners expect these terms.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n30">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n31">Homeostasis is the regulation of internal conditions (blood glucose, temperature, water levels) to maintain optimum conditions for enzyme action. All control systems use receptors, coordination centres, and effectors.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">The nervous system transmits electrical impulses along sensory, relay, and motor neurones. Synapses transmit signals chemically between neurones using neurotransmitters.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">Reflex actions are automatic, rapid responses that bypass the conscious brain (receptor &rarr; sensory neurone &rarr; relay neurone &rarr; motor neurone &rarr; effector), protecting the body from harm.</li></ul>
