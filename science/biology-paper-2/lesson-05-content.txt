# Genetic Inheritance and Inherited Disorders

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Genetic Inheritance &mdash; The Basics</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Every characteristic you inherit &mdash; from eye colour to blood type &mdash; is controlled by <dfn class="term" data-def="A short section of DNA on a chromosome that codes for a specific protein and therefore a specific characteristic.">genes</dfn>. Genes are short sections of DNA found on <dfn class="term" data-def="Thread-like structures made of DNA found in the nucleus of a cell. Humans have 23 pairs (46 total).">chromosomes</dfn> in the nucleus of your cells. Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes (46 in total), with one chromosome in each pair inherited from each parent.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Most genes come in different versions called <dfn class="term" data-def="Different versions of the same gene. For example, the gene for eye colour has alleles for brown and blue.">alleles</dfn>. Because you have two copies of each chromosome (one from Mum, one from Dad), you carry two alleles for every gene. The combination of alleles you carry is your <dfn class="term" data-def="The combination of alleles an organism has for a particular gene, written using letters such as Bb or ff.">genotype</dfn>, and the physical characteristic that results &mdash; what you can actually see &mdash; is your <dfn class="term" data-def="The observable physical characteristic of an organism, determined by its genotype and sometimes the environment.">phenotype</dfn>.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Genotype = the alleles you have (e.g. Bb).</strong> <strong>Phenotype = what you look like (e.g. brown eyes).</strong> The genotype determines the phenotype.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Dominant and Recessive Alleles</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Some alleles are <dfn class="term" data-def="An allele that is always expressed in the phenotype, even when only one copy is present. Shown with a capital letter (e.g. B).">dominant</dfn> &mdash; they are always expressed in the phenotype when at least one copy is present. Dominant alleles are written with a capital letter (e.g. <strong>B</strong>). Other alleles are <dfn class="term" data-def="An allele that is only expressed in the phenotype when two copies are present (homozygous recessive). Shown with a lowercase letter (e.g. b).">recessive</dfn> &mdash; they are only expressed when two copies are present (no dominant allele to &ldquo;mask&rdquo; them). Recessive alleles are written with a lowercase letter (e.g. <strong>b</strong>).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">This gives three possible genotype combinations for any gene:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>BB</strong> &mdash; <dfn class="term" data-def="Having two identical alleles for a gene, e.g. BB or bb.">homozygous</dfn> dominant (two dominant alleles). Phenotype: dominant trait shown. <strong>Bb</strong> &mdash; <dfn class="term" data-def="Having two different alleles for a gene, e.g. Bb. The dominant allele determines the phenotype.">heterozygous</dfn> (one dominant, one recessive). Phenotype: dominant trait shown (the dominant allele masks the recessive). <strong>bb</strong> &mdash; homozygous recessive (two recessive alleles). Phenotype: recessive trait shown.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n9">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A heterozygous individual (Bb) is sometimes called a <strong>carrier</strong> of the recessive allele. They show the dominant phenotype but can pass the recessive allele to their children.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Punnett Squares</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A grid diagram used to predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross.">Punnett square</dfn> is a simple grid used to work out the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross. You place one parent&rsquo;s alleles along the top and the other parent&rsquo;s alleles down the side, then fill in the boxes to show every possible combination.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n12">Imagine both parents are heterozygous (Bb) for a trait where B (brown eyes) is dominant over b (blue eyes).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n13">Set up the Punnett square: Parent 1 alleles (B and b) go along the top. Parent 2 alleles (B and b) go down the side. Fill in the four boxes: <strong>BB</strong>, <strong>Bb</strong>, <strong>Bb</strong>, <strong>bb</strong>.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14">Results: <strong>1 in 4 (25%)</strong> chance of BB (homozygous dominant &mdash; brown eyes). <strong>2 in 4 (50%)</strong> chance of Bb (heterozygous &mdash; brown eyes). <strong>1 in 4 (25%)</strong> chance of bb (homozygous recessive &mdash; blue eyes). Overall ratio of phenotypes: <strong>3 brown : 1 blue</strong>.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15">Remember: these are <em>probabilities</em>, not guarantees. Having four children does not mean exactly three will have brown eyes and one blue &mdash; each child is an independent event.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Inherited Disorders</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Some disorders are caused by faulty alleles that can be passed from parents to children. The AQA spec requires you to know two inherited disorders: one caused by a dominant allele and one caused by a recessive allele.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Cystic Fibrosis (Recessive Disorder)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><dfn class="term" data-def="A genetic disorder caused by a recessive allele that leads to thick, sticky mucus in the lungs, digestive system, and other organs.">Cystic fibrosis</dfn> is caused by a recessive allele (f). A person must inherit two copies of the faulty allele (ff) to have the disorder. People with the genotype Ff are <strong>carriers</strong> &mdash; they do not show symptoms but can pass the allele to their children.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Cystic fibrosis causes the body to produce thick, sticky mucus that clogs the lungs and digestive system. Symptoms include difficulty breathing, frequent lung infections, and problems digesting food. About 1 in 25 people in the UK carry the recessive allele.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Punnett Square: Two Carriers of Cystic Fibrosis (Ff &times; Ff)</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n21">If both parents are carriers (Ff), the Punnett square gives: <strong>FF</strong> (unaffected, not a carrier), <strong>Ff</strong> (carrier, unaffected), <strong>Ff</strong> (carrier, unaffected), <strong>ff</strong> (has cystic fibrosis).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22">Probability: <strong>1 in 4 (25%)</strong> chance of having cystic fibrosis. <strong>2 in 4 (50%)</strong> chance of being a carrier. <strong>1 in 4 (25%)</strong> chance of being completely unaffected (FF). This means <strong>3 in 4 (75%)</strong> of children will not show symptoms, but <strong>2 out of those 3</strong> will be carriers.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Polydactyly (Dominant Disorder)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><dfn class="term" data-def="A genetic disorder caused by a dominant allele where a person is born with extra fingers or toes.">Polydactyly</dfn> is caused by a dominant allele (D). A person only needs one copy of the dominant allele (Dd or DD) to have the condition. This means an affected parent has at least a 50% chance of passing it to each child (if the affected parent is heterozygous, Dd).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Polydactyly causes a person to be born with extra fingers or toes. It is usually not life-threatening and the extra digits can often be surgically removed. Unlike cystic fibrosis, polydactyly cannot be &ldquo;carried&rdquo; without showing symptoms &mdash; if you have the dominant allele, you have the condition.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Cystic fibrosis = recessive (ff)</strong> &mdash; must inherit two faulty alleles; carriers (Ff) show no symptoms. <strong>Polydactyly = dominant (Dd or DD)</strong> &mdash; only one copy needed; no carriers exist because the allele is always expressed.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Sex Determination</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28">In humans, biological sex is determined by the 23rd pair of chromosomes &mdash; the <dfn class="term" data-def="The pair of chromosomes that determines biological sex. Females have XX and males have XY.">sex chromosomes</dfn>. Females have two X chromosomes (<strong>XX</strong>) and males have one X and one Y chromosome (<strong>XY</strong>). All other chromosome pairs (1&ndash;22) are called autosomes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Because a mother is XX, she can only pass on an X chromosome. A father is XY, so he can pass on either an X or a Y. This means the father&rsquo;s sperm determines the sex of the child.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n31">Result: <strong>2 in 4 (50%)</strong> chance of being female (XX) and <strong>2 in 4 (50%)</strong> chance of being male (XY). The probability is equal for each pregnancy &mdash; previous children&rsquo;s sex has no effect on the next.</p>
  </div></div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n32">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The Y chromosome carries the <strong>SRY gene</strong>, which triggers the development of male sex organs. Without the Y chromosome (i.e. XX), female development occurs by default.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n33">Embryo Screening and Ethical Issues</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n34">During IVF or pregnancy, embryos and foetuses can be tested for inherited disorders such as cystic fibrosis. This is called <dfn class="term" data-def="Testing an embryo or foetus for the presence of alleles that cause genetic disorders, allowing parents to make informed decisions.">genetic screening</dfn>. It raises important ethical questions that the exam may ask you to discuss.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Arguments for screening:</strong> It prevents children being born with serious, life-limiting conditions. Parents can make informed choices about continuing a pregnancy. It reduces suffering for the child and family. It could eventually reduce the frequency of harmful alleles in the population.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Arguments against screening:</strong> Some people believe every embryo has a right to life regardless of genetic conditions. It could lead to a &ldquo;designer baby&rdquo; culture where embryos are selected for non-medical traits. The conditions tested for (like polydactyly) may not be life-threatening. Some religious groups believe only God should decide these matters. There is a risk of creating discrimination against people living with genetic conditions.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n37">In AQA Trilogy exams, you will often be given a Punnett square to complete or interpret. Always label your alleles clearly (capital for dominant, lowercase for recessive), show all four offspring genotypes, and state the probability as a ratio, fraction, or percentage. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about inherited disorders, make sure you state whether the allele is dominant or recessive, give the genotypes of affected individuals and carriers, and use a Punnett square if the question asks you to predict outcomes.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n38">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n39">Alleles are different versions of a gene. Dominant alleles (capital letter) are expressed with just one copy; recessive alleles (lowercase) need two copies to be expressed. Your genotype (alleles) determines your phenotype (what you look like).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40">Cystic fibrosis is caused by a recessive allele (ff) &mdash; carriers (Ff) show no symptoms. Polydactyly is caused by a dominant allele (D) &mdash; only one copy is needed, so there are no carriers.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Sex is determined by the 23rd chromosome pair: XX = female, XY = male. The father&rsquo;s sperm determines the sex of the child, with a 50:50 probability.</li></ul>
