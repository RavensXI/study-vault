# Genetic Engineering and Ecology

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Genetic Engineering</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="The process of modifying the genome of an organism by introducing a gene from another organism to produce a desired characteristic.">Genetic engineering</dfn> is a process where scientists modify the <dfn class="term" data-def="The entire set of genetic material (DNA) in an organism.">genome</dfn> of an organism by introducing a gene from a different organism. This gives the modified organism a desirable characteristic it did not have before. The technique is used in agriculture, medicine, and industry.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The basic steps of genetic engineering are:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4"><strong>Step 1:</strong> The desired gene is identified and cut out of the DNA of one organism using <dfn class="term" data-def="Biological molecules (enzymes) that cut DNA at specific sequences, used to isolate genes in genetic engineering.">restriction enzymes</dfn>. These enzymes act like molecular scissors, cutting at precise recognition sequences in the DNA.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n5"><strong>Step 2:</strong> The gene is inserted into a <dfn class="term" data-def="A carrier used to transfer a gene into a target cell, usually a bacterial plasmid or a virus.">vector</dfn> &mdash; usually a bacterial <dfn class="term" data-def="A small circular piece of DNA found in bacteria, separate from the main chromosome, used as a vector in genetic engineering.">plasmid</dfn> or a virus. The same restriction enzyme is used to cut the plasmid open, creating &ldquo;sticky ends&rdquo; that are complementary to the cut gene.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><strong>Step 3:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="An enzyme that joins together pieces of DNA, used to seal a gene into a plasmid during genetic engineering.">Ligase</dfn> is used to join the gene into the plasmid, sealing the sticky ends together. The vector then carries the gene into the target organism&rsquo;s cells. The modified cells express the new gene and produce the desired protein or characteristic.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In genetic engineering, the same restriction enzyme must be used to cut both the gene of interest and the plasmid. This ensures the sticky ends are complementary and can be joined together by ligase.</p>
</div>

Examples include crops engineered to be resistant to insect attack, resistant to herbicides, or able to produce higher yields. There are also ethical concerns about large corporations controlling seed supplies and farmers becoming dependent on them. The exam expects you to evaluate both sides of this debate.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Classification of Living Organisms</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Scientists classify organisms into groups based on their similarities and differences. This makes it easier to study and identify millions of different species, understand evolutionary relationships, and monitor biodiversity. The traditional system was developed by <strong>Carl Linnaeus</strong> in the 18th century.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>The Linnaean system</strong> organises organisms into a hierarchy of groups. From largest to smallest: <strong>Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species</strong>. Each organism is given a two-part Latin name using its genus and species &mdash; this is called <dfn class="term" data-def="A system of naming organisms using two Latin names: the genus name (capitalised) and the species name (lowercase), e.g. Homo sapiens.">binomial nomenclature</dfn>. For example, humans are <em>Homo sapiens</em>. The first name (genus) begins with a capital letter, and the species name is lowercase. The name is always written in italics or underlined when handwritten.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Carl Woese proposed the three-domain system by analysing the biochemistry of organisms, particularly differences in ribosomal RNA. The three domains &mdash; Archaea, Bacteria, and Eukarya &mdash; sit above the kingdom level and provide a more accurate picture of evolutionary relationships than the earlier five-kingdom model.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n15">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A classification system proposed by Carl Woese that divides all life into three broad categories &mdash; Archaea, Bacteria, and Eukarya &mdash; based on differences in ribosomal RNA.">three-domain system</dfn> replaced the older five-kingdom model because advances in biochemistry and genome analysis showed the five-kingdom system was too simple. <strong>Archaea</strong> are primitive bacteria-like organisms often found in extreme environments &mdash; they are sometimes called <dfn class="term" data-def="Organisms that live in extreme conditions such as very high temperatures, high salt concentrations, or high pressure.">extremophiles</dfn>. <strong>Bacteria</strong> are true bacteria (eubacteria). <strong>Eukarya</strong> includes all organisms with cells containing a nucleus &mdash; animals, plants, fungi, and protists.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Ecosystems and Communities</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">An <dfn class="term" data-def="The interaction of a community of living organisms (biotic) with the non-living (abiotic) parts of their environment.">ecosystem</dfn> is the interaction of a community of living organisms with the non-living parts of their environment. Ecosystems can be as small as a rock pool or as large as the Amazon rainforest.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">To understand ecosystems, you need to know these key terms precisely &mdash; examiners will test whether you can tell them apart. A <dfn class="term" data-def="The place where an organism lives, which provides the conditions and resources it needs to survive.">habitat</dfn> is the place where an organism lives. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A group of organisms of the same species living in the same area at the same time.">population</dfn> is a group of organisms of the same species living in the same area. A <dfn class="term" data-def="All the populations of different species living and interacting in the same area.">community</dfn> is all the populations of different species living together in the same area.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n21"><dfn class="term" data-def="Non-living factors that affect a community, such as light intensity, temperature, moisture level, soil pH, wind speed, and carbon dioxide concentration.">Abiotic factors</dfn> are non-living conditions that affect where organisms can live. The main abiotic factors you need to know are: <strong>light intensity</strong> (affects photosynthesis and plant growth), <strong>temperature</strong> (affects enzyme activity and metabolic rate), <strong>moisture level</strong> (water availability for organisms), <strong>soil pH and mineral content</strong> (affects plant growth), <strong>wind speed and direction</strong> (affects transpiration and seed dispersal), and <strong>carbon dioxide levels</strong> (affects the rate of photosynthesis in plants).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><dfn class="term" data-def="Living factors that affect a community, such as availability of food, new predators, new pathogens, and competition between species.">Biotic factors</dfn> are living factors that affect a community. Key biotic factors include: <strong>availability of food</strong> (limits population size), <strong>new predators arriving</strong> (reduces prey populations), <strong>new pathogens</strong> (cause disease and reduce populations), and <strong>competition</strong> between organisms for the same resources. A common exam error is mixing up abiotic and biotic &mdash; remember that <strong>a</strong>biotic means <strong>a</strong>bsence of life (non-living).</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Competition and Interdependence</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">Organisms within a community depend on each other for survival &mdash; this is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The relationship between organisms in a community where each species depends on others for resources, shelter, pollination, or seed dispersal.">interdependence</dfn>. For example, bees depend on flowers for nectar, while flowers depend on bees for pollination. If one species is removed from a community, it can affect the whole food web.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><dfn class="term" data-def="The rivalry between organisms for the same limited resources such as food, water, light, space, or mates.">Competition</dfn> occurs when organisms compete for the same limited resources. <strong>Plants</strong> compete for light, water, space, and mineral ions from the soil. <strong>Animals</strong> compete for food, water, territory, and mates. Within a <dfn class="term" data-def="A community where all species and environmental factors are in balance, so population sizes remain roughly constant over time.">stable community</dfn>, all the species and environmental factors are balanced so that population sizes remain roughly constant. Tropical rainforests and ancient oak woodlands are examples of stable communities.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Adaptations &mdash; Structural, Behavioural, and Functional</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n26">Organisms have features called <dfn class="term" data-def="Features of an organism that increase its chances of survival and reproduction in its environment, developed through natural selection.">adaptations</dfn> that help them survive in their environment. These develop over many generations through natural selection. There are three types:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Structural adaptations</strong> are physical features of an organism&rsquo;s body. For example, the thick fur and small ears of an Arctic fox reduce heat loss in cold environments. Cacti have thick stems to store water and spines instead of leaves to reduce water loss by transpiration. Camels have large flat feet for walking on sand and thick eyelashes to protect against sandstorms.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Behavioural adaptations</strong> are actions organisms take to survive. For example, many birds migrate to warmer climates in winter to find food, and penguins huddle together to conserve heat.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Functional adaptations</strong> relate to internal body processes. For example, desert animals such as kangaroo rats produce very concentrated urine to conserve water. Some bacteria are extremophiles with enzymes that function at very high temperatures, allowing them to live in volcanic vents.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n30">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Extremophiles are organisms adapted to live in extreme environments such as high-temperature volcanic vents, very salty lakes, or high-pressure deep-sea habitats. Many extremophiles are classified as Archaea in the three-domain system.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n31">Questions on genetic engineering often ask you to describe the process step by step &mdash; make sure you name the enzymes (restriction enzymes to cut, ligase to join) and mention the vector (plasmid). For ecology questions, be precise with your terminology: don&rsquo;t confuse &ldquo;community&rdquo; (all species in an area) with &ldquo;population&rdquo; (one species in an area). Extended response questions may ask you to evaluate GM crops &mdash; always give arguments for <em>and</em> against, with specific examples such as golden rice, before reaching a balanced conclusion.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n32">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n33">Genetic engineering uses restriction enzymes and ligase to cut, transfer, and insert genes from one organism into another via a vector such as a bacterial plasmid.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n34">The three-domain system (Archaea, Bacteria, Eukarya) proposed by Carl Woese replaced the five-kingdom model, based on analysis of ribosomal RNA and biochemistry.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n35">Ecosystems involve the interaction of communities with abiotic and biotic factors; organisms are interdependent and compete for limited resources; adaptations (structural, behavioural, functional) help organisms survive.</li></ul>
