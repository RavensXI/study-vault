# Variation, Evolution and Natural Selection

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Variation?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">No two organisms are exactly alike &mdash; even identical twins have minor differences. <dfn class="term" data-def="The differences in characteristics between individuals of the same species.">Variation</dfn> is the term for these differences between individuals of the same species. Variation matters because it is the raw material that natural selection acts on: without differences between organisms, evolution could not happen.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Variation is caused by two factors. <dfn class="term" data-def="Variation caused by differences in DNA inherited from parents, including through mutations, meiosis, and sexual reproduction.">Genetic variation</dfn> results from differences in <dfn class="term" data-def="The chemical molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the development and functioning of living organisms, found in the nucleus of cells.">DNA</dfn> inherited from parents. This includes features like eye colour, blood group, and the ability to roll your tongue. <dfn class="term" data-def="Variation caused by the conditions in which an organism lives, such as diet, climate, or lifestyle.">Environmental variation</dfn> results from the conditions an organism lives in &mdash; for example, a plant grown in shade will be taller and thinner than the same species grown in full sunlight. Most characteristics are influenced by <strong>both</strong> genes and the environment. Height, for instance, is partly genetic but also affected by diet and health during childhood.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Variation can be <strong>genetic</strong> (caused by mutations, meiosis, and sexual reproduction), <strong>environmental</strong> (caused by conditions like diet, climate, or lifestyle), or <strong>both</strong>. Most human features &mdash; such as height, weight, and skin colour &mdash; are the result of a combination of genetic and environmental factors.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Mutations &mdash; The Source of New Variation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A random change in the sequence of bases in DNA, which may alter the protein produced by a gene.">mutation</dfn> is a random change in the sequence of bases in DNA. Mutations happen continuously &mdash; every time a cell divides, its DNA is copied, and occasionally errors occur. Most mutations have no effect on the organism because they occur in non-coding regions of DNA or do not change the protein produced. Very rarely, a mutation leads to a new characteristic that gives the organism a survival advantage. These rare beneficial mutations are the driving force behind evolution.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Mutations can be harmful, neutral, or beneficial. A harmful mutation might produce a faulty protein that causes a genetic disorder. A beneficial mutation might help an organism survive better in its environment &mdash; for example, a mutation that makes a bacterium resistant to an antibiotic. The rate of mutation can be increased by exposure to certain environmental factors called <dfn class="term" data-def="Agents that increase the rate of mutation, including certain chemicals, ionising radiation, and ultraviolet light.">mutagens</dfn>, such as ionising radiation, ultraviolet light, and certain chemicals.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Natural Selection</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which organisms with characteristics best suited to their environment are more likely to survive, reproduce, and pass on their alleles to the next generation.">Natural selection</dfn> is the mechanism by which evolution occurs. It was first proposed by Charles Darwin after his voyage on HMS Beagle in the 1830s, where he observed variation between species on different islands &mdash; most famously the finches of the Gal&aacute;pagos, which had different beak shapes suited to different food sources.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Four Steps of Natural Selection</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Step 1 &mdash; Variation:</strong> Within any population, individuals show variation in their characteristics due to differences in their genes. A mutation may produce a new, advantageous trait.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Step 2 &mdash; Competition:</strong> Organisms compete for limited resources such as food, water, territory, and mates. Not all individuals will survive and reproduce.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Step 3 &mdash; Survival of the fittest:</strong> Individuals with characteristics best suited to the environment are more likely to survive, find food, avoid predators, and resist disease. Darwin called this &ldquo;survival of the fittest.&rdquo;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Step 4 &mdash; Inheritance:</strong> The advantageous <dfn class="term" data-def="Alternative forms of a gene. Different alleles code for different versions of a characteristic, such as brown or blue eye colour.">alleles</dfn> are passed on to the next generation through reproduction. Over many generations, the beneficial allele becomes more common in the population &mdash; the species has <dfn class="term" data-def="The gradual change in the inherited characteristics of a population over successive generations, driven by natural selection.">evolved</dfn>.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Natural selection can be remembered as: <strong>variation &rarr; competition &rarr; survival of the fittest &rarr; inheritance</strong>. Over many generations, advantageous alleles become more common and the species evolves. In exams, use the word &ldquo;alleles&rdquo; rather than &ldquo;genes&rdquo; for full marks.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Selective Breeding</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which humans choose organisms with desirable characteristics to breed together, so that these characteristics appear more strongly in the offspring. Also called artificial selection.">Selective breeding</dfn> (also called artificial selection) is the process by which humans choose plants or animals with desirable <dfn class="term" data-def="Observable features or traits of an organism, determined by genes and/or the environment.">characteristics</dfn> and breed them together. Over many generations, the desired trait becomes more pronounced. Selective breeding has been used for thousands of years to produce food crops with higher yields, cattle that produce more milk, and dogs with particular temperaments or appearances.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Examples of Selective Breeding</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Food plants:</strong> Wild wheat had small grains and was difficult to harvest. Over generations of selective breeding, farmers chose the plants with the largest grains and strongest stems. Modern wheat produces far more food per plant than its wild ancestor.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Dairy cattle:</strong> Cows that naturally produced more milk were bred together. Today, a modern dairy cow can produce over 20 litres of milk per day &mdash; far more than its wild relatives.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Dogs:</strong> All domestic dog breeds descend from wolves. Selective breeding over thousands of years has produced breeds ranging from Great Danes to Chihuahuas, each selected for different traits such as size, speed, herding instinct, or companionship.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n20">The main disadvantage of selective breeding is that it reduces <dfn class="term" data-def="The variety of different alleles within a population. A large gene pool means greater genetic diversity and better resilience.">genetic variation</dfn> within a population. Breeding closely related organisms together over many generations can lead to <dfn class="term" data-def="Breeding between closely related individuals, which reduces genetic variation and can increase the risk of genetic disorders and vulnerability to disease.">inbreeding</dfn>, which increases the risk of inherited disorders and makes the population more vulnerable to new diseases &mdash; if all individuals are genetically similar, a single disease could wipe out the entire population.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Evidence for Evolution: Fossils</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><dfn class="term" data-def="The preserved remains or traces of organisms that lived thousands or millions of years ago, found in rocks, ice, peat, or amber.">Fossils</dfn> are the preserved remains or traces of organisms that lived thousands or millions of years ago. They are found in rocks, ice, peat bogs, and amber. Fossils provide crucial evidence that life on Earth has changed over time.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>How Fossils Form</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n23">Fossils can form in three main ways. First, from the <strong>hard parts</strong> of organisms (bones, teeth, shells) that do not decay easily &mdash; these are gradually replaced by minerals over millions of years in a process called <strong>mineralisation</strong>. Second, as <strong>casts or impressions</strong> left in mud or sand that later turns to rock &mdash; footprints and leaf imprints are common examples. Third, by <strong>preservation in special conditions</strong> where decay cannot occur, such as in ice, peat bogs, or amber (tree resin), which prevent bacteria from breaking down the organism.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n24">The fossil record shows how organisms have changed over time. Fossils of the <strong>simplest organisms</strong> are found in the <strong>oldest rocks</strong>, and fossils of more <strong>complex organisms</strong> appear in <strong>newer rocks</strong>. This supports the theory that simple life forms gradually evolved into more complex ones over billions of years. Scientists can also trace how specific features of a species have changed &mdash; for example, the evolution of the modern horse from a small, multi-toed ancestor to the large, single-hoofed animal we see today.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">However, the fossil record is <strong>incomplete</strong>. Many early organisms were <strong>soft-bodied</strong> and decayed completely without leaving a trace. Some fossils have been <strong>destroyed</strong> by geological processes such as volcanic eruptions, tectonic movements, and erosion. Others simply have <strong>not been found yet</strong>. These gaps mean that scientists cannot always trace the full evolutionary history of every species.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Antibiotic-Resistant Bacteria</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><dfn class="term" data-def="Bacteria that have evolved to survive exposure to antibiotics that would normally kill them or stop their growth.">Antibiotic-resistant bacteria</dfn> provide some of the strongest and most observable evidence for natural selection happening in real time. <dfn class="term" data-def="Medicines that kill bacteria or prevent their growth inside the body. They do not work against viruses.">Antibiotics</dfn> are medicines that kill bacteria or stop their growth. However, some bacteria have developed resistance to antibiotics &mdash; and this is a clear, real-world example of natural selection.</p>

<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n28">
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 1</div>
    <h4>Random mutation</h4><p>Within a population of bacteria, a random mutation gives one bacterium resistance to a particular antibiotic.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 2</div>
    <h4>Antibiotic applied</h4><p>The antibiotic kills the non-resistant bacteria, but the resistant bacterium survives because its mutation gives it a selective advantage.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 3</div>
    <h4>Reproduction</h4><p>The resistant bacterium reproduces rapidly by binary fission (bacteria can divide every 20 minutes), passing the resistance allele to all its offspring.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 4</div>
    <h4>Resistant population</h4><p>Over time, the entire population becomes resistant. The antibiotic is no longer effective, and a different treatment is needed.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>MRSA</strong> (methicillin-resistant <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em>) is a well-known example of an antibiotic-resistant &ldquo;superbug.&rdquo; It is very difficult to treat and is a major problem in hospitals. To slow the development of resistance: doctors should only prescribe antibiotics when truly necessary; patients must always <strong>complete the full course</strong>; and antibiotics should <strong>not</strong> be used for viral infections (where they have no effect).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Extinction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><dfn class="term" data-def="The permanent loss of all living members of a species, so that the species no longer exists anywhere on Earth.">Extinction</dfn> occurs when there are no remaining individuals of a species alive anywhere on Earth. Over 99% of all species that have ever lived are now extinct &mdash; it is a natural part of the history of life. However, human activities have greatly increased the rate of extinction in recent centuries.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Causes of Extinction</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n32">Species can become extinct due to several factors: a <strong>new predator</strong> that the species has no defence against; a <strong>new disease</strong> that spreads through the population; <strong>increased competition</strong> from a new species for the same resources; <strong>environmental changes</strong> such as climate change or habitat destruction that the species cannot adapt to quickly enough; or a <strong>catastrophic event</strong> such as a massive volcanic eruption or asteroid impact. The mass extinction of the dinosaurs around 66 million years ago was caused by an asteroid impact that dramatically changed the global climate.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n33">Species with low genetic variation are especially vulnerable to extinction. If all individuals in a population are genetically similar (for example, after extensive selective breeding or inbreeding), a single new disease or environmental change could kill the entire population because none of them carry the alleles needed to survive it. This is one reason why maintaining <dfn class="term" data-def="The variety of different species living in a habitat or ecosystem.">biodiversity</dfn> and genetic diversity is so important.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">For 6-mark extended response questions on natural selection, always structure your answer as a clear sequence: <strong>variation</strong> exists within a population due to mutations &rarr; <strong>competition</strong> for resources &rarr; individuals with the <strong>best-adapted characteristics survive</strong> &rarr; they <strong>reproduce and pass on</strong> the advantageous allele &rarr; over many generations the population <strong>evolves</strong>. Use a specific example (such as antibiotic-resistant bacteria or peppered moths) and name the characteristic that gives the advantage. A common mistake is saying that organisms &ldquo;choose&rdquo; to change or &ldquo;need&rdquo; to adapt &mdash; natural selection is a random, unintentional process driven by variation and competition, not by the organism&rsquo;s &ldquo;choice.&rdquo;</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">Variation between individuals is caused by genetic factors (mutations, meiosis, sexual reproduction), environmental factors (diet, climate), or both &mdash; and mutations are the ultimate source of new genetic variation.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Natural selection follows a sequence: variation &rarr; competition &rarr; survival of the fittest &rarr; inheritance of advantageous alleles, leading to evolution over many generations.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Antibiotic-resistant bacteria (e.g. MRSA) are real-time evidence for natural selection; fossils show how organisms changed over geological time; and extinction removes species that cannot adapt to changing conditions.</li></ul>
