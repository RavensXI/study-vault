# Hormones in Reproduction and Contraception

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Hormones in Human Reproduction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The human <dfn class="term" data-def="The system of glands that produce hormones and release them into the blood to regulate body processes.">endocrine system</dfn> controls reproduction through a group of hormones produced by the pituitary gland and the ovaries. Understanding how these hormones interact is essential for this topic &mdash; the exam often asks you to describe the roles of individual hormones and explain how they control the <dfn class="term" data-def="The monthly cycle of changes in the female reproductive system, typically lasting around 28 days, controlled by hormones.">menstrual cycle</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">During puberty, the pituitary gland (a small gland at the base of the brain) begins releasing hormones that trigger the development of secondary sexual characteristics. In males, the testes produce <dfn class="term" data-def="The main male reproductive hormone, produced by the testes, which stimulates sperm production and the development of secondary sexual characteristics.">testosterone</dfn>, which stimulates sperm production and causes changes such as a deeper voice, facial hair, and increased muscle mass. In females, the ovaries begin producing oestrogen, which triggers egg maturation and changes such as breast development, widening of the hips, and the start of the menstrual cycle.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Four hormones control the menstrual cycle: <strong>FSH</strong> (follicle stimulating hormone), <strong>LH</strong> (luteinising hormone), <strong>oestrogen</strong>, and <strong>progesterone</strong>. FSH and LH are produced by the pituitary gland. Oestrogen and progesterone are produced by the ovaries.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Menstrual Cycle</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The menstrual cycle lasts approximately 28 days and is controlled by the interaction of the four key hormones. It prepares the body for pregnancy each month. If no fertilisation occurs, the cycle resets.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Days 1&ndash;4 &mdash; Menstruation:</strong> The uterus lining breaks down and is shed through the vagina as a period. Hormone levels are low at this stage.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Days 4&ndash;14 &mdash; Repair and build-up:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Follicle stimulating hormone &mdash; released by the pituitary gland, it causes an egg to mature in the ovary and stimulates the ovaries to produce oestrogen.">FSH</dfn> is released by the pituitary gland and causes an egg to mature inside a follicle in the ovary. The maturing follicle produces <dfn class="term" data-def="A hormone produced by the ovaries that causes the uterus lining to thicken and inhibits further FSH production.">oestrogen</dfn>, which causes the uterus lining to thicken with blood vessels, ready to receive a fertilised egg.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Day 14 &mdash; Ovulation:</strong> A surge in <dfn class="term" data-def="Luteinising hormone &mdash; released by the pituitary gland, it triggers ovulation (the release of a mature egg from the ovary).">LH</dfn> triggers <dfn class="term" data-def="The release of a mature egg from the ovary, typically occurring around day 14 of the menstrual cycle.">ovulation</dfn> &mdash; the release of the mature egg from the ovary into the fallopian tube. High oestrogen levels stimulate the pituitary gland to release this surge of LH.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Days 14&ndash;28 &mdash; Maintenance:</strong> After ovulation, the empty follicle becomes the corpus luteum and produces <dfn class="term" data-def="A hormone produced by the corpus luteum in the ovary that maintains the uterus lining and inhibits FSH and LH production.">progesterone</dfn>. Progesterone maintains the thick uterus lining. If no fertilisation occurs, the corpus luteum breaks down, progesterone levels fall, and the lining is shed &mdash; the cycle starts again.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Hormone Interactions &mdash; Negative Feedback</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The four hormones do not work in isolation &mdash; they interact through <dfn class="term" data-def="A control mechanism where a change in a condition triggers a response that reverses the change, keeping conditions stable.">negative feedback</dfn>. This means rising levels of one hormone can inhibit (reduce) the production of another, keeping the cycle in balance.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>FSH stimulates oestrogen production.</strong> Rising oestrogen then inhibits further FSH release (so only one egg usually matures). At the same time, high oestrogen stimulates LH release, which triggers ovulation. After ovulation, progesterone inhibits both FSH and LH &mdash; preventing another egg from being released while the body waits to see if pregnancy occurs. When progesterone drops (because the corpus luteum breaks down), FSH is no longer inhibited and the cycle restarts.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Oestrogen inhibits FSH but stimulates LH. Progesterone inhibits both FSH and LH. This negative feedback loop ensures only one egg matures per cycle and prevents multiple ovulations.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Contraception</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><dfn class="term" data-def="Methods used to prevent pregnancy, which can be hormonal, barrier, or surgical.">Contraception</dfn> means preventing pregnancy. The AQA spec requires you to know about hormonal and non-hormonal methods, and to be able to evaluate their effectiveness, side effects, and suitability.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Hormonal Methods of Contraception</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Oral contraceptive pill:</strong> Contains hormones (oestrogen and/or progesterone). The combined pill contains both hormones &mdash; it prevents ovulation by maintaining high oestrogen and progesterone, which inhibits FSH and LH release. The progesterone-only pill thickens cervical mucus to prevent sperm reaching the egg. Effectiveness: over 99% if used correctly. Possible side effects include headaches, nausea, and blood clots (rare).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Contraceptive injection:</strong> A slow-release injection of progesterone given every 8&ndash;13 weeks. It inhibits ovulation and thickens cervical mucus. Very effective but cannot be reversed quickly once injected. May cause irregular periods.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Contraceptive implant:</strong> A small flexible rod inserted under the skin of the upper arm. It releases progesterone slowly for up to 3 years. Over 99% effective. May cause irregular bleeding in some users.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Contraceptive patch:</strong> A skin patch that releases oestrogen and progesterone through the skin. Changed weekly for three weeks, then a week off. Effectiveness: 99.8%. Side effects similar to the combined pill.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Non-Hormonal Methods of Contraception</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Condoms (male and female):</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="A physical device that prevents sperm from reaching the egg &mdash; condoms and diaphragms are examples of barrier methods.">Barrier methods</dfn> that physically prevent sperm from reaching the egg. Condoms are 98% effective when used correctly and are the only method that also protects against sexually transmitted infections (STIs). No hormonal side effects.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Diaphragm or cap:</strong> A flexible dome placed over the cervix before sex. Used with spermicide. Around 92&ndash;96% effective. Must be fitted correctly and left in place for several hours after sex.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Intrauterine device (IUD):</strong> A small T-shaped device inserted into the uterus by a doctor. The copper IUD releases copper ions that are toxic to sperm and also prevents a fertilised egg from implanting. Over 99% effective and lasts 5&ndash;10 years. It is non-hormonal but can cause heavier periods.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Surgical methods:</strong> Sterilisation involves cutting or tying the fallopian tubes (female) or the vas deferens (male vasectomy). Permanent and highly effective. Difficult to reverse.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Abstinence and natural family planning:</strong> Avoiding sex during the fertile window (around ovulation). No side effects but requires careful monitoring of the cycle and is less reliable than other methods.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Hormonal contraception works by maintaining high levels of oestrogen and/or progesterone, which inhibit FSH and LH through negative feedback &mdash; preventing ovulation. Barrier methods physically block sperm from reaching the egg. Only condoms protect against STIs.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">The Use of Hormones to Treat Infertility (Higher Tier)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><dfn class="term" data-def="The inability to conceive a child after regular unprotected sex for a year or more.">Infertility</dfn> affects many couples. Hormones can be used to help women who have difficulty conceiving. Two main treatments involve reproductive hormones:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>FSH and LH injections (fertility drugs):</strong> Women who do not ovulate naturally can be given FSH to stimulate egg maturation, followed by LH to trigger ovulation. This is called <dfn class="term" data-def="Using FSH and LH injections to stimulate egg production and ovulation in women who have difficulty conceiving naturally.">fertility treatment</dfn>. A risk is that multiple eggs may be released, increasing the chance of multiple births (twins, triplets), which carry higher health risks for both mother and babies.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>IVF (in vitro fertilisation):</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="In vitro fertilisation &mdash; a fertility treatment where eggs are collected from the ovaries, fertilised with sperm in a laboratory dish, and one or two embryos are transferred to the uterus.">IVF</dfn> is a more involved process. The woman is given FSH and LH to stimulate the ovaries to produce several eggs. These eggs are then collected surgically and fertilised with sperm in a laboratory dish. The fertilised eggs develop into embryos, and one or two are transferred into the woman&rsquo;s uterus. Remaining embryos can be frozen for future use.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Evaluating IVF &mdash; Benefits and Drawbacks</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Benefits:</strong> IVF allows couples who cannot conceive naturally to have children. Success rates have improved over time. Embryos can be screened for genetic disorders before implantation. Unused embryos can be stored for later attempts.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Drawbacks:</strong> IVF is emotionally and physically demanding for the woman. Success rates are not guaranteed &mdash; typically around 30% per cycle for women under 35, lower for older women. The hormone treatment can cause side effects such as hot flushes, headaches, and ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome. Multiple embryos may implant, leading to multiple births. There are ethical concerns about the fate of unused embryos &mdash; some people believe life begins at fertilisation and object to embryos being destroyed or frozen indefinitely. IVF is also expensive, with NHS funding varying by area.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n33">AQA commonly asks you to compare contraceptive methods or evaluate IVF. For &ldquo;compare and explain&rdquo; questions, always state the similarity or difference clearly, then explain why using your knowledge of hormones. For extended response questions on IVF, structure your answer with benefits on one side and drawbacks on the other, and finish with a conclusion. Remember that IVF content is Higher Tier only &mdash; if you are sitting Higher, expect a question on it.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n34">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n35">Four hormones control the menstrual cycle: FSH and LH (from the pituitary gland) and oestrogen and progesterone (from the ovaries). They interact through negative feedback to regulate egg maturation, ovulation, and the uterus lining.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n36">Hormonal contraception prevents pregnancy by inhibiting FSH and LH (so ovulation does not occur). Barrier methods physically block sperm. Only condoms protect against STIs.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">IVF uses FSH and LH to stimulate egg production. Eggs are fertilised in a lab and embryos transferred to the uterus. Benefits include enabling conception; drawbacks include low success rates, side effects, and ethical concerns about unused embryos.</li></ul>
