# Ecosystems, Biodiversity and Human Impact

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Levels of Organisation in an Ecosystem</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A community of living organisms interacting with each other and their non-living environment in a particular area.">ecosystem</dfn> is the interaction of a community of living organisms (the biotic components) with the non-living (abiotic) parts of their environment. To understand how ecosystems work, you need to know the hierarchy of organisation.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">An individual organism is a single living thing. A <dfn class="term" data-def="All the organisms of one species living in a particular area at a particular time.">population</dfn> is all the organisms of one species in an area. A <dfn class="term" data-def="All the populations of different species living and interacting in an area.">community</dfn> is all the populations of different species in an area. An ecosystem includes both the community and the abiotic factors such as temperature, light, water, soil, and pH.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Remember the hierarchy: <strong>organism &rarr; population &rarr; community &rarr; ecosystem</strong>. Exam questions often ask you to define each level or explain the difference between a community and an ecosystem (a community is just the living organisms; an ecosystem also includes the abiotic environment).</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n5">Within an ecosystem, organisms compete for resources. Animals compete for food, territory, water, and mates. Plants compete for light, water, space, and mineral ions from the soil. Species that are better adapted to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce &mdash; this is natural selection at work within the ecosystem.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n6"><strong>Abiotic factors</strong> (non-living) include light intensity, temperature, moisture level, soil pH, wind speed, carbon dioxide concentration, and oxygen levels in water. Changes in abiotic factors can affect the distribution and abundance of organisms &mdash; for example, plants grow faster in higher light intensities.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Biotic factors</strong> (living) include food availability, new predators arriving, new pathogens, and competition from other species. For example, the introduction of grey squirrels to the UK led to a decline in red squirrels because greys outcompete them for food and carry squirrel pox.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">How Materials Are Cycled</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Materials in an ecosystem are constantly recycled. Living organisms take in materials, use them, and return them to the environment when they die and decompose. Two key cycles you need to know are the <dfn class="term" data-def="The biogeochemical cycle by which carbon moves between the atmosphere, living organisms, oceans, and rocks through processes such as photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, and decomposition.">carbon cycle</dfn> and the <dfn class="term" data-def="The continuous movement of water between the Earth&rsquo;s surface, atmosphere, and underground through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and transpiration.">water cycle</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>The carbon cycle:</strong> Carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere by photosynthesis in plants and algae. Carbon is passed along food chains when animals eat plants and other animals. Carbon is returned to the atmosphere through respiration (by all living organisms), combustion of fossil fuels and wood, and decomposition of dead organisms by microorganisms. Some carbon becomes locked in fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) or sedimentary rocks (limestone) for millions of years.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>The water cycle:</strong> Water evaporates from oceans, lakes, and rivers, and transpires from plants. Water vapour rises, cools, and condenses to form clouds. Precipitation (rain, snow) falls back to the surface, where it flows into rivers or soaks into groundwater, eventually returning to the sea. This cycle is powered by energy from the sun.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Decomposition is essential to both cycles. <dfn class="term" data-def="Microorganisms such as bacteria and fungi that break down dead organic matter, returning nutrients and carbon to the soil and atmosphere.">Decomposers</dfn> (bacteria and fungi) break down dead plant and animal material, releasing carbon dioxide through respiration and returning mineral ions to the soil. Without decomposition, nutrients would be locked in dead organisms and unavailable for new growth.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">What Is Biodiversity?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><dfn class="term" data-def="The variety of all the different species of organisms on Earth, or within an ecosystem.">Biodiversity</dfn> is the variety of all living species in an ecosystem or on Earth as a whole. High biodiversity means a wide range of different species are present. Ecosystems with high biodiversity are more stable and more resilient to environmental change because if one species is lost, others can fill its role in the food web.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Scientists measure biodiversity by studying the number of different species in an area (species richness) and the abundance of each species. To estimate population size in a habitat, scientists use sampling techniques such as <dfn class="term" data-def="A square frame (usually 0.5m &times; 0.5m) placed randomly on the ground to count organisms within it, used to estimate population size and distribution.">quadrats</dfn> placed randomly and <dfn class="term" data-def="A line laid across a habitat along which organisms are recorded at regular intervals to study changes in distribution.">transects</dfn> to study distribution. Random sampling avoids bias and makes results more representative.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Sampling Techniques in Detail</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Quadrats:</strong> A 0.5m &times; 0.5m frame placed on the ground at random positions (using random number generators to pick coordinates). Count the number of organisms of each species inside the quadrat. Repeat in multiple positions and calculate a mean. To estimate total population: mean count per quadrat &times; total area &divide; area of one quadrat.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Transects:</strong> A measuring tape is laid in a straight line across a habitat (e.g. from a pond edge up a hillside). Quadrats are placed at regular intervals along the line to record how species distribution changes with distance. This is useful for investigating the effect of an abiotic factor that changes across the habitat (e.g. light, moisture).</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Human Threats to Biodiversity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">The human population is growing rapidly, and our activities are reducing biodiversity worldwide. The AQA spec identifies several key threats.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Waste management:</strong> Humans produce large quantities of waste. Unless properly handled, this pollutes water (sewage, fertiliser runoff causing <dfn class="term" data-def="Excessive nutrient enrichment of water, often from fertiliser runoff, leading to algal blooms that block light and deplete oxygen, killing aquatic organisms.">eutrophication</dfn>), air (smoke, acidic gases), and land (landfill, toxic chemicals). Pollution kills organisms directly and destroys habitats.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Land use:</strong> Building, quarrying, farming, and dumping waste all reduce the amount of land available for other organisms. As the human population grows, more land is converted for agriculture, housing, and industry, destroying natural habitats and reducing biodiversity.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The cutting down and removal of large areas of forest, often in tropical regions, to clear land for agriculture, cattle ranching, or timber.">Deforestation</dfn></strong> in tropical areas has severe consequences: loss of habitat for millions of species, release of stored carbon (increasing atmospheric CO&#8322;), loss of trees that would absorb CO&#8322; through photosynthesis, and disruption of the water cycle (less transpiration means less rainfall). Large-scale deforestation in the Amazon and Southeast Asia is a major driver of both biodiversity loss and climate change.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Global warming:</strong> The burning of fossil fuels releases carbon dioxide, and farming livestock produces methane &mdash; both are <dfn class="term" data-def="Gases in the atmosphere (including carbon dioxide, methane, and water vapour) that absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, trapping heat and warming the Earth.">greenhouse gases</dfn> that trap heat in the Earth&rsquo;s atmosphere. This leads to rising global temperatures, which cause sea level rise (from melting ice caps and thermal expansion), changes in the distribution of species, loss of polar habitats, more extreme weather events, and changes in migration patterns and breeding seasons.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Greenhouse Effect Explained</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n24">Short-wave radiation from the sun passes through the atmosphere and warms the Earth&rsquo;s surface. The Earth re-emits this energy as long-wave infrared radiation. Greenhouse gases (CO&#8322;, methane, water vapour) absorb some of this infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back towards the Earth. This keeps the planet warm enough to support life &mdash; the natural greenhouse effect. However, human activities have increased the concentration of greenhouse gases, enhancing the greenhouse effect and causing average global temperatures to rise.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Maintaining Biodiversity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Scientists and governments recognise that biodiversity must be actively protected. The AQA spec requires you to know programmes and strategies for maintaining biodiversity at local, national, and global levels.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Breeding programmes:</strong> Zoos and wildlife centres run captive breeding programmes for endangered species, then reintroduce them to the wild. Examples include the red kite in the UK and the Arabian oryx. <strong>Seed banks:</strong> Organisations like the Millennium Seed Bank store seeds from thousands of plant species to protect genetic diversity and allow future reintroduction if species become extinct in the wild.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Protected areas:</strong> National parks, nature reserves, and marine conservation zones protect habitats from development and allow ecosystems to recover. <strong>Hedgerow and habitat restoration:</strong> Farmers are encouraged (sometimes through government grants) to maintain hedgerows, field margins, and wildlife corridors that connect fragmented habitats.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Government and Global Action</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Legislation:</strong> Laws such as the Wildlife and Countryside Act (1981) make it illegal to harm protected species or damage their habitats. <strong>Recycling and waste reduction:</strong> Reducing the amount of waste going to landfill limits habitat destruction and pollution. <strong>Sustainable development:</strong> Meeting the needs of the current population without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. This includes sustainable forestry (replanting trees), sustainable fishing (quotas to prevent overfishing), and reducing carbon emissions through renewable energy.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Global agreements:</strong> International cooperation is essential because biodiversity loss and climate change are global problems. Agreements such as the Paris Agreement (2015) aim to limit global temperature rise. The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) restricts the trade of threatened animals and plants across borders.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n31">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The conflict between human needs and biodiversity is a common 6-mark question topic. You should be able to discuss how measures like breeding programmes, seed banks, protected areas, and legislation help maintain biodiversity &mdash; and acknowledge that these measures can conflict with economic development (e.g. farmers losing land to nature reserves, or industries restricted by environmental regulations).</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n32">For extended response questions on ecosystems and biodiversity, structure your answer clearly: state the problem (e.g. deforestation), explain the biological impact (loss of habitat, reduced biodiversity, increased CO&#8322;), and then evaluate the solutions (breeding programmes, legislation, sustainable practices). Always use specific examples &mdash; naming real species, places, or laws scores higher than vague generalisations. For calculate questions on sampling, show your working and include units.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n33">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n34">An ecosystem includes both the living community and the abiotic environment. Materials such as carbon and water are constantly recycled through decomposition, respiration, photosynthesis, and physical processes.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n35">Human activities &mdash; waste, land use, deforestation, and burning fossil fuels &mdash; reduce biodiversity and contribute to global warming through increased greenhouse gas emissions.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n36">Biodiversity can be maintained through breeding programmes, seed banks, protected areas, legislation, and international agreements &mdash; but these must balance conservation with human economic needs.</li></ul>
