# The Endocrine System and Blood Glucose Control

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Human Endocrine System</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A system of glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream to regulate body functions.">endocrine system</dfn> is made up of glands that produce chemicals called <dfn class="term" data-def="Chemical messengers produced by glands and carried in the blood to target organs, where they trigger a response.">hormones</dfn>. Unlike electrical impulses in the nervous system, hormones are carried in the <strong>blood</strong> to their <dfn class="term" data-def="The specific organ or tissue that a hormone acts on to produce a response.">target organs</dfn>, where they produce a response. This lesson covers AQA spec sections 4.5.3.1, 4.5.3.2, and 4.5.3.7 &mdash; all frequently tested on Paper 2.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The endocrine system works more <strong>slowly</strong> than the nervous system, but its effects are <strong>longer lasting</strong>. Nervous responses happen in milliseconds and are short-lived. Hormonal responses take seconds to minutes to begin but can last for hours, days, or even longer.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The nervous system produces <strong>fast, short-lived</strong> responses using electrical impulses. The endocrine system produces <strong>slower, longer-lasting</strong> responses using chemical messengers (hormones) carried in the blood.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Major Endocrine Glands</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">You need to know the main glands, the hormones they produce, and the role of each hormone. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A pea-sized gland at the base of the brain that secretes hormones controlling other glands &mdash; often called the master gland.">pituitary gland</dfn> sits at the base of the brain and is often called the &ldquo;master gland&rdquo; because it secretes several hormones that act on <strong>other glands</strong>, stimulating them to produce their own hormones.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Pituitary gland</strong> (brain): The master gland &mdash; produces hormones including FSH and LH that stimulate other glands. It controls growth, reproduction, and water balance.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Thyroid gland</strong> (neck): Produces <dfn class="term" data-def="A hormone produced by the thyroid gland that controls the metabolic rate &mdash; how quickly the body uses energy.">thyroxine</dfn>, which controls the body&rsquo;s <strong>metabolic rate</strong> &mdash; how quickly cells convert food into energy.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Adrenal glands</strong> (above kidneys): Produce <dfn class="term" data-def="A hormone released from the adrenal glands in response to stress or danger &mdash; increases heart rate, blood flow to muscles, and blood glucose levels.">adrenaline</dfn>, the &ldquo;fight or flight&rdquo; hormone, released in response to stress or danger.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Pancreas</strong> (abdomen): Produces <dfn class="term" data-def="A hormone produced by the pancreas that lowers blood glucose levels by causing cells to take up glucose and the liver to convert glucose into glycogen.">insulin</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="A hormone produced by the pancreas that raises blood glucose levels by causing the liver to convert glycogen back into glucose (Higher Tier only).">glucagon</dfn> to control blood glucose levels.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Ovaries</strong> (female): Produce <strong>oestrogen</strong>, which controls the menstrual cycle and female secondary sexual characteristics. <strong>Testes</strong> (male): Produce <strong>testosterone</strong>, which controls male secondary sexual characteristics.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Control of Blood Glucose Concentration</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The blood glucose level must be kept within a narrow range. If it rises too high or drops too low, cells cannot function properly &mdash; enzymes rely on stable conditions. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A large gland behind the stomach that has both endocrine and digestive functions &mdash; it monitors blood glucose levels and produces insulin and glucagon.">pancreas</dfn> is the organ that <strong>monitors and controls</strong> blood glucose concentration.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">After you eat a meal, carbohydrates are digested into glucose. This glucose is absorbed into the blood, causing blood glucose levels to <strong>rise</strong>. The pancreas detects this rise and responds by releasing <strong>insulin</strong> into the blood.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Insulin</strong> lowers blood glucose by causing body cells to take up glucose from the blood, and by causing the liver to convert excess glucose into <dfn class="term" data-def="A storage polysaccharide made from glucose and stored in the liver and muscles &mdash; it can be converted back to glucose when energy is needed.">glycogen</dfn> for storage. Glycogen is stored in the liver and muscles.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">What Happens When Blood Glucose Is Too Low (Higher Tier)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">If blood glucose levels fall too low &mdash; for example, during exercise or between meals &mdash; the pancreas detects this drop and releases the hormone <strong>glucagon</strong>. Glucagon travels in the blood to the liver, where it causes <strong>glycogen to be converted back into glucose</strong>. This glucose is then released into the blood, raising the blood glucose level back to normal.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Insulin and glucagon work together as an <strong>antagonistic pair</strong> &mdash; they have opposite effects. This is an example of <dfn class="term" data-def="A control mechanism where the output of a system feeds back to reduce or reverse the original change, maintaining stable conditions.">negative feedback</dfn>, where a change in conditions triggers a response that reverses that change, bringing the system back to its normal level.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Negative Feedback Cycle &mdash; Step by Step</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Blood glucose too high:</strong> Pancreas detects the rise &rarr; pancreas releases <strong>insulin</strong> &rarr; insulin causes cells to absorb glucose and liver to convert glucose to glycogen &rarr; blood glucose falls back to normal &rarr; pancreas reduces insulin secretion.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Blood glucose too low:</strong> Pancreas detects the drop &rarr; pancreas releases <strong>glucagon</strong> &rarr; glucagon causes liver to convert glycogen back to glucose &rarr; glucose released into blood &rarr; blood glucose rises back to normal &rarr; pancreas reduces glucagon secretion.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n21">This cycle repeats continuously, keeping blood glucose levels stable. The key idea is that the <strong>response reverses the change</strong> &mdash; that is what makes it negative feedback.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Negative Feedback and Thyroxine (Higher Tier)</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Negative feedback also controls <strong>thyroxine</strong> levels. When thyroxine levels in the blood are too low, the pituitary gland releases <strong>thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH)</strong>, which stimulates the thyroid gland to produce more thyroxine. As thyroxine levels rise, the pituitary gland detects this and <strong>reduces TSH production</strong>, so the thyroid produces less thyroxine. This keeps thyroxine &mdash; and therefore metabolic rate &mdash; stable.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Adrenaline</strong> is produced by the adrenal glands in response to fear, stress, or excitement. It prepares the body for &ldquo;fight or flight&rdquo; by increasing heart rate, diverting blood flow to the muscles, and raising blood glucose levels (by causing glycogen to be broken down). Adrenaline release is controlled by the nervous system rather than by negative feedback &mdash; it is a rapid, short-term response.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n25">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact &mdash; Negative Feedback</div>
  <p>Negative feedback is a control mechanism where a change in conditions triggers a response that <strong>reverses</strong> that change, restoring the system to its normal level. Both blood glucose (insulin/glucagon) and thyroxine (TSH) are controlled by negative feedback.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Diabetes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><dfn class="term" data-def="A condition where the body cannot properly regulate blood glucose levels &mdash; either because the pancreas does not produce insulin (Type 1) or because the body's cells no longer respond to it (Type 2).">Diabetes</dfn> is a condition where the body cannot control blood glucose levels properly. There are two types, and the exam will expect you to compare them clearly.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Type 1 Diabetes</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><dfn class="term" data-def="A form of diabetes caused by the destruction of insulin-producing cells in the pancreas &mdash; treated with insulin injections.">Type 1 diabetes</dfn> occurs when the pancreas <strong>does not produce insulin</strong>. The immune system destroys the insulin-producing cells. Without insulin, blood glucose levels can rise to dangerously high levels after eating.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Treatment:</strong> Insulin injections, usually several times a day. The person must carefully monitor their blood glucose levels and adjust their insulin dose based on what they eat and their activity levels. There is no cure &mdash; this is a lifelong condition.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Cause:</strong> It is thought to be an <strong>autoimmune</strong> condition &mdash; the body&rsquo;s own immune system attacks the pancreas cells. It usually develops in childhood or young adulthood and is <strong>not linked to lifestyle</strong>.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Type 2 Diabetes</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><dfn class="term" data-def="A form of diabetes where the body's cells no longer respond properly to insulin &mdash; strongly linked to obesity and treated with diet, exercise, and sometimes medication.">Type 2 diabetes</dfn> occurs when the body&rsquo;s cells <strong>no longer respond to insulin</strong> properly &mdash; this is called <strong>insulin resistance</strong>. The pancreas may still produce insulin, but it has little effect. Blood glucose levels can therefore rise dangerously.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Treatment:</strong> A <strong>carbohydrate-controlled diet</strong> and <strong>regular exercise</strong>. Some patients may also need medication. Unlike Type 1, it does not usually require insulin injections (although some severe cases may).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Risk factors:</strong> <strong>Obesity</strong> is the major risk factor. A sedentary lifestyle, an unhealthy diet high in sugar and fat, family history, and ethnicity also increase the risk. Type 2 diabetes is more common in adults, and the rising rates of obesity mean it is becoming increasingly common worldwide.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n34">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact &mdash; Type 1 vs Type 2 Diabetes</div>
  <p><strong>Type 1:</strong> Pancreas does not produce insulin &rarr; treated with insulin injections &rarr; autoimmune, not lifestyle-related. <strong>Type 2:</strong> Body cells no longer respond to insulin &rarr; treated with diet and exercise &rarr; obesity is the main risk factor.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n35">Interpreting Blood Glucose Data</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36">Exam questions often show graphs comparing blood glucose levels in a healthy person, a person with Type 1 diabetes, and a person with Type 2 diabetes after a meal. In a <strong>healthy person</strong>, blood glucose rises after eating and then returns to normal within about two hours as insulin is released. In a person with <strong>Type 1 diabetes</strong>, the level rises steeply and stays high because no insulin is produced. In a person with <strong>Type 2 diabetes</strong>, the level rises and falls more slowly than normal because cells respond poorly to insulin.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37">When interpreting these graphs, describe <strong>what happens</strong> (the glucose level rises/falls), explain <strong>why</strong> (insulin is/is not produced or cells do/do not respond), and compare the <strong>rate and peak</strong> of the blood glucose changes between the individuals shown.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n38">When comparing Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes, always state the <strong>cause</strong>, the <strong>treatment</strong>, and the <strong>risk factors</strong> for each. A common exam mistake is saying Type 2 diabetes means &ldquo;the pancreas does not produce enough insulin&rdquo; &mdash; the correct answer is that <strong>cells no longer respond to insulin</strong>. For negative feedback questions, describe the full loop: stimulus &rarr; receptor (pancreas) &rarr; hormone released &rarr; target organ response &rarr; condition returns to normal &rarr; hormone secretion reduces.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n39">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n40">The endocrine system uses <strong>hormones</strong> carried in the blood to produce slower, longer-lasting responses than the nervous system. The <strong>pituitary gland</strong> is the master gland that controls other glands.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Blood glucose is controlled by <strong>insulin</strong> (lowers glucose, converts to glycogen) and <strong>glucagon</strong> (raises glucose, converts glycogen back to glucose) in a <strong>negative feedback</strong> cycle.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42"><strong>Type 1 diabetes</strong> = pancreas produces no insulin (treat with injections). <strong>Type 2 diabetes</strong> = cells do not respond to insulin (treat with diet/exercise). Obesity is the main risk factor for Type 2.</li></ul>
