# The Heart, Blood and Circulatory Disease

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Double Circulatory System</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The human body uses a <dfn class="term" data-def="A circulatory system in which blood passes through the heart twice on each complete circuit of the body &mdash; once to the lungs and once to the rest of the body.">double circulatory system</dfn>. This means blood passes through the heart twice during each full circuit. One loop carries blood from the heart to the lungs and back (the pulmonary circuit), where oxygen is picked up and carbon dioxide is removed. The second loop carries oxygenated blood from the heart to the rest of the body and returns deoxygenated blood back to the heart (the systemic circuit).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">A double circulatory system is more efficient than a single one because blood returning from the lungs is pumped a second time by the heart before reaching body tissues. This maintains high pressure, ensuring organs receive oxygen quickly enough for aerobic respiration.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In a double circulatory system, blood enters the heart twice on each journey around the body: once from the lungs (oxygenated) and once from the body (deoxygenated).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Structure of the Heart</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A muscular organ that pumps blood around the body through a network of blood vessels.">heart</dfn> is a muscular organ divided into four chambers. The two upper chambers are called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The two upper chambers of the heart that receive blood from veins.">atria</dfn> (singular: atrium) and the two lower chambers are the <dfn class="term" data-def="The two lower chambers of the heart that pump blood out through arteries.">ventricles</dfn>. The right side of the heart handles deoxygenated blood, while the left side handles oxygenated blood. Valves between the chambers prevent blood from flowing backwards.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The left ventricle has a thicker muscular wall than the right ventricle because it needs to pump blood at high pressure all the way around the body. The right ventricle only pumps blood the shorter distance to the lungs.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Coronary arteries:</strong> Small arteries that branch off the aorta and supply the heart muscle itself with oxygenated blood and nutrients.</p>

  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The natural resting heart rate is controlled by a group of cells in the right atrium that act as a <dfn class="term" data-def="A group of cells in the right atrium of the heart that produce electrical impulses to regulate the heartbeat.">pacemaker</dfn>. If the natural pacemaker is faulty, an artificial pacemaker &mdash; a small electrical device &mdash; can be implanted to correct irregular heart rhythms.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Types of Blood Vessel</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">The body contains three types of blood vessel, each adapted to its function.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Arteries</strong> carry blood away from the heart at high pressure. They have thick, elastic, muscular walls to withstand the surge of blood pumped with each heartbeat. Arteries branch into smaller vessels called arterioles, which eventually lead to capillaries.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Veins</strong> carry blood back towards the heart at low pressure. They have thinner walls than arteries and contain valves to prevent the backflow of blood. Skeletal muscles around veins help squeeze blood along.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Capillaries</strong> are microscopic vessels only one cell thick. They form networks that pass close to every cell in the body. Their thin walls allow the exchange of substances such as oxygen, carbon dioxide, glucose, and urea between the blood and surrounding tissues by diffusion.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Blood and Its Components</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><dfn class="term" data-def="A tissue made up of plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets that transports substances around the body.">Blood</dfn> is classified as a tissue. It consists of four main components, each with a specific role.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Plasma</strong> is the liquid, straw-coloured part of blood. It makes up about 55% of blood volume. <dfn class="term" data-def="The liquid component of blood that transports dissolved substances including glucose, amino acids, carbon dioxide, urea, hormones, and antibodies.">Plasma</dfn> transports dissolved substances around the body, including glucose and amino acids (to cells), carbon dioxide (to the lungs), urea (to the kidneys for excretion), hormones, and antibodies.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Red blood cells</strong> transport oxygen from the lungs to body tissues. They are <dfn class="term" data-def="Shaped like a disc that is thinner in the centre than at the edges, increasing the surface area to volume ratio for efficient gas exchange.">biconcave</dfn> in shape (like a flattened disc), which increases the surface area to volume ratio for efficient oxygen absorption. Red blood cells contain <dfn class="term" data-def="An iron-containing protein in red blood cells that binds reversibly with oxygen to form oxyhaemoglobin.">haemoglobin</dfn> &mdash; a protein that binds with oxygen in the lungs to form oxyhaemoglobin, and releases it where oxygen concentration is lower in body tissues. Red blood cells have no nucleus, leaving more room for haemoglobin.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>White blood cells</strong> are part of the immune system. They defend the body against infection by engulfing pathogens (phagocytosis) or producing antibodies and antitoxins. Unlike red blood cells, white blood cells have a nucleus.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Platelets</strong> are small cell fragments (without a nucleus) that help blood to clot at the site of a wound. <dfn class="term" data-def="Small cell fragments in the blood that help form clots at wound sites to prevent blood loss and infection.">Platelets</dfn> gather at the wound, triggering a cascade of reactions that form a mesh of fibrin threads, creating a scab. This prevents excessive blood loss and stops pathogens from entering.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n28">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Red blood cells are adapted for oxygen transport in three ways: biconcave shape (large surface area), packed with haemoglobin (binds oxygen), and no nucleus (more space for haemoglobin).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n29">Coronary Heart Disease</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><dfn class="term" data-def="A non-communicable disease in which layers of fatty material build up inside the coronary arteries, reducing blood flow to the heart muscle.">Coronary heart disease</dfn> (CHD) is a non-communicable disease. It occurs when layers of fatty material build up inside the <dfn class="term" data-def="Small arteries that branch from the aorta and supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood and glucose.">coronary arteries</dfn>, narrowing them and reducing blood flow. This means the heart muscle receives less oxygen and glucose, which can lead to chest pain (angina) or, in severe cases, a heart attack.</p>

    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Stents:</strong> A <dfn class="term" data-def="A small mesh tube inserted into a narrowed coronary artery to hold it open and restore blood flow to the heart muscle.">stent</dfn> is a small mesh tube inserted into a narrowed coronary artery during surgery. It holds the artery open, restoring normal blood flow. Stents are effective and relatively low risk, but the artery can narrow again over time, and the patient needs to take blood-thinning medication.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Statins:</strong> These are drugs that reduce blood cholesterol levels, slowing down the rate at which fatty deposits build up inside arteries. Statins must be taken long-term and can cause side effects such as headaches, muscle pain, and liver damage in some patients.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Faulty heart valves:</strong> If heart valves become faulty &mdash; either not opening fully or developing a leak &mdash; they can be replaced with biological valves (from human donors or other animals) or mechanical valves (made from metal or polymers). Faulty valves reduce the efficiency of blood flow through the heart.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Heart transplants and artificial hearts:</strong> In cases of complete heart failure, a donor heart (or heart and lungs) can be transplanted. Artificial hearts are sometimes used temporarily to keep patients alive while waiting for a transplant, or to allow a damaged heart time to rest and recover. They are not a permanent solution.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n37">When answering questions about blood vessels, always link structure to function &mdash; for example, &ldquo;arteries have thick elastic walls <em>because</em> they must withstand high pressure.&rdquo; For 6-mark &ldquo;extended response&rdquo; questions on coronary heart disease treatments, make sure you evaluate both advantages <em>and</em> disadvantages of each treatment, and reach a clear conclusion. Command words like &ldquo;evaluate&rdquo; require you to weigh up evidence and make a judgement.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n38">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n39">The heart is part of a double circulatory system &mdash; blood is pumped to the lungs then pumped again to the body. Know the four chambers, the four key blood vessels (aorta, vena cava, pulmonary artery, pulmonary vein), and the coronary arteries.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40">Blood has four components: plasma (transports dissolved substances), red blood cells (carry oxygen via haemoglobin), white blood cells (fight infection), and platelets (clotting).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Coronary heart disease is caused by fatty deposits narrowing the coronary arteries. Treatments include stents, statins, valve replacements, transplants, and artificial hearts &mdash; each with specific advantages and disadvantages you must be able to evaluate.</li></ul>
