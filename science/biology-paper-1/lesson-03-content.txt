# Organisation and the Digestive System

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Principles of Organisation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The human body is organised into a hierarchy of structures, each more complex than the last. <dfn class="term" data-def="The smallest structural and functional unit of a living organism.">Cells</dfn> are the basic building blocks of all living organisms. A group of cells with a similar structure and function is called a <dfn class="term" data-def="A group of similar cells that work together to carry out a particular function.">tissue</dfn>. Tissues are grouped together to form <dfn class="term" data-def="A structure made up of a group of different tissues working together to perform a specific function.">organs</dfn>, and organs are organised into <dfn class="term" data-def="A group of organs that work together to carry out a major body function, such as the digestive system.">organ systems</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The digestive system is a perfect example of an organ system &mdash; several different organs work together to digest food and absorb the nutrients the body needs. Understanding this hierarchy (cells &rarr; tissues &rarr; organs &rarr; organ systems &rarr; organism) is essential for AQA Biology, as it shows how the body is organised to carry out complex functions efficiently.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The hierarchy of organisation in the body is: <strong>cells &rarr; tissues &rarr; organs &rarr; organ systems &rarr; organism</strong>. The digestive system is an organ system in which the stomach, small intestine, pancreas, liver, and other organs work together to break down and absorb food.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Human Digestive System</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The role of the digestive system is to break down large, insoluble food molecules into small, soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream and transported to cells. This process involves both <strong>mechanical digestion</strong> (physical breakdown by chewing and churning) and <strong>chemical digestion</strong> (breakdown by <dfn class="term" data-def="Biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions in living organisms. Each enzyme has a specific active site that fits only one substrate.">enzymes</dfn>).</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7">The digestive system includes the following organs, each with a specific role:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Mouth:</strong> Food is chewed into smaller pieces (mechanical digestion) and mixed with <dfn class="term" data-def="A fluid produced by the salivary glands in the mouth, containing the enzyme amylase which begins the digestion of starch.">saliva</dfn>, which contains the enzyme amylase. This begins the chemical digestion of starch into sugars.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Oesophagus:</strong> A muscular tube that pushes food from the mouth to the stomach by peristalsis (waves of muscle contraction).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Stomach:</strong> A muscular organ that churns food and mixes it with <strong>hydrochloric acid</strong> and <strong>protease enzymes</strong>. The acid kills bacteria and provides the acidic pH (around pH 2) that stomach proteases need to work.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Small intestine:</strong> Where the final stages of chemical digestion occur and where the products of digestion are <strong>absorbed</strong> into the bloodstream. The inner wall is covered in tiny finger-like projections called <dfn class="term" data-def="Tiny finger-like projections lining the inner wall of the small intestine that increase the surface area for absorption of digested food molecules.">villi</dfn>, which increase the surface area for absorption.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Large intestine:</strong> Absorbs water from undigested food, forming solid faeces.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Pancreas:</strong> A gland that produces protease, lipase, and amylase enzymes, which are released into the small intestine.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Liver:</strong> Produces <dfn class="term" data-def="An alkaline substance made by the liver and stored in the gall bladder. It neutralises stomach acid and emulsifies fats into small droplets to increase the surface area for lipase.">bile</dfn>, which is stored in the gall bladder and released into the small intestine.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Enzymes and Digestion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Digestive enzymes are <strong>biological catalysts</strong> &mdash; they speed up the breakdown of large food molecules without being used up themselves. Each enzyme has a specifically shaped <dfn class="term" data-def="The region of an enzyme where the substrate binds. Its shape is complementary to the substrate, allowing the enzyme to catalyse a specific reaction.">active site</dfn> that fits only one type of substrate, like a lock and key. This is called the <strong>lock and key theory</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">There are three main groups of digestive enzymes, and you must know what each one does, where it is produced, and what it breaks food down into:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Amylase</strong> (a carbohydrase) breaks down starch into simple sugars (maltose/glucose). It is produced in the <em>salivary glands</em>, the <em>pancreas</em>, and the <em>small intestine</em>.</p>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n19">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Proteases</strong> break down proteins into amino acids. They are produced in the <em>stomach</em> (pepsin, which works best in acidic conditions), the <em>pancreas</em>, and the <em>small intestine</em>.</p>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Lipases</strong> break down lipids (fats and oils) into glycerol and fatty acids. They are produced in the <em>pancreas</em> and the <em>small intestine</em>.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">The Role of Bile</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Bile plays a crucial role in digestion, even though it is not an enzyme. It is made in the <strong>liver</strong> and stored in the <strong>gall bladder</strong>. When food enters the small intestine, bile is released and has two important functions:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>1. Neutralisation:</strong> Bile is <strong>alkaline</strong>, so it neutralises the hydrochloric acid that comes from the stomach. This is important because enzymes in the small intestine work best in slightly alkaline conditions (around pH 7&ndash;8). Without bile, the acid from the stomach would make the pH too low for these enzymes to function.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>2. Emulsification of fats:</strong> Bile <dfn class="term" data-def="The process of breaking down large fat globules into smaller droplets, increasing the surface area available for lipase enzymes to act on.">emulsifies</dfn> fats &mdash; it breaks large fat globules into much smaller droplets. This massively increases the <strong>surface area</strong> of the fat, giving lipase enzymes a much larger area to work on. This speeds up the rate of fat digestion.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n25">Enzymes can only work on the <strong>surface</strong> of food molecules. When bile breaks large fat globules into tiny droplets, the total surface area exposed to lipase increases dramatically. Think of it like this: a whole block of butter has a small surface area, but if you broke it into thousands of tiny pieces, the combined surface area would be far greater. More surface area means more lipase molecules can bind to fat at the same time, so digestion happens much faster.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">Enzymes are proteins, and their activity is affected by <strong>temperature</strong> and <strong>pH</strong>. Each enzyme has an optimum temperature and pH at which it works fastest.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">If the temperature is <strong>too low</strong>, molecules move slowly and collide with the active site less frequently, so the reaction rate is low. As temperature increases, molecules move faster and collide more often, increasing the rate. At the <strong>optimum temperature</strong> (around 37&deg;C for most human enzymes), the rate is at its maximum.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">If the temperature rises <strong>above the optimum</strong>, the enzyme&rsquo;s active site changes shape &mdash; the enzyme is <dfn class="term" data-def="When an enzyme loses its specific shape due to high temperature or extreme pH, so the substrate can no longer fit into the active site and the enzyme stops working.">denatured</dfn>. The substrate no longer fits, and the enzyme can no longer catalyse the reaction. Denaturation is permanent.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">Similarly, extreme changes in <strong>pH</strong> can denature enzymes. Stomach protease (pepsin) works best at pH 2 (very acidic), while enzymes in the small intestine work best at around pH 7&ndash;8 (slightly alkaline). This is why bile&rsquo;s role in neutralising stomach acid is so important.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Products of Digestion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Once food has been fully digested, the small, soluble molecules are absorbed through the walls of the small intestine into the bloodstream. These products are then used by the body:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Simple sugars</strong> (glucose) are used in <strong>respiration</strong> to release energy, or converted into starch, glycogen, or cellulose for storage. <strong>Amino acids</strong> are used to build new <strong>proteins</strong> for growth and repair. <strong>Glycerol and fatty acids</strong> are used to build new <strong>lipid</strong> molecules for cell membranes and energy storage.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n35">In AQA Trilogy exams, enzyme questions are very common. Always name the <em>specific enzyme</em>, the <em>substrate</em> it acts on, and the <em>products</em> formed (e.g. &ldquo;Lipase breaks down lipids into glycerol and fatty acids&rdquo;). For bile questions, remember that bile is <strong>not an enzyme</strong> &mdash; it emulsifies fats and neutralises acid. If asked about factors affecting enzyme activity, explain <em>why</em> using the lock and key model: at high temperatures, the active site changes shape (denatures), so the substrate no longer fits.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n36">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n37">The digestive system is organised as cells &rarr; tissues &rarr; organs &rarr; organ systems. The stomach, small intestine, pancreas, and liver all work together to digest and absorb food.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Three enzyme groups do the chemical work: amylase (starch &rarr; sugars), proteases (proteins &rarr; amino acids), and lipases (lipids &rarr; glycerol + fatty acids). Know where each is produced.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">Bile is not an enzyme &mdash; it neutralises stomach acid and emulsifies fats to increase surface area for lipase, speeding up fat digestion.</li></ul>
