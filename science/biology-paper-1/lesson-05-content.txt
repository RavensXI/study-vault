# Health, Non-Communicable Diseases and Plant Tissues

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Health and Disease</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="A state of complete physical and mental well-being, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.">Health</dfn> is defined as a state of physical and mental well-being. It is not simply the absence of disease. Many factors affect health, including diet, stress, and life situations, and these can have a profound effect on both physical and mental well-being.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Diseases fall into two broad categories. <dfn class="term" data-def="Diseases caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, protists) that can be passed from one organism to another.">Communicable diseases</dfn> are caused by pathogens and can spread between organisms. <dfn class="term" data-def="Diseases that cannot be transmitted between organisms &mdash; they are often long-term and linked to lifestyle or genetic factors.">Non-communicable diseases</dfn> (NCDs) cannot be passed from person to person and are often linked to lifestyle or genetic factors. Both types are major causes of ill health worldwide.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Different types of disease can interact. For example, viruses living in cells can trigger cancers, immune system defects increase susceptibility to infections, immune reactions caused by pathogens can trigger allergies such as asthma, and severe physical ill health can lead to depression and other mental illness.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Risk Factors for Non-Communicable Diseases</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><dfn class="term" data-def="Something that increases the likelihood of developing a disease &mdash; can be a lifestyle choice, a substance in the body, or an environmental factor.">Risk factors</dfn> are linked to an increased rate of a disease. They can be aspects of a person&rsquo;s lifestyle, substances in their body, or substances in their environment. For some risk factors, a causal mechanism has been scientifically proven. For others, only a correlation has been identified.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">The AQA spec requires you to know several key risk factor links:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Diet, smoking, and exercise</strong> affect the risk of <strong>cardiovascular disease</strong>. A diet high in saturated fat raises blood cholesterol, which can lead to fatty deposits in arteries.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Obesity</strong> is a risk factor for <strong>Type 2 diabetes</strong>. Excess body fat can make cells resistant to insulin.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Alcohol</strong> affects the <strong>liver</strong> (causing cirrhosis) and <strong>brain function</strong> (impairing judgement and reaction times).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Smoking</strong> is a risk factor for <strong>lung disease</strong> and <strong>lung cancer</strong>. Tar in cigarettes contains carcinogens that damage lung tissue.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Smoking and alcohol during pregnancy</strong> affect <strong>unborn babies</strong>, causing low birth weight, premature birth, and foetal alcohol syndrome.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Carcinogens</strong>, including <strong>ionising radiation</strong> (such as UV light and X-rays), are risk factors for <strong>cancer</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Many non-communicable diseases are caused by the interaction of several factors rather than a single cause. Students must be able to discuss the human and financial cost of NCDs to individuals, local communities, nations, and globally.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Cancer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Cancer is the result of changes in cells that lead to uncontrolled growth and division. Normally, cell division is tightly controlled by genes. When these control mechanisms fail &mdash; due to mutations in DNA &mdash; cells can divide rapidly and form a mass called a <dfn class="term" data-def="An abnormal mass of cells formed when cells divide uncontrollably. Can be benign (contained) or malignant (cancerous and invasive).">tumour</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">There are two types of tumour. <dfn class="term" data-def="A tumour that is contained in one area, usually within a membrane, and does not spread to other parts of the body.">Benign tumours</dfn> are growths of abnormal cells that are contained in one area, usually within a membrane. They do not invade other parts of the body and are not cancerous, though they can cause problems if they press on nearby organs.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><dfn class="term" data-def="A cancerous tumour whose cells can invade neighbouring tissues and spread to other parts of the body through the blood, forming secondary tumours.">Malignant tumours</dfn> are cancers. Their cells invade neighbouring tissues and can spread to different parts of the body in the blood, where they form <dfn class="term" data-def="New tumours that form when malignant cells break away from the primary tumour and travel through the blood to other parts of the body.">secondary tumours</dfn>. This spread of cancer cells is what makes malignant tumours dangerous and potentially fatal.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n21">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Benign tumours</strong> = contained, do not spread, not cancer. <strong>Malignant tumours</strong> = invade other tissues, spread through blood, form secondary tumours, ARE cancer. The key distinction the exam tests is whether the tumour can spread (metastasise).</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n22">Scientists have identified both <strong>lifestyle risk factors</strong> (such as smoking, obesity, UV exposure, and drinking alcohol) and <strong>genetic risk factors</strong> for various types of cancer. Having a risk factor does not guarantee someone will develop cancer &mdash; it increases the probability.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Malignant:</strong> Cells grow rapidly, invade surrounding healthy tissue, can break away and travel in the blood, form secondary tumours at distant sites, can be life-threatening. Treatment may include surgery, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Plant Tissues</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Just like animals, plants are organised into tissues, organs, and organ systems. A plant tissue is a group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function. The AQA spec requires knowledge of five key plant tissues:</p>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Epidermal tissue:</strong> Covers the outer surface of the plant. It produces a waxy cuticle on leaves to reduce water loss.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Palisade mesophyll:</strong> Found near the upper surface of the leaf. Packed with chloroplasts for photosynthesis.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Spongy mesophyll:</strong> Found below the palisade layer. Has air spaces to allow gases (carbon dioxide and oxygen) to diffuse through the leaf.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Plant tissue that transports water and dissolved mineral ions from the roots to the stems and leaves. Made of hollow, dead cells strengthened by lignin.">Xylem</dfn>:</strong> Transports water and dissolved mineral ions from roots to stems and leaves. Composed of hollow, dead cells strengthened by lignin.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Plant tissue that transports dissolved sugars from the leaves to the rest of the plant for use or storage. Made of elongated living cells with pores in their end walls.">Phloem</dfn>:</strong> Transports dissolved sugars from leaves to the rest of the plant. Composed of elongated living cells with pores in their end walls allowing cell sap to pass through.</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Meristem tissue</strong> is found at the growing tips of shoots and roots. It contains rapidly dividing undifferentiated cells that can develop into any type of plant cell, enabling growth throughout the plant&rsquo;s life.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n33">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The leaf is a plant organ. For the exam, you must know the tissues within it: epidermis (with waxy cuticle), palisade mesophyll (photosynthesis), spongy mesophyll (gas exchange), xylem and phloem (transport), and guard cells surrounding stomata (control gas exchange and water loss).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n34">The Plant Organ System</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n35">The roots, stem, and leaves form a plant organ system for the transport of substances. <dfn class="term" data-def="The loss of water vapour from the surface of a plant, mainly through the stomata of the leaves, creating a continuous flow of water up through the xylem.">Transpiration</dfn> is the loss of water vapour from the surface of a plant, mainly through open stomata in the leaves. As water evaporates from the leaf surface, more water is pulled up through the xylem from the roots &mdash; this continuous flow is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The continuous movement of water from roots through the xylem to the leaves, driven by transpiration.">transpiration stream</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36">The rate of transpiration is affected by four environmental factors: increasing <strong>temperature</strong>, decreasing <strong>humidity</strong>, increasing <strong>air movement</strong> (wind), and increasing <strong>light intensity</strong> all increase the rate of transpiration. Guard cells open and close the stomata to balance gas exchange for photosynthesis against water loss.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><dfn class="term" data-def="The transport of dissolved sugars through phloem tissue from the leaves (where they are made by photosynthesis) to the rest of the plant for immediate use or storage.">Translocation</dfn> is the movement of dissolved sugars through phloem from the leaves (where sugars are made by photosynthesis) to the rest of the plant for immediate use or storage. Unlike transpiration, translocation requires energy and moves substances in both directions.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n38"><strong>Root hair cells</strong> are adapted for efficient uptake of water by osmosis and mineral ions by active transport. Their long, thin extensions increase the surface area in contact with soil water.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n39"><strong>Temperature:</strong> Higher temperature increases the kinetic energy of water molecules, so more evaporate from the leaf surface. <strong>Humidity:</strong> Lower humidity means a steeper concentration gradient between the inside of the leaf and the outside air, so water diffuses out faster. <strong>Air movement:</strong> Wind carries away water vapour from the leaf surface, maintaining a steep diffusion gradient. <strong>Light intensity:</strong> More light causes stomata to open wider for photosynthesis, allowing more water to escape.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n40">For questions on non-communicable diseases, always name the <strong>specific risk factor</strong> and <strong>link it to the specific disease</strong> (e.g. &ldquo;smoking increases the risk of lung cancer because tar contains carcinogens&rdquo;). For compare questions on tumours, structure your answer clearly: state the feature, describe it for benign, then describe it for malignant. For plant transport questions, don&rsquo;t confuse xylem (water, up only, dead cells) with phloem (sugars, both directions, living cells).</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n41">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n42">Health is complete physical and mental well-being. Risk factors (diet, smoking, alcohol, obesity, carcinogens) increase the chance of non-communicable diseases &mdash; many NCDs involve multiple interacting factors.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n43">Benign tumours are contained and do not spread; malignant tumours invade tissues and spread via the blood to form secondary tumours. Both lifestyle and genetic factors affect cancer risk.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44">Xylem transports water upward (dead, hollow, lignin-strengthened cells); phloem transports sugars in both directions (living cells with pores). Transpiration rate increases with temperature, wind, light, and lower humidity.</li></ul>
