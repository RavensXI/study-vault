# Photosynthesis and Respiration

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Photosynthesis &mdash; The Key Reaction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="An endothermic chemical reaction in which plants use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.">Photosynthesis</dfn> is the process by which green plants and algae make their own food. It takes place in the <dfn class="term" data-def="Organelles found in plant cells and algae that contain chlorophyll and are the site of photosynthesis.">chloroplasts</dfn>, which contain the green pigment <dfn class="term" data-def="A green pigment found in chloroplasts that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis.">chlorophyll</dfn>. Chlorophyll absorbs light energy, which is then used to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The word equation is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>carbon dioxide + water &xrarr; glucose + oxygen</strong><br>In symbols: <strong>6CO&#8322; + 6H&#8322;O &xrarr; C&#8326;H&#8321;&#8322;O&#8326; + 6O&#8322;</strong><br>Photosynthesis is an <strong>endothermic</strong> reaction &mdash; energy is transferred from the environment to the chloroplasts by light.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">You must be able to recognise the chemical symbols CO&#8322;, H&#8322;O, O&#8322; and C&#8326;H&#8321;&#8322;O&#8326;. The exam may ask you to write the equation in words or symbols, or to identify photosynthesis as endothermic.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Rate of Photosynthesis</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Several factors affect how fast photosynthesis occurs. The three main factors are <dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of light falling on a plant per unit area &mdash; one of the key limiting factors for the rate of photosynthesis.">light intensity</dfn>, <dfn class="term" data-def="The proportion of carbon dioxide in the surrounding air &mdash; a limiting factor for photosynthesis.">carbon dioxide concentration</dfn>, and temperature. The amount of chlorophyll also affects the rate &mdash; plants with fewer chloroplasts or yellow leaves photosynthesise more slowly.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Each of these factors can become a <dfn class="term" data-def="A factor that limits the rate of a reaction when it is in short supply, even if other factors are present in abundance.">limiting factor</dfn>. A limiting factor is whichever factor is in shortest supply at a given time. For example, on a bright sunny day, CO&#8322; concentration may be the limiting factor because there is plenty of light but not enough carbon dioxide for the plant to photosynthesise any faster.</p>

Beyond that point, the graph levels off because another factor (such as CO&#8322; or temperature) becomes limiting. At very low light, photosynthesis stops entirely.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Carbon dioxide concentration:</strong> Increasing CO&#8322; concentration increases the rate of photosynthesis until something else becomes limiting. In greenhouses, farmers may add extra CO&#8322; to boost plant growth.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Temperature:</strong> Photosynthesis is controlled by enzymes. As temperature rises, the rate increases because molecules have more kinetic energy. However, above about 40&ndash;45&deg;C, enzymes denature and the rate drops sharply to zero.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Chlorophyll:</strong> Plants affected by disease, mineral deficiency, or genetic conditions may have less chlorophyll, reducing the rate of photosynthesis.</p>

  <p><strong>Required Practical 6:</strong> You must know how to investigate the effect of light intensity on the rate of photosynthesis using pondweed. Count oxygen bubbles or measure the volume of gas collected at different distances from a lamp. The independent variable is light intensity (distance from lamp) and the dependent variable is the rate of gas production.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Uses of Glucose</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">The glucose produced by photosynthesis is not just used for energy. Plants use it for several purposes:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Respiration:</strong> Glucose is broken down in <dfn class="term" data-def="The process of breaking down glucose to transfer energy for life processes &mdash; occurs continuously in every living cell.">respiration</dfn> to transfer energy for life processes. <strong>Storage as starch:</strong> Glucose is converted into insoluble starch, which can be stored in leaves, roots, and tubers without affecting water balance. <strong>Storage as fats and oils:</strong> Seeds often store energy as lipids. <strong>Cellulose:</strong> Glucose molecules are joined together to make cellulose, which strengthens cell walls. <strong>Amino acids and proteins:</strong> Glucose is combined with nitrate ions absorbed from the soil to make amino acids, which are then built into proteins.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Aerobic Respiration</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><dfn class="term" data-def="Respiration that uses oxygen to break down glucose completely, releasing a large amount of energy, carbon dioxide, and water.">Aerobic respiration</dfn> is the process of breaking down glucose using oxygen. It happens continuously in every living cell &mdash; in both animals and plants. It is an exothermic reaction, meaning it transfers energy to the surroundings.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">The equation for aerobic respiration is:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>glucose + oxygen &xrarr; carbon dioxide + water</strong><br>In symbols: <strong>C&#8326;H&#8321;&#8322;O&#8326; + 6O&#8322; &xrarr; 6CO&#8322; + 6H&#8322;O</strong><br>This is the reverse of the photosynthesis equation. Aerobic respiration transfers <strong>much more energy</strong> than anaerobic respiration.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n21">The energy released by respiration is used for chemical reactions to build larger molecules, for movement, and for keeping warm. Respiration is not the same as breathing &mdash; breathing is the mechanical process that ventilates the lungs, while respiration is the chemical reaction inside cells.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Anaerobic Respiration</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><dfn class="term" data-def="Respiration without oxygen that incompletely breaks down glucose, transferring much less energy than aerobic respiration.">Anaerobic respiration</dfn> occurs when cells cannot get enough oxygen. Because the oxidation of glucose is incomplete, far less energy is transferred compared to aerobic respiration.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>In animal muscles:</strong> glucose &xrarr; lactic acid. The build-up of <dfn class="term" data-def="A toxic by-product of anaerobic respiration in muscles that causes fatigue and cramp.">lactic acid</dfn> causes muscles to become fatigued and stop contracting efficiently. An <dfn class="term" data-def="The extra oxygen the body needs after vigorous exercise to break down the lactic acid that has accumulated in muscles.">oxygen debt</dfn> builds up &mdash; this is the extra oxygen needed after exercise to break down the accumulated lactic acid in the liver, where it is converted back to glucose.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>In plants and yeast:</strong> glucose &xrarr; ethanol + carbon dioxide. This process is called <dfn class="term" data-def="Anaerobic respiration in yeast cells, producing ethanol and carbon dioxide &mdash; used commercially in bread-making and brewing.">fermentation</dfn> and has important economic uses: brewing alcoholic drinks and making bread rise (CO&#8322; creates bubbles in dough).</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Response to Exercise</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">During exercise, the body&rsquo;s demand for energy increases. To supply the muscles with more oxygenated blood, the body responds by increasing heart rate, breathing rate, and breath volume (the volume of air in each breath).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">If the muscles cannot get oxygen fast enough during vigorous exercise, anaerobic respiration takes over. Lactic acid accumulates in the muscles, causing fatigue and pain. After exercise, you continue to breathe heavily to repay the oxygen debt. The blood carries lactic acid to the liver, where oxygen is used to convert it back into glucose.</p>

This is why you pant after sprinting &mdash; your body is taking in extra oxygen to break down the lactic acid in the liver. The heart rate and breathing rate remain elevated until the oxygen debt is repaid.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Metabolism</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><dfn class="term" data-def="The sum of all the chemical reactions that take place in a cell or the body, powered by energy from respiration.">Metabolism</dfn> is the sum of all the chemical reactions in a cell or the body. The energy transferred by respiration drives these enzyme-controlled reactions. You need to know the key metabolic processes:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Building up (anabolic):</strong> Conversion of glucose to starch, glycogen, and cellulose. Formation of lipid molecules from one glycerol and three fatty acids. Use of glucose and nitrate ions to form amino acids, then proteins. <strong>Breaking down (catabolic):</strong> Respiration itself (breaking glucose down for energy). Breakdown of excess proteins to form urea for excretion by the kidneys (deamination).</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n33">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Metabolism links together many topics across the biology specification. Remember: <strong>respiration provides the energy</strong> for all metabolic reactions. Glucose &xrarr; starch/glycogen/cellulose. Glycerol + 3 fatty acids &xrarr; lipids. Glucose + nitrates &xrarr; amino acids &xrarr; proteins. Excess proteins &xrarr; urea (excreted by kidneys).</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">When comparing aerobic and anaerobic respiration in the exam, set up a clear table or use structured paragraphs covering three aspects: whether oxygen is needed, the products, and the relative amount of energy transferred. For calculation questions on photosynthesis rate, remember that light intensity follows an inverse square relationship with distance &mdash; if you halve the distance from the lamp, light intensity quadruples. Always show your working for full marks.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">Photosynthesis (endothermic) converts CO&#8322; + H&#8322;O into glucose + O&#8322; using light energy in chloroplasts. Limiting factors are light intensity, CO&#8322; concentration, temperature, and chlorophyll.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Aerobic respiration (exothermic) transfers much more energy than anaerobic respiration. In muscles, anaerobic respiration produces lactic acid and creates an oxygen debt; in yeast, it produces ethanol + CO&#8322; (fermentation).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Metabolism is the sum of all reactions in the body &mdash; powered by energy from respiration. Key examples include glucose &xrarr; starch, glycerol + fatty acids &xrarr; lipids, and excess proteins &xrarr; urea.</li></ul>
