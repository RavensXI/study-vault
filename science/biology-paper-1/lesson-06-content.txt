# Communicable Diseases and Human Defences

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Communicable Diseases?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A disease that can be spread from one organism to another.">communicable disease</dfn> is an illness caused by a <dfn class="term" data-def="A microorganism that causes disease, such as a bacterium, virus, protist or fungus.">pathogen</dfn> &mdash; a microorganism that enters the body and causes infection. Pathogens include bacteria, viruses, fungi and protists. They can spread between organisms by direct contact, by water, by air (droplet inhalation) or by vectors such as insects.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Once inside the body, bacteria and viruses reproduce rapidly. Bacteria release <dfn class="term" data-def="Poisonous substances produced by bacteria that damage tissues and cause symptoms of illness.">toxins</dfn> that damage tissues and make us feel ill. Viruses work differently &mdash; they invade living cells, take over the cell&rsquo;s machinery to make copies of themselves, and destroy the host cell in the process.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Pathogens are microorganisms that cause infectious disease. The four types are bacteria, viruses, fungi and protists. Bacteria produce toxins; viruses damage cells by reproducing inside them.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Viral Diseases</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><strong>Measles</strong> is caused by a virus spread by inhalation of droplets from sneezes and coughs. Symptoms include fever and a red skin rash. Measles is a serious illness that can be fatal if complications arise, which is why most young children in the UK are vaccinated against it.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>HIV</strong> (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) initially causes a flu-like illness. It is spread by sexual contact or exchange of body fluids, such as sharing needles. Unless controlled with <dfn class="term" data-def="Drugs used to manage HIV infection by slowing the virus's reproduction inside the body.">antiretroviral drugs</dfn>, HIV attacks the body&rsquo;s immune cells. Late-stage HIV infection is called AIDS &mdash; when the immune system is so damaged it can no longer fight other infections or cancers.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV)</strong> is a widespread plant pathogen. It causes a distinctive &ldquo;mosaic&rdquo; pattern of discolouration on leaves, reducing <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which plants use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.">photosynthesis</dfn> and stunting the plant&rsquo;s growth.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Salmonella</strong> is a type of food poisoning caused by bacteria. It is spread by eating contaminated food or food prepared in unhygienic conditions. Symptoms include fever, abdominal cramps, vomiting and diarrhoea &mdash; caused both by the bacteria themselves and the toxins they secrete. In the UK, poultry are vaccinated against Salmonella to help control its spread.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Gonorrhoea</strong> is a sexually transmitted disease (STD) caused by a bacterium. Symptoms include a thick yellow or green discharge from the vagina or penis and pain when urinating. It was once easily treated with penicillin, but many antibiotic-resistant strains have now appeared. The spread of gonorrhoea can be reduced by using barrier methods of contraception such as condoms, and by treating infected individuals with antibiotics.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Rose black spot</strong> is a fungal disease of plants. Purple or black spots develop on leaves, which often turn yellow and drop early. This reduces photosynthesis and slows the plant&rsquo;s growth. The fungus spreads through the environment by water or wind. It can be treated with fungicides or by removing and destroying affected leaves.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Malaria</strong> is caused by a protist pathogen. The malarial protist is spread by mosquitoes, which act as <dfn class="term" data-def="An organism that spreads a pathogen between hosts without being affected by the disease itself.">vectors</dfn> &mdash; they carry the protist from person to person when they bite. Malaria causes recurrent episodes of fever and can be fatal. Its spread is controlled by preventing mosquitoes from breeding (draining stagnant water) and by using mosquito nets to avoid bites.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Human Defence Systems</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">The human body has two lines of defence against pathogens. The first is a set of <strong>non-specific defences</strong> &mdash; physical and chemical barriers that try to stop pathogens entering the body in the first place.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Non-specific defences include: the <strong>skin</strong> (acts as a physical barrier), the <strong>nose</strong> (hairs and mucus trap particles), the <strong>trachea and bronchi</strong> (lined with mucus and cilia that sweep trapped pathogens to the back of the throat), and the <strong>stomach</strong> (produces hydrochloric acid that kills most pathogens).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">The Immune Response</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">If pathogens get past the non-specific defences, the body&rsquo;s <dfn class="term" data-def="The body's internal system that identifies and destroys pathogens using white blood cells.">immune system</dfn> is triggered. White blood cells are the key players in this response. They defend the body in three main ways:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>1. <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which white blood cells engulf and digest pathogens.">phagocytosis</dfn></strong> &mdash; Some white blood cells (phagocytes) detect pathogens and engulf them. They surround the pathogen, take it into the cell and digest it with enzymes. This is a non-specific response &mdash; phagocytes attack any invading pathogen.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>2. Antibody production</strong> &mdash; Other white blood cells (lymphocytes) recognise specific <dfn class="term" data-def="Unique proteins on the surface of a pathogen that trigger an immune response.">antigens</dfn> on the surface of a pathogen. Each pathogen has unique antigens. The lymphocytes produce specific <dfn class="term" data-def="Proteins produced by white blood cells that bind to specific antigens on a pathogen, marking it for destruction.">antibodies</dfn> that lock onto these antigens and mark the pathogen for destruction. Antibodies are specific &mdash; each type only works against one particular pathogen.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>3. Antitoxin production</strong> &mdash; Some white blood cells produce <dfn class="term" data-def="Substances produced by white blood cells that neutralise the toxins released by bacteria.">antitoxins</dfn> that neutralise the toxins produced by bacteria, reducing the damage to tissues.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n21">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>White blood cells defend the body by: (1) phagocytosis &mdash; engulfing and digesting pathogens, (2) producing antibodies that target specific antigens, and (3) producing antitoxins that neutralise bacterial toxins.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n23">In the exam, you may be asked to <strong>describe</strong> how a named disease is spread and prevented, or to <strong>explain</strong> the role of white blood cells. A common mistake is confusing antibodies with antitoxins &mdash; remember that antibodies target specific antigens on pathogens, while antitoxins neutralise the poisons (toxins) that bacteria release. For extended response questions on this topic, structure your answer by type of pathogen (virus, bacterium, fungus, protist) and give a named example for each.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n24">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n25">Pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi and protists) cause communicable diseases and can spread by contact, air, water or vectors.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n26">Non-specific defences (skin, nose, trachea, stomach acid) form the body&rsquo;s first barrier against infection.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n27">White blood cells fight infection through phagocytosis, antibody production and antitoxin production.</li>
</ul>
