# Cell Structure and Microscopy

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Eukaryotic and Prokaryotic Cells</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All living things are made of cells. Cells are divided into two fundamental types: <dfn class="term" data-def="A cell with a true nucleus enclosed by a membrane, and membrane-bound organelles such as mitochondria. Animal cells and plant cells are eukaryotic.">eukaryotic</dfn> cells and <dfn class="term" data-def="A small, simple cell with no true nucleus. The genetic material is a single loop of DNA that floats freely in the cytoplasm. Bacteria are prokaryotic.">prokaryotic</dfn> cells. Understanding the differences between these two types is a key part of AQA spec 4.1.1.1 and regularly tested in exams.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Eukaryotic cells include both animal and plant cells. They have a <strong>nucleus</strong> that encloses their genetic material, along with other membrane-bound <dfn class="term" data-def="A specialised structure within a cell that carries out a specific function, such as the nucleus, mitochondria or ribosomes.">organelle</dfn>s such as mitochondria and ribosomes. Prokaryotic cells &mdash; bacteria &mdash; are much smaller and simpler. They lack a true nucleus: instead, their genetic material is a single loop of DNA that floats freely in the cytoplasm. Prokaryotes may also contain small rings of extra DNA called <dfn class="term" data-def="Small rings of extra DNA found in prokaryotic cells. They can replicate independently and often carry genes for antibiotic resistance.">plasmid</dfn>s.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Eukaryotic cells (animal and plant) have a nucleus that encloses their DNA. Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) have no true nucleus &mdash; their DNA is a single loop in the cytoplasm, and they may also contain plasmids.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">A typical animal cell is around 10&ndash;30 &micro;m (micrometres) in diameter. A prokaryotic bacterial cell is much smaller, typically 1&ndash;5 &micro;m. You need to be comfortable with order of magnitude calculations using standard form, and the prefixes centi (10<sup>&minus;2</sup>), milli (10<sup>&minus;3</sup>), micro (10<sup>&minus;6</sup>) and nano (10<sup>&minus;9</sup>).</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Animal and Plant Cells</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Both animal and plant cells are eukaryotic, but they contain different organelles. You must be able to name each organelle and explain how its structure relates to its function (spec 4.1.1.2).</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Animal Cell Organelles and Their Functions</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Nucleus</strong> &mdash; contains the cell&rsquo;s genetic material (DNA) and controls the cell&rsquo;s activities. The DNA carries the instructions for making proteins.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Cytoplasm</strong> &mdash; a jelly-like substance where most chemical reactions take place. Enzymes in the cytoplasm control these metabolic reactions.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Cell membrane</strong> &mdash; a thin layer that surrounds the cell and controls which substances enter and leave. It is selectively permeable.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Organelles found in the cytoplasm where aerobic respiration takes place, transferring energy for the cell&rsquo;s processes.">Mitochondria</dfn></strong> &mdash; the site of aerobic respiration, where energy is transferred from glucose. Cells that need lots of energy (such as muscle cells) contain more mitochondria.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n12"><strong>Ribosomes</strong> &mdash; tiny structures in the cytoplasm where protein synthesis occurs. They are found in both eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Plant cells have all of the organelles listed above, plus three additional structures:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Chloroplasts</strong> &mdash; contain the green pigment chlorophyll and are the site of <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which plants use light energy to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen. It occurs in chloroplasts.">photosynthesis</dfn>. They absorb light energy to make glucose.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Permanent vacuole</strong> &mdash; a large, fluid-filled space containing cell sap (a weak solution of sugars and salts). It helps keep the cell rigid and supports the plant.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Cell wall</strong> &mdash; made of cellulose and surrounds the cell membrane. It strengthens and supports the cell, preventing it from bursting when water enters by osmosis.</p>
<!-- DIAGRAM -->
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Plant cells have a cell wall (cellulose), chloroplasts (photosynthesis) and a permanent vacuole (cell sap) in addition to the organelles found in animal cells. Bacterial cells have a cell wall but no chloroplasts, no true nucleus and no mitochondria.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Cell Specialisation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Not every cell in an organism looks the same. Cells become <dfn class="term" data-def="A cell that has developed specific structural features to carry out a particular function effectively, such as a sperm cell or root hair cell.">specialised</dfn> &mdash; they develop particular structures that allow them to perform a specific function. The exam regularly asks you to explain how a cell&rsquo;s structure relates to its function (spec 4.1.1.3).</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Examples of Specialised Cells</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Sperm cell</strong> &mdash; has a streamlined head, a long tail (flagellum) for swimming, and many mitochondria to provide energy for movement. It carries the male DNA to the egg.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Nerve cell</strong> &mdash; has a long axon to carry electrical impulses over long distances, branched endings (dendrites) to connect with other nerve cells, and a myelin sheath to insulate the signal and speed up transmission.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Muscle cell</strong> &mdash; contains many mitochondria to supply energy for contraction. Muscle cells are elongated so they can contract and relax to produce movement.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Root hair cell</strong> &mdash; has a long, thin extension (the root hair) that increases the surface area for absorbing water and mineral ions from the soil.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Xylem cell</strong> &mdash; a hollow, dead tube reinforced with lignin that transports water and mineral ions from roots to leaves in a continuous column.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Phloem cell</strong> &mdash; has sieve plates with pores that allow dissolved sugars to flow through the plant from leaves to other parts (translocation).</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Cell Differentiation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The process by which a cell becomes specialised is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which a cell develops specific structural and functional features to carry out a particular role. Once differentiated, the cell is specialised.">differentiation</dfn>. As a cell differentiates, it acquires the sub-cellular structures it needs for its function (spec 4.1.1.4).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Most animal cells differentiate at an <strong>early stage</strong> of development. Once they have specialised, they generally cannot change into a different cell type, and cell division in mature animals is mainly for repair and replacement. Plant cells, however, retain the ability to differentiate <strong>throughout their lives</strong>. This is why you can take a cutting from a plant and it will grow into a new individual.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Animal cells differentiate early and are then permanently specialised. Plant cells can differentiate throughout life &mdash; this is why cuttings can grow into new plants.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Microscopy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">Microscopes allow us to see cells and sub-cellular structures that are invisible to the naked eye. There are two main types you need to know about: <strong>light microscopes</strong> and <strong>electron microscopes</strong> (spec 4.1.1.5).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32">Light microscopes use glass lenses and visible light to produce a magnified image. They have a maximum useful magnification of about &times;1,500 and a resolution (the ability to distinguish two close points as separate) of around 200 nm. Light microscopes were first developed in the 1590s and allowed scientists to discover cells for the first time.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Electron microscopes fire a beam of electrons at the specimen rather than using light. They have a much higher <dfn class="term" data-def="The ability to distinguish between two points that are close together. Higher resolution means finer detail can be seen.">resolution</dfn> (down to about 0.2 nm) and a useful magnification of up to &times;2,000,000. This allows biologists to see sub-cellular structures such as ribosomes and the internal structure of mitochondria in much finer detail.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Magnification Formula</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n34">You must be able to use the magnification formula:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>magnification = size of image &divide; size of real object</strong></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n36">This can be rearranged using a formula triangle. If the image of a cell is 30 mm across and the real cell is 0.03 mm (30 &micro;m), then: magnification = 30 &divide; 0.03 = &times;1,000. Always check both measurements are in the <strong>same units</strong> before dividing. Express very large or very small answers in standard form where appropriate.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><strong>Required Practical 1</strong> asks you to use a light microscope to observe, draw and label plant and animal cells, including a magnification scale on your drawing. To prepare a slide, you place a thin sample on a glass slide, add a drop of water or stain (such as iodine), and lower a coverslip at an angle to avoid trapping air bubbles. You then view the slide starting on the lowest magnification objective and focus using the coarse and fine adjustment knobs.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n38">When answering questions on cell structure, always link the organelle to its <strong>function</strong> &mdash; for example, &ldquo;mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration.&rdquo; In magnification calculations, the most common mistake is forgetting to convert units so that both image size and real size are in the same unit (usually mm or &micro;m) before dividing. Show every step of your working for full marks on &ldquo;Calculate&rdquo; questions.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n39">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n40">Eukaryotic cells (animal and plant) have a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles; prokaryotic cells (bacteria) have no true nucleus and contain plasmids.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n41">Plant cells have a cell wall, chloroplasts and a permanent vacuole in addition to the organelles in animal cells.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n42">Cells specialise through differentiation &mdash; animal cells differentiate early, plant cells can differentiate throughout life.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n43">Electron microscopes have higher magnification and resolution than light microscopes, and the magnification formula is: magnification = image size &divide; real size.</li>
</ul>
