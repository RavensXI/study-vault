# Cell Division and Transport

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Chromosomes and the Genetic Material</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The part of a cell that contains chromosomes made of DNA and controls the cell's activities.">nucleus</dfn> of every cell contains thread-like structures called <dfn class="term" data-def="Structures found in the nucleus made of DNA molecules, each carrying a large number of genes.">chromosomes</dfn>. Each chromosome is a long molecule of DNA, and each chromosome carries a large number of genes. In body cells, chromosomes are found in pairs &mdash; human body cells contain 23 pairs, giving 46 chromosomes in total.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Human body cells have <strong>23 pairs of chromosomes</strong> (46 in total). Each chromosome is made of <strong>DNA</strong> and carries many <strong>genes</strong>.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n4">The Cell Cycle and Mitosis</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Cells divide in a series of stages called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The series of stages a cell goes through to grow, replicate its DNA, and divide into two identical daughter cells.">cell cycle</dfn>. The cell cycle allows organisms to grow, repair damaged tissue, and replace worn-out cells. Before a cell divides, it must prepare by growing larger and increasing the number of sub-cellular structures such as ribosomes and mitochondria. The DNA then replicates so that each chromosome is copied, producing two identical copies.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The stage of the cell cycle where the cell actually divides its nucleus is called <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of cell division that produces two genetically identical daughter cells from one parent cell.">mitosis</dfn>. During mitosis, one set of chromosomes is pulled to each end of the cell and the nucleus divides. Finally, the cytoplasm and cell membrane divide to form two identical daughter cells. Each new cell has exactly the same number of chromosomes &mdash; and exactly the same genetic information &mdash; as the original parent cell.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Stage 2 &mdash; Mitosis:</strong> One set of chromosomes is pulled to each end of the cell and the nucleus divides into two.</p>

<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>AQA requires you to know the <strong>three overall stages</strong> of the cell cycle, but you do <strong>not</strong> need to name the individual phases of mitosis (prophase, metaphase, etc.).</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Stem Cells</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An undifferentiated cell that can divide to produce more cells of the same type and can differentiate into specialised cells.">stem cell</dfn> is an undifferentiated cell that can divide to produce many more cells of the same type. Certain stem cells can also differentiate into specialised cell types. There are three main sources of stem cells that the AQA specification requires you to know.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Embryonic stem cells</strong> are found in early human embryos. They can differentiate into almost any type of human cell, making them extremely useful for medical research. <strong>Adult stem cells</strong> are found in bone marrow and can form many types of cells, including blood cells, but they are more limited than embryonic stem cells. <strong>Meristem tissue</strong> in plants contains stem cells that can differentiate into any type of plant cell throughout the plant&rsquo;s life.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Medical uses:</strong> Stem cell treatments may help conditions such as diabetes and paralysis. In therapeutic cloning, an embryo is produced with the same genes as the patient, so stem cells from it are not rejected by the patient&rsquo;s immune system.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Risks:</strong> There is a potential risk of transferring viral infections through stem cell treatments.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Ethical objections:</strong> Some people object to embryonic stem cell research because it involves the destruction of a human embryo, which they consider to be a potential human life. Others argue the potential to save lives justifies the research.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Plant stem cells:</strong> Meristem cells can be used to produce clones of plants quickly and economically. This is used to clone rare species to protect them from extinction and to produce large numbers of crop plants with desirable features such as disease resistance.</p>
</div></div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Diffusion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><dfn class="term" data-def="The net movement of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration.">Diffusion</dfn> is the spreading out of particles of any substance in solution, or particles of a gas, resulting in a net movement from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration. It is a passive process &mdash; it does not require energy from the cell.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Important examples of diffusion in biology include oxygen and carbon dioxide moving in and out of cells during gas exchange, and the waste product urea moving from cells into the blood plasma for excretion by the kidneys.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n21">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Three factors affect the rate of diffusion: the <strong>concentration gradient</strong> (difference in concentration), the <strong>temperature</strong>, and the <strong>surface area</strong> of the membrane.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n22">A single-celled organism has a relatively large <dfn class="term" data-def="The ratio of an organism's surface area to its volume, which affects the rate of exchange of substances.">surface area to volume ratio</dfn>, allowing sufficient transport of molecules by diffusion alone. Multicellular organisms need specialised exchange surfaces (such as the lungs, small intestine, gills, roots, and leaves) because their surface area to volume ratio is much smaller relative to their needs. These exchange surfaces are adapted by having a large surface area, thin membranes for a short diffusion path, a rich blood supply, and ventilation.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Osmosis</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><dfn class="term" data-def="The diffusion of water from a dilute solution to a concentrated solution through a partially permeable membrane.">Osmosis</dfn> is the diffusion of water from a dilute solution (where water molecules are in higher concentration) to a concentrated solution (where water molecules are in lower concentration) through a <dfn class="term" data-def="A membrane that allows small molecules like water to pass through but blocks larger solute molecules.">partially permeable membrane</dfn>. Like diffusion, osmosis is a passive process and does not require energy from respiration.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Active Transport</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><dfn class="term" data-def="The movement of substances from a more dilute solution to a more concentrated solution (against the concentration gradient), requiring energy from respiration.">Active transport</dfn> moves substances from a more dilute solution to a more concentrated solution &mdash; this means it works <strong>against</strong> the concentration gradient. Unlike diffusion and osmosis, active transport requires energy from <dfn class="term" data-def="The process in cells that transfers energy from glucose, which can be aerobic (with oxygen) or anaerobic (without oxygen).">respiration</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">There are two key examples the AQA specification requires. First, active transport allows <strong>mineral ions</strong> to be absorbed into plant root hair cells from very dilute solutions in the soil &mdash; plants need these minerals for healthy growth. Second, it allows <strong>sugar molecules</strong> to be absorbed from the gut into the blood, even though the blood already has a higher sugar concentration than the gut contents. The sugar is then used for cell respiration.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>The three transport processes compared: <strong>Diffusion</strong> &mdash; high to low concentration, passive (no energy). <strong>Osmosis</strong> &mdash; water only, dilute to concentrated through a partially permeable membrane, passive. <strong>Active transport</strong> &mdash; low to high concentration (against the gradient), requires energy from respiration.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n30">When answering questions on transport in cells, always state the <strong>direction</strong> of movement relative to the concentration gradient. For diffusion and osmosis, say &ldquo;from high to low concentration&rdquo; (or &ldquo;dilute to concentrated&rdquo; for osmosis). For active transport, say &ldquo;against the concentration gradient&rdquo; and mention that it &ldquo;requires energy from respiration.&rdquo; A common mistake is confusing osmosis with diffusion &mdash; remember, osmosis is specifically the movement of <strong>water</strong> through a <strong>partially permeable membrane</strong>. In 6-mark extended response questions, always compare the three processes using a clear structure: name, direction, energy requirement, and a biological example.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n31">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n32">The cell cycle consists of three stages: growth and DNA replication, mitosis (nuclear division), and cytoplasm/membrane division &mdash; producing two genetically identical daughter cells.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n33">Stem cells (embryonic, adult, and plant meristem) are undifferentiated cells that can divide and specialise &mdash; they have medical potential but raise ethical concerns.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n34">Substances move in and out of cells by diffusion (passive, high to low), osmosis (water through a partially permeable membrane, passive), and active transport (against the gradient, requires energy from respiration).</li></ul>
