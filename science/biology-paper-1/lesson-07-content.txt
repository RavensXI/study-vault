# Vaccination, Antibiotics and Drug Development

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Overview</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">This lesson covers three closely linked topics from AQA specification sections 4.3.1.7, 4.3.1.8 and 4.3.1.9: how <dfn class="term" data-def="The process of introducing a small quantity of dead or inactive pathogen into the body to stimulate the immune system to produce antibodies, providing future protection against that disease.">vaccination</dfn> protects individuals and populations, how antibiotics and painkillers are used to treat disease, and how new drugs are discovered, developed and tested before they reach patients.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">These topics connect directly to the immune response you studied in the previous lesson. Vaccination uses the body&rsquo;s own defence system, while antibiotics and other drugs work alongside it. Understanding the difference between <strong>preventing</strong> disease (vaccination), <strong>curing</strong> disease (antibiotics) and <strong>treating symptoms</strong> (painkillers) is essential for the exam.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">How Vaccination Works</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Vaccination involves introducing a small quantity of <strong>dead or inactive</strong> forms of a pathogen into the body. These cannot cause the disease, but they carry the same <dfn class="term" data-def="Unique proteins on the surface of a pathogen that the immune system recognises as foreign, triggering an immune response.">antigens</dfn> as the live pathogen. When the immune system detects these antigens, white blood cells respond by producing specific <dfn class="term" data-def="Proteins produced by white blood cells (lymphocytes) that bind to specific antigens on pathogens, marking them for destruction or neutralising them directly.">antibodies</dfn> that match the pathogen&rsquo;s antigens.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The crucial advantage of vaccination is <strong>immunological memory</strong>. After the initial immune response, the body retains <strong>memory lymphocytes</strong> that &ldquo;remember&rdquo; the pathogen&rsquo;s antigens. If the same pathogen enters the body in the future, these memory cells trigger a much faster and stronger antibody response &mdash; destroying the pathogen before it can cause illness.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Vaccination introduces <strong>dead or inactive</strong> pathogens &rarr; white blood cells produce <strong>antibodies</strong> &rarr; <strong>memory lymphocytes</strong> remain in the body &rarr; if the same pathogen re-enters, antibodies are produced <strong>rapidly</strong>, preventing infection. The vaccine itself does not cure disease &mdash; it trains the immune system to respond.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n8">When a large proportion of a population is vaccinated, the spread of the pathogen is significantly reduced because there are fewer people who can become infected and pass it on. This is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The indirect protection from an infectious disease that occurs when a large percentage of the population is immune, reducing the chance of the pathogen spreading to those who are not immune.">herd immunity</dfn>. It protects people who <em>cannot</em> be vaccinated &mdash; such as very young babies, pregnant women, or people with weakened immune systems &mdash; because the pathogen cannot find enough hosts to spread through the population.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9">The AQA specification requires you to be able to evaluate the global use of vaccination in preventing disease. Vaccination programmes have been enormously successful: smallpox was completely eradicated by 1980 through a global vaccination campaign, and diseases like measles, polio and diphtheria have been dramatically reduced. However, if vaccination rates drop, herd immunity is lost and outbreaks can return.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Antibiotics and Painkillers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><dfn class="term" data-def="Medicines that kill or prevent the growth of bacteria inside the body without damaging human cells. They have no effect on viruses.">Antibiotics</dfn>, such as <dfn class="term" data-def="The first antibiotic, discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928 from the Penicillium mould. It kills bacteria by preventing them from forming cell walls.">penicillin</dfn>, are medicines that kill infective bacteria inside the body. They have dramatically reduced deaths from bacterial diseases since their widespread use began in the 1940s. It is important to understand that <strong>specific bacteria must be treated by specific antibiotics</strong> &mdash; not all antibiotics work against all types of bacteria.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p><strong>Antibiotics kill bacteria but cannot kill viruses.</strong> This is because viruses reproduce inside human cells. Destroying the virus would mean destroying the body&rsquo;s own cells, which is why it is so difficult to develop drugs that target viruses without causing damage to body tissues. Painkillers treat the <strong>symptoms</strong> of disease (such as pain and fever) but do not kill pathogens.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n13">Mutations in bacterial DNA can produce new strains that are <dfn class="term" data-def="The ability of bacteria to survive and reproduce despite exposure to antibiotics, often resulting from natural selection of mutant strains.">antibiotic resistant</dfn>. When antibiotics are used, non-resistant bacteria are killed but resistant ones survive. These resistant bacteria then reproduce, passing on the resistance gene. Over time, the population of resistant bacteria grows &mdash; a process driven by natural selection.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>MRSA</strong> (methicillin-resistant <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em>) is a well-known example of an antibiotic-resistant strain. To reduce the development of resistant strains, three measures are essential:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n15">
<li><strong>Doctors should not prescribe antibiotics inappropriately</strong> &mdash; for example, not for viral infections or non-serious illnesses</li>
<li><strong>Patients must complete their full course</strong> of antibiotics so all bacteria are killed and none survive to develop resistance</li>
<li><strong>Agricultural use of antibiotics should be restricted</strong> to prevent resistance developing in animal bacteria and transferring to humans</li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n16">The development of new antibiotics is costly and slow, and is unlikely to keep up with the emergence of new resistant strains. This is why preventing resistance is so critical.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Discovery and Development of Drugs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Traditionally, drugs were extracted from plants and microorganisms. The AQA specification highlights three important examples:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n19">
<li><strong>Digitalis</strong> &mdash; a heart drug originating from <strong>foxgloves</strong></li>
<li><strong>Aspirin</strong> &mdash; a painkiller originating from <strong>willow</strong> bark</li>
<li><strong>Penicillin</strong> &mdash; discovered by <strong>Alexander Fleming</strong> in 1928 from the <strong><em>Penicillium</em> mould</strong></li>
</ul>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Today, most new drugs are <strong>synthesised by chemists</strong> in the pharmaceutical industry. However, the starting point may still be a chemical extracted from a plant or microorganism.</p>

<span>The Drug Testing Process &mdash; Preclinical and Clinical Trials</span>
<svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
</button>
<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n21">Before any new drug can be used on patients, it must be extensively tested for three things: <strong>toxicity</strong> (is it safe?), <strong>efficacy</strong> (does it work?) and <strong>dose</strong> (what is the right amount?). The testing process has two stages:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Preclinical testing</strong> is carried out in a laboratory. The drug is first tested on <strong>cells and tissues</strong> to check for toxicity, and then on <strong>live animals</strong> to see how it behaves in a whole organism. This stage identifies drugs that are too dangerous to test on humans.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Clinical trials</strong> use human volunteers. Very <strong>low doses</strong> are given at the start to check for safety. If the drug is found to be safe, further trials are carried out at higher doses to find the <strong>optimum dose</strong> &mdash; the amount that is most effective with the fewest side effects.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24">In <dfn class="term" data-def="A clinical trial in which neither the patient nor the doctor knows whether the patient is receiving the real drug or a placebo, reducing bias in the results.">double blind trials</dfn>, some patients are given a <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that looks like the real drug but contains no active ingredient, used in clinical trials to test whether the drug being trialled has a genuine effect beyond the psychological impact of taking a medicine.">placebo</dfn> &mdash; a substance that looks identical to the drug but contains no active ingredient. Neither the patient nor the doctor knows who has received the real drug and who has received the placebo. This eliminates <strong>bias</strong> and ensures that any improvement is genuinely caused by the drug, not by the patient&rsquo;s belief that they are being treated.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Results from clinical trials are only published after they have been checked through <strong>peer review</strong> &mdash; scrutiny by other scientists &mdash; to ensure the methods were sound and the conclusions are valid.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact &mdash; Drug Testing Stages</div>
<p>Preclinical testing (cells, tissues, animals) &rarr; Clinical trials (healthy volunteers, then patients) &rarr; Low dose first, then optimum dose &rarr; Double blind trials with placebos to eliminate bias &rarr; Peer review before publication. New drugs are tested for <strong>toxicity</strong>, <strong>efficacy</strong> and <strong>dose</strong>.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n27">When answering questions on vaccination, always explain the <strong>mechanism</strong>: dead/inactive pathogen &rarr; antibodies produced &rarr; memory cells remain &rarr; rapid secondary response. Do not just say &ldquo;vaccines stop you getting ill.&rdquo; For drug development questions, remember the three things drugs are tested for: <strong>toxicity, efficacy and dose</strong>. A common mistake is confusing antibiotics with painkillers &mdash; antibiotics <em>kill bacteria</em>, painkillers only <em>treat symptoms</em>. Neither works on viruses.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n28">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Vaccination</strong> introduces dead or inactive pathogens to stimulate antibody production and create memory lymphocytes. Immunising a large proportion of the population creates <strong>herd immunity</strong>, protecting those who cannot be vaccinated.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Antibiotics</strong> kill bacteria (not viruses). Overuse and misuse drive <strong>antibiotic resistance</strong> through natural selection. MRSA is a key example. Painkillers treat symptoms but do not kill pathogens.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">New drugs are tested for <strong>toxicity, efficacy and dose</strong> through preclinical testing (cells, tissues, animals) and then clinical trials (humans). <strong>Double blind trials</strong> with placebos eliminate bias, and results undergo <strong>peer review</strong>.</li></ul>
