The 1920s Boom

Study Notes

In the 1920s, the USA experienced a period of rapid economic growth known as the 'Boom.' Mass production, hire purchase, and new advertising techniques fuelled a consumer society that transformed American life.

Why did the US Economy Boom?

Several factors combined to create the economic boom of the 1920s:

  • Republican Government Policies: The Republican presidents of the 1920s (Harding, Coolidge, and Hoover) believed in laissez-faire – the idea that the government should interfere as little as possible in business. They kept taxes low and put tariffs (taxes) on foreign goods to protect American businesses from competition.
  • New Industries and Mass Production: New consumer goods like cars, radios, refrigerators, and vacuum cleaners were produced on a massive scale. Henry Ford pioneered the assembly line at his car factories, making the Model T affordable for ordinary Americans. By 1929, there were 26 million cars registered in the USA.
  • Hire Purchase: Many Americans could now buy expensive goods on credit – paying a small amount each month. This meant more people could afford new products, boosting demand.
  • Advertising: New advertising techniques using radio, billboards, and magazines convinced Americans to buy the latest products. This created a culture of consumerism.
  • The Stock Market: Many Americans began buying shares (stocks) in companies, hoping to make quick profits as share prices rose. This was called speculation. The rising stock market created a sense of confidence and wealth.
  • Natural Resources: The USA had vast natural resources like oil, coal, and iron, which fuelled industrial growth.
  • World War One: During WWI, the USA had lent money to European countries and sold them weapons and supplies. After the war, European economies were weak, allowing America to dominate world trade.

Who Benefited from the Boom?

The boom did not benefit everyone equally. Those who did well included:

  • Businessmen and industrialists who owned factories and companies
  • Workers in new industries like car manufacturing and electrical goods
  • City dwellers who had access to new consumer goods and entertainment
  • Stock market speculators who made profits as share prices rose

Who Did NOT Benefit?

Many Americans were left behind by the boom:

  • Farmers: Overproduction led to falling prices for crops. Many farmers went bankrupt and lost their land. Around 600,000 farmers lost their farms during the 1920s.
  • African Americans: Most lived in the southern states and worked as sharecroppers on farms. They faced racial discrimination and poverty. Even in the north, they were often given the lowest-paid jobs.
  • Native Americans: They were the poorest group in 1920s America, living on reservations with limited opportunities.
  • Workers in old industries: Industries like coal, textiles, and leather struggled to compete with new materials and technologies.
  • Recent immigrants: Many worked in low-paid jobs and faced discrimination.

When writing about the 1920s Boom, always remember to discuss both sides. The examiner wants to see that you understand the boom was not experienced equally. A strong answer will explain who benefited and who was left behind, with specific examples.

Conclusion

The 1920s Boom was a period of remarkable economic growth driven by new industries, mass production, easy credit, and government policies that favoured business. It created a new consumer culture and a sense of optimism in America. However, the prosperity was unevenly distributed – farmers, African Americans, Native Americans, and workers in older industries were largely excluded from the boom. This inequality would become a major problem when the economy crashed in 1929.
