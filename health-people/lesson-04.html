Public Health in Medieval Towns

Medieval towns were unhealthy and unpleasant. Rapid growth, no scientific knowledge, and weak local governments meant most towns faced serious public health problems. These conditions set the stage for the Black Death in 1348.

The Key Problems

Most medieval towns faced the same problems:

Key Fact — Public Health Problems in Towns
  • Waste: There were no proper sewer systems. Household rubbish and human waste were thrown into the streets or dumped into rivers. Cesspits overflowed regularly and contaminated the surrounding area.
  • Water: Wells and rivers were often contaminated by nearby cesspits and industrial waste from butchers, tanners, and dyers. There was no understanding that dirty water could spread disease.
  • Overcrowding: Cramped, smoky houses packed closely together helped spread diseases like tuberculosis. Families often shared single rooms, and ventilation was poor.
  • Animals: Livestock were kept within town walls and animal waste added to the filth. Butchers slaughtered animals in the streets and dumped offal in open areas.

The main reasons were no scientific knowledge and weak local governments. People did not know about germs. They believed bad smells — miasma — caused disease. Town councils had limited power and money to enforce public health rules.

Attempts at Improvement

Medieval towns were not completely lawless. Town councils did try to manage the filth, even if their efforts were often small-scale and inconsistent:

Fines: Councils fined people for dumping waste in the street. But enforcement was patchy and fines were often too small to stop it.

Muck-rakers: Some towns hired muck-rakers to clear the worst rubbish. Their work was limited and could not keep up with the constant dumping.

Gong farmers: Gong farmers were paid to empty cesspits and privies, usually at night — one of the worst jobs in medieval society.

Water supply: Some towns provided clean water through conduits and maintained public wells. London’s Great Conduit (1236) carried fresh water from springs outside the city.

Public bathhouses: Available in some towns, though they declined over time due to fears about spreading disease and immorality.

Key Fact — Specific Public Health Laws
  • 1298: King Edward I ordered public toilets to be built in York because conditions were too unhygienic for his soldiers.
  • 1330: Glamorgan council banned butchers from throwing waste onto streets.
  • 1371: London banned the killing of large animals within the city.
  • 1388: Parliament passed a law fining people £20 (equivalent to roughly £15,000 today) for throwing waste into rivers.

These laws show that authorities were aware of the problems and tried to act — but enforcement was difficult and the measures were rarely enough to make a significant difference.

Monasteries: The Exception

Unlike filthy towns, monasteries were often the cleanest and healthiest places in medieval Britain. Monks had the resources and discipline to create public health systems far ahead of anything in towns.

Key Fact — Why Were Monasteries So Clean?
  • Wealth and knowledge: Monasteries had the money and education to build advanced water and sanitation systems. They could afford to plan and maintain infrastructure.
  • Excellent sanitation: They built latrines (toilets) that carried waste away through drainage channels, keeping it separate from their water supply.
  • Fresh water: Many monasteries had piped water supplies, using lead or wooden pipes to bring clean water from springs directly into the monastery grounds.
  • Routine and hygiene: The monastic rule required regular washing, clean clothing, and organised daily routines. This discipline helped maintain high standards of cleanliness.
  • Healthcare facilities: Monasteries had infirmaries for sick monks, herb gardens for growing medicinal plants, and access to medical texts preserved in their libraries.

This contrast shows that the knowledge and resources existed to create better public health — but most towns lacked the money, authority, and organisation to do it.

The Black Death (1348–1350)

The Black Death was the most catastrophic event in medieval history. It arrived in England in summer 1348 and swept across the country at terrifying speed. By 1350, it had killed one-third to one-half of England’s population — an estimated 1.5 to 2.5 million people. We now know it was caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, carried by fleas on rats — but medieval people had no knowledge of this.

How Did It Arrive?

The Black Death started in Central Asia and spread west along trade routes. It reached Europe on merchant ships carrying infected rats and fleas. The disease arrived at Melcombe Regis (now Weymouth) in Dorset in June 1348 and spread rapidly through England over the next two years.

Europe was already weakened before the plague arrived. Poor harvests in the five years before 1348 had left much of the population malnourished and vulnerable. When the plague struck, weakened immune systems meant people had even less ability to fight it off.

Types and Symptoms

The Black Death took two main forms:

Key Fact — Two Forms of Plague
  • Bubonic plague: Spread by flea bites from infected rats. Symptoms included painful swollen lumps in the groin, armpits, or neck (called buboes), high fever, headaches, vomiting, and a rash of dark blotches on the skin. Around 60–70% of those infected died, usually within a week.
  • Pneumonic plague: Attacked the lungs and was spread through coughing and sneezing. This form was even more contagious and almost always fatal, often killing within 24–48 hours. Victims coughed up blood and struggled to breathe.

What Did People Believe Caused It?

Without knowledge of germs, people desperately searched for explanations:

Attempted Treatments and Prevention

People tried many methods to prevent or cure the plague, all based on their mistaken beliefs about its causes:

Flagellants: Groups of flagellants travelled town to town, publicly whipping themselves to beg God to end the plague. The Pope eventually condemned the movement.

Carrying posies and herbs: People carried flowers, herbs, and spices to ward off “bad air.” Some held vinegar-soaked cloths over their noses.

Bloodletting and purging: Physicians applied standard humoral treatments — bleeding patients and prescribing laxatives. These were completely useless against plague.

Quarantine: Some towns tried quarantine, boarding up infected houses or refusing entry to travellers from affected areas. This was one of the few measures that actually worked.

Prayer and processions: Churches held special services and masses to beg God for mercy. Many people made pilgrimages to holy shrines.

Fires and bells: Some believed smoke or vibrations from church bells could drive off the miasma.

The Impact of the Black Death

The Black Death had consequences far beyond the death toll itself:

1348–1350

The Black Death kills between one-third and one-half of England’s population

An estimated 1.5–2.5 million people died. Some villages were completely wiped out. Labour became desperately scarce.

1349 onwards

Labour shortage transforms society

With so many dead, surviving peasants could demand higher wages and better conditions. Some left their manors to find better-paid work elsewhere, weakening the feudal system.

1351

Statute of Labourers

The government attempted to freeze wages at pre-plague levels and prevent peasants from leaving their manors. This caused widespread resentment and was largely unenforceable.

1361, 1369, 1375

Plague returns repeatedly

The Black Death was not a one-off event. It returned in waves throughout the 14th century and beyond, continuing to disrupt society and reinforce the belief that disease was God’s punishment.

Short-term: The immediate response was mostly religious — prayer, flagellation, and persecution of minorities. Some practical steps were taken: quarantine, mass plague pits, street cleaning. But the scale of death overwhelmed any organised response.

Long-term: The plague forced people to question why traditional remedies and prayer had failed. It did not change medical theory overnight, but it planted seeds of doubt. The huge labour shortage shifted power from landowners to peasants, and some towns began taking public health more seriously. Repeated outbreaks also led to quarantine developing as a public health measure.

Impact on the Church: Prayer had failed to stop the plague, weakening the Church’s authority. Many priests died, leaving parishes without leadership. Some people began questioning whether the Church had all the answers — though this shift was gradual and would not fully develop until the Renaissance and Reformation.

When plague returned to London in 1665 — over 300 years later — people still did not know the real cause. The responses were very similar to 1348, showing how little understanding had changed.

London authorities issued “Plague Orders” that included a range of measures:

  • Quarantine of infected houses: Homes where plague was found were locked shut for 40 days, with a red cross painted on the door and a watchman posted outside to prevent anyone leaving.
  • Killing stray dogs and cats: Authorities ordered the slaughter of stray animals, ironically removing some of the very creatures that killed the rats carrying the disease.
  • Banning large gatherings: Public events and meetings were restricted to limit the spread of infection.
  • Burning infected clothes and bedding: Belongings of plague victims were destroyed in an attempt to stop the disease spreading.
  • Women “searchers”: Women were paid to examine the sick and the dead, identifying plague cases so that households could be quarantined.

The Great Plague of 1665 killed about 100,000 people in London — roughly a quarter of the city. Three centuries apart, the two outbreaks show a striking pattern: without understanding Yersinia pestis and how it spread, people relied on the same measures — quarantine, prayer, and trying to purify the air.

Exam Tip

Don’t describe medieval towns as completely filthy with no rules. For top marks, show nuance: acknowledge the serious problems, but give specific examples of attempts to improve — gong farmers, fines, conduits. When writing about the Black Death, don’t just list the horrors — explain why people responded the way they did, linking responses to their beliefs about what caused disease.

Conclusion

Medieval towns were unhealthy due to contaminated water, street waste, and overcrowding — but not completely lawless. Efforts like muck-rakers and fines show people did try. Monasteries proved better public health was possible with resources and organisation. The Black Death of 1348 was a turning point — its catastrophic death toll exposed the limits of existing medical knowledge and began to weaken trust in traditional remedies and Church authority.

Key Takeaways

  • Medieval towns had serious public health problems: Contaminated water, street waste, overcrowding, and miasma theory all contributed. But councils did try to improve conditions — showing this balance matters for top marks.
  • The Black Death killed one-third to one-half of England’s population: Arriving in 1348, people blamed God, miasma, planets, and minority groups. Only quarantine had any real effect.
  • Long-term consequences were significant: The plague weakened the feudal system, damaged Church authority, and encouraged people to question traditional medicine — laying groundwork for the Renaissance.
