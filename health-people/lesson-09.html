Anaesthetics & Antiseptics

The Three Problems of Surgery

Before the 19th century, surgery was a brutal and dangerous ordeal. Surgeons faced three deadly problems: pain (patients were fully conscious during operations), infection (wounds frequently turned septic after surgery), and blood loss (there was no way to replace lost blood). Speed was the surgeon’s only friend — the best could amputate a leg in under a minute. Solving these three problems would take decades of discovery, fierce opposition, and ultimately the pressure of two world wars.

Key Fact

The three barriers to successful surgery were pain, infection, and blood loss. Each had to be solved before surgery could become truly safe and effective. Pain was tackled first with anaesthetics, infection with antiseptics, and blood loss with transfusions.

Solving Pain: Anaesthetics

Early Attempts

For centuries, surgeons had tried to reduce pain using alcohol, opium, or even knocking patients unconscious with a blow to the head. None of these methods were reliable or safe. In 1842, American doctor Crawford Long used ether during a minor operation, and in 1846 William Morton publicly demonstrated its use in Boston. However, ether had serious drawbacks — it irritated the lungs, made patients vomit, and was highly flammable.

James Simpson and Chloroform (1847)

Scottish doctor James Simpson was determined to find a better anaesthetic. In 1847, he and his colleagues tested chloroform by inhaling it themselves — they all fell unconscious, proving it worked. Chloroform acted faster than ether, was easier to administer, and did not irritate the lungs. Simpson quickly began using it in childbirth and surgery.

Key Fact

In 1853, Queen Victoria used chloroform during the birth of Prince Leopold. Her public endorsement was a turning point — it made anaesthetics socially acceptable and helped overcome religious and medical opposition.

Anaesthetics faced significant opposition from several quarters. Some religious groups argued that pain was God’s punishment — particularly in childbirth, citing the Bible (“in sorrow thou shalt bring forth children”). Many surgeons were reluctant to change their methods and distrusted a new, untested substance. There were also genuine medical concerns: the correct dosage of chloroform was difficult to calculate, and in 1848 a young patient named Hannah Greener died from a chloroform overdose, which fuelled fears about its safety.

Despite this opposition, the benefits of pain-free surgery were undeniable. Queen Victoria’s endorsement in 1853 silenced much of the religious criticism, and as more operations were performed successfully with chloroform, medical opposition gradually faded.

The “Black Period” of Surgery (1846–1870)

Paradoxically, the introduction of anaesthetics actually made surgery more dangerous for a time. This era is known as the “Black Period” of surgery. Because patients were now unconscious and pain-free, surgeons attempted longer, more complex, and deeper operations. However, they still did not understand what caused infection — the miasma theory still dominated, and surgeons operated in blood-stained coats with unwashed hands, seeing the grime as a badge of experience.

Key Fact

During the Black Period, the death rate from surgery actually increased. Anaesthetics had solved the problem of pain, but without antiseptics, the deeper and longer operations simply exposed patients to greater risk of deadly infection. One problem had been solved, but another had been made worse.

Solving Infection: Antiseptic Surgery

Joseph Lister and Carbolic Acid (1867)

Joseph Lister read Louis Pasteur’s Germ Theory (1861) and made a crucial connection: if germs caused disease, then killing germs during surgery could prevent infection. In 1867, Lister began using carbolic acid (phenol) as an antiseptic. He sprayed it in the operating theatre, soaked surgical dressings in it, and used it to clean wounds and instruments. The results were dramatic — his post-operative death rates fell significantly.

Key Fact

Lister reduced the death rate from gangrene in his male surgical ward from 46% to 15% between 1864 and 1866. He published his findings in The Lancet in 1867, providing clear statistical evidence that antiseptic methods saved lives.

Lister faced fierce opposition, much like Simpson before him. Many surgeons argued that the carbolic spray was unpleasant — it cracked and dried out their skin, slowed down operations, and irritated the lungs of everyone in the theatre. Some argued that Lister’s success was simply due to cleanliness rather than germ-killing, and that his methods were unnecessarily elaborate.

Other surgeons simply did not accept Germ Theory and therefore saw no reason to kill germs they did not believe existed. Lister also did not help his own cause — he changed his methods frequently, which made it difficult for others to replicate his results consistently. Despite this, by the 1890s the weight of evidence became overwhelming and antiseptic methods were widely adopted.

From Antiseptic to Aseptic Surgery

By the 1890s, surgeons moved beyond Lister’s antiseptic approach to aseptic surgery. Rather than trying to kill germs during an operation, the goal was to prevent them from being present at all. Operating theatres were thoroughly cleaned, instruments were steam-sterilised, and surgeons began wearing surgical gowns, masks, and rubber gloves. This represented a fundamental shift — from fighting infection to preventing it entirely.

War and the Acceleration of Surgical Progress

The 20th century’s two world wars acted as a massive catalyst for surgical progress. The sheer scale of the conflicts and the new types of injuries from modern weaponry like machine guns and high-explosive shells forced surgeons to innovate at an incredible pace. Wounds were often deep and contaminated with bacteria from the soil of the trenches, leading to deadly infections like gas gangrene.

Solving Blood Loss: Transfusions

Blood loss had always been a killer during surgery, but war made solving this problem urgent. In 1901, Karl Landsteiner discovered the different human blood groups (A, B, O), explaining why some earlier transfusions had killed patients — incompatible blood types caused fatal reactions. In 1915, American doctor Richard Lewisohn found that adding sodium citrate stopped blood from clotting, meaning it could be stored and transported. The first ever blood bank was established in 1917 for the Battle of Cambrai.

Key Fact

The development of blood transfusion required three key breakthroughs: Landsteiner’s blood groups (1901) to make transfusions safe, Lewisohn’s sodium citrate (1915) to allow blood storage, and the first blood bank (1917) to make mass transfusion practical on the battlefield.

Seeing Inside the Body: X-Rays

X-rays were discovered by Wilhelm Rontgen in 1895. For the first time, doctors could see inside the human body without cutting it open. During World War One, Marie Curie developed mobile X-ray units, known as “petites Curies,” which she personally drove to the front lines. These allowed surgeons to locate bullets and shrapnel fragments precisely before operating, saving countless lives.

Rebuilding Bodies: Plastic Surgery

Harold Gillies pioneered new plastic surgery techniques at The Queen’s Hospital in Sidcup during World War One, developing the pedicle tube graft to treat soldiers with devastating facial injuries. His work was continued in World War Two by his cousin, Archibald McIndoe, who treated badly burned RAF pilots at the Queen Victoria Hospital in East Grinstead, developing new skin grafting techniques and pioneering the use of saline baths to prevent infection.

Exam Tip

When writing about the revolution in surgery, structure your answer around the three problems (pain, infection, blood loss) and show how each was solved over time. For 20th-century surgery, always use warfare as a key factor driving change — the wars created both the need (new types of injuries) and the opportunity (government funding and urgency) for rapid innovation.

Conclusion

The revolution in surgery was a long journey from the agonising, infection-ridden operations of the early 19th century to the safe, aseptic procedures of the 20th. It required individual genius, scientific breakthroughs, and the terrible pressure of two world wars to overcome each of surgery’s three deadly problems.

Key Takeaways

  • Anaesthetics solved pain but created new dangers: Simpson’s chloroform (1847) freed patients from agony, but the “Black Period” showed that painless surgery without antiseptics actually increased death rates — proving that solving one problem could worsen another.
  • Lister’s antiseptics linked science to surgery: By applying Pasteur’s Germ Theory to the operating theatre, Lister proved that infections were caused by germs and could be prevented — paving the way from antiseptic to aseptic surgery by the 1890s.
  • War accelerated surgical progress: The two world wars forced breakthroughs in blood transfusion, X-ray technology, and plastic surgery, demonstrating how warfare acts as a catalyst for medical innovation when the scale of suffering demands rapid solutions.
