Anaesthetics & Antiseptics

The Three Problems of Surgery

Before the 19th century, surgery was brutal and dangerous. Surgeons faced three deadly problems: pain (patients were fully conscious), infection (wounds often turned septic), and blood loss (there was no way to replace lost blood). Speed was the surgeon’s only friend — the best could amputate a leg in under a minute. Solving these problems took decades of discovery and the pressure of two world wars.

Key Fact

The three barriers to successful surgery were pain, infection, and blood loss. Each had to be solved before surgery could become truly safe and effective. Pain was tackled first with anaesthetics, infection with antiseptics, and blood loss with transfusions.

Solving Pain: Anaesthetics

Early Attempts

For centuries, surgeons tried to reduce pain with alcohol, opium, or even knocking patients out with a blow to the head. None were reliable or safe. In 1799, Sir Humphry Davy discovered that nitrous oxide (“laughing gas”) reduced pain, but did not make the patient fully unconscious. In 1844, a public demonstration during a tooth extraction was considered a failure when the patient appeared to still feel pain.

In 1842, American doctor Crawford Long used ether during a minor operation. In 1846, William Morton publicly demonstrated it in Boston. But ether had serious drawbacks — it irritated the lungs, made patients vomit, and was highly flammable.

James Simpson and Chloroform (1847)

Scottish doctor James Simpson wanted a better anaesthetic. In 1847, he and his colleagues were testing different chemicals when someone knocked over a bottle of chloroform. Simpson’s wife found them all unconscious on the floor. This shows the role of chance in medical progress. Chloroform acted faster than ether, was easier to use, and did not irritate the lungs. Simpson quickly began using it in childbirth and surgery.

Key Fact

In 1853, Queen Victoria used chloroform during the birth of Prince Leopold. This was made possible by John Snow — the same doctor who would later prove cholera was waterborne. Snow calculated accurate dosages based on the patient’s age, sex, and weight, making chloroform reliable enough for royal use. He also convinced Prince Albert that anaesthetics were safe. Victoria’s public endorsement was a turning point — it made anaesthetics socially acceptable and helped overcome religious and medical opposition.

Anaesthetics faced strong opposition. Some religious groups argued pain was God’s punishment — especially in childbirth, citing the Bible (“in sorrow thou shalt bring forth children”). Many surgeons distrusted a new, untested substance. There were also real medical concerns: dosage was hard to calculate, and in 1848 a young patient named Hannah Greener died from a chloroform overdose.

Despite this, the benefits of pain-free surgery were undeniable. Queen Victoria’s endorsement in 1853 silenced much of the religious criticism, and medical opposition gradually faded as more operations succeeded.

The “Black Period” of Surgery (1846–1870)

Anaesthetics actually made surgery more dangerous for a time. This era is known as the “Black Period” of surgery. With patients now unconscious, surgeons attempted longer, deeper operations. But they still did not understand infection — the miasma theory still dominated, and surgeons operated in blood-stained coats with unwashed hands, seeing the grime as a badge of experience.

Key Fact

During the Black Period, the death rate from surgery actually increased. Anaesthetics had solved the problem of pain, but without antiseptics, the deeper and longer operations simply exposed patients to greater risk of deadly infection. One problem had been solved, but another had been made worse.

Solving Infection: Antiseptic Surgery

Joseph Lister and Carbolic Acid (1865)

Joseph Lister read Pasteur’s Germ Theory (1861) and made a key connection: if germs caused disease, killing them during surgery could prevent infection. In August 1865, Lister tested this idea. An 11-year-old boy named Jamie Greenlees had a compound fracture — bones sticking through the skin. Normally, this meant amputation and a high chance of death. Instead, Lister set the bones and applied dressings soaked in carbolic acid (phenol). Six weeks later, Jamie walked out of hospital with his leg intact — the first successful antiseptic surgery.

Lister published his findings in The Lancet in 1867. He developed a carbolic spray machine that pumped a mist of carbolic acid over the patient during surgery. He also soaked dressings in it and used it to clean wounds and instruments. His post-operative death rates fell dramatically.

Key Fact

Lister reduced the death rate from gangrene in his male surgical ward from 46% to 15% between 1864 and 1866, providing clear statistical evidence that antiseptic methods saved lives.

Lister faced fierce opposition. Many surgeons said the carbolic spray was unpleasant — it cracked their skin, slowed operations, and irritated lungs. Some argued his success was just due to cleanliness, not germ-killing. Charlton Bastian publicly attacked Lister’s claims. Many surgeons resented being told their methods were killing patients.

Others simply did not accept Germ Theory and saw no reason to kill germs they did not believe existed. Lister also changed his methods often, making them hard for others to replicate. Still, by the 1890s the evidence was overwhelming and antiseptic methods were widely adopted.

From Antiseptic to Aseptic Surgery

By the 1890s, surgeons moved beyond Lister’s antiseptic approach to aseptic surgery. Instead of killing germs during an operation, the goal was to prevent them being present at all. Theatres were cleaned, instruments steam-sterilised, and surgeons wore gowns, masks, and rubber gloves. This was a shift from fighting infection to preventing it.

War and the Acceleration of Surgical Progress

The two world wars drove rapid surgical progress. New weapons like machine guns and high-explosive shells caused injuries surgeons had never seen before. Wounds were often deep and contaminated with bacteria from trench soil, leading to deadly infections like gas gangrene.

Solving Blood Loss: Transfusions

Blood loss had always been a killer, but war made solving it urgent. In 1901, Karl Landsteiner discovered the different human blood groups (A, B, O), explaining why some earlier transfusions had killed patients — incompatible blood caused fatal reactions. In 1915, Richard Lewisohn found that adding sodium citrate stopped blood clotting, so it could be stored and transported. The first blood bank was set up in 1917 for the Battle of Cambrai.

Key Fact

The development of blood transfusion required three key breakthroughs: Landsteiner’s blood groups (1901) to make transfusions safe, Lewisohn’s sodium citrate (1915) to allow blood storage, and the first blood bank (1917) to make mass transfusion practical on the battlefield.

Seeing Inside the Body: X-Rays

X-rays were discovered by Wilhelm Rontgen in 1895. For the first time, doctors could see inside the body without cutting it open. During World War One, Marie Curie developed mobile X-ray units — “petites Curies” — which she drove to the front lines. These let surgeons locate bullets and shrapnel before operating, saving countless lives.

Rebuilding Bodies: Plastic Surgery

Harold Gillies pioneered plastic surgery at The Queen’s Hospital in Sidcup during World War One, developing the pedicle tube graft to treat soldiers with severe facial injuries. In World War Two, his cousin Archibald McIndoe treated burned RAF pilots at the Queen Victoria Hospital in East Grinstead, developing new skin grafting techniques and using saline baths to prevent infection.

Exam Tip

Structure answers around the three problems (pain, infection, blood loss) and show how each was solved over time. For 20th-century surgery, use warfare as a key factor — wars created both the need (new types of injuries) and the opportunity (government funding and urgency) for rapid progress.

Conclusion

Surgery went from agonising, infection-ridden operations to safe, aseptic procedures. It took individual genius, scientific breakthroughs, and the pressure of two world wars to overcome surgery’s three deadly problems.

Key Takeaways

  • Anaesthetics solved pain but created new dangers: Simpson’s chloroform (1847) ended the agony, but the “Black Period” showed that painless surgery without antiseptics actually increased death rates.
  • Lister linked science to surgery: By applying Germ Theory from 1865, Lister proved infections could be prevented — paving the way to aseptic surgery by the 1890s.
  • War accelerated progress: The world wars forced breakthroughs in blood transfusion, X-rays, and plastic surgery.
