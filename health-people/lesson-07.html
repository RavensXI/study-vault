Jenner & the Smallpox Vaccine

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Introduction: The Speckled Monster

Smallpox was one of the most feared diseases in 18th-century Britain. It killed around 30% of those who caught it, and survivors were often left with terrible scarring and sometimes blindness. Preventing it was one of the biggest medical challenges of the age.

Early Attempts: Inoculation

Before vaccination, the only way to try to prevent smallpox was inoculation. This was introduced to Britain by Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, who had seen it used in Turkey.

Inoculation involved deliberately infecting a healthy person with a small amount of pus from a real smallpox sore. The hope was that they would get a mild dose of the disease and then be immune for life.

Key Fact — Problems with Inoculation
  • It was a huge gamble — some people were successfully immunised, but others caught a deadly dose of smallpox and died.
  • Inoculated people could spread the disease to others while they were ill.
  • It was expensive, meaning only the wealthy could afford it.

Jenner’s Breakthrough: The First Vaccination

Edward Jenner was a country doctor in Gloucestershire. He had studied under the famous surgeon John Hunter, who taught him to test ideas through careful experimentation, famously telling him: “Don’t think, try.” Jenner made a crucial observation: local milkmaids who caught cowpox — a much milder disease caught from cows — never seemed to catch smallpox.

1796

Jenner tests his theory

Jenner took pus from a cowpox sore on a milkmaid named Sarah Nelmes and inserted it into a small cut on a young boy called James Phipps. James developed a slight fever but was soon well again.

1796 (weeks later)

The crucial test

Jenner then inoculated James with actual smallpox pus. The boy did not get sick at all — he was immune. Jenner had proved that cowpox infection protected against smallpox.

1796–1798

Over 20 further tests

Jenner carried out over 20 additional tests to verify his theory, using the rigorous method he had learned from John Hunter. Only once he was confident did he publish.

1798

Jenner publishes his findings

Jenner published his research, calling his new method vaccination, from the Latin word for cow, ‘vacca’.

1852

Vaccination made compulsory

After decades of debate, the British government made smallpox vaccination compulsory for all children, recognising its life-saving potential.

Opposition and Acceptance

Despite its success, Jenner’s vaccination faced strong opposition. Understanding why people resisted such an effective treatment is important for your exam answers.

Religious objections: Some believed it was against God’s will to put animal matter into a human body. They saw disease as divine punishment and felt vaccination interfered with God’s plan.

Mistrust from doctors: Doctors who earned their living from inoculations opposed vaccination because it threatened their income. Others were sceptical of a treatment from a country doctor rather than a university-trained physician.

Scientific limitations: Jenner could not explain why his vaccine worked. He had observed its effects but could not describe the biological mechanism. Without Germ Theory (not published until 1861), this was impossible.

Fear and misinformation: Satirical cartoons played on fears about putting animal material into humans. The most famous was by James Gillray in 1802, showing vaccinated people sprouting cow-like features. This is an iconic primary source you may see in exam questions.

Organised opposition: The Anti-Vaccination League had around 80,000 members at its peak. They organised protest marches and once burned an effigy of Jenner. Some doctors performed the technique badly, and when patients died it fuelled further opposition.

Key Fact — Why Vaccination Succeeded

Despite opposition, the evidence was overwhelming: vaccination worked. It was safer than inoculation, cheaper, and could be given to anyone. Jenner’s work spread rapidly around the world — by 1803 the USA was using vaccination, and in 1805 Napoleon ordered the entire French army to be vaccinated. Back in Britain, government support eventually led to vaccination being made compulsory in 1852, dramatically reducing smallpox deaths.

Factors Behind Jenner’s Success

Jenner’s breakthrough was not down to a single cause. Several factors worked together.

Key Fact — Factors
  • The individual: Jenner’s careful observation of milkmaids and his willingness to test his theory through experiment.
  • Science and technology: His systematic approach to testing — inoculating James Phipps first with cowpox, then with smallpox — followed a scientific method.
  • Communication: Publishing his findings allowed other doctors to replicate and verify his results.
  • Government: Parliament awarded Jenner a total of £30,000 (a huge sum at the time) to continue his research, demonstrating the government factor at play. The decision to make vaccination compulsory in 1852 was crucial for widespread adoption.
Exam Tip

When writing about Jenner, think about the factors behind his success — the individual, science, chance (folk knowledge about milkmaids), and government action. Also consider the limitations: he could not explain why it worked, and his method only worked for smallpox. A general theory of vaccination did not come until Pasteur and Koch.

Conclusion

Jenner’s work was a major turning point. He created the first-ever vaccine, proving it was possible to prevent disease, not just treat it. His method only applied to smallpox and he could not explain the science behind it, but he laid the groundwork for vaccination programmes that would save millions of lives.

Key Takeaways

  • From inoculation to vaccination: Inoculation was risky and expensive. Jenner’s vaccination used harmless cowpox to create immunity to deadly smallpox — far safer and more accessible.
  • Opposition delayed progress: Religious objections, doctors’ self-interest, and Jenner’s inability to explain the science all slowed acceptance. Vaccination did not become compulsory until 1852.
  • Multiple factors drove success: Jenner’s individual skill, scientific method, publication, and government action all played essential roles.
