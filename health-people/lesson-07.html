Jenner & the Smallpox Vaccine

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Introduction: The Speckled Monster

Smallpox was one of the most feared diseases in 18th-century Britain. It killed around 30% of those who caught it, and survivors were often left with terrible scarring and sometimes blindness. Finding a way to prevent this devastating disease was one of the most important medical challenges of the age.

Early Attempts: Inoculation

Before vaccination, the only way to try and prevent smallpox was a method called inoculation. This technique was introduced to Britain by Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, who had seen it used in Turkey.

Inoculation involved deliberately infecting a healthy person with a small amount of pus from a real smallpox sore. The hope was that they would get a mild dose of the disease and then be immune for life.

Key Fact — Problems with Inoculation
  • It was a huge gamble — some people were successfully immunised, but others caught a deadly dose of smallpox and died.
  • Inoculated people could spread the disease to others while they were ill.
  • It was expensive, meaning only the wealthy could afford it.

Jenner’s Breakthrough: The First Vaccination

Edward Jenner was a country doctor in Gloucestershire. He made a crucial observation: local milkmaids who caught cowpox — a much milder disease caught from cows — never seemed to catch smallpox. This observation, based on local folk knowledge, would lead to one of the most important breakthroughs in medical history.

1796

Jenner tests his theory

Jenner took pus from a cowpox sore on a milkmaid named Sarah Nelmes and inserted it into a small cut on a young boy called James Phipps. James developed a slight fever but was soon well again.

1796 (weeks later)

The crucial test

Jenner then inoculated James with actual smallpox pus. The boy did not get sick at all — he was immune. Jenner had proved that cowpox infection protected against smallpox.

1798

Jenner publishes his findings

Jenner published his research, calling his new method vaccination, from the Latin word for cow, ‘vacca’.

1852

Vaccination made compulsory

After decades of debate, the British government made smallpox vaccination compulsory for all children, recognising its life-saving potential.

Opposition and Acceptance

Despite its success, Jenner’s vaccination faced significant opposition from several quarters. Understanding why people resisted such an effective treatment is important for your exam answers.

Religious objections: Some people believed it was against God’s will to deliberately put animal matter into a human body. They saw disease as divine punishment and felt vaccination was interfering with God’s plan.

Mistrust from doctors: Many doctors who made their living from performing inoculations opposed vaccination because it threatened their income. Others were genuinely sceptical of a treatment developed by a country doctor rather than a university-trained physician.

Scientific limitations: At the time, Jenner could not explain why his vaccine worked. He had observed its effects but could not describe the biological mechanism behind it. Without Germ Theory (which would not be published until 1861), this was impossible to explain.

Fear and misinformation: Satirical cartoons showed vaccinated people sprouting cow-like features, playing on public fears about putting animal material into the human body.

Key Fact — Why Vaccination Succeeded

Despite opposition, the evidence was overwhelming: vaccination worked. It was safer than inoculation, cheaper, and could be given to anyone. Government support eventually led to it being made compulsory in 1852, dramatically reducing smallpox deaths.

Factors Behind Jenner’s Success

Jenner’s breakthrough was not down to a single cause. Several factors worked together:

Key Fact — Factors
  • The individual: Jenner’s careful observation of milkmaids and his willingness to test his theory through experiment.
  • Science and technology: His systematic approach to testing — inoculating James Phipps first with cowpox, then with smallpox — followed a scientific method.
  • Communication: Publishing his findings allowed other doctors to replicate and verify his results.
  • Government: The decision to make vaccination compulsory in 1852 was crucial for widespread adoption.
Exam Tip

When writing about Jenner, think about the factors that led to his success — the role of the individual, science, chance (local folk knowledge about milkmaids), and government action. Also consider the limitations: Jenner could not explain why his vaccine worked, and his method only worked for smallpox, not other diseases. It was not until Pasteur and Koch that a general theory of vaccination could be developed.

Conclusion

Edward Jenner’s work was a massive turning point in the history of medicine. He created the first-ever vaccine, proving that it was possible to prevent disease rather than just treating it after the fact. Although his method only applied to smallpox and he could not explain the science behind it, his approach laid the groundwork for the vaccination programmes that would save millions of lives in the centuries to come.

Key Takeaways

  • From inoculation to vaccination: Inoculation was risky and expensive. Jenner’s vaccination used harmless cowpox to create immunity to deadly smallpox — a far safer and more accessible method.
  • Opposition delayed progress: Religious objections, doctors’ self-interest, and Jenner’s inability to explain the science behind his vaccine all slowed acceptance. It took until 1852 for vaccination to become compulsory.
  • Multiple factors drove success: Jenner’s individual genius, scientific method, communication through publication, and eventual government action all played essential roles in making vaccination a reality.
