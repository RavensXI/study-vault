The Christian Church & Medieval Medicine

In the Middle Ages, the Christian Church was very powerful and controlled almost every part of life, including medicine. It ran the universities where doctors trained, decided which books could be studied, and shaped how ordinary people understood illness. The Church taught that God sent illness as a punishment for sin or as a test of faith.

Religious remedies — prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, and repentance — were often the first response to illness. However, the Church also provided the only organised care for the sick and poor in medieval Britain.

Medieval people also believed in miasma — the idea that “bad air” caused disease. Foul-smelling vapours from rotting matter, swamps, and sewage were thought to enter the body and make people ill. This belief lasted for centuries, influencing public health into the 19th century. During plague outbreaks, people carried posies of flowers or sweet herbs to ward off the “poisonous” air.

Medieval Hospitals

The Church was the main provider of hospital care in medieval Britain. By 1500, there were over 1,200 hospitals across the country. These were very different from modern hospitals — their purpose was to care for the sick, not cure them. Most were small and attached to monasteries or run by religious orders. Only around 10% actually cared for the sick. The rest offered “hospitality” in its original sense: a place to rest, eat, and pray, not receive medical treatment.

Key Fact — Medieval Hospitals
  • Run by monks, nuns, or religious orders — not trained doctors
  • Provided food, shelter, warmth, and prayer for the sick and poor
  • Rarely attempted to treat or cure diseases
  • Often refused entry to patients with infectious diseases to protect other residents
  • The focus was on spiritual care: patients were encouraged to pray and confess their sins

Monastery Sanitation

Monks had the knowledge, money, and discipline to create much better public health systems than towns had. Monasteries had excellent water supplies and built latrines to carry away sewage, keeping their water source separate from waste. They also had infirmaries for sick monks and herb gardens to grow plants for remedies.

This made monasteries some of the cleanest and healthiest places in medieval Britain. Regular washing, clean water, and organised waste disposal put them far ahead of most towns.

Monastery herb gardens were an important source of remedies. One example is foxglove, used to treat “feebleness of the heart.” A chemical from foxglove called digitalis is still used in modern medicine for heart conditions — one of the clearest examples of a medieval remedy with real scientific value.

The Church: Help or Hindrance?

A key exam theme is whether the Church helped or hindered medical progress. The answer is both — and examiners expect you to show this balance.

By 1300, there were fewer than 100 trained physicians in all of England. Most ordinary people could never afford one and instead relied on local healers, wise women, or the Church for their healthcare.

Roger Bacon, a 13th-century Franciscan friar, argued that doctors should carry out experiments rather than just relying on Galen. For challenging Church teaching, he was arrested and imprisoned — a clear warning to anyone who dared question accepted ideas.

Exam Tip

For questions about the Church’s role, you must give a balanced argument. Explain both the positive contributions and the negative effects. The strongest answers reach a judgement on whether the Church did more to help or hinder — and back it up with specific evidence.

Islamic Medicine

While Europe was in the “Dark Ages” after the fall of Rome, the Islamic world was in a golden age of learning. Islamic scholars translated, preserved, and built upon Greek and Roman medical texts — especially Hippocrates and Galen. Without them, much of this knowledge would have been lost forever.

The Islamic faith actively encouraged learning. The Quran taught that Muslims had a duty to care for the sick and seek cures. Medicine was valued and funded across the Islamic world, leading to major advances.

Rhazes (al-Razi, c.854–925 AD)

Rhazes (also known as al-Razi) was a Persian physician who made several breakthroughs. He was the first doctor to clearly distinguish between smallpox and measles — two diseases previously confused. He did this through careful clinical observation, recording each disease’s symptoms in detail.

Rhazes wrote over 200 medical texts and ran a hospital in Baghdad. He used clinical observation in the tradition of Hippocrates — carefully watching patients and recording symptoms rather than just relying on ancient texts.

Ibn Sina / Avicenna (c.980–1037 AD)

Ibn Sina (known in Europe as Avicenna) was perhaps the most influential Islamic physician. His masterwork, The Canon of Medicine, compiled and organised all known medical knowledge from the Greek, Roman, and Islamic traditions.

Key Fact — The Canon of Medicine

Ibn Sina’s Canon of Medicine was translated into Latin and became the standard medical textbook in European universities for over 500 years. It covered topics including the properties of drugs, the diagnosis and treatment of diseases, and the importance of diet, exercise, and hygiene. It contained descriptions of over 760 different drugs and their effects.

Ibn al-Nafis (c.1213–1288 AD)

Ibn al-Nafis was a Syrian physician who criticised Galen’s ideas about how blood moved through the body. Galen claimed blood was made in the liver, “burned up” by the organs, and passed between the two sides of the heart through invisible pores. Ibn al-Nafis rejected this. He correctly argued that blood circulated around the body and passed through the lungs — centuries before William Harvey proved full circulation in 1628.

Islamic Contributions to Pharmacy

Islamic scholars also made major advances in pharmacy. Through alchemy, they developed techniques like distillation to create purer, more effective medicines. They set up the first dedicated pharmacies and developed systems for testing and cataloguing drugs.

These advances fed back into European medicine when Islamic texts were translated into Latin, especially in Spain and Sicily. The knowledge Islamic scholars preserved and advanced was essential to the medical breakthroughs of the European Renaissance.

The Crusades & Knowledge Transfer

The Crusades (holy wars from 1095) brought thousands of Europeans into direct contact with Islamic medicine. Returning crusaders brought back medical knowledge, herbs, and surgical techniques far more advanced than anything in Europe. This was one of the key ways Islamic medical knowledge reached the West.

Usamah ibn Munqidh (1095–1188), a Muslim writer, recorded a striking story. An Arab doctor was treating a knight’s infected leg with clean dressings — and it was healing well. A European doctor arrived, dismissed the approach, and insisted on amputation. The knight died. The same European doctor then cut open a woman’s skull to treat her. She also died.

This story — told from a Muslim perspective — shows how far behind European surgery was compared to the Islamic world. It is useful evidence for exam answers about Islamic medicine’s contribution.

Religious encouragement: The Quran encouraged Muslims to seek knowledge and care for the sick — unlike the Christian Church’s focus on disease as God’s punishment.

Wealth and stability: The Islamic Empire was wealthy and stable. Rulers funded hospitals, libraries, and centres of learning. The great library at Baghdad, the House of Wisdom, attracted scholars from across the world.

Translation movement: Islamic scholars translated Greek and Roman texts into Arabic, preserving knowledge being lost in post-Roman Europe. They did not just copy these texts — they critiqued, corrected, and expanded them.

Practical emphasis: European medicine focused on theory and ancient authority. Islamic physicians placed greater emphasis on observation, experimentation, and practical results.

Conclusion

The Church was the backbone of medieval healthcare, providing care and building the cleanest communities in monasteries. But its control over ideas and emphasis on supernatural causes of disease also blocked scientific progress. Meanwhile, Islamic scholars preserved and advanced ancient medical knowledge, making breakthroughs that would later help transform European medicine.

Key Takeaways

  • The Church both helped and hindered medicine: It provided hospitals, preserved texts, and built clean monasteries — but also banned dissection, promoted Galen uncritically, and encouraged belief in supernatural cures. Show this balance for top marks.
  • Islamic scholars preserved and advanced ancient knowledge: Rhazes, Ibn Sina, and Ibn al-Nafis translated Greek and Roman texts, corrected errors, and made breakthroughs in observation, pharmacy, and blood circulation.
  • Medieval hospitals were about care, not cure: They provided food, shelter, and prayer, but rarely attempted medical treatment.
