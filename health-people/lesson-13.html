The Creation of the NHS

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Why Was the NHS Needed?

Before the NHS, healthcare in Britain was a confusing and deeply unequal patchwork. The rich could afford private doctors, but ordinary working people had to pay for every visit, rely on charity hospitals, or go without. Women and children were particularly vulnerable — the National Insurance Act of 1911 only covered working men, not their families.

Key Fact

Before 1948, healthcare in Britain was split between private doctors (for the wealthy), voluntary hospitals (for charity cases), council-run hospitals (often former workhouses), and the “panel system” under National Insurance. Millions of people — especially women, children, and the elderly — fell through the gaps.

The Impact of World War Two

The Second World War transformed attitudes towards healthcare and the role of the state. The shared experience of rationing, the Blitz, and evacuation created a powerful sense of national unity. People who had sacrificed together expected a better Britain after the war.

In 1939, the government created the Emergency Medical Service (EMS) to prepare for mass casualties from bombing. For the first time, the government coordinated hospitals across the country, funded treatment centrally, and directed doctors where they were needed.

The EMS proved a state-run system could work. It also exposed how unequal existing healthcare was — when city children were evacuated to the countryside, rural communities were shocked by their poor health. This fuelled demand for a fairer system after the war.

The Beveridge Report (1942)

In 1942, economist Sir William Beveridge published a landmark report that became a bestseller. He identified “five giants” blocking progress: Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, and Idleness. The report proposed a welfare state to look after citizens “from the cradle to the grave.” This included a national health service free to all.

Key Fact — Beveridge’s Five Giants
  • Want — poverty and lack of basic necessities
  • Disease — inadequate healthcare for the majority
  • Ignorance — lack of education
  • Squalor — poor housing conditions
  • Idleness — unemployment

The Beveridge Report sold over 600,000 copies. It captured the wartime mood that ordinary people deserved better, became the blueprint for the post-war welfare state, and was a key reason Labour won the 1945 general election in a landslide.

Creating the NHS

In 1945, Labour won a landslide victory over Churchill’s Conservatives, partly on the promise of implementing Beveridge’s plan. The task of creating the NHS fell to Aneurin “Nye” Bevan, Minister for Health — a Welsh politician from a mining community who had grown up seeing the effects of poverty and poor healthcare.

Key Fact — Three Core Principles of the NHS
  • Universal — available to every person in Britain, regardless of wealth, class, or location
  • Comprehensive — covering all medical treatment, from GP visits to surgery to dentistry
  • Free at the point of delivery — paid for through general taxation, so no one would face a bill when they were ill

The NHS represented the nationalisation of healthcare — a radical departure from the laissez-faire approach that had dominated for centuries. Previously, if you were ill, it was up to you (or your family, or charity) to find and pay for treatment. The NHS replaced this with the principle that the state would take responsibility for the nation’s health.

1942

Beveridge Report published

Identifies the “five giants” and proposes a comprehensive welfare state including a national health service. The report becomes a bestseller.

July 1945

Labour wins landslide election

Clement Attlee’s Labour Party defeats Churchill’s Conservatives, winning on a promise of social reform. Aneurin Bevan is appointed Minister for Health.

1946

National Health Service Act passed

Bevan steers the NHS Act through Parliament despite fierce opposition from doctors and Conservatives. The Act sets out the legal framework for the new service.

5th July 1948

The NHS officially begins

The National Health Service opens its doors. For the first time in history, free healthcare is available to every man, woman, and child in Britain.

Overcoming the Opposition

Creating the NHS was not simply a matter of passing a law. Bevan faced fierce opposition, particularly from the medical profession. The British Medical Association (BMA) launched a sustained campaign against the proposals.

Why Did Doctors Oppose the NHS?

Doctors feared becoming salaried government employees and losing their professional freedom. Many consultants earned large incomes from private practice and refused to give this up. GPs feared being told where to work. In January 1948 — just months before the NHS was due to launch — a BMA poll showed 90% of members would refuse to join the new service.

Key Fact

Bevan famously said he had “stuffed their mouths with gold” to win over the doctors. His key compromise was to allow hospital consultants to continue private practice alongside their NHS work, and to let GPs remain independent contractors rather than salaried employees. This broke the BMA’s resistance.

Bevan was a shrewd negotiator. Rather than winning over all doctors at once, he split the medical profession. He offered hospital consultants generous salaries, private beds within NHS hospitals, and the freedom to continue private practice. “Merit awards” for top consultants could double their income.

He also let GPs remain independent contractors rather than state employees, preserving their autonomy. By winning over the most senior doctors first, Bevan isolated the BMA’s opposition. By early 1948, resistance crumbled and most doctors signed up.

Opposition Beyond the Doctors

The BMA was not the only opposition. The upper and middle classes feared the higher taxes needed to fund a free health service. Some believed free healthcare would create a “something for nothing” culture. Conservative politicians worried about the huge cost. Even Winston Churchill spoke out against the plans, calling the NHS “a curse on the country.”

The Impact of the NHS

The NHS’s impact was immediate. In its first year, 8.5 million dental patients were treated, 5.25 million pairs of spectacles were issued, and millions received treatment they had put off for years. For the first time, a woman could see a doctor without asking her husband for money.

Demand for NHS services far exceeded expectations. Bevan had assumed costs would fall as the backlog of untreated illness cleared. Instead, costs rose year on year as new treatments appeared and public expectations grew.

By 1951, the government introduced charges for dental treatment and spectacles. Bevan resigned in protest, arguing this betrayed the founding principle of free care. The tension between rising costs and free healthcare has remained a central challenge ever since.

Exam Tip

Don’t just describe what the NHS is. Explain why it was created — WWII (shared sacrifice, the EMS proving a state system could work), the Beveridge Report (the “five giants”), and the 1945 election. Show how these factors combined to make the NHS possible.

Conclusion

The NHS was born from World War Two’s shared sacrifice and Beveridge’s vision for a fairer society. Bevan’s determination and political skill turned that vision into reality, overcoming fierce opposition from the medical profession.

Key Takeaways

  • WWII was the catalyst: The war created both practical proof (the Emergency Medical Service) and the political will (shared sacrifice) for a state-run health system.
  • Bevan was the architect: He overcame BMA opposition by “stuffing their mouths with gold” — compromises on private practice and GP independence were essential.
  • The NHS changed everything: Healthcare became universal, comprehensive, and free at the point of delivery — but the tension between rising costs and founding principles began almost immediately.
