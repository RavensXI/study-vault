Modern Public Health Challenges

0:00 / 0:00

The Paradox of Progress

Modern public health is, in many ways, a victim of its own success. Vaccination, antibiotics, better sanitation, and the NHS have all extended life expectancy. But living longer has created new challenges. The diseases that dominate modern Britain are not the infectious epidemics of the past, but long-term degenerative conditions and illnesses linked to the way we live.

Key Fact

In 1900, average life expectancy in Britain was around 47 years. By 2020, it had risen to over 81 years. This extraordinary improvement is the result of centuries of medical progress — but it means today’s biggest health challenges are diseases of old age and lifestyle, not the infectious diseases that once killed millions.

New Diseases & Lifestyle Challenges

Long-Term Degenerative Conditions

As people live longer, the biggest medical challenges are conditions where the body slowly breaks down over time. These include:

  • Cancer — abnormal cell growth that can affect almost any part of the body. Risk increases significantly with age.
  • Alzheimer’s disease — a progressive brain disorder that destroys memory and cognitive function. It is the most common cause of dementia.
  • Parkinson’s disease — a condition affecting movement, causing tremors, stiffness, and difficulty with balance and coordination.

Lifestyle Diseases

Many of today’s most common illnesses are linked to modern lifestyles — poor diet, lack of exercise, smoking, and heavy drinking. These lifestyle diseases include:

  • Type 2 diabetes — strongly linked to obesity and poor diet. Cases have risen dramatically in recent decades.
  • Heart disease — the leading cause of death in the UK, linked to smoking, high-fat diets, and lack of exercise.
  • Some cancers — including lung cancer (linked to smoking), liver cancer (linked to alcohol), and bowel cancer (linked to processed meat and low-fibre diets).

Mental Health

Mental health is now one of the biggest public health challenges. Depression, anxiety, and eating disorders affect millions in Britain. Mental health services have always received less funding than physical health, despite affecting a similar number of people. Stigma has also made it harder for people to seek help, though awareness campaigns have begun to change this.

Antibiotic Resistance

One of the most serious global health threats today is antibiotic resistance. Decades of overusing antibiotics — in both human medicine and farming — have created superbugs: bacteria that have evolved to survive the drugs designed to kill them.

Key Fact

MRSA is one of the best-known superbugs. If antibiotic resistance continues to grow, routine operations like hip replacements, caesarean sections, and even dental surgery could become life-threatening because doctors would be unable to prevent or treat post-operative infections. The World Health Organization has called antibiotic resistance “one of the biggest threats to global health.”

Antibiotics have been overprescribed for decades — given for viral infections (where they have no effect), not finished by patients (letting partly resistant bacteria survive), and used routinely in farming to promote growth. Each time antibiotics are used wrongly, it gives bacteria a chance to evolve resistance.

At the same time, drug companies have invested less in new antibiotics because they are less profitable than drugs for long-term conditions. The result is a growing gap between resistant infections and the drugs available to treat them.

Challenges for the NHS

An Ageing Population

Britain’s population is ageing. Better healthcare and rising life expectancy mean more people are living into their 80s and 90s. This is a triumph of medical progress, but it puts huge pressure on the NHS. Older people are far more likely to have chronic conditions — heart disease, diabetes, dementia — that need expensive, ongoing care.

Key Fact

People aged 65 and over account for around 40% of NHS spending, despite making up less than 20% of the population. As the proportion of elderly people grows, the financial pressure on the NHS will only increase — raising difficult questions about how healthcare is funded and prioritised.

Rising Costs & Expectations

Modern medicine can achieve extraordinary things — but at huge cost. Treatments like chemotherapy, radiotherapy, robotic surgery, and gene therapy are all expensive. The NHS now costs around £201 billion per year to run. At the same time, public expectations have risen — people expect the latest treatments and shorter waiting times. Balancing demand against limited resources is one of the NHS’s greatest ongoing challenges.

Government Public Health Campaigns

Faced with rising lifestyle diseases, governments have used campaigns and laws to change behaviour and prevent illness. This continues the tradition of government action in public health that stretches back to the Public Health Acts of the nineteenth century.

Smoking bans: England banned smoking in enclosed public places in 2007. Smoking rates have fallen sharply, reducing lung cancer, heart disease, and respiratory illness.

Sugar tax: The 2018 Soft Drinks Industry Levy pushes manufacturers to cut sugar in drinks. It targets childhood obesity and type 2 diabetes.

Alcohol limits: Government campaigns promote lower recommended limits (14 units per week) to reduce liver disease, some cancers, and alcohol-related harm.

COVID-19 response: The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021) was the most dramatic government public health action in living memory. National lockdowns, mass testing, and the fastest vaccine rollout in history showed the scale of modern intervention — but also exposed inequalities in health outcomes based on income, ethnicity, and geography.

The Rise of Alternative Medicine & Skepticism

Despite huge advances in modern medicine, interest has grown in alternative medicine — treatments like acupuncture, aromatherapy, homeopathy, and hypnotherapy that sit outside mainstream evidence-based medicine. Some people turn to these when conventional medicine cannot cure their condition, or because they prefer a more “natural” approach.

Vaccine Skepticism

A more dangerous trend is growing vaccine skepticism. In 1998, Andrew Wakefield published a fraudulent study claiming the MMR vaccine caused autism. The study was later thoroughly discredited — Wakefield was struck off the medical register and the paper was retracted — but the damage was done. Vaccination rates dropped, and measles — almost eliminated — began to return.

Key Fact

The MMR scare is a powerful example of how misinformation can undermine public health. Lower vaccination rates in some areas led to outbreaks of measles — a preventable disease. It demonstrates that public health progress is never permanent and can be reversed by loss of public trust.

The Thalidomide Disaster

Key Fact

The thalidomide disaster is one of modern medicine’s most important warnings. In the late 1950s, thalidomide was prescribed to pregnant women for morning sickness. It caused severe birth defects — thousands of babies were born with shortened or missing limbs. The scandal led to much stricter drug testing and regulation, and is a stark reminder that thorough testing is essential before treatments reach patients.

Genetic Medicine & DNA

One of the most exciting areas of modern medicine is genetic medicine — using knowledge of DNA and genes to prevent, diagnose, and treat disease. This field has been transformed by the Human Genome Project.

1953

Watson and Crick discover the structure of DNA

James Watson and Francis Crick (building on Rosalind Franklin’s X-ray work) identify the double-helix structure of DNA, the molecule that carries genetic instructions for all living things.

1990–2003

The Human Genome Project

An international project to map the entire human genetic code. It identified approximately 20,000–25,000 human genes and created the foundation for genetic medicine.

2000s onwards

Gene therapy trials begin

Gene therapy — replacing or repairing faulty genes to treat disease — enters clinical trials for conditions including certain cancers, inherited blindness, and blood disorders.

Present day

Personalised medicine

Personalised medicine allows treatments to be tailored to a patient’s individual genetic profile. Cancer patients, for example, can have their tumour’s DNA analysed to find the most effective drug for their specific type.

Key Fact — Genetic Testing in Practice

In 2013, actress Angelina Jolie publicly announced that genetic testing had revealed she carried a mutated BRCA1 gene, giving her a very high risk of developing breast cancer — her mother, grandmother, and aunt had all died of cancer. She chose to have a preventative double mastectomy to reduce her risk. This high-profile case showed how DNA knowledge can now allow people to take preventative action against diseases they have not yet developed — a revolutionary shift from treating illness after it appears to preventing it before it begins.

Genetic medicine fits into the broader course narrative. Just as Jenner’s vaccination, Pasteur’s germ theory, and Fleming’s penicillin each changed everything, genetic medicine could transform twenty-first century healthcare. It also raises ethical questions about privacy, cost, and access — echoing debates about the NHS and public health throughout the course.

Examiners increasingly expect students to show awareness of modern developments and connect them to broader themes — especially the role of science and technology in medical change.

Exam Tip

Think about the “paradox of progress” — medical successes creating new problems. Antibiotics saved millions but created superbugs. Living longer means more demand for expensive care. The NHS provides universal healthcare but struggles with costs. This evaluative thinking — showing that progress brings both benefits and new challenges — is what examiners reward at the highest levels.

Exam Tip — Cure vs Prevention

Modern public health has two broad approaches: cure (treating diseases through drugs, surgery, or technology like chemotherapy, transplants, and gene therapy) and prevention (stopping diseases before they happen through vaccination, lifestyle campaigns, or laws like the smoking ban and sugar tax). Strong exam answers discuss both and evaluate which has had more impact. This framework shows the analytical thinking that earns top marks.

Conclusion

Modern public health is shaped by the paradox of progress. Past breakthroughs — vaccination, antibiotics, sanitation, the NHS — have extended life expectancy, but also created new challenges. Today’s biggest threats are lifestyle illnesses, antibiotic resistance from decades of overuse, the financial strain of an ageing population, and the need to harness genetic medicine while keeping it accessible and affordable.

Key Takeaways

  • The paradox of progress: Medical success has created new problems — lifestyle diseases, superbugs, and the rising costs of an ageing population are all consequences of earlier breakthroughs.
  • Government intervention continues: From smoking bans and sugar taxes to the COVID-19 lockdowns, the government remains central to public health — continuing the tradition that began with the nineteenth-century Public Health Acts.
  • Genetic medicine is the new frontier: The Human Genome Project, gene therapy, and personalised medicine could transform twenty-first century healthcare — just as vaccination, germ theory, and antibiotics did before.
