Modern Public Health Challenges

The Paradox of Progress

Modern public health is, in many ways, a victim of its own success. The breakthroughs of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries — vaccination, antibiotics, improved sanitation, the NHS — have dramatically extended life expectancy. But living longer has created an entirely new set of challenges. The diseases that dominate modern Britain are not the infectious epidemics of the past, but long-term degenerative conditions and illnesses linked to the way we live.

Key Fact

In 1900, average life expectancy in Britain was around 47 years. By 2020, it had risen to over 81 years. This extraordinary improvement is the result of centuries of medical progress — but it means today’s biggest health challenges are diseases of old age and lifestyle, not the infectious diseases that once killed millions.

New Diseases & Lifestyle Challenges

Long-Term Degenerative Conditions

As people live longer, the biggest medical challenges are conditions where the body’s cells and organs gradually deteriorate over time. These include:

  • Cancer — abnormal cell growth that can affect almost any part of the body. Risk increases significantly with age.
  • Alzheimer’s disease — a progressive brain disorder that destroys memory and cognitive function. It is the most common cause of dementia.
  • Parkinson’s disease — a condition affecting movement, causing tremors, stiffness, and difficulty with balance and coordination.

Lifestyle Diseases

Many of today’s most common illnesses are directly linked to modern lifestyles — poor diet, lack of exercise, smoking, and excessive alcohol consumption. These lifestyle diseases include:

  • Type 2 diabetes — strongly linked to obesity and poor diet. Cases have risen dramatically in recent decades.
  • Heart disease — the leading cause of death in the UK, linked to smoking, high-fat diets, and lack of exercise.
  • Some cancers — including lung cancer (linked to smoking), liver cancer (linked to alcohol), and bowel cancer (linked to processed meat and low-fibre diets).

Antibiotic Resistance

One of the most serious global health threats of the twenty-first century is antibiotic resistance. Decades of overusing and misusing antibiotics — both in human medicine and in farming — have created superbugs: bacteria that have evolved to survive the drugs designed to kill them.

Key Fact

MRSA is one of the best-known superbugs. If antibiotic resistance continues to grow, routine operations like hip replacements, caesarean sections, and even dental surgery could become life-threatening because doctors would be unable to prevent or treat post-operative infections. The World Health Organization has called antibiotic resistance “one of the biggest threats to global health.”

Antibiotics have been overprescribed for decades — given for viral infections (where they have no effect), not completed by patients (allowing partially resistant bacteria to survive), and used routinely in livestock farming to promote growth. Each time antibiotics are used unnecessarily or incompletely, it gives bacteria an opportunity to evolve resistance.

At the same time, pharmaceutical companies have invested less in developing new antibiotics because they are less profitable than drugs for long-term conditions. The result is a growing gap between resistant infections and the drugs available to treat them.

Challenges for the NHS

An Ageing Population

Britain’s population is ageing. Improved healthcare and rising life expectancy mean that more people are living into their 80s and 90s. While this is a triumph of medical progress, it places enormous pressure on the NHS. Older people are far more likely to live with chronic conditions — heart disease, diabetes, dementia — that require expensive, ongoing care over many years.

Key Fact

People aged 65 and over account for around 40% of NHS spending, despite making up less than 20% of the population. As the proportion of elderly people grows, the financial pressure on the NHS will only increase — raising difficult questions about how healthcare is funded and prioritised.

Rising Costs & Expectations

Modern medicine can achieve extraordinary things — but at extraordinary cost. New drugs, technologies, and treatments (such as targeted cancer therapies, robotic surgery, and gene therapy) are often very expensive. At the same time, public expectations have risen: people expect access to the latest treatments and shorter waiting times. Balancing demand against finite resources is one of the NHS’s greatest ongoing challenges.

Government Public Health Campaigns

Faced with the rise of lifestyle diseases, governments have increasingly used public health campaigns and legislation to try to change behaviour and prevent illness before it occurs. This represents a continuation of the tradition of government intervention in public health that stretches back to the Public Health Acts of the nineteenth century.

Smoking bans: The ban on smoking in enclosed public places (2007 in England) was one of the most significant public health measures of the twenty-first century. Smoking rates have fallen dramatically, reducing rates of lung cancer, heart disease, and respiratory illness.

Sugar tax: Introduced in 2018, the Soft Drinks Industry Levy encourages manufacturers to reduce sugar content in drinks. It targets childhood obesity and type 2 diabetes, two of the fastest-growing public health problems.

Alcohol limits: Government campaigns have promoted lower recommended alcohol limits (14 units per week for both men and women) to reduce liver disease, some cancers, and alcohol-related accidents.

COVID-19 response: The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021) was the most dramatic example of government public health action in living memory. National lockdowns, mass testing, the furlough scheme, and the fastest vaccine rollout in history demonstrated the scale of modern government intervention — but also exposed inequalities in health outcomes based on income, ethnicity, and geography.

The Rise of Alternative Medicine & Skepticism

Despite the enormous advances of modern medicine, there has been a growing interest in alternative medicine — treatments such as acupuncture, aromatherapy, homeopathy, and hypnotherapy that sit outside mainstream evidence-based medicine. Some people turn to these when conventional medicine cannot cure their condition, or because they prefer a more “natural” approach.

Vaccine Skepticism

A more dangerous trend is the growth of vaccine skepticism. In 1998, Andrew Wakefield published a fraudulent study claiming a link between the MMR vaccine and autism. Although the study was later thoroughly discredited — Wakefield was struck off the medical register and the paper was retracted — the damage was done. Vaccination rates dropped, and measles — a disease that had been almost eliminated — began to return.

Key Fact

The MMR scare is a powerful example of how misinformation can undermine public health. Lower vaccination rates in some areas led to outbreaks of measles — a preventable disease. It demonstrates that public health progress is never permanent and can be reversed by loss of public trust.

Genetic Medicine & DNA

One of the most exciting frontiers in modern medicine is genetic medicine — the use of knowledge about DNA and genes to prevent, diagnose, and treat disease. This field has been transformed by the Human Genome Project.

1953

Watson and Crick discover the structure of DNA

James Watson and Francis Crick (building on Rosalind Franklin’s X-ray work) identify the double-helix structure of DNA, the molecule that carries genetic instructions for all living things.

1990–2003

The Human Genome Project

An international project to map the entire human genetic code. It identified approximately 20,000–25,000 human genes and created the foundation for genetic medicine.

2000s onwards

Gene therapy trials begin

Gene therapy — replacing or repairing faulty genes to treat disease — enters clinical trials for conditions including certain cancers, inherited blindness, and blood disorders.

Present day

Personalised medicine

Personalised medicine allows treatments to be tailored to a patient’s individual genetic profile. Cancer patients, for example, can have their tumour’s DNA analysed to find the most effective drug for their specific type.

Genetic medicine represents the cutting edge of medical progress and fits into the broader narrative of the course. Just as Jenner’s vaccination, Pasteur’s germ theory, and Fleming’s penicillin each represented a breakthrough that changed everything, genetic medicine has the potential to transform healthcare in the twenty-first century. It also raises new ethical questions about privacy, cost, and access — echoing the debates around the NHS and public health that run throughout the course.

Examiners increasingly expect students to show awareness of modern developments and to connect them to the broader themes of the course — particularly the role of science and technology as factors in medical change.

Exam Tip

Think about the “paradox of progress” — how medical successes have created new problems. Antibiotics saved millions but created superbugs. Living longer has increased demand for expensive care. The NHS provides universal healthcare but struggles with rising costs. This kind of evaluative thinking — showing that progress brings both benefits and new challenges — is exactly what examiners reward at the highest levels.

Conclusion

Modern public health is shaped by the paradox of progress. The medical breakthroughs of the past — vaccination, antibiotics, improved sanitation, the NHS — have extended life expectancy dramatically, but in doing so they have created new challenges. Today’s biggest threats are lifestyle illnesses linked to diet, exercise, and smoking; antibiotic resistance caused by decades of overuse; the financial strain of caring for an ageing population; and the need to harness the extraordinary potential of genetic medicine while keeping it accessible and affordable.

Key Takeaways

  • The paradox of progress: Medical success has created new problems — lifestyle diseases, superbugs, and the growing costs of caring for an ageing population are all, in a sense, the consequences of earlier breakthroughs.
  • Government intervention continues: From smoking bans and sugar taxes to the COVID-19 lockdowns, the government remains a central actor in public health — continuing the tradition of intervention that began with the Public Health Acts of the nineteenth century.
  • Genetic medicine is the new frontier: The Human Genome Project, gene therapy, and personalised medicine have the potential to transform healthcare in the twenty-first century — just as vaccination, germ theory, and antibiotics did in earlier eras.
