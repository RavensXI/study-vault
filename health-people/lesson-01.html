The Four Humours & Supernatural

Introduction: How Did People Explain Disease?

For most of history, nobody understood what caused disease. Without microscopes or modern science, doctors relied on observation, philosophy, and religion to explain illness. The ideas they came up with — from the Four Humours to supernatural explanations — dominated medicine for over a thousand years.

This lesson covers the key ideas about disease in the ancient and medieval periods: the work of Hippocrates and Galen, supernatural beliefs, and the role of astrology in diagnosis.

Hippocrates & the Theory of the Four Humours

Hippocrates (c.460–370 BC) was an Ancient Greek physician often called the “Father of Medicine.” Before him, most Greeks believed illness was a punishment from the gods. Hippocrates argued that disease had natural causes and looked for physical explanations instead.

Key Fact — The Four Humours

Hippocrates believed the body contained four main fluids, or “humours”:

  • Blood — linked to spring, air, and a hot & wet nature
  • Yellow Bile — linked to summer, fire, and a hot & dry nature
  • Black Bile — linked to autumn, earth, and a cold & dry nature
  • Phlegm — linked to winter, water, and a cold & wet nature

If these humours were perfectly balanced, the person was healthy. If one became too dominant, they fell ill.

Hippocrates’ Approach: Clinical Observation

Hippocrates also promoted clinical observation — carefully watching patients, recording symptoms, and tracking how illness progressed. This pushed doctors towards evidence and reason rather than prayers or rituals.

He compiled medical texts known as the Hippocratic Corpus, covering diagnosis, treatment, surgery, and the role of diet and exercise. These texts were studied for centuries and became the foundation of Western medical training.

Key Fact — The Hippocratic Oath

The Hippocratic Oath is a set of ethical rules for doctors attributed to Hippocrates. It included promises to treat patients to the best of one’s ability, do no harm, and keep patient information private. Versions of this oath are still used by doctors today.

Galen & the Theory of Opposites

Galen (c.129–216 AD) was a Roman physician of Greek origin. He studied anatomy in Egypt by examining dead bodies and bones, then spent 15 years as a doctor at a gladiator school — giving him rare hands-on experience of injuries and wound treatment. He built on Hippocrates’ ideas by developing the Theory of Opposites: if a patient had too much of one humour, treat them with the “opposite” quality to restore balance.

Galen also proved that the brain, not the heart, controlled the body by cutting a pig’s spinal cord at different points. However, because he dissected animals rather than humans, many of his anatomical observations contained errors that went unchallenged for centuries.

Key Fact — Theory of Opposites in Practice

A fever (too much blood — hot & wet) would be treated with something cold or dry. A cold (too much phlegm — cold & wet) would be treated with pepper or hot spices. This gave doctors a logical system for choosing treatments, even though the theory itself was wrong.

Key Fact — Treating Each Humour

Each humour had a specific treatment designed to remove or reduce it when in excess:

  • Too much blood → bleeding (using cups or leeches to draw blood out)
  • Too much yellow bile → vomiting, induced by giving the patient emetics
  • Too much black bile → purging, using laxatives to empty the bowels
  • Too much phlegm → warming and sweating the patient to dry out the excess moisture

Why Did Galen’s Ideas Last So Long?

Galen’s ideas dominated European medicine for over 1,500 years. Why did they last so long?

Volume of writing: Galen produced over 350 books on anatomy, disease, and treatment. His work became the standard reference across Europe and the Islamic world.

Church support: Galen believed the body was perfectly designed, which fitted the Christian belief that God created humans. The Church promoted his work — challenging Galen meant challenging the Church.

No way to check: The Church banned human dissection for much of the medieval period, so nobody could test whether Galen’s descriptions were correct. Many were based on animal dissections and contained serious errors.

Collapse of the Roman Empire: After Rome fell in 410 AD, much knowledge was lost. The texts that survived — often preserved by Islamic scholars — were treated with huge respect. Few dared question the ancients.

Supernatural Beliefs About Disease

Although Hippocrates and Galen promoted natural explanations, supernatural beliefs remained very common throughout the medieval period. For most ordinary people — and for the Church — disease was linked to religion.

God’s Punishment

The most common supernatural explanation was that disease was a punishment from God for sinful behaviour. The Bible described God sending plagues to punish the wicked, and medieval people took this literally. If someone fell ill, it was often assumed they had angered God.

If disease came from God, the logical cure was a religious one: prayer, repentance, fasting, pilgrimage, or donating to the Church. Sick people visited holy shrines, prayed to healing saints, or touched sacred relics hoping to be cured.

Key Fact

Because people believed God sent disease, the Church played a central role in healthcare. Priests were often consulted before doctors, and prayer and pilgrimage were among the most common “treatments.”

The Role of Astrology in Diagnosis

Astrology played a major role in medieval medicine. Physicians consulted zodiac charts to diagnose illness and decide when to treat patients, believing the stars and planets could affect the balance of humours.

Different zodiac signs were linked to different body parts — Aries to the head, Pisces to the feet. A doctor might refuse to perform bloodletting if the stars were not in a favourable position. Even university-trained physicians were expected to understand astrology.

Without microscopes, there was no way to see germs. People noticed disease could spread and seemed linked to the seasons, but had no scientific way to explain why. Supernatural and astrological explanations made sense with the knowledge available.

The Church reinforced these beliefs. It controlled education, universities, and most books, so ideas that contradicted religious teaching were suppressed. Most people were illiterate and relied on their priest to explain the world.

Key Individuals: A Timeline

c.460–370 BC

Hippocrates

Developed the Four Humours theory, promoted clinical observation, and argued disease had natural — not supernatural — causes. Credited with the Hippocratic Oath.

c.129–216 AD

Galen

Built on Hippocrates with the Theory of Opposites. Wrote over 350 books. His ideas were promoted by the Church and dominated European medicine for 1,500+ years.

Medieval Period (c.500–1500)

Supernatural beliefs dominate

Most people still believed disease was sent by God as punishment. Astrology, prayer, and pilgrimage remained central to medical practice.

Exam Tip

When writing about the Four Humours, link it to bigger themes: observation vs. religion, why ideas stayed the same for so long (Church support, no technology, respect for ancients), and how the Renaissance later challenged them. Examiners reward answers that show continuity and change.

Conclusion

The Four Humours was one of the earliest attempts to explain how the body works. Although it was wrong, it was a key shift towards natural — rather than supernatural — explanations for disease. Yet supernatural beliefs stayed central to everyday medicine throughout the medieval period.

Key Takeaways

  • Hippocrates introduced natural explanations: The Four Humours said illness came from imbalanced bodily fluids, not the gods. His focus on clinical observation laid the groundwork for evidence-based medicine.
  • Galen dominated for 1,500+ years: Church support, dissection bans, and the sheer volume of his writing made his ideas almost impossible to challenge — even the errors.
  • Supernatural beliefs coexisted with rational medicine: Even educated doctors used astrology alongside the Four Humours. For most people, prayer and pilgrimage came first. This mix of rational and supernatural thinking is key for the exam.
