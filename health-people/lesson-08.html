The Germ Theory: Pasteur and Koch

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Introduction: The Invisible Enemy

For centuries, people had no idea what actually caused disease. Theories ranged from “bad air” (miasma) to punishment from God to imbalances in the Four Humours. In the 1860s, two scientists — one French, one German — would finally reveal the true cause: tiny, invisible organisms called germs.

Louis Pasteur and the Germ Theory (1861)

Louis Pasteur was a French chemist, not a doctor. He was hired by a brewing company to find out why their beer was going sour. It was this practical, industrial problem — not a medical question — that led to one of the most important discoveries in the history of medicine.

Through his experiments, Pasteur discovered that tiny living organisms, which he called microbes or “germs,” were floating in the air and causing the beer to spoil. This was a revolutionary idea because most scientists at the time believed in spontaneous generation — the idea that decay and life simply appeared out of nowhere.

To prove that germs were in the air and not just appearing out of nothing, Pasteur conducted a famous experiment. He placed broth in a flask with a long, curved “swan neck.” Air could enter, but the curve trapped any airborne microbes before they could reach the broth.

The broth in the sealed swan-necked flask stayed fresh. But when the neck was broken off, allowing microbes to fall directly into the broth, it quickly went cloudy and sour. This conclusively proved that germs in the air caused decay — not spontaneous generation.

Key Fact — Pasteur’s Germ Theory (1861)
  • Germs are present in the air.
  • Germs cause decay.
  • These same germs could also be responsible for causing disease in humans.

Robert Koch: The Microbe Hunter

While Pasteur had the big idea — that germs cause disease — the German doctor Robert Koch provided the practical proof. He took Pasteur’s theory and developed a systematic way to identify the specific microbes that caused specific diseases. This was the crucial next step.

Koch’s Methods

Koch developed two key techniques that made it possible to study bacteria with precision:

Key Fact — Koch’s Techniques
  • Growing bacteria: Koch was the first to grow bacteria on a solid medium using agar jelly plates. This allowed him to create pure cultures — samples containing only one type of bacterium — so he could study each germ individually.
  • Staining bacteria: Koch used industrial chemical dyes to stain bacteria, making them visible under a microscope. Different bacteria absorbed different dyes, allowing him to tell them apart.
1861

Pasteur publishes Germ Theory

Pasteur proves that microbes in the air cause decay, proposing that germs could also cause disease in humans.

1876

Koch identifies the anthrax bacterium

Using his new techniques of growing and staining bacteria, Koch identified the exact bacterium that caused anthrax — the first time a specific germ was linked to a specific disease.

1882

Koch identifies the tuberculosis bacterium

Koch identified the bacterium responsible for tuberculosis (TB), one of the biggest killers of the 19th century. This was proof that different germs caused different diseases.

1883

Koch identifies the cholera bacterium

Koch identified the bacterium that caused cholera, confirming the earlier findings of John Snow, who had linked cholera to contaminated water in 1854.

The Impact on Britain

The new Germ Theory was not accepted straight away in Britain. Many doctors were resistant to changing their long-held beliefs. However, several British doctors helped promote the new ideas:

British doctors like William Roberts and William Cheyne helped promote the new ideas of Pasteur and Koch in Britain. Their advocacy was important in persuading the medical establishment to take Germ Theory seriously.

Germ Theory had a chain of consequences that transformed medicine:

  • It confirmed John Snow’s findings about cholera being spread through contaminated water (1854), providing the scientific explanation for what Snow had observed.
  • It inspired Joseph Lister to develop antiseptic surgery in 1867, dramatically reducing deaths from infection after operations.
  • It enabled Koch’s student Paul Ehrlich to develop “magic bullets” — chemical drugs that could target and destroy specific germs inside the body.
Key Fact — The Rivalry

Pasteur (France) and Koch (Germany) were fierce rivals, driven partly by national competition between France and Germany after the Franco-Prussian War (1870–71). This rivalry actually accelerated progress, as each tried to outdo the other in identifying germs and developing vaccines.

Exam Tip

Germ Theory is a classic “turning point” in history. In exam answers, explain how it changed the direction of medicine: before Germ Theory, doctors treated symptoms without knowing causes. After it, they could identify specific germs, develop targeted treatments, and prevent diseases through vaccines and public health measures. Always link Pasteur’s theory to Koch’s practical proof and its wider consequences (Lister, Ehrlich, public health reform).

Conclusion

Pasteur gave the world the theory — that germs cause disease — and Koch gave the world the practical methods to prove it and use it. Together, their work transformed medicine from guesswork into science. The identification of specific germs for specific diseases opened the door to targeted treatments, vaccines, and the public health reforms that would save millions of lives.

Key Takeaways

  • Pasteur provided the theory: His 1861 Germ Theory proved that microbes cause decay and proposed they cause disease. His swan-necked flask experiment disproved spontaneous generation once and for all.
  • Koch provided the proof: Using agar jelly plates and chemical dyes, Koch identified the specific bacteria causing anthrax (1876), tuberculosis (1882), and cholera (1883), proving that different germs cause different diseases.
  • The impact was transformative: Germ Theory confirmed Snow’s cholera findings, inspired Lister’s antiseptic surgery, and enabled Ehrlich’s “magic bullets” — changing the entire direction of medical science.
