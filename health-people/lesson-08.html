The Germ Theory: Pasteur and Koch

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Introduction: The Invisible Enemy

For centuries, nobody knew what caused disease. People blamed “bad air” (miasma), punishment from God, or imbalances in the Four Humours. In the 1860s, two scientists — one French, one German — revealed the true cause: tiny, invisible organisms called germs.

Louis Pasteur and the Germ Theory (1861)

Louis Pasteur was a French chemist, not a doctor. He was asked to investigate why beer and wine were going sour in the barrel — a serious problem for France’s wine industry. This practical, industrial problem — not a medical question — led to one of the biggest discoveries in medicine.

Pasteur discovered that tiny living organisms — microbes or “germs” — were floating in the air and causing the beer to spoil. This was revolutionary. Most scientists believed in spontaneous generation — the idea that decay and life simply appeared out of nowhere.

Pasteur placed broth in a flask with a long, curved “swan neck.” Air could enter, but the curve trapped any airborne microbes before they reached the broth.

The broth stayed fresh. But when the neck was broken off, letting microbes fall in, it quickly went cloudy and sour. This proved that germs in the air caused decay — not spontaneous generation.

Key Fact — Pasteur’s Germ Theory (1861)
  • Germs are present in the air.
  • Germs cause decay.
  • These same germs could also be responsible for causing disease in humans.

Pasteur then received government funding to investigate a disease killing French silkworms. He proved germs were causing it — a key step in linking germs to disease in living organisms, not just decay in liquids.

Robert Koch: The Microbe Hunter

Pasteur had the big idea — that germs cause disease. The German doctor Robert Koch provided the practical proof. Koch studied under Professor Friedrich Henle, one of the first scientists to challenge spontaneous generation. He also served as a surgeon during the Franco-Prussian War (1870–71), giving him first-hand experience of wounds and infection. Koch took Pasteur’s theory and developed a way to identify the specific microbes that caused specific diseases.

Koch’s Methods

Koch developed key techniques for studying bacteria. He established the principle of specificity — that specific germs cause specific diseases. This meant each disease could be identified, targeted, and treated individually.

Key Fact — Koch’s Techniques
  • Growing bacteria: Koch was the first to grow bacteria on a solid medium using agar jelly plates. This allowed him to create pure cultures — samples containing only one type of bacterium — so he could study each germ individually.
  • Staining bacteria: Koch used industrial chemical dyes to stain bacteria, making them visible under a microscope. Different bacteria absorbed different dyes, allowing him to tell them apart.
  • Photographing bacteria: Koch developed methods of photographing microbes under the microscope, creating permanent visual records that other scientists around the world could study and verify.
  • Proving infection: Koch proved bacteria caused disease by injecting mice with anthrax bacteria and observing them fall ill. This provided direct experimental evidence that a specific germ caused a specific disease in a living organism.
1861

Pasteur publishes Germ Theory

Pasteur proves that microbes in the air cause decay, proposing that germs could also cause disease in humans.

1876

Koch identifies the anthrax bacterium

Using his new techniques of growing and staining bacteria, Koch identified the exact bacterium that caused anthrax — the first time a specific germ was linked to a specific disease.

1882

Koch identifies the tuberculosis bacterium

Koch identified the bacterium responsible for tuberculosis (TB), one of the biggest killers of the 19th century. This was proof that different germs caused different diseases.

1883

Koch identifies the cholera bacterium

Koch identified the bacterium that caused cholera, confirming the earlier findings of John Snow, who had linked cholera to contaminated water in 1854.

1905

Koch awarded the Nobel Prize

Koch was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for his work on tuberculosis, recognising his enormous contribution to medical science.

Koch’s team — nicknamed “the Microbe Hunters” — identified over 21 disease-causing bacteria, including those responsible for cholera, typhoid, meningitis, and plague.

The Impact on Britain

Germ Theory was not accepted straight away in Britain. Many doctors resisted changing their beliefs. Improved microscope technology was a key enabling factor — without better lenses, scientists could not have seen bacteria clearly enough to study them. Several British doctors helped promote the new ideas:

British doctors like William Roberts and William Cheyne helped promote Pasteur and Koch’s ideas in Britain.

A key turning point came during the cattle plague of 1866. Professor Lionel Beale investigated and found the disease was caused by microscopic living particles — strong evidence for Germ Theory. From 1870, physicist John Tyndall used his reputation and speaking skills to convince the British scientific establishment.

Germ Theory had a chain of consequences:

  • It confirmed John Snow’s findings about cholera spreading through contaminated water (1854).
  • It inspired Joseph Lister to develop antiseptic surgery in 1867, cutting deaths from infection after operations.
  • It enabled Koch’s student Paul Ehrlich to develop “magic bullets” — drugs that could target and destroy specific germs in the body.
Key Fact — The Rivalry

Pasteur (France) and Koch (Germany) were fierce rivals, driven partly by national competition between France and Germany after the Franco-Prussian War (1870–71). This rivalry actually accelerated progress, as each tried to outdo the other in identifying germs and developing vaccines.

Exam Tip

Germ Theory is a classic “turning point.” Show how it changed the direction of medicine: before it, doctors treated symptoms without knowing causes. After it, they could identify specific germs, develop targeted treatments, and prevent diseases through vaccines and public health. Always link Pasteur’s theory to Koch’s proof and its wider consequences (Lister, Ehrlich, public health reform).

Conclusion

Pasteur gave the world the theory — that germs cause disease — and Koch gave the practical methods to prove it. Together, they turned medicine from guesswork into science, opening the door to targeted treatments, vaccines, and public health reforms.

Key Takeaways

  • Pasteur provided the theory: His 1861 Germ Theory proved microbes cause decay and proposed they cause disease. His swan-necked flask experiment disproved spontaneous generation.
  • Koch provided the proof: Using agar jelly plates and chemical dyes, he identified bacteria causing anthrax (1876), TB (1882), and cholera (1883).
  • The impact was huge: Germ Theory confirmed Snow’s cholera findings, inspired Lister’s antiseptic surgery, and enabled Ehrlich’s “magic bullets.”
