Public Health: Industrial Britain

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Problem: Industrial Cities & Laissez-Faire

The Industrial Revolution transformed Britain, but it also created a public health disaster. Millions flooded into cities like Manchester, Leeds, and London to work in factories. London grew from about 1 million people in 1801 to over 5.5 million by 1901. Manchester grew from 334,000 to over 2.1 million. These cities had overcrowded housing, contaminated water, open sewers, and no sanitation. Families were crammed into tiny, damp back-to-back houses with no running water or toilets.

Key Fact

The government followed a policy of “laissez-faire” — meaning they believed the government should not interfere in people’s lives. Wealthy taxpayers did not want to pay for improvements that would benefit the poor. Many people also believed in “self-help” — the idea that poverty and poor health were a person’s own responsibility, and that it was not the government’s job to intervene. This attitude meant that despite appalling conditions, the government refused to act for decades.

Disease thrived in these conditions. Cholera arrived in Britain in 1831, killing over 50,000 people. Further epidemics struck in 1848, 1854, and 1866. Cholera could kill a healthy person within hours — and it finally forced the government to reconsider its laissez-faire approach.

Key Individuals

Edwin Chadwick

Edwin Chadwick was a civil servant and the most important campaigner for public health reform. In 1842, he published his landmark report, “The Sanitary Conditions of the Labouring Population.” He used statistics to show the link between filthy living conditions and disease. His key argument was economic: it would be cheaper to pay taxes for public health than to keep supporting sick workers, widows, and orphans through the Poor Law.

Key Fact

Chadwick’s report revealed that the average life expectancy for a labourer in Manchester was just 17 years, compared to 38 for a gentleman in a rural area. These shocking statistics helped build the case for government intervention, though change was painfully slow.

Chadwick was deeply unpopular. Wealthy ratepayers resented paying for improvements to benefit the poor. Local councils objected to being told what to do by central government. Chadwick was seen as arrogant and interfering. He still believed in the miasma theory — that disease was caused by bad smells, not germs — but cleaning up cities removed the germs anyway.

John Snow and the Broad Street Pump (1854)

During a cholera outbreak in London in 1854, doctor John Snow investigated deaths in Soho. He marked every cholera death on a map and noticed they clustered around the Broad Street water pump. A cesspit near the pump was leaking sewage into the water supply. When the pump handle was removed, the outbreak stopped. Snow also noted that workers at a nearby brewery had not fallen ill — they drank beer, not water from the pump.

Key Fact

Snow’s investigation proved that cholera was water-borne, not caused by miasma (bad air). His work is considered a founding moment of epidemiology. However, his findings were initially rejected by many because they contradicted the dominant miasma theory, and the government did not act on his conclusions for years. Crucially, Snow could prove the pattern — that contaminated water caused cholera — but he could not explain why, because he had no knowledge of germs. This is why many doctors rejected his findings and continued to believe in miasma.

Government Action: The Public Health Acts

The 1848 Public Health Act

Chadwick’s report and the 1848 cholera epidemic pushed Parliament to act. The 1848 Public Health Act was Britain’s first public health law. It created a General Board of Health and allowed (but did not force) towns to set up local boards of health. Crucially, it was not compulsory — towns could ignore it, and many did. A first step, but a weak one.

Laissez-faire attitudes were still dominant. Many MPs were wealthy landowners who refused to pay higher taxes. Local councils resented government interference. The Act only required a board of health if the death rate exceeded 23 per 1,000 — so some of the worst areas still avoided action. The General Board of Health was abolished in 1858, partly due to opposition to Chadwick’s heavy-handed approach.

The Great Stink (1858)

The summer of 1858 was very hot, and the River Thames — London’s main sewer — became so foul that the stench overwhelmed the Houses of Parliament. MPs could not work. Parliament was suspended. This event, the “Great Stink,” finally convinced MPs to invest in solving London’s sewage problem — but only because it affected them directly.

Key Fact

Engineer Joseph Bazalgette was hired to build London’s new sewer system. He designed over 1,300 miles of underground sewers, carrying waste away from the city and pumping it downstream. The project took years to complete but transformed London’s public health and virtually eliminated cholera from the capital.

The 1875 Public Health Act

The 1875 Public Health Act was a turning point. Unlike the 1848 Act, it was compulsory. It forced all local councils to provide clean water, build proper sewers, collect rubbish, and appoint a Medical Officer of Health. Streets had to be paved and lit. Housing standards were set. This marked the end of laissez-faire in public health — the government accepted it had a duty to protect citizens’ health.

Key Fact

The shift from the voluntary 1848 Act to the compulsory 1875 Act is one of the most important changes in this topic. The 1875 Act succeeded because it forced every local authority to act, leaving no room for inaction. It was backed by growing scientific understanding (Germ Theory, 1861), the political pressure of new voters after the 1867 Reform Act, and the undeniable evidence of Snow and Chadwick.

1842

Chadwick’s Report

Edwin Chadwick publishes “The Sanitary Conditions of the Labouring Population,” using statistics to prove the link between filth and disease.

1848

First Public Health Act

The first ever public health law, but not compulsory. Creates a General Board of Health. Most towns ignore it.

1854

John Snow and the Broad Street Pump

Snow maps cholera deaths in Soho, proving the disease is water-borne. His findings are initially rejected by the medical establishment.

1858

The Great Stink

The stench from the Thames forces Parliament to act. Bazalgette is hired to build London’s sewer system.

1861

Pasteur’s Germ Theory

Louis Pasteur proves that germs cause disease, providing the scientific explanation for why dirty water and sewage spread cholera.

1884

Koch identifies the cholera bacterium

Robert Koch identifies the specific bacterium that causes cholera, providing the scientific proof that validated Snow’s earlier work and strengthened the case for clean water supplies.

1867

Second Reform Act

Gives the vote to many working-class men in towns. Politicians now need to address the concerns of poorer voters to win elections.

1875

Second Public Health Act

Compulsory legislation forces all councils to provide clean water, proper sewers, rubbish collection, and medical officers of health.

Multiple factors worked together. Chadwick’s statistics showed the economic cost of inaction. Snow proved cholera spread through contaminated water. Pasteur’s Germ Theory (1861) explained why dirty conditions caused disease. The Great Stink forced Parliament to act. And the 1867 Reform Act gave the vote to many working-class men, so politicians had to address poorer voters’ concerns.

No single factor was enough alone. It was the combination of science, campaigners, dramatic events, and political change that ended laissez-faire.

Exam Tip

When explaining why the government’s attitude changed, link factors together. Don’t just list events — show how Chadwick’s evidence, Snow’s proof, Germ Theory, the Great Stink, and the 1867 Reform Act built on each other. The best answers explain why the combination mattered more than any single factor.

Conclusion

The government moved from laissez-faire refusal to full legal responsibility for public health during the 19th century. Cholera epidemics, individual campaigners, scientific breakthroughs, and political reform all drove this change.

Key Takeaways

  • Laissez-faire delayed action for decades: Despite Chadwick’s evidence (1842) and Snow’s proof (1854), compulsory public health legislation did not arrive until 1875 — over thirty years of preventable death.
  • Individuals drove change against fierce opposition: Chadwick, Snow, and Bazalgette each faced resistance but built the evidence base that made reform inevitable.
  • The 1875 Act ended laissez-faire: The shift from the voluntary 1848 Act to the compulsory 1875 Act is the key turning point — the government accepted that public health was its responsibility.
