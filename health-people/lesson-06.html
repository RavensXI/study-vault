Continuity & Change in Medicine

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Introduction: A Time of Transition

The 18th century was a time of transition for medicine in Britain. Vesalius, Paré, and Harvey had made important breakthroughs, but many old ideas about disease and treatment stubbornly persisted. This lesson looks at what stayed the same (continuity) and what was genuinely new (change) in how people understood and treated disease.

What Stayed the Same (Continuity)

Even though science was becoming more important, many old ideas about disease were still popular among ordinary people.

Key Fact — Persistent Old Beliefs
  • Miasma — the belief that “bad air” or foul smells caused illness — was still widely accepted.
  • The ancient Greek idea of the Four Humours had not disappeared entirely.
  • Many people still believed that illness was a punishment from God.

The Medical “Marketplace”

For most people, healthcare in the 1600s–1700s was like a marketplace — they could choose between different practitioners, none of whom were reliably effective:

Key Fact — Who Could You Visit for Medical Help?
  • Barber-surgeons: Poorly trained practitioners who gave haircuts and performed minor operations like tooth-pulling and bloodletting. Surgery was seen as a low-status craft.
  • Apothecaries: Sold medicines and potions, often with little formal training. They were the forerunners of modern pharmacists and often the only medical help for poorer people.
  • Wise women: Used traditional knowledge of herbs and plants, sometimes mixed with superstition. Often the first point of contact for rural communities.
  • Quack doctors: Travelling salesmen who sold fake “cure-all” medicines. There was no regulation to stop them, and desperate people were easy targets.

The lack of regulation meant medical treatment was very inconsistent. Wealthy patients could afford physicians, but even physicians often relied on outdated ideas like bloodletting and purging.

New Treatments from Overseas

As European exploration expanded in the 16th and 17th centuries, new treatments arrived from abroad:

  • Quinine from South American tree bark was used to treat malaria and was one of the first genuinely effective imported medicines.
  • Opium was used as a powerful painkiller, though it caused serious addiction.
  • Tobacco was believed to be a cure-all for almost every illness — a claim since disproved.

Supernatural Beliefs Persisted

Even as new scientific ideas spread, supernatural beliefs remained widespread. Many people believed in the King’s Touch — the idea that the monarch’s touch could cure scrofula (a form of tuberculosis known as the “King’s Evil”). Thousands attended royal ceremonies hoping to be healed. This shows how slowly scientific thinking replaced superstition.

What Was New (Change)

The 18th century was part of the Enlightenment, when science and reason were becoming very important. This spirit of enquiry began to influence medicine.

Key Fact — Key Changes in the 18th Century
  • The growth of hospitals: Before, hospitals were mostly places of “care” run by the Church. New hospitals were now built with the aim of curing patients.
  • Doctors trained in hospitals: Physicians began observing patients in wards, learning through direct experience rather than only from books.
  • Free dispensaries: Set up for the poor, these provided medicine and advice without requiring payment for a doctor’s visit.

Case Study: John Hunter (1728–1793)

John Hunter shows the new scientific approach to medicine. Often called the “Father of Scientific Surgery”, he pushed a new way of thinking about medical practice.

Instead of accepting old ideas from books, Hunter taught that surgeons must learn through observation and experiment. He was a skilled anatomist who carried out many dissections to understand how the body worked.

Hunter collected thousands of anatomical specimens (preserved body parts, organs, and bones) in a museum at his home. He used these to teach students with hands-on experience rather than just textbook lectures.

He experimented on himself and on animals, studying how diseases progressed and how the body healed. He improved techniques for treating wounds and aneurysms, helping turn surgery from a simple craft into a respected science.

His collection later became the basis of the Hunterian Museum at the Royal College of Surgeons in London.

Hunter’s Surgical Innovations

One of Hunter’s most celebrated achievements was treating a popliteal aneurysm — a dangerously swollen artery behind the knee. The standard treatment was amputation, which was brutal and often fatal. Hunter tied off the artery higher up the leg, allowing other blood vessels to expand and keep the leg alive. This showed his approach in action: observe the problem, hypothesise a solution, test it.

Getting bodies for dissection was a major problem. Henry VIII had granted surgeons just 4 hanged criminals per year for anatomical study. As demand grew, this was hopelessly inadequate.

The result was a grim trade in stolen corpses. “Resurrection men” stole fresh bodies from graves and sold them to surgeons and medical schools. Hunter himself obtained bodies this way — many surgeons saw it as a necessary evil.

Hunter was so dedicated that he deliberately infected himself with a sexually transmitted disease to study its effects. This was an extreme and dangerous example of his belief that direct observation was the only way to truly understand disease.

He also ran a large medical practice, trained hundreds of students, and wrote influential books on dentistry, gunshot wounds, and venereal diseases. His teaching shaped a generation of surgeons who spread his evidence-based methods.

Hunter and the Professionalisation of Surgery

Hunter argued that surgeons and barbers should be separated and that surgery should become a respected profession, not a trade practised by people who also cut hair. His campaigning helped establish the Royal College of Surgeons in 1800, which still oversees surgery in England today.

Key Fact — John Hunter

John Hunter promoted observation and experiment over reliance on old textbooks. He collected thousands of anatomical specimens to teach students, pioneered new surgical techniques (including tying off aneurysms instead of amputating), and helped establish surgery as a respected profession through the Royal College of Surgeons. His methods helped transform surgery into a respected, evidence-based science.

Exam Tip

For questions about this period, discuss both continuity and change. Examiners want to see that you understand progress was not straightforward — old beliefs like miasma and the Four Humours persisted even as new ideas emerged. A balanced answer covering both sides will score higher marks.

Conclusion

The 18th century was a time of transition. New scientific thinking was taking hold — especially through figures like John Hunter — but most people still relied on old beliefs and traditional remedies. Real, widespread change would not come until the 19th century.

Key Takeaways

  • Old beliefs persisted: Miasma, the Four Humours, and supernatural explanations remained common, alongside quackery and herbal remedies.
  • New institutions emerged: Hospitals shifted from care to cure, doctors trained through observation, and free dispensaries helped the poor.
  • John Hunter bridged old and new: He championed observation and experimentation, helping transform surgery into a respected, evidence-based profession.
