Continuity & Change in Medicine

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Introduction: A Time of Transition

The 18th century was a fascinating period for medicine in Britain. While Vesalius, Paré, and Harvey had made important breakthroughs, many of the old ideas about disease and treatment stubbornly persisted alongside the new. This lesson examines what stayed the same (continuity) and what was genuinely new (change) in how people understood and treated disease.

What Stayed the Same (Continuity)

Even though science was becoming more important, many old ideas about what caused disease were still very popular among ordinary people. Understanding these continuities is just as important as understanding the changes.

Key Fact — Persistent Old Beliefs
  • Miasma — the belief that “bad air” or foul smells caused illness — was still widely accepted.
  • The ancient Greek idea of the Four Humours had not disappeared entirely.
  • Many people still believed that illness was a punishment from God.

For most ordinary people, treatment was a mixture of traditional, herbal remedies passed down through families. They might visit an apothecary for potions or even a “wise woman” for a charm or herbal cure.

This was also a time of widespread quackery, where untrained people sold fake medicines, promising miracle cures for everything. There was no regulation to stop them, and desperate people were easy targets for these fraudsters.

What Was New (Change)

The 18th century was part of the Enlightenment, a period when science, reason, and new ideas were becoming very important. This spirit of enquiry began to influence medicine in several significant ways.

Key Fact — Key Changes in the 18th Century
  • The growth of hospitals: Before, hospitals were mostly places of “care” run by the Church. New hospitals were now built with the aim of curing patients.
  • Doctors trained in hospitals: Physicians began observing patients in wards, learning through direct experience rather than only from books.
  • Free dispensaries: Set up for the poor, these provided medicine and advice without requiring payment for a doctor’s visit.

Case Study: John Hunter (1728–1793)

John Hunter is a key figure who demonstrates the new scientific approach to medicine. Often called the “Father of Scientific Surgery”, he encouraged a fundamentally new way of thinking about medical practice.

Instead of just accepting old ideas from books, Hunter taught that surgeons must learn through observation and experiment. He was a skilled anatomist and carried out many dissections to better understand how the human body worked.

Hunter collected thousands of anatomical specimens (preserved body parts, organs, and bones) which he kept in a museum at his home. He used this collection to teach his students, giving them hands-on experience rather than just lectures from textbooks.

He experimented on himself and on animals, studying how diseases progressed and how the body healed. He improved techniques for treating wounds and aneurysms, and his insistence on scientific method helped turn surgery from a simple craft performed by barber-surgeons into a more respected science.

Hunter’s collection eventually became the basis of the Hunterian Museum at the Royal College of Surgeons in London, inspiring generations of surgeons to come.

Key Fact — John Hunter

John Hunter promoted observation and experiment over reliance on old textbooks. He collected thousands of anatomical specimens to teach students. His methods helped transform surgery into a respected, evidence-based science.

Exam Tip

For questions about this period, remember to discuss both continuity and change. Examiners want to see that you understand progress was not straightforward — old beliefs like miasma and the Four Humours persisted even as new scientific ideas were emerging. A balanced answer that covers both sides will score higher marks.

Conclusion

The 18th century was a time of transition for medicine in Britain. While new scientific thinking was beginning to take hold — especially through figures like John Hunter — the majority of people still relied on old beliefs and traditional remedies. Real, widespread change would not come until the discoveries of the 19th century.

Key Takeaways

  • Old beliefs persisted: Miasma, the Four Humours, and supernatural explanations for disease remained common, alongside quackery and traditional herbal remedies.
  • New institutions and ideas emerged: Hospitals shifted from places of care to places of cure, doctors began training through observation, and free dispensaries helped the poor access medicine.
  • John Hunter bridged old and new: As the “Father of Scientific Surgery,” Hunter championed observation and experimentation, helping to transform surgery into a respected, evidence-based profession.
