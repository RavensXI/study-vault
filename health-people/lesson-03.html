Medieval Surgery & Hospitals

Diagnosis in the Medieval Period

Medieval physicians believed illness was caused by an imbalance in the Four Humours. To work out which humour was off balance, they used two main tools: a urine chart and a zodiac chart.

The physician examined the patient’s urine — checking its colour, smell, and even taste — and compared it to the chart to spot the imbalance. They then checked the zodiac chart to find the best time to treat, based on the stars and planets. This mix of humoral theory and astrology was standard medical practice.

Common Medieval Treatments

Since physicians believed illness came from a humoral imbalance, treatments aimed to restore that balance. Some seem extreme today, but they were logical within humoral medicine.

Bloodletting

Bloodletting was the most common treatment for nearly every condition. It aimed to remove excess blood — the humour most often blamed for illness. There were three main methods:

Key Fact — Methods of Bloodletting
  • Venesection: Cutting open a vein with a sharp blade and draining blood into a bowl. The physician would use the zodiac chart to decide which vein to cut and when.
  • Leeches: Placing blood-sucking leeches on the patient’s skin to draw out blood more slowly and with less risk.
  • Cupping: Placing heated glass cups on the skin. As the air inside cooled, it created suction that drew blood to the surface.

Purging

To remove excess humours other than blood, patients were forced to empty their bodies. Emetics made patients vomit, while laxatives emptied the bowels. The aim was always the same: physically remove the humour thought to be causing illness.

Herbal Remedies

Local apothecaries or “wise women” created remedies from plants and minerals. Many were based on the Theory of Opposites — using a “hot” herb to treat a “cold” illness, for example. Some had real value (honey as an antiseptic, willow bark for pain — which contains a chemical related to aspirin), but many were useless or even harmful.

Some remedies worked, even though people did not understand why. Honey has natural antibacterial properties. Willow bark contains salicin, which the body converts into salicylic acid — the active ingredient in aspirin. Mint helped digestion, and garlic has proven antimicrobial properties.

But many other remedies were useless or dangerous — using ground-up gems, animal dung, or mercury. Without scientific testing, there was no way to tell effective remedies from harmful ones.

Exam Tip

Always explain why a treatment was used. Don’t just say “they performed bloodletting.” Say “they performed bloodletting because they believed excess blood was causing illness, and removing it would restore the balance of the Four Humours.” Linking treatments to the ideas behind them earns higher marks.

Medieval Surgery

Surgery was a separate craft from medicine and a last resort. It was not done by university-trained physicians but by barber surgeons — tradesmen who also cut hair and pulled teeth. Surgery was seen as lower-status: a manual craft, not an intellectual one.

The Three Problems of Surgery

Medieval surgeons faced three huge problems that made any operation very dangerous:

Key Fact — The Three Problems
  • Pain: There were no effective anaesthetics. Patients were sometimes given alcohol or a sponge soaked in opium to dull the pain, but these were unreliable. Many patients went into shock or moved during surgery, making precise work impossible.
  • Infection: With no understanding of germs, surgical instruments were not sterilised and surgeons did not wash their hands. Wounds frequently became infected after operations, and many patients died from infection rather than the original injury.
  • Bleeding: Surgeons had no reliable way to stop heavy bleeding during or after operations. Without the ability to transfuse blood or effectively seal blood vessels, patients could easily bleed to death during even minor procedures.

“Laudable Pus”

Many surgeons believed that pus forming in a wound was a good sign — proof the body was healing by expelling harmful humours. They called this “laudable pus”. In reality, pus is a clear sign of infection. This meant surgeons often took no steps to prevent infection — and sometimes encouraged it, with fatal results.

Key Named Surgeons

Although surgery was seen as a lower craft, several individuals pushed surgical knowledge forward:

Key Fact — Abulcasis (Albucasis, c.936–1013)

Abulcasis was an Islamic surgeon from Cordoba in Muslim Spain, often called the “Father of Modern Surgery.” He wrote Al Tasrif, a 30-volume medical encyclopaedia that included detailed descriptions of surgical procedures and illustrations of instruments. He invented 26 new surgical instruments and popularised the use of cauterisation to seal wounds and stop bleeding.

Key Fact — Hugh & Theodoric of Lucca (13th century)

Hugh of Lucca and his son Theodoric worked at the University of Bologna. Crucially, they criticised the widespread belief in “laudable pus” and argued that pus was harmful, not helpful. They used wine to clean wounds and reduce infection — an idea that went against accepted medical teaching but was far closer to modern antiseptic practice.

Key Fact — John of Arderne (c.1307–1392)

John of Arderne was an English surgeon who wrote Practica, a surgical text with detailed illustrations of operations and instruments. He used opium as a pain reliever during surgery and developed a successful treatment for anal fistula — a painful condition common among knights who spent long hours on horseback. Arderne also campaigned to raise the status of surgery by separating surgeons from barbers, helping to establish the Guild of Surgeons.

What Procedures Could Be Performed?

Despite these limits, barber surgeons did carry out a range of procedures — mostly surface-level operations or emergencies where the patient would die without intervention:

Amputation: Removing a badly injured or gangrenous limb. Done quickly to limit pain and bleeding, then cauterised (sealed with a red-hot iron).

Trepanning: Drilling a hole in the skull to release evil spirits or relieve pressure. Some patients survived — we know from archaeological evidence.

Setting broken bones: Surgeons could set simple fractures and make splints. Compound fractures (bone breaking through skin) were usually fatal due to infection.

Cauterisation: Sealing wounds with a red-hot iron to stop bleeding. Very painful, but could prevent blood loss.

Pulling teeth: One of the most common procedures, since tooth decay was widespread.

At the Battle of Shrewsbury (1403), the 16-year-old Prince Henry (later Henry V) was struck in the face by an arrow. The shaft was pulled out, but the arrowhead stayed six inches deep in his skull. Surgeon John Bradmore invented a special screw-like tool to grip and extract it, saving the prince’s life. Surviving portraits of Henry V are often in profile — to hide the scar.

This case shows that medieval surgeons could achieve impressive results when necessity demanded innovation.

War as a Driver of Surgical Progress

Warfare drove surgical improvement. Most barber surgeons gained experience on the battlefield, treating sword and arrow wounds. The Wars of the Roses (1455–1487) gave over 30 years of surgical practice and pushed innovation in treating battlefield injuries. War created constant demand for practitioners who could amputate limbs, extract arrows, and cauterise wounds quickly.

Anatomy & Dissection

In 1315, public dissection was first allowed at the University of Bologna. Mondino de Luzzi wrote Anathomia, the standard dissection manual for over 200 years. But dissection was mainly used to confirm Galen’s ideas, not challenge them. The professor read from Galen while an assistant cut the body. Any differences were blamed on the body, not on Galen. It took until the Renaissance for anatomists like Vesalius to use dissection to overturn Galen’s mistakes.

Who Provided Medical Care?

Different types of practitioners provided different levels of care. Access to treatment depended heavily on wealth and social class:

Key Fact — Types of Medical Practitioners
  • Physicians: University-trained doctors who studied ancient texts. They diagnosed illness and prescribed treatments but rarely got their hands dirty. Only the wealthy could afford to consult a physician.
  • Barber surgeons: Tradesmen who performed surgery, pulled teeth, and cut hair. They learned through apprenticeships, not university training. Attempts were made to professionalise surgery and raise its status, including the formation of the Guild of Surgeons, which sought to separate trained surgeons from ordinary barbers.
  • Apothecaries: Prepared and sold herbal remedies and medicines. Similar to a modern pharmacist but without scientific training.
  • Wise women: Women in local communities who had knowledge of herbal remedies passed down through generations. Often the first point of contact for the poor.
  • The Church: Priests offered spiritual healing through prayer, and monasteries provided hospital care focused on rest and religious devotion.
Exam Tip

Don’t present medieval people as “stupid.” Their treatments were logical within their understanding of the Four Humours. The best answers explain the reasoning behind treatments, acknowledge some remedies had real value, and identify the barriers (lack of technology, Church control, respect for ancient authority) that prevented progress.

Conclusion

Medieval treatments put the Theory of the Four Humours into practice, leading to bloodletting and purging. Surgery was a separate, brutal craft — barber surgeons struggled with pain, infection, and bleeding. Some herbal remedies had real value, but without scientific understanding, treatments were often useless or dangerous.

Key Takeaways

  • Treatments were based on humoral theory: Bloodletting, purging, and herbal remedies all aimed to restore the balance of the Four Humours. Always link treatments to the ideas behind them in your answers.
  • Surgery was dangerous and a last resort: Barber surgeons faced pain, infection, and bleeding. “Laudable pus” shows how the lack of germ theory made outcomes worse.
  • Access to care depended on wealth: Physicians served the rich; the poor relied on wise women, apothecaries, or the Church.
