Scheduling

Spaced Repetition

Spread it out over days and weeks

What the Research Says

Your brain forgets things on a predictable curve. Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered this in 1885: without review, you lose about 70% of new information within 24 hours. But each time you revisit material at the right moment — just as you’re about to forget — the memory becomes stronger and lasts longer. This is the spacing effect, and it’s one of the most reliable findings in all of psychology.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Ebbinghaus (1885) Discovered the forgetting curve — memory decays exponentially without review Foundation of all spacing research
Cepeda et al. (2006) Meta-analysis of 254 studies confirmed spacing produces stronger long-term retention than massing Effect holds across ages and subjects
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Distributed (spaced) practice rated HIGH utility across all learning contexts One of only two top-rated techniques
Bjork & Bjork (1992) “Desirable difficulties” — spacing feels harder but produces deeper learning Explains why cramming feels effective but isn’t

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    List all your topics — Write out every lesson across all four units. You have 60 lessons — this is your master list.
  2. 2
    Build a 6-week timetable — Start at least 6 weeks before your exam. Schedule 2–3 topics per day, 30–45 minutes per session.
  3. 3
    Schedule at increasing intervals — After studying a topic, schedule it again at Day 1, Day 3, Day 7, Day 14, then Day 30. Each review gets shorter because you remember more.
  4. 4
    Traffic-light after each test — After each retrieval attempt, rate the topic: Green (confident), Amber (some gaps), Red (major gaps). Reds get shorter intervals; greens can be spaced further apart.
  5. 5
    Adjust your spacing — Move green topics to longer gaps (14–30 days). Keep red topics on short cycles (1–3 days) until they turn amber or green.

Review

Plan once, then follow the schedule daily. Each review session uses retrieval practice (brain dumps or self-quizzing) — not re-reading. Start 6–8 weeks before your exam to give every topic at least 4 review cycles.

Tracking one lesson: Health & the People, Lesson 5 — The Black Death and its impact

Week 1 — Initial study + Day 1 review

Monday: Study L5 (Black Death) for 20 minutes using retrieval practice. Brain dump key facts: arrived 1348, killed 30–50% of population, spread by fleas on rats, flagellants, physicians tried bloodletting and lancing buboes, led to labour shortages and Peasants’ Revolt.

Tuesday: Quick 10-minute retrieval — write down everything from yesterday. Rate: Amber (forgot specific treatments and death toll figures).

Week 1 — Day 3 review

Thursday: 10-minute retrieval. This time recall is better — remembered plague doctors, flagellants, 1.5 million deaths. Still fuzzy on the link to the Peasants’ Revolt (1381). Rate: Amber.

Week 2 — Day 7 review

Monday: 8-minute retrieval. Solid on causes, symptoms, treatments, death toll. Now connects to Statute of Labourers (1351) and Peasants’ Revolt (1381). Rate: Green. Move to 14-day gap.

Week 4 — Day 21 review

Monday: 5-minute retrieval. Still strong. Quick check confirms all key facts intact. Rate: Green. Final review scheduled for Week 6 (exam week).

Total time spent on this one lesson: about 53 minutes spread over 6 weeks — compared to cramming it all in one 60-minute session the night before, which research shows produces far worse results.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Cramming the night before — Massed practice creates short-term familiarity that fades within days. Spacing the same time across weeks produces lasting memory.
  • ❌
    Revising the same topic every day — Daily repetition of the same material gives diminishing returns. Space it out and use the gaps to study other topics.
  • ❌
    Ignoring weaker topics — It’s tempting to keep reviewing what you already know. Use the traffic-light system to force yourself back to red topics.
  • ❌
    Starting too late — Spacing only works if you start early enough to fit in multiple review cycles. Six weeks is the minimum; eight is better.
