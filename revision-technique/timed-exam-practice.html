Exam readiness

Timed Exam Practice

Write like it’s the real thing

What the Research Says

Knowing the content is not enough — you also need to practise applying it under exam conditions. Timed practice combines retrieval (recalling knowledge), application (using the right exam structure), and time management. AQA examiner reports repeatedly highlight the same weaknesses: running out of time, not answering the question asked, and failing to use the correct structure. The only way to fix these is practice.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Practice testing rated HIGH utility — one of the two most effective revision strategies across all research Top-rated technique
Roediger & Karpicke (2006) Transfer-appropriate processing — practising in the same format as the test produces the best results Practice under exam conditions
AQA Examiner Reports Recurring weaknesses: students don’t plan answers, run out of time on final questions, and describe instead of explain Timed practice directly addresses these

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Choose a practice question — Use the practice questions on each lesson page, or pick from a past paper. Make sure you know which question type it is so you can use the right structure.
  2. 2
    Set your timer — Use the correct time allocation: 4 marks = 5 min, 8 marks = 12 min, 12 marks = 16 min, 16+4 SPaG = 25 min. Start the timer before you write anything.
  3. 3
    Write without notes — Closed-book only. This is retrieval practice in exam format. If you can’t remember something, leave a gap and move on — don’t look it up.
  4. 4
    Stop when the timer ends — Even if you haven’t finished. Learning to work within the time limit is part of the skill. Note where you ran out.
  5. 5
    Self-mark against the level descriptors — Use the Exam Technique guides for the mark scheme. Be honest about which level your answer reaches and why.
  6. 6
    Note 2–3 specific improvements — Don’t just write “do better”. Identify exactly what would move your answer up a level: more evidence? Better explanation? Clearer judgement?

Practise 2–3 timed answers per week in the final 4 weeks before the exam. Vary the question types — don’t just practise your favourites. Use the timing guide above and stick to it strictly.

Plan (3 min)

Stated factor: Appeasement — Munich 1938, Chamberlain gave Sudetenland to Hitler, encouraged aggression, showed League/Britain wouldn’t act
Other factor 1: Treaty of Versailles — harsh terms created resentment, Hitler exploited grievances, remilitarisation of Rhineland
Other factor 2: Hitler’s foreign policy — Lebensraum, rearmament, Anschluss, invasion of Poland
Judgement: Appeasement important but Hitler’s aggression was the direct cause

Paragraph 1: Stated factor (6 min)

Appeasement was a significant cause of the Second World War because it allowed Hitler to grow stronger without facing consequences. At the Munich Conference in September 1938, Chamberlain agreed to let Hitler take the Sudetenland from Czechoslovakia in exchange for a promise of “peace for our time.” This was disastrous because it showed Hitler that Britain and France would not use force to stop him, encouraging further aggression. Within six months, Hitler had taken the rest of Czechoslovakia (March 1939), breaking his promise and proving that appeasement had failed. By this point, Germany was far stronger militarily than it had been in 1936, when the remilitarisation of the Rhineland could have been stopped easily.

Paragraph 2: Other factor + comparison (6 min)

However, Hitler’s own aggressive foreign policy was arguably a more direct cause of the war. His aims — Lebensraum (living space), a Greater Germany, and the destruction of the Treaty of Versailles — made conflict almost inevitable regardless of how Britain responded. Hitler rearmed Germany from 1935, remilitarised the Rhineland in 1936, annexed Austria in March 1938, and finally invaded Poland on 1 September 1939. Each step was planned and deliberate. While appeasement failed to stop him, it was Hitler who chose to keep pushing. Even if Britain had confronted him earlier, his ideology meant he would have sought war eventually.

Conclusion + judgement (4 min)

Overall, I agree with the statement to a limited extent. Appeasement was a serious miscalculation that allowed Hitler to grow stronger and made war harder to win when it finally came. However, the main reason for the outbreak of war was Hitler’s deliberate policy of aggression. Appeasement gave him the opportunity, but it was his decision to invade Poland that directly triggered the conflict. Without Hitler’s expansionist aims, appeasement would not have led to war.

Self-marking

Level 4 (13–16): Yes — explains stated factor and other factor with specific evidence, makes a clear judgement weighing them against each other.
Estimated mark: 14/16 + 3 SPaG = 17/20
Improvements: (1) Could add a third factor (Treaty of Versailles created the conditions for resentment) for more complexity. (2) Conclusion could be more nuanced — acknowledge that appeasement and Hitler’s aggression worked together. (3) Check spelling of “Sudetenland” and “Czechoslovakia” more carefully.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Writing without a timer — Untimed practice doesn’t prepare you for exam conditions. If you always have “as long as you need”, you’ll run out of time on the real thing.
  • ❌
    Looking at notes while writing — Open-book practice tests your ability to organise information, not retrieve it. Closed-book is essential for building exam-ready recall.
  • ❌
    Not self-marking — Writing an answer and moving on wastes half the learning opportunity. Self-marking against level descriptors is where you identify what to improve.
  • ❌
    Only practising favourite question types — Students tend to avoid the questions they find hardest, which are exactly the ones that need the most practice. Use the Exam Technique guides for every question type.
  • ❌
    Not using the Exam Technique guides — Each question type has a specific structure that examiners expect. Practising without knowing the formula means you’re guessing at the format instead of nailing it.
