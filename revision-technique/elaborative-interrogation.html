Elaborative Interrogation

Ask “why?” and “how?” to deepen understanding

What the Research Says

Most students revise by memorising facts: dates, names, events. But GCSE History exams reward explanation — you need to say WHY things happened and HOW they connect. Elaborative interrogation forces you to ask these questions while revising, which builds the deeper understanding that turns a Level 2 answer into a Level 4.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Pressley et al. (1987) Students who asked “why?” while reading recalled 72% of material, vs. 37% for those who just read Nearly double the recall
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Rated elaborative interrogation as MODERATE utility — highly effective when students already have some background knowledge Ideal for revision (not first learning)
Chi et al. (1994) Self-explanation — explaining material to yourself — produced better learning than simply studying examples Explaining builds understanding

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Read a section of your notes — Pick one lesson or part of a lesson. Read through it once to remind yourself of the content.
  2. 2
    Stop at each key fact — Every time you hit an important name, date, event, or claim, pause. Don’t just move on.
  3. 3
    Ask “Why?” or “How?” — Why did this happen? How did it lead to what came next? Why was this significant? How does this connect to other events you’ve studied?
  4. 4
    Answer from memory first — Try to explain the answer in your own words before checking your notes. Write it down in one or two sentences.
  5. 5
    Build a causal chain — Link your “why” answers together into a chain: A happened because of B, which led to C, which caused D. This is exactly the kind of analysis examiners reward.
  6. 6
    Self-test — Close your notes and try to reconstruct the causal chain from memory. Check for gaps.

Each topic takes about 15 minutes with this method. Use it after you’ve already studied a topic at least once — you need some background knowledge for the “why” questions to work. Best combined with retrieval practice: do a brain dump first, then use elaborative interrogation on the parts you recalled.

Fact → Why question → Answer

Fact: Mary Queen of Scots was executed in February 1587.

Why? Why did Elizabeth finally agree to execute Mary after keeping her prisoner for 19 years?

Answer: The Babington Plot (1586) was the final straw. Walsingham’s spies intercepted coded letters proving Mary had agreed to a plan to assassinate Elizabeth and place Mary on the throne with Spanish support. This was undeniable evidence of treason, and Parliament pressured Elizabeth to act.

Building the causal chain

Why was Walsingham able to intercept the letters? → He had set up an elaborate spy network and allowed the plot to develop so he could gather proof against Mary.

Why did Mary agree to the assassination plot? → She had been imprisoned for 19 years with no prospect of release, and Catholic supporters kept encouraging her that Spain would intervene.

How did the execution affect Elizabeth’s position? → It removed the Catholic figurehead in England, but it enraged Philip II of Spain and pushed him toward launching the Armada in 1588.

Complete causal chain

Catholic plots against Elizabeth → Walsingham builds spy network → Babington Plot intercepted (1586) → Mary proven to have agreed to assassination → Elizabeth pressured by Parliament → Mary executed (Feb 1587) → Philip II enraged → Spanish Armada launched (1588)

Why this works: Instead of memorising “Mary was executed in 1587” as an isolated fact, you now understand the chain of causes and consequences. This is exactly what an 8-mark “Write an account” or “Explain the significance” question requires.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Only asking “what” questions — “What happened in 1587?” tests recall but doesn’t build understanding. Always push to “why” and “how”.
  • ❌
    Accepting vague answers — “Because it was important” is not an explanation. Push yourself to give specific reasons with evidence.
  • ❌
    Skipping the self-test — Building the causal chain is only half the work. You need to close your notes and reconstruct it from memory to lock it in.
  • ❌
    Not connecting across lessons — The best causal chains link events from different lessons. Mary’s execution connects to the Armada, which connects to Elizabeth’s consolidation of power.
