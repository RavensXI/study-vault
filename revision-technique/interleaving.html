Interleaving

Mix topics to strengthen recall

What the Research Says

Most students study one topic at a time until they feel confident, then move on. This is called “blocking”. Interleaving is the opposite: you deliberately mix different topics within the same session. It feels harder and less productive, but research consistently shows it produces better long-term retention and the ability to tell different types of questions apart in the exam.

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Choose 3 topics from different units — Pick one from Conflict, one from Health, one from Elizabethan (or America). The more different they are, the better the interleaving effect.
  2. 2
    12 minutes per topic with retrieval practice — For each topic, do a quick brain dump (5 min), check gaps (3 min), then answer one practice question from memory (4 min). Then switch.
  3. 3
    Switch between topics — After 12 minutes, move to the next topic even if you don’t feel “done”. The switch is the point — it forces your brain to reload different information.
  4. 4
    End with a mixed quiz — In the final 9 minutes, answer one question from each of the three topics. This tests your ability to retrieve the right knowledge for the right question — exactly what the exam demands.

A full interleaved session takes about 45 minutes. Use this 2–3 times per week in the final 4 weeks before the exam. It works best once you’ve already studied each topic at least once — interleaving is for strengthening and connecting, not for first-time learning.

Block 1: Health — Germ Theory (12 min)

Brain dump (5 min): Pasteur published Germ Theory 1861. Proved microorganisms caused decay. Koch identified specific bacteria — TB 1882, cholera 1883. Used agar plates and dyes. Pasteur developed chicken cholera vaccine (1879) and anthrax vaccine (1881). Led to antiseptic surgery (Lister, carbolic acid 1867).

Gap check (3 min): Missed — Pasteur’s swan-neck flask experiment; Koch’s postulates (4 steps to prove a germ causes a disease); opposition from miasma believers.

Quick question (4 min): “Explain the significance of Pasteur’s Germ Theory.” Wrote two paragraphs from memory.

Block 2: Conflict — League structure (12 min)

Brain dump (5 min): Assembly (met once a year, all members), Council (met 5x/year, permanent + temporary members), Secretariat (admin), Permanent Court of International Justice. Permanent Council members: Britain, France, Italy, Japan. Decisions needed unanimous agreement. No army. Relied on moral authority, economic sanctions, military action (last resort).

Gap check (3 min): Missed — ILO (International Labour Organisation); Mandates Commission; Health Organisation. Also forgot that the Covenant was part of the Treaty of Versailles.

Quick question (4 min): “Write an account of how the League of Nations was organised.” Wrote a structured narrative.

Block 3: Elizabethan — Spanish Armada (12 min)

Brain dump (5 min): 1588. Philip II sent 130 ships. Reasons: Elizabeth supported Dutch rebels, Drake’s raids on Spanish ports (singeing the King’s beard 1587), execution of Mary Queen of Scots 1587, religious differences. English used fire ships at Calais. Armada scattered, sailed around Scotland, storms wrecked many ships. Only about half returned to Spain.

Gap check (3 min): Missed — Duke of Medina Sidonia (inexperienced commander); plan to pick up Duke of Parma’s troops from Netherlands; English ships were faster and more manoeuvrable; Tilbury speech.

Quick question (4 min): “Explain what was important about the defeat of the Spanish Armada.”

Mixed quiz (9 min) — 3 minutes each

Q1 (Health): Name Koch’s four postulates. Could only remember 3 — need to revisit.

Q2 (Conflict): Why was unanimous voting a weakness of the League? Answered well — any member could block action.

Q3 (Elizabethan): Give three reasons why Philip II launched the Armada. Got all three with evidence.

Why this works: Switching between unrelated topics forces your brain to actively retrieve each one fresh, rather than coasting on short-term familiarity. The mixed quiz at the end mimics the real exam, where you move between different papers and topics.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Studying one topic all session — Blocking feels productive because you get “into the zone”, but the comfort is a sign your brain isn’t working hard enough to build lasting memory.
  • ❌
    Switching every 2 minutes — Too-fast switching doesn’t give you time to engage meaningfully with each topic. Aim for 10–15 minutes per block.
  • ❌
    Only interleaving within one unit — Mixing three Conflict & Tension lessons is better than blocking, but mixing across units gives the strongest effect because the topics are most different.
  • ❌
    Giving up because it feels harder — Interleaving should feel harder than blocking. That difficulty is the signal that deeper learning is happening. Trust the research.
