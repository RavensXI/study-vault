Lesson 9 of 15

The Spanish Armada

Overlay:

The Spanish Armada

In 1588, King Philip II of Spain launched a massive fleet — known as the Spanish Armada — to invade England. It was one of the most dramatic events of Elizabeth’s reign and a turning point in European history. The Armada’s defeat was a huge boost to English national pride and cemented Elizabeth’s reputation as a strong leader.

Key Fact — The Armada in Numbers

The Spanish Armada consisted of around 130 ships carrying approximately 30,000 men. The plan was to sail up the English Channel, collect the Duke of Parma’s army of 30,000 soldiers from the Netherlands, and then invade England.

Why Did Philip II Launch the Armada?

  • Religion: Philip was a devout Catholic and wanted to overthrow Protestant Elizabeth and restore Catholicism in England. The Pope had excommunicated Elizabeth in 1570 and supported the invasion.
  • Political provocation: Elizabeth had signed the Treaty of Nonsuch in 1585, openly supporting Dutch Protestant rebels against Spanish rule in the Netherlands. Philip saw this as an act of war.
  • Drake’s raid on Cadiz (1587): Sir Francis Drake attacked the Spanish port of Cadiz, destroying around 30 ships and delaying the Armada by a year. This was known as ‘singeing the King of Spain’s beard’ and deeply angered Philip.
  • Mary, Queen of Scots: Her execution in February 1587 removed Philip’s hope of placing a Catholic monarch on the English throne through diplomacy. Invasion now seemed the only option.
  • English privateering: English sailors like Drake and Hawkins had been attacking Spanish treasure ships for years, stealing huge amounts of gold and silver.

The English Plan and Strengths

The English navy was commanded by Lord Howard of Effingham, with Sir Francis Drake as his vice-admiral. England had several key advantages:

  • Faster ships: English galleons were smaller, faster, and more manoeuvrable than the large Spanish galleons.
  • Better cannons: English ships carried longer-range culverins that could fire from a safer distance and reload more quickly.
  • Experienced sailors: English crews were experienced in Atlantic sailing and fighting in home waters.
  • Home advantage: The English knew the Channel waters and could resupply from their own ports.

Key Events of the Armada Campaign

29 July 1588 — Sighting: The Armada was spotted off the coast of Cornwall. Beacons were lit along the south coast to warn of the approaching fleet.

31 July – 4 August — Channel battles: The English engaged the Armada as it sailed up the English Channel in a defensive crescent formation. English ships used their speed and long-range guns to harass the Spanish but could not break the formation.

6–7 August — Fireships at Calais: The Armada anchored near Calais to wait for the Duke of Parma’s army. At midnight, the English sent eight unmanned ships filled with flammable materials into the Spanish fleet. Panicking, Spanish captains cut their anchor ropes and scattered, breaking their defensive formation.

8 August — Battle of Gravelines: With the Spanish fleet scattered, the English attacked at close range. Several Spanish ships were sunk or badly damaged. The wind pushed the remaining ships northwards, away from Parma’s army.

August–September — The journey home: Unable to return through the Channel, the surviving Spanish ships were forced to sail around Scotland and Ireland. Terrible storms wrecked many ships on the rocky coasts. Only about half the Armada made it back to Spain.

Why Was the Armada Defeated?

Key Fact — Reasons for Defeat

The Armada’s defeat was caused by a combination of factors. No single reason explains it — English tactics, ship design, Spanish mistakes, and the weather all played a part.

English Tactics and Leadership

Lord Howard and Drake made excellent tactical decisions. The use of fireships at Calais was the turning point, breaking the Spanish formation and causing chaos. English captains fought bravely and used their ships’ speed to their advantage.

Ship Design and Technology

English galleons were built for speed and manoeuvrability, while many Spanish ships were slower transport vessels. English culverins had a longer range and could be reloaded faster than Spanish cannons, which were designed for close-range broadsides.

Spanish Weaknesses and Mistakes

  • The commander, the Duke of Medina Sidonia, had very little naval experience — he was chosen because of his high rank, not his ability.
  • The plan relied on perfect coordination between the Armada and the Duke of Parma’s army, but communication was slow and unreliable.
  • Spanish cannonballs were often poor quality and their cannons took much longer to reload.
  • Parma’s army was blockaded by Dutch rebel ships and could not reach the Armada.

Weather

The so-called ‘Protestant Wind’ played a crucial role. Strong winds prevented the Spanish from regrouping after Gravelines, and devastating storms around Scotland and Ireland destroyed many of the remaining ships. Elizabeth had a special medal struck with the words “God blew and they were scattered.”

The Significance of the Armada’s Defeat

  • National pride: The victory was a massive boost to English confidence and national identity. Elizabeth’s famous speech at Tilbury — “I know I have the body of a weak and feeble woman, but I have the heart and stomach of a king” — became legendary.
  • Protestant England secured: The defeat ended any realistic chance of England being forced back to Catholicism by a foreign power.
  • England as a naval power: The victory helped establish England as a serious naval force, challenging Spain’s dominance of the seas.
  • Propaganda: Elizabeth used the victory to strengthen her image as ‘Gloriana’, a monarch chosen by God to lead England.
  • War continued: Despite the Armada’s failure, the war with Spain continued until 1604. Spain sent two more armadas (in 1596 and 1597), both destroyed by storms.
Exam Tip

When explaining why the Armada failed, aim for a balanced answer. Don’t just list the reasons — explain how they connected. For example, the fireships at Calais (English tactics) only worked because the Spanish were waiting for Parma (a flaw in the plan), and the weather finished the job. The best answers show the interplay between factors rather than treating them in isolation.

Conclusion

The defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588 was a defining moment of Elizabeth’s reign. It resulted from a combination of English naval skill, innovative tactics like the Calais fireships, superior ship design, serious flaws in the Spanish plan, and devastating weather. The victory secured Protestant England, boosted national pride, and established England as a growing naval power — though the war with Spain would drag on for another sixteen years.

Key Takeaways

  • Multiple causes of defeat: English tactics, better ships, Spanish mistakes, and the weather all contributed — no single factor explains the outcome.
  • The fireships were the turning point: Breaking the Spanish crescent formation at Calais was the decisive moment that made English victory possible at Gravelines.
  • Huge symbolic significance: The Armada’s defeat boosted national pride, secured Protestantism in England, and was used as powerful propaganda by Elizabeth’s government.
