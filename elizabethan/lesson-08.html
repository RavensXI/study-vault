Conflict with Spain — The Road to War

In 1558, England and Spain were on reasonable terms. Philip II had even proposed marriage to Elizabeth. But over the next 30 years, relations fell apart — ending with the Spanish Armada in 1588.

Religious Differences

Protestant England vs. Catholic Spain. Philip II saw himself as Catholicism’s defender in Europe. Elizabeth’s Religious Settlement (1559) made England officially Protestant, which Philip opposed. In 1570, the Pope excommunicated Elizabeth, encouraging Catholic powers like Spain to act against her.

Key Fact — Philip II’s Ambition

Philip II was Europe’s most powerful ruler. He controlled Spain, the Spanish Netherlands, large parts of Italy, and a vast American empire. He believed it was his religious duty to restore Catholicism to England — and he had the wealth and military to try.

Political and Personal Rivalry

Philip II had been King of England during his marriage to Mary I. He found it hard to accept England becoming a Protestant rival. As England grew in power under Elizabeth, the two nations were drawn into competition.

English Support for Dutch Rebels

The Netherlands was ruled by Spain, but many Dutch people were Protestant and resented harsh Spanish rule. A Dutch revolt had been going on since the 1560s. Elizabeth cautiously supported the rebels:

  • She allowed Dutch rebel ships (“Sea Beggars”) to use English ports
  • English volunteers fought alongside the Dutch
  • In 1585, Elizabeth signed the Treaty of Nonsuch, openly sending 7,000 soldiers and money to help the Dutch — a key turning point
  • The Earl of Leicester led the English forces in the Netherlands
Key Fact — The Treaty of Nonsuch, 1585

The Treaty of Nonsuch was a turning point. By openly sending troops and money to Dutch rebels, Elizabeth was effectively declaring war on Spain. Philip saw it as direct aggression that justified military retaliation.

Rivalry at Sea

English privateers like Sir Francis Drake and Sir John Hawkins attacked Spanish treasure ships and ports in the Americas. Elizabeth secretly encouraged and profited from this.

  • San Juan de Ulúa (1568): Spanish forces attacked English ships in a Mexican port, increasing bitterness between the two nations
  • Drake’s circumnavigation (1577–1580): Drake sailed around the world, plundering huge amounts of Spanish treasure. Elizabeth took a share of the profits and knighted Drake on his return
  • Drake’s raid on Cadiz (1587): Drake attacked Cadiz, destroying ships and supplies being prepared for the Armada — known as “singeing the King of Spain’s beard”

Mary, Queen of Scots

Philip II supported Catholic plots to overthrow Elizabeth and replace her with Mary, Queen of Scots. He funded missionaries and plotters in England. Mary’s execution in February 1587 was the final straw — it removed the Catholic claimant Philip had hoped to put on the throne and gave him a personal reason for revenge.

Exam Tip

Do not treat the conflict with Spain as a single-cause event. Show how multiple factors — religious, political, economic — built up tension over time. Use dates to show the escalation, and identify the Treaty of Nonsuch (1585) as the key turning point.

Conclusion

Anglo-Spanish relations collapsed over 30 years. Religious divisions, Philip’s ambition, English support for Dutch rebels, privateering, and the plots around Mary, Queen of Scots all pushed the two nations towards war. By 1587, conflict was almost unavoidable.

Key Takeaways

  • Multiple connected causes: Religion, politics, privateering, the Dutch revolt, and Mary’s execution all contributed to the breakdown.
  • Treaty of Nonsuch (1585): Elizabeth’s open military support for Dutch rebels made war with Spain almost certain.
  • Mary’s execution (1587): Gave Philip II both the personal motivation and political justification to launch the Armada.
