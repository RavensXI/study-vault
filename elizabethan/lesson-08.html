Lesson 8 of 15

Conflict with Spain

Overlay:

Conflict with Spain — The Road to War

At the start of Elizabeth I’s reign in 1558, England and Spain were reasonably friendly. Philip II of Spain had even proposed marriage to Elizabeth. However, relations gradually worsened over the next 30 years, leading to the Spanish Armada in 1588. Understanding the multiple causes of this conflict is essential for the exam.

Religious Differences

Protestant England vs. Catholic Spain. Philip II saw himself as the defender of Catholicism in Europe. Elizabeth’s Religious Settlement of 1559 made England officially Protestant, which Philip deeply opposed. In 1570, the Pope excommunicated Elizabeth, encouraging Catholic powers like Spain to act against her.

Key Fact — Philip II’s Ambition

Philip II of Spain was the most powerful ruler in Europe. He controlled Spain, the Spanish Netherlands (modern Belgium and the Netherlands), large parts of Italy, and a vast empire in the Americas. He genuinely believed it was his religious duty to restore Catholicism to England, and he had the wealth and military power to attempt it.

Political and Personal Rivalry

Spain was the dominant European power with a vast empire. Philip II had been King of England during his marriage to Mary I and found it hard to accept England becoming a Protestant rival. As England grew in confidence and power under Elizabeth, the two nations were increasingly drawn into competition.

English Support for Dutch Rebels

The Netherlands was ruled by Spain, but many Dutch people were Protestant and resented harsh Spanish rule. A Dutch revolt against Spain had been going on since the 1560s. Elizabeth cautiously supported the Dutch rebels:

  • She allowed Dutch rebel ships (“Sea Beggars”) to use English ports
  • English volunteers fought alongside the Dutch
  • In 1585, Elizabeth signed the Treaty of Nonsuch, openly agreeing to send 7,000 soldiers and money to help the Dutch — this was a key turning point
  • The Earl of Leicester was sent to lead the English forces in the Netherlands
Key Fact — The Treaty of Nonsuch, 1585

The Treaty of Nonsuch was a turning point in Anglo-Spanish relations. By openly sending troops and money to help the Dutch rebels, Elizabeth was effectively declaring war on Spain. Philip II saw this as a direct act of aggression that justified military retaliation.

Rivalry at Sea

English privateers (privately owned ships with unofficial royal permission) like Sir Francis Drake and Sir John Hawkins attacked Spanish treasure ships and ports in the Americas. Elizabeth secretly encouraged and profited from these activities.

  • The attack at San Juan de Ulúa (1568) — Spanish forces attacked English ships in a Mexican port, increasing bitterness between the two nations
  • Drake’s circumnavigation (1577–1580) — Drake sailed around the world, plundering vast amounts of Spanish treasure along the way. Elizabeth received a share of the profits and knighted Drake on his return.
  • Drake’s raid on Cadiz (1587) — Drake attacked the Spanish port of Cadiz, destroying ships and supplies being prepared for the Armada. This was known as “singeing the King of Spain’s beard.”

Mary, Queen of Scots

Philip II supported various Catholic plots to overthrow Elizabeth and replace her with Mary, Queen of Scots. He funded Catholic missionaries and plotters in England. Mary’s execution in February 1587 was the final straw for Philip — it removed the Catholic claimant he had hoped to put on the English throne and gave him a personal motivation for revenge.

How the Causes Connected

No single factor caused the conflict. Instead, these causes built on each other over nearly 30 years:

  • Religious differences created suspicion and hostility
  • English privateering damaged Spain economically and angered Philip
  • English support for Dutch rebels threatened Spain’s control of the Netherlands
  • Catholic plots linked Spain to attempts to overthrow Elizabeth
  • Mary’s execution provided Philip with his final justification
Exam Tip

Avoid presenting the conflict with Spain as a single-cause event. Show the interconnectedness of multiple factors — religious, political, economic, and individual actions — building up tension over time. Examiners want to see that you understand how these causes combined and escalated. Use dates to show the chronological build-up of tension, and identify the Treaty of Nonsuch (1585) as the key turning point.

Conclusion

Relations between England and Spain soured dramatically over 30 years. What began as a manageable relationship deteriorated due to deep religious divisions, Philip II’s ambition, Elizabeth’s support for Dutch rebels, English privateering, and plots surrounding Mary, Queen of Scots. By 1587, direct conflict seemed almost inevitable.

Key Takeaways

  • Multiple interconnected causes: Religion, politics, trade, privateering, the Dutch revolt, and Mary’s execution all contributed to the breakdown in relations.
  • The Treaty of Nonsuch (1585): Elizabeth’s open military support for the Dutch rebels was the key turning point that made war with Spain almost certain.
  • A gradual escalation: Tensions built up over nearly 30 years, with each provocation leading to a harsher response from the other side.
  • Mary’s execution (1587): Provided Philip II with the personal motivation and political justification to launch the Spanish Armada.
