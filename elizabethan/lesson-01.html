Elizabeth’s Accession & Early Problems

Elizabeth I became queen in 1558 at the age of 25. Her early life was full of danger and hardship, but those experiences shaped her into a sharp and determined leader.

The Tudors: An Unlikely Dynasty

The Tudor family were never expected to rule England. Owen Tudor was a Welsh servant in the king’s household who married the king’s widow — a huge scandal. When Henry VI suffered from mental illness, a power struggle called the Wars of the Roses tore England apart for 30 years. In 1485, Henry Tudor — the last Lancastrian lord standing — defeated Richard III at the Battle of Bosworth Field. The unlikely Tudor dynasty had begun.

Henry VII’s eldest son Arthur died weeks after marrying the Spanish princess, Catherine of Aragon. To keep the alliance with Spain, Henry had his second son marry Arthur’s widow. That second son became Henry VIII — Elizabeth’s father.

Birth and Early Childhood

Elizabeth was born on 7 September 1533 to Henry VIII and Anne Boleyn. Henry had caused a religious earthquake to marry Anne. The Pope refused to grant him a divorce from Catherine of Aragon, so Henry broke away from the Catholic Church and set up the Church of England.

Henry desperately wanted a son. When Elizabeth was born a girl, he was disappointed. Anne then had two miscarriages and could not produce a male heir. Henry grew tired of her and had her executed in 1536, when Elizabeth was just two. Elizabeth was declared illegitimate and removed from royal favour.

Education and Character

Despite her difficult start, Elizabeth received an outstanding education. She learned Latin, Greek, French, and Italian, along with history, philosophy, and religion. Her tutor Roger Ascham admired her intelligence and dedication.

Elizabeth also loved the arts. She enjoyed plays, dancing, and music, and took a keen interest in new discoveries — her reign would overlap with the Renaissance.

Key Fact — Elizabeth’s Languages

Elizabeth spoke five languages fluently — English, Latin, Greek, French, and Italian. This gave her a major advantage in diplomacy, letting her negotiate directly with foreign ambassadors without translators.

Religious Turmoil

England’s religion kept changing with each new monarch. Henry VIII’s third wife, Jane Seymour, gave him a son — Edward VI. Edward became king aged just 9 and was raised as a Protestant. He and his Protestant advisors pushed England further away from Catholicism. Catholic priests who refused to change risked punishment or execution.

Edward died aged 15. His older sister Mary I took over. Mary was Catherine of Aragon’s daughter and a devoted Catholic. She reversed Edward’s changes, married the Catholic King of Spain, Philip II, and burned around 300 Protestants at the stake in just four years. This earned her the nickname “Bloody Mary.”

A third group also emerged: the Puritans. These were very strict Protestants who wanted a simple life based purely on the Bible, with no music, theatre, or decoration in churches.

Political Danger

Under Mary, Elizabeth was in real danger. Mary suspected her of plotting and locked her in the Tower of London in 1554. Elizabeth’s famous “Tide Letter,” begging Mary to believe her innocence, shows how clever and persuasive she could be.

As a teenager, Elizabeth was also assaulted by Thomas Seymour, a powerful lord who was 25 years older. He wanted to marry her to gain power — she was only about 13. This experience, along with watching her mother’s execution and her sister’s manipulation by Philip II, made Elizabeth deeply distrustful of men’s intentions.

The Road to the Throne

Mary died childless in November 1558. Elizabeth became queen, but the country was in a terrible state. One of her Privy Councillors, Armagil Waad, described England as “exhausted,” with the people “disorderly,” a lack of good leaders and soldiers, and wars with France and Scotland.

Elizabeth’s Strengths as a Leader

Elizabeth had learned from the mistakes of her father, brother, and sister. She saw how quick, angry decisions often led to disaster. As a result, she deliberately delayed important decisions — taking her time to weigh up all sides before acting.

She understood the power of image. She had seen how unpopular Mary became, so she always tried to present herself in a positive way to win the public’s support.

One of her first moves was appointing William Cecil (later Lord Burghley) as her chief advisor. He was loyal and capable. She also introduced the 1559 Religious Settlement, which aimed to end the religious fighting by creating a middle ground. These early decisions helped to steady England quickly.

Exam Tip

The exam starts from 1558, but Elizabeth’s background is still useful context. Mentioning her early experiences — like the religious changes or the Thomas Seymour incident — shows the examiner you understand why she acted the way she did. Always explain causes, not just describe events.

Conclusion

Elizabeth’s early life was full of danger, but those experiences made her cautious, clever, and resilient. By 1558 she faced religious division, debt, foreign threats, and prejudice against her gender — yet she moved quickly to steady the country.

Key Takeaways

  • A dangerous path to power: Elizabeth survived her mother’s execution, being declared illegitimate, and imprisonment in the Tower before becoming queen.
  • A country in crisis: In 1558 England was divided by religion, deep in debt, militarily weak, and threatened by France and Scotland.
  • A prepared leader: Elizabeth learned from her predecessors’ mistakes, appointed strong advisors like William Cecil, and introduced the Religious Settlement to bring stability.
