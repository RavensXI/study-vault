The Rise of the Gentry & Theatre

Overlay:

The Rise of the Gentry & Theatre

Elizabethan England is often called a ‘Golden Age’ — a time of growing wealth, cultural achievement, and national confidence. For some groups, particularly the gentry, this was certainly true. At the same time, Elizabethan theatre flourished, producing some of the greatest works in the English language. However, it is important to remember that this prosperity was not shared equally.

Elizabethan Society: The Great Chain of Being

Elizabethans believed in a strict social hierarchy, often called the ‘Great Chain of Being’. Everyone had a fixed place in society, from the monarch at the top to the poorest labourer at the bottom:

Key Fact — Social Hierarchy
  • The Monarch — at the very top, appointed by God
  • The Nobility — dukes, earls, and barons; the richest and most powerful
  • The Gentry — wealthy landowners, knights, and esquires; growing in importance
  • Yeomen — small landowners and prosperous farmers
  • Merchants and craftsmen — growing wealth from trade in towns
  • Labourers and servants — the majority of the population
  • The poor and unemployed — a large and growing group

The Rise of the Gentry

The gentry were the big winners of the Elizabethan period. They were wealthy landowners who sat below the nobility but above ordinary people. During Elizabeth’s reign, their wealth and influence grew enormously.

Why Did the Gentry Prosper?

  • Land from the monasteries: After Henry VIII dissolved the monasteries in the 1530s, vast amounts of Church land was sold cheaply. Many gentry families bought this land and grew rich from farming it.
  • Rising agricultural prices: Prices for wool, grain, and other farm products rose steadily, making land ownership very profitable.
  • Trade and investment: Many gentry invested in trade, mining, and new industries, further increasing their wealth.
  • Education: The gentry sent their sons to grammar schools and universities, giving them the skills to enter law, politics, and government service.
  • Political influence: Gentry served as JPs (Justices of the Peace) and MPs, giving them real power at both local and national level.

Case Study: Bess of Hardwick

Elizabeth Talbot, known as Bess of Hardwick, is one of the most remarkable examples of the rising gentry. Born into a minor gentry family in Derbyshire, she married four times — each time to a wealthier husband. Through shrewd marriages and excellent business sense (including investments in mining and money lending), she became one of the richest women in England, second only to the Queen herself.

Bess built magnificent houses, most famously Hardwick Hall in Derbyshire (completed 1597), which was so famous for its enormous windows that people said: “Hardwick Hall, more glass than wall.” The hall was a deliberate display of her wealth and status.

How the Wealthy Displayed Their Status

  • Great houses: The gentry and nobility built grand new homes with large windows, symmetrical designs, and elaborate gardens. These were designed to impress visitors and display wealth.
  • Fashionable clothing: Expensive fabrics, ruffs, and jewellery showed status. Sumptuary Laws technically restricted what different classes could wear, though enforcement was weak.
  • Education: Sending children to grammar schools and universities was a mark of gentility.
  • Entertaining: Hosting feasts, music, and performances showed wealth and generosity.

Elizabethan Theatre

One of the greatest achievements of the Elizabethan age was the flourishing of English theatre. Plays became enormously popular entertainment for people of all social classes.

Key Fact — The Globe Theatre

The most famous Elizabethan theatre was The Globe, built in 1599 on the south bank of the River Thames in London. It could hold around 3,000 spectators. Wealthy audience members sat in covered galleries, while ordinary people (called ‘groundlings’) stood in the open yard for just one penny.

Why Did Theatre Flourish?

  • Growing population of London: London’s population grew rapidly, providing large audiences.
  • Royal patronage: Elizabeth and her court enjoyed plays. Acting companies needed the patronage of a noble or royal figure to perform legally.
  • Purpose-built theatres: The first purpose-built theatre, simply called ‘The Theatre’, was built by James Burbage in 1576. Others followed, including The Rose, The Swan, and The Globe.
  • Brilliant playwrights: The era produced extraordinary writers, above all William Shakespeare, whose plays are still performed worldwide today. Other major playwrights included Christopher Marlowe and Ben Jonson.

Opposition to Theatre

Not everyone approved of the theatre. Puritans opposed it strongly, believing plays encouraged immoral behaviour, laziness, and sinfulness. The City of London authorities banned theatres from the city itself, which is why they were built outside the city walls, particularly in Southwark on the south bank of the Thames. There were also concerns that large gatherings at theatres could spread plague.

Theatre was one of the few forms of entertainment that crossed class boundaries. Both the wealthy and the poor attended performances, though they sat in very different areas. Plays dealt with themes like power, love, betrayal, and justice — topics that resonated with all audiences.

All actors were men — women were not allowed to perform on stage. Female roles were played by boys or young men. Acting companies like the Lord Chamberlain’s Men (later the King’s Men) became famous and successful.

Shakespeare wrote around 37 plays during his career, including tragedies (Hamlet, Macbeth), comedies (A Midsummer Night’s Dream), and histories (Henry V, Richard III). His work explored the full range of human experience and is considered some of the greatest literature ever written in English.

Exam Tip

Always remember that the ‘Golden Age’ was not golden for everyone. When discussing the prosperity of the gentry or the cultural achievements of the theatre, contrast this with the experience of the poor. A strong answer shows that different groups experienced Elizabethan England very differently. Avoid sweeping statements like “everyone was rich” or “life was good for all.”

Conclusion

For the gentry, Elizabethan England was genuinely a time of growing wealth and influence, as families like Bess of Hardwick built grand houses and gained political power. The flourishing of theatre, led by Shakespeare, was one of the era’s greatest cultural achievements. However, this prosperity was not shared by everyone — the rigid social hierarchy meant that while some grew richer, many ordinary people remained poor. The ‘Golden Age’ depended very much on who you were.

Key Takeaways

  • The gentry rose dramatically: They benefited from cheap monastery land, rising prices, trade, and education to become the most dynamic social group.
  • Theatre was a major cultural achievement: Purpose-built theatres and brilliant playwrights like Shakespeare made this a golden age for English drama.
  • Prosperity was unequal: The wealth of the gentry and the brilliance of the theatre should be balanced against the poverty experienced by many ordinary people.
