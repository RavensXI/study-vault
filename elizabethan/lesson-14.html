Threats to Elizabeth’s Rule

Overlay:

Threats to Elizabeth’s Rule

Throughout her reign, Elizabeth I faced a range of serious threats to her power, her Protestant settlement, and even her life. These threats came from several directions: Catholic plots at home, the challenge of Mary, Queen of Scots, the military might of Spain, the demands of Puritans, and the ever-present succession crisis caused by Elizabeth’s refusal to marry. This lesson brings together all these threats and asks a crucial exam question: how serious was each one, and which posed the greatest danger?

Key Fact — Overview of Threats

The main threats to Elizabeth can be grouped into five categories:

  • Catholic plots — Ridolfi (1571), Throckmorton (1583), Babington (1586)
  • Mary, Queen of Scots — a Catholic rival with a claim to the throne (in England 1568–1587)
  • Spain — the most powerful Catholic country in Europe, culminating in the Armada (1588)
  • Puritans — extreme Protestants who challenged the Religious Settlement
  • The succession crisis — Elizabeth’s refusal to name an heir created dangerous uncertainty

1. The Catholic Threat

The Catholic threat to Elizabeth was persistent and came from both inside and outside England. The Papal Bull of 1570 (Regnans in Excelsis) excommunicated Elizabeth and declared that Catholics did not have to obey her. This effectively gave Catholic plotters permission from the Pope to overthrow or even assassinate the Queen.

The Major Catholic Plots

The Ridolfi Plot (1571): An Italian banker, Roberto Ridolfi, planned to use Spanish troops to overthrow Elizabeth, put Mary, Queen of Scots on the throne, and restore Catholicism. The Duke of Norfolk was involved. The plot was discovered by William Cecil and Norfolk was executed in 1572. The plot showed that Catholic plots had foreign backing, but it was poorly organised and easily discovered.

The Throckmorton Plot (1583): Francis Throckmorton acted as a go-between for Mary, Queen of Scots and the Spanish ambassador. The plan involved a French invasion supported by Spain and the Pope to free Mary and overthrow Elizabeth. Sir Francis Walsingham’s spy network uncovered the plot. Throckmorton was tortured and executed. The Spanish ambassador was expelled from England.

The Babington Plot (1586): The most serious plot. Anthony Babington, a young Catholic nobleman, planned to assassinate Elizabeth, free Mary, and trigger a Catholic uprising supported by Spain. Crucially, Walsingham intercepted coded letters between Babington and Mary, in which Mary appeared to give her approval for the assassination. This was the evidence needed to put Mary on trial. Babington and his co-conspirators were executed.

How Serious Was the Catholic Threat?

  • Serious because: The plots had foreign backing (Spain, the Pope), involved assassination plans, and could have triggered civil war or invasion. Seminary priests and Jesuit missionaries (arriving from the 1570s onwards) kept Catholicism alive in England.
  • Less serious because: Most English Catholics were loyal to Elizabeth and did not support violent plots. The plots were poorly organised and were discovered by Walsingham’s efficient spy network. The plotters never had enough support to succeed.

2. Mary, Queen of Scots

Mary Stuart was Elizabeth’s Catholic cousin and had a strong claim to the English throne. After being forced to abdicate in Scotland, she fled to England in 1568 and was kept under house arrest for 19 years. She became a focal point for every Catholic plot against Elizabeth.

Key Fact — Mary’s Execution

After the Babington Plot proved Mary had supported plans to assassinate Elizabeth, she was tried, found guilty of treason, and executed on 8 February 1587 at Fotheringhay Castle. Elizabeth reportedly hesitated for months before signing the death warrant, knowing it would set a dangerous precedent (a monarch executing another monarch) and would likely provoke Spain.

How Serious Was the Threat from Mary?

  • Serious because: Mary had a legitimate claim to the English throne and was the figurehead of every major Catholic plot. As long as she lived, there was always someone for Catholic conspirators to rally around. Her presence in England for 19 years was a constant source of danger.
  • Less serious because: Mary was a prisoner with no army, no resources, and no freedom. She relied entirely on others to act on her behalf, and those others repeatedly failed. Her execution in 1587 removed the threat permanently.

3. Spain

Spain under Philip II was the most powerful country in Europe, with vast wealth from its American empire and the strongest military forces on the continent. Spain was a Catholic superpower and Philip saw himself as the defender of the Catholic faith.

How Serious Was the Spanish Threat?

  • Serious because: Spain had the military and financial resources to mount a genuine invasion. The Armada of 1588 was the largest naval force ever assembled at that time. Spain supported Catholic plots in England and could have combined invasion with internal rebellion. Spain also sent two further armadas in 1596 and 1597.
  • Less serious because: The Armada failed disastrously due to English tactics, better ships, and weather. Spain was also stretched fighting wars in the Netherlands and could not give England its undivided attention. The later armadas were destroyed by storms before even reaching England. Despite the threat, Spain never managed to land troops on English soil.

4. The Puritan Challenge

Puritans were extreme Protestants who believed the Religious Settlement of 1559 had not gone far enough to remove Catholic practices from the Church of England. They wanted simpler services, no vestments, no bishops, and a ‘purer’ form of worship.

How Serious Was the Puritan Threat?

  • Serious because: Puritans challenged Elizabeth’s authority as Supreme Governor of the Church. Some Puritan MPs tried to push through religious reforms in Parliament, directly challenging the royal prerogative. The ‘Marprelate Tracts’ (1588–1589) were pamphlets that openly mocked and attacked bishops. Puritans had support in Parliament and among some powerful figures.
  • Less serious because: Puritans never threatened Elizabeth’s life or planned to overthrow her — they wanted to reform the Church, not remove the Queen. They had no foreign backing and no military capability. Archbishop Whitgift effectively suppressed organised Puritan opposition by the 1590s. Most Puritans were loyal subjects who simply wanted further religious reform.

5. The Succession Crisis

Elizabeth never married and never named an heir. This created a dangerous vacuum: if Elizabeth died suddenly, there was no clear successor, which could have led to civil war, foreign intervention, or the collapse of the Protestant settlement.

How Serious Was the Succession Threat?

  • Serious because: Without a clear heir, Elizabeth’s death could have plunged England into chaos. Parliament repeatedly pressed Elizabeth to marry or name a successor, and she repeatedly refused. The uncertainty encouraged Catholic plots (since Mary, Queen of Scots was a possible alternative). It also gave foreign powers like Spain a potential opening.
  • Less serious because: Elizabeth survived. She lived until 1603, by which time Robert Cecil had secretly arranged the smooth succession of James VI of Scotland (James I of England). The transition was peaceful, suggesting the threat, while real, never actually materialised into crisis.

Which Threat Was the Most Dangerous?

Key Fact — Making a Judgement

There is no single ‘right answer’ to this question — what matters is that you can justify your argument with evidence. Here are the strongest cases:

  • Mary, Queen of Scots was arguably the most dangerous threat because she was the focal point that connected all the other Catholic threats. Without Mary, Catholic plots had no realistic candidate to replace Elizabeth.
  • Spain was the only threat with the military power to actually invade and conquer England. If the Armada had succeeded, it would have been catastrophic.
  • The combination of threats was what made Elizabeth’s position truly dangerous. Mary + Catholic plots + Spanish military power + the succession crisis together created a web of interconnected dangers.

How Elizabeth Dealt with the Threats

Elizabeth and her government used several strategies to manage these threats:

  • Walsingham’s spy network: Sir Francis Walsingham built an extensive intelligence service that infiltrated Catholic networks, intercepted letters, and uncovered plots before they could succeed.
  • Harsh laws against Catholics: From the 1580s, it became treason to be a Catholic priest in England. Recusancy fines (for not attending Church of England services) were increased. Seminary priests and Jesuits were hunted, arrested, and often executed.
  • Mary’s execution (1587): Removed the figurehead of Catholic opposition, though at the cost of provoking Spain.
  • Military preparedness: Investment in the navy paid off with the defeat of the Armada in 1588.
  • The Religious Settlement as a compromise: The ‘middle way’ satisfied most people, isolating both Catholic and Puritan extremists.
  • Control of Parliament: Elizabeth used her prerogative to prevent Parliament from forcing her hand on marriage, succession, or religious reform.
Exam Tip

Questions about threats to Elizabeth often ask you to evaluate or compare. The key skills are: (1) Explain each threat clearly with specific evidence (names, dates, events). (2) Assess how serious each was — consider what could have happened if the threat had succeeded. (3) Show how the threats were interconnected (e.g., Mary was the focus of Catholic plots, which were backed by Spain). (4) Reach a clear judgement about which was most dangerous and explain why. The best answers don’t just describe — they argue.

Conclusion

Elizabeth faced threats from multiple directions throughout her reign: Catholic plots, Mary Queen of Scots, Spain, Puritans, and the succession crisis. Each was dangerous in its own way, but the most serious threats were arguably those that combined multiple dangers — particularly the connection between Mary, Catholic conspiracies, and Spanish military power. Elizabeth survived through a combination of shrewd political judgement, Walsingham’s intelligence network, military investment, and a measure of good fortune. The fact that she ruled successfully for 45 years is testament to her skill in managing these threats.

Key Takeaways

  • Threats were interconnected: Catholic plots, Mary, and Spain were closely linked, making them more dangerous together than individually.
  • Walsingham’s spy network was crucial: His intelligence work uncovered every major Catholic plot before it could succeed.
  • Elizabeth managed the threats skilfully: Through diplomacy, the Religious Settlement, military investment, and careful use of power, she survived 45 years on the throne.
