Was It Really a ‘Golden Age’?

Elizabeth’s reign (1558–1603) has long been called a ‘Golden Age’ — a time of cultural brilliance, national pride, exploration, and growing wealth. But how accurate is this label? There is strong evidence on both sides. This lesson examines it all and helps you form a balanced judgement — exactly what the exam requires.

Evidence FOR a ‘Golden Age’

1. Cultural Achievement: Theatre and Literature

There was not a single theatre in England when Elizabeth came to power. By the end of her reign, purpose-built theatres like The Globe (1599) attracted huge audiences from all classes. William Shakespeare, Christopher Marlowe, and Ben Jonson wrote plays still performed worldwide. Elizabeth loved the arts — in 1583 she paid to set up her own company of actors, ‘The Queen’s Men’. Her court was entertained by around 30 musicians.

Court patronage: Nobles set up acting companies to impress Elizabeth. Robert Dudley created the ‘Earl of Leicester’s Men’. Lord Hunsdon founded the ‘Lord Chamberlain’s Men’, which Shakespeare joined.

Government approval: Elizabeth loved plays, so local councils who had previously banned them now allowed them. New plays were showcased at court first. The government used theatre for propaganda — Shakespeare’s Richard III portrayed Elizabeth’s grandfather Henry VII as the nation’s saviour and Richard III as an evil monster.

Distraction for the poor: Not everyone was getting richer. The government felt attractions like theatre would keep people entertained and make rebellion less likely.

Wealth: Merchants and traders had money for plays. Investors saw profits in building new theatres. Cheap standing tickets meant most people could afford to go.

Plays took place in daylight — there was no artificial lighting. A trumpet at around 3pm announced the start. Coloured flags flew above the theatre: white for comedy, red for history, black for tragedy.

All social classes could attend. Cheap standing tickets were in the ‘pit’ in front of the stage. The galleries had covered seating. The rich sat in the Lord’s Rooms above the stage — on display to the whole audience. Actors wore colourful costumes and used trap doors and sound effects. Women could not perform, so boys played female roles. Audiences were often rowdy, shouting and heckling.

Not everyone approved. Large crowds could spread disease, especially the plague. Crime was a problem — cut-purses operated without effective policing. The City Council banned theatres from within the city walls.

The government also feared theatres could inspire rebellion — the Earl of Essex used a performance of Richard II to signal his uprising in 1601. Puritans saw theatres as a drunken, lawless distraction from worshipping God.

2. Exploration and Naval Achievement

English sailors like Drake, Hawkins, and Raleigh explored new parts of the world, challenged Spanish dominance, and brought wealth to England. Drake’s circumnavigation of the globe (1577–1580) was a remarkable achievement. The defeat of the Spanish Armada in 1588 brought huge national pride and established England as a serious naval power.

3. Growing Prosperity for Some

The gentry grew much richer, building grand houses like Hardwick Hall and investing in land, trade, and education. Merchants prospered from growing trade. London grew rapidly into one of Europe’s most important cities. For the upper and middle classes, this was a time of real opportunity.

4. Architecture and the ‘Great Rebuilding’

Wealthy families built magnificent country houses in the Renaissance style, with large windows, symmetrical designs, and elaborate gardens. Hardwick Hall, Longleat House, and Burghley House still stand today. This building boom reflected both new wealth and cultural confidence.

5. Relative Religious Stability

Elizabeth’s Religious Settlement of 1559 created a ‘middle way’ that most people could accept. Compared to the religious chaos under Henry VIII, Edward VI, and Mary I, her reign brought stability. England avoided the devastating religious wars that tore apart France.

6. National Pride and Identity

The Armada victory, exploration, and cultural achievements all built a growing sense of English national identity. Elizabeth — the ‘Virgin Queen’, ‘Gloriana’ — became a powerful symbol of English greatness. Her coronation involved 10 days of celebrations; she was carried through the streets on a platform of golden silk. Progresses in summer let people outside London see her in her glory. Portraits were displayed in nobles’ houses to prove their loyalty.

Evidence AGAINST a ‘Golden Age’

1. Widespread Poverty and Inequality

For many ordinary people, life was far from golden. The population grew from about 3 million to over 4 million, putting huge pressure on food, housing, and jobs. Prices rose by around 400% during the 16th century, while wages barely doubled. Bad harvests in the 1590s caused real hardship. Vagabonds and beggars were a growing problem, and the Poor Laws offered harsh, limited relief.

2. Religious Persecution

The Religious Settlement was enforced through fines, imprisonment, and execution. Catholics who refused to attend Church of England services faced recusancy fines. From the 1580s, it was treason to be a Catholic priest. Around 200 Catholics were executed during Elizabeth’s reign. Puritans also faced restrictions.

3. Ongoing Threats and Conflict

Elizabeth’s reign was not peaceful. She faced Catholic plots, Mary Queen of Scots, and Spain. England was at war with Spain from 1585 until after her death. The costly Nine Years’ War in Ireland (1594–1603) drained resources. The Essex Rebellion of 1601 revealed tensions at the heart of government.

4. The Slave Trade

Exploration had a dark side. John Hawkins was one of the first Englishmen to trade in enslaved people, transporting captured Africans to Spanish colonies. Elizabeth herself invested in and profited from these voyages. This was the beginning of English involvement in the transatlantic slave trade.

5. Limited Rights and Opportunities

Society was rigidly hierarchical. Women could not vote, hold public office, or attend university. Social mobility was very limited. The ‘Great Chain of Being’ kept everyone in their ‘place’, and challenging it was considered both sinful and illegal.

6. Propaganda and Image Control

Much of the ‘Golden Age’ image was deliberately created through propaganda. Portraits were carefully controlled. The ‘Cult of Gloriana’ was a political strategy. There were only 60 printing presses in England, and all pamphlets and books had to be approved by the Privy Council before publication. When one play seemed to criticise the Queen, all theatres were shut down for weeks. The reality for most people was far less glamorous than the image projected.

Reaching a Balanced Judgement

Key Fact — ‘How Far’ Questions

When the exam asks “How far do you agree that Elizabeth’s reign was a ‘Golden Age’?”, you need to:

  • Give evidence for the label (theatre, exploration, prosperity for some, national pride)
  • Give evidence against (poverty, persecution, conflict, inequality, propaganda)
  • Reach a clear judgement — was it a Golden Age? For whom? To what extent?

A strong conclusion might argue it was a Golden Age for certain groups (the gentry, merchants, the culturally privileged) but not for the majority, who faced poverty, hardship, and limited opportunities.

The ‘Golden Age’ label was partly created after Elizabeth’s death, as people looked back with nostalgia during the less popular Stuart reigns. Much of what we think of as the ‘Golden Age’ was shaped by Elizabethan propaganda and later romanticisation.

Exam Tip

This is a classic ‘how far do you agree’ question. Don’t just list points for and against — show how the evidence connects and make a clear argument. For example: “While theatre and exploration were genuine achievements, they mainly benefited the wealthy. For the poor majority, life meant rising prices, food shortages, and harsh punishments. The ‘Golden Age’ was real for some, but far from universal.” Always back your judgement with specific evidence.

Conclusion

Whether Elizabeth’s reign was a ‘Golden Age’ depends on perspective. For the gentry, merchants, and the culturally privileged, it was a time of real achievement — Shakespeare, Drake, the Armada, grand architecture. But for the poor, persecuted Catholics, women, and those caught in England’s wars, life was harsh and far from golden. The label is partially justified — it reflects the experience of a privileged minority, not most Elizabethans.

Key Takeaways

  • Evidence for: Theatre, exploration, the Armada, architecture, and growing wealth for the upper classes all support the ‘Golden Age’ label.
  • Evidence against: Poverty, persecution, war, the slave trade, rigid hierarchy, censorship, and propaganda challenge the idea it was golden for everyone.
  • It depends on perspective: The ‘Golden Age’ was real for some but not for the majority. The label is partly a product of propaganda and later nostalgia.
