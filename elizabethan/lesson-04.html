The Marriage & Succession Problem

Elizabeth was 25 when she became queen, and everyone assumed she would marry quickly. The question of who she would marry and who would succeed her dominated her entire reign.

Why Was Marriage a Big Deal?

  • Producing an heir: Elizabeth was the last of Henry VIII’s children. If she died without an heir, the Tudor dynasty would die with her — risking civil war.
  • Keeping out Mary, Queen of Scots: Without a Protestant heir, the Catholic Mary Stuart had the strongest claim to the throne. Marriage and children would block her path.
  • A woman ruler: Many believed a woman could not rule alone and needed a husband to govern. How could she lead an army into battle?
  • Alliances: Marriage could seal a powerful alliance with another country.
  • Danger of the wrong choice: A foreign husband might drag England into wars. An English one might cause jealousy and rebellion among rival nobles.

Potential Suitors

Pros: Dudley and Elizabeth had been childhood friends. They spent hours together almost every day — he was clearly her favourite. He was a Protestant, which suited England’s religious settlement.

Cons: Dudley was already married. In 1560, his wife Amy Robsart was found dead after falling down stairs. Rivals spread rumours that Dudley had murdered her so he could marry Elizabeth. The scandal was so great that Dudley had to be banished from court for a time. Marrying him would have caused huge jealousy among other nobles.

Pros: King of the most powerful country in Europe. A match would seal a strong alliance against France.

Cons: Philip had been married to Elizabeth’s sister Mary I, and this had been very unpopular. England had been dragged into Spain’s wars, and as a result lost the port of Calais to France. Philip was fiercely Catholic and wanted to turn all of Europe back to Catholicism. He proposed marriage, but Elizabeth never seriously considered it — and the rejection angered him.

Pros: A genuine contender later in the reign. He regularly visited Elizabeth’s court in the 1570s, and she was fond of him — calling him her “little frog.” A French alliance would help counter Spain.

Cons: Catholic — and he refused to give up his religion during negotiations. He was about 21 years younger than Elizabeth. The St Bartholomew’s Day Massacre (1572), in which thousands of French Protestants were killed, turned English public opinion strongly against a French Catholic prince. By the 1580s, Elizabeth was considered too old to have children.

Key Fact — Marriage as Diplomacy

Elizabeth used the possibility of marriage as a diplomatic tool for over 25 years. She kept foreign suitors interested, playing rival powers off against each other — without ever committing. In 1566, she told Parliament: “At present it is not convenient; nor never shall be without some peril unto you and certain danger unto me.”

Why Did Elizabeth Never Marry?

  • Keeping her independence: Tudor men were in charge of their wives. By not marrying, Elizabeth kept all the power for herself.
  • No good option: A foreign husband could drag England under foreign control. An English one would provoke jealousy and possibly rebellion.
  • Danger of childbirth: Giving birth was risky — it often resulted in death. Elizabeth had seen her sister Mary I fail to produce an heir despite her marriage.
  • Past experience: Mary I’s marriage to Philip II had been hated. Elizabeth’s own mother, Anne Boleyn, had been executed by Henry VIII.
  • The “Virgin Queen” image: Elizabeth built the image of being married only to her kingdom. This became powerful propaganda.
Exam Tip

You may be asked a “how convincing” question about why Elizabeth did not marry. Cover both personal and political reasons. Explain the pros and cons of each suitor with specific examples — e.g. Mary I’s marriage to Philip leading to the loss of Calais, or the Dudley scandal.

Conclusion

Elizabeth faced constant pressure to marry, but every option carried serious risks. She turned the problem into a strength — using marriage talks as diplomacy and building the “Virgin Queen” image.

Key Takeaways

  • Constant pressure: Parliament and the Privy Council pushed Elizabeth to marry and produce an heir. Without one, the Tudor dynasty would end and Catholic Mary Stuart was next in line.
  • Every option had drawbacks: A foreign husband risked dragging England into wars (as Philip II had done). An English one risked jealousy and rebellion (as the Dudley scandal showed).
  • A deliberate strategy: Elizabeth used marriage talks as a diplomatic tool for over 25 years, keeping rivals guessing while holding onto her power.
