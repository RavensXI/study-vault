The Religious Settlement of 1559

Overlay:

The Religious Settlement of 1559

When Elizabeth I became Queen in 1558, England was divided by religion. Her predecessor, Mary I, was a devout Catholic and had persecuted Protestants. Before that, under Edward VI, England had been Protestant. Elizabeth wanted to find a peaceful solution, a ‘middle way’, that most people could accept. This was known as the Religious Settlement of 1559.

What Did the Settlement Say?

The Religious Settlement was made up of two main Acts of Parliament:

  • The Act of Supremacy (1559): This made Elizabeth the Supreme Governor of the Church of England. This was important because it meant she was in charge, not the Pope in Rome. The title ‘Supreme Governor’ rather than ‘Supreme Head’ was a deliberate compromise — it was less offensive to Catholics who believed only Christ could be head of the Church.
  • The Act of Uniformity (1559): This set out the rules for religious worship and services. It said that all churches had to use the Book of Common Prayer. This book included a mix of traditional Catholic and newer Protestant ideas. Everyone had to attend church on Sundays or face a fine of one shilling.
Key Fact — The ‘Middle Way’

Elizabeth’s Settlement was deliberately designed as a compromise. It kept some things that pleased Catholics (e.g. bishops, church decorations, clergy vestments) while including Protestant elements (e.g. services in English, the monarch as head of the Church, clergy could marry). The aim was to create a church that the majority of English people could accept.

The Royal Injunctions of 1559

Elizabeth also issued a set of Royal Injunctions — instructions for how the Church should be run. These included rules about how clergy should dress, how churches should look, and that each church must have an English Bible. Commissioners were sent around the country to check that the rules were being followed.

Challenges to the Settlement

Catholics

Many English Catholics wanted to remain loyal to the Pope and the traditional Catholic faith. Some were willing to attend the new Church of England services to avoid fines (known as recusancy fines), but others refused. The Pope excommunicated Elizabeth in 1570, cutting her off from the Catholic Church and encouraging Catholics to disobey her.

Puritans

These were very strict Protestants who felt the Religious Settlement hadn’t gone far enough to remove all traces of Catholicism. They wanted simpler services, plainer churches, and were against things like priests wearing special robes (vestments) or bishops being in charge.

Elizabeth’s Approach

Initially, Elizabeth was quite tolerant as long as people outwardly conformed to her settlement. However, as threats from both Catholics and Puritans grew, her government took a harsher line.

Exam Tip

When answering questions about the Religious Settlement, make sure you explain why Elizabeth chose a ‘middle way’. Consider the pressures she was under from both Catholics and Protestants, and the need for stability in England after years of religious changes. Show that you understand it was a political decision as much as a religious one.

Conclusion

Elizabeth I’s Religious Settlement of 1559 was a clever attempt to bring religious peace to England. While it didn’t please everyone and faced significant challenges from both Catholics and Puritans, it largely succeeded in establishing a Church of England that most people could accept.

Key Takeaways

  • A deliberate compromise: The Settlement combined elements of both Catholic and Protestant worship to appeal to as many people as possible.
  • Two key Acts: The Act of Supremacy made Elizabeth Supreme Governor; the Act of Uniformity imposed the Book of Common Prayer and church attendance.
  • Opposition from both sides: Catholics objected to the break from Rome, while Puritans felt the reforms did not go far enough.
