Mary, Queen of Scots

Mary, Queen of Scots was Elizabeth’s Catholic cousin and one of the biggest threats to her reign. For 19 years (1568–1587), Mary was held prisoner in England, becoming a constant focal point for Catholic plots.

Mary’s Claim to the English Throne

Mary Stuart was the granddaughter of Margaret Tudor, Henry VIII’s sister. This gave her a strong blood claim to the English throne. Many Catholics believed Mary’s claim was stronger than Elizabeth’s, because the Pope had never recognised Henry VIII’s marriage to Anne Boleyn — making Elizabeth illegitimate in Catholic eyes. Mary had even claimed the title of Queen of England while married to the French king.

Key Fact — Mary’s Background

Mary became Queen of Scotland when she was just six days old in 1542. She was raised in France, married the French king (Francis II), and returned to Scotland as a widow in 1561. She was Catholic in a country turning Protestant, which caused tension from the start.

Mary’s Downfall in Scotland

Mary’s time as Queen of Scotland was troubled. Her second husband, Lord Darnley, was murdered in 1567 in suspicious circumstances. Just three months later, Mary married the Earl of Bothwell — the chief suspect in Darnley’s murder. This outraged the Scottish nobles.

The Scottish lords rebelled. Mary was forced to abdicate in favour of her infant son, James VI, in July 1567. After escaping prison, she lost a final battle and fled across the border into England in May 1568. She arrived on the coast of north-west England, exhausted and in rough clothing, and was taken to Carlisle Castle where she demanded to see her cousin Elizabeth.

Elizabeth’s Dilemma

Mary’s arrival put Elizabeth in an impossible position. She had five options, and none were good:

Elizabeth chose option 5 — what some historians have called “the least worst option.” Mary was kept in various English castles under guard for the next 19 years. Elizabeth refused to visit or release her, despite Mary’s many moving letters to her cousin. But Mary’s existence as a Catholic claimant made her a magnet for conspirators.

The Northern Rebellion, 1569

Mary’s presence quickly triggered a crisis. The Duke of Norfolk — England’s most powerful nobleman, who had royal blood and Catholic sympathies — planned to marry Mary. Elizabeth was furious. Norfolk was imprisoned, and his supporters were ordered to attend court. Instead, the Earls of Northumberland and Westmorland rebelled.

The rebellion had a mix of religious, political, and economic causes:

  • Religion: The northern countryside still clung to Catholic beliefs. The rebels wanted to restore Catholicism and free Mary.
  • Power: The Council of the North had taken power from the traditional nobility. Elizabeth gave important jobs to men she could control directly, angering the old Earls.
  • Money: The Earls had lost land. Northumberland had lost valuable copper mines. They blamed Elizabeth’s advisors — especially Robert Cecil.

In November 1569, nearly 5,000 rebels marched south. They forced their way into Durham Cathedral, overturned the Protestant communion table, held a Catholic Mass, and destroyed Protestant prayer books. The rebels wore Catholic badges and carried Catholic banners.

Key Fact — Why the Northern Rebellion Failed
  • Mary was moved further south before the rebels could reach her
  • No foreign help arrived — Spain and the Pope did not send support
  • Many northern gentry refused to join
  • A royal army of 14,000 men was raised, far outnumbering the rebels
  • Westmorland escaped and died in poverty in the Netherlands
  • Northumberland was captured in 1572. He was offered the chance to save his life by renouncing his Catholic beliefs — he refused and was beheaded

The Consequences of the Northern Rebellion

Elizabeth’s response was harsh. Around 800 rebels were executed as a warning. The rebellion proved that Mary’s presence in England was a genuine threat — and that some English Catholics were willing to take up arms.

The aftermath led to tighter controls on Catholics and greater suspicion of Mary. The Duke of Norfolk was arrested and later executed for treason in 1572 after his role in the Ridolfi Plot was uncovered.

Why Did Elizabeth Keep Mary Imprisoned for 19 Years?

Despite pressure from Parliament and advisors, Elizabeth refused to execute Mary for almost two decades:

  • Royal blood: Monarchs were appointed by God. Executing one could set a precedent that endangered Elizabeth herself.
  • Family ties: Mary was Elizabeth’s cousin. Killing a relative would look barbaric.
  • International consequences: Mary’s death could provoke war with France or Spain.
  • Lack of proof: For years, there was no clear evidence that Mary had personally approved any assassination plot.
Exam Tip

A common 8-mark question is: “What was important about the arrival of Mary, Queen of Scots in England in 1568?” Cover multiple factors: her claim to the throne, her Catholic faith, how she became a figurehead for plots (like the Northern Rebellion), and Elizabeth’s impossible choice about what to do with her. Don’t just describe events — explain why they mattered.

Conclusion

Mary was arguably the single greatest threat to Elizabeth’s reign. Her claim to the throne, Catholic faith, and role as a figurehead for plots made her a constant danger. Keeping her alive was risky — but executing a fellow queen seemed unthinkable.

Key Takeaways

  • A dangerous rival: Mary had a legitimate claim many Catholics considered stronger than Elizabeth’s. She had even claimed the title of Queen of England.
  • The Northern Rebellion (1569): A mix of religious, political, and economic causes. Nearly 5,000 rebels marched to free Mary and restore Catholicism. It was crushed, but proved the threat was real.
  • Elizabeth’s dilemma: Every option had serious drawbacks. Imprisonment was “the least worst option” — but it made Mary a permanent magnet for plotters.
