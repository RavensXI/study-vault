Elizabethan Exploration

Overlay:

Elizabethan Exploration

Elizabeth’s reign saw a dramatic expansion in English seafaring and exploration. Driven by the desire for wealth, new trade routes, national prestige, and the chance to challenge Spanish dominance, English sailors ventured further than ever before. These voyages laid the foundations for the future British Empire, but they also had a darker side, including the beginnings of English involvement in the transatlantic slave trade.

Key Fact — Why Explore?

Elizabethan exploration was driven by four main motives:

  • Wealth and trade — gold, silver, spices, silk, and new markets
  • Challenging Spain — privateering and breaking Spain’s monopoly on New World riches
  • National prestige — adventure, fame, and expanding English influence
  • New technology — improved navigation, maps, and ship design made longer voyages possible

What Made Exploration Possible?

  • Better ships: New designs like the galleon were faster and could carry more supplies for long voyages.
  • Improved navigation: Instruments like the astrolabe and the magnetic compass allowed sailors to navigate more accurately. Better maps (especially Mercator’s world map of 1569) gave a clearer picture of the world.
  • Royal support: Elizabeth encouraged and sometimes secretly funded voyages, especially those that targeted Spanish interests.
  • Privateering licences: The Queen granted ‘letters of marque’ allowing English captains to legally attack enemy ships and keep a share of the plunder.

Key Explorers and Their Voyages

Sir John Hawkins

Hawkins was one of the first English sailors to become involved in the transatlantic slave trade. In the 1560s, he made several voyages capturing enslaved people in West Africa and selling them to Spanish colonies in the Americas. His voyages were highly profitable but illegal under Spanish law. In 1568, Hawkins’s fleet was attacked by the Spanish at San Juan de Ulúa in Mexico, an event that deepened English hostility towards Spain. Hawkins later became Treasurer of the Navy and played a key role in modernising the English fleet before the Armada.

Sir Francis Drake

Drake was the most famous English explorer and privateer of the age. He was driven by a hatred of Spain (stemming from San Juan de Ulúa) and a desire for wealth and adventure.

  • Circumnavigation of the globe (1577–1580): Drake became the first Englishman to sail around the world. He raided Spanish ports and treasure ships along the Pacific coast of South America, returning to England with an estimated £600,000 in treasure — more than the Crown’s annual income. Elizabeth knighted him aboard his ship, the Golden Hind.
  • Raid on Cadiz (1587): Drake attacked the Spanish port, destroying around 30 ships and delaying the Armada by a year (‘singeing the King of Spain’s beard’).
  • The Spanish Armada (1588): Drake served as vice-admiral of the English fleet.

Sir Walter Raleigh

Raleigh was a courtier, soldier, and explorer who is best known for his attempts to establish an English colony in North America.

  • Roanoke (1585–1590): Raleigh funded two attempts to establish a colony on Roanoke Island (in modern-day North Carolina). The first colony was abandoned. The second colony, established in 1587, became known as the ‘Lost Colony’ — when a supply ship returned in 1590, all the colonists had vanished. The only clue was the word ‘CROATOAN’ carved into a post.
  • El Dorado: Raleigh also led expeditions to South America searching for the legendary golden city of El Dorado, but never found it.
  • New goods: Raleigh is often credited with introducing tobacco and potatoes to England from the New World, though this is debated by historians.

What Did These Voyages Achieve?

Increased wealth: Privateering and trade brought enormous profits to England. Drake’s circumnavigation alone brought back treasure worth more than the annual royal income.

New knowledge: Voyages mapped new lands and seas, improving understanding of geography, navigation, and the natural world.

Challenging Spain: English exploration directly challenged Spain’s dominance of the oceans and the New World, contributing to the growing rivalry that led to the Armada.

Foundation of empire: Although Roanoke failed, these early attempts at colonisation laid the groundwork for future English colonies in North America (such as Jamestown in 1607).

National pride: The exploits of Drake, Hawkins, and Raleigh became a source of enormous national pride and contributed to the idea of Elizabethan England as a ‘Golden Age’.

The slave trade: A darker legacy — Hawkins’s voyages marked the beginning of English involvement in the transatlantic slave trade, which would grow to cause immense suffering over the next three centuries.

Exam Tip

When writing about Elizabethan exploration, always link the motives of the explorers to their actual voyages and the outcomes. Don’t just describe what happened — explain why it mattered. Also, remember to mention the darker aspects (the slave trade, the failure of Roanoke) alongside the achievements. Examiners reward balanced answers that show awareness of different perspectives.

Conclusion

Elizabethan exploration was driven by economic ambition, rivalry with Spain, technological advances, and a thirst for adventure and national glory. While the voyages of Drake, Hawkins, and Raleigh brought wealth, knowledge, and prestige to England, they also had significant negative consequences, particularly the beginning of the English slave trade. The failure of Roanoke showed the limits of Elizabethan colonisation, but these early ventures laid the foundations for England’s future as a global maritime power.

Key Takeaways

  • Multiple motives: Explorers were driven by wealth, rivalry with Spain, national prestige, and improved technology — these factors were interconnected.
  • Drake’s circumnavigation was a turning point: It brought enormous wealth, challenged Spanish dominance, and boosted English national pride.
  • Mixed legacy: Alongside genuine achievements in exploration and navigation, the voyages also marked the start of English involvement in the slave trade.
