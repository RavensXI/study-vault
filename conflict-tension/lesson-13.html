The Nazi-Soviet Pact

The End of Appeasement

Chamberlain’s promise of “peace for our time” lasted less than six months. In March 1939, Hitler invaded the rest of Czechoslovakia, tearing up the Munich Agreement and proving beyond doubt that he could not be trusted. Appeasement was dead.

Key Fact

Unlike the Sudetenland — which had a German-speaking majority and could be justified under self-determination — the rest of Czechoslovakia was not German. Hitler’s invasion in March 1939 proved his aims went far beyond “uniting German-speaking peoples.” He wanted empire.

Britain and France responded by issuing a guarantee to Poland — if Hitler attacked Poland, they would declare war. The era of giving in to Hitler’s demands was over. The question was no longer whether war would come, but when.

Stalin’s Dilemma

The Soviet leader Joseph Stalin was suspicious of both sides and faced an impossible choice. He received alliance offers from both the Western democracies and Nazi Germany, and had strong reasons to distrust each.

Stalin had deep reasons for distrusting the Western powers. The USSR had been excluded from the Munich Conference in 1938 — a major international decision about Eastern Europe was made without consulting the largest Eastern European power.

Stalin believed Britain and France wanted Nazism and Communism to destroy each other, leaving the Western democracies safe. He saw the Munich Agreement as evidence that they were willing to redirect Hitler’s aggression eastward rather than confront it.

He also felt the British and French guarantee to Poland was weak — after all, they had failed to protect Czechoslovakia. Why would Poland be any different?

Hitler’s book Mein Kampf openly stated his aim to seize Soviet land for Lebensraum (living space) and destroy Communism. Nazi ideology was violently anti-Communist — any deal with Hitler was, by definition, temporary.

Stalin understood that an alliance with Germany would not bring lasting peace — but it might buy the time he desperately needed.

The Nazi-Soviet Pact

On 23 August 1939, the world was stunned when Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union — sworn ideological enemies — signed a Nazi-Soviet Pact. Also known as the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, it was a non-aggression agreement that contained devastating secret clauses.

Key Fact — The Secret Protocol

The Pact included a secret clause agreeing to divide Poland between Germany and the USSR. It also gave Stalin a sphere of influence over the Baltic states (Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania) and parts of Finland — providing a crucial buffer zone against future German attack.

Why Hitler Signed

For Hitler, the Pact was a tactical masterstroke. It gave him a green light to invade Poland without fear of Soviet attack from the east. Crucially, it meant Germany could avoid a two-front war — the nightmare that had helped destroy Germany in World War One.

Why Stalin Signed

Stalin’s decision was logical, if cynical. The Red Army was critically weakened after his purges of the 1930s had removed most of its experienced officers. The Pact gave him time to rearm. He also gained territory — the secret deal gave the USSR the eastern half of Poland and influence over the Baltic states, creating a buffer zone between the Soviet heartland and Nazi Germany.

Key Fact — Stalin’s Calculation

Stalin had been alienated by the Western powers at Munich. He did not trust their guarantee to Poland. Signing with Hitler was not an endorsement of Nazism — it was a survival strategy: gain time, gain territory, and let the capitalist nations fight each other first.

Timeline: The Road to War

March 1939

Hitler invades the rest of Czechoslovakia

German troops march into Prague. The Munich Agreement is torn up. Hitler has proven that appeasement has failed — his ambitions go far beyond uniting German speakers.

March 1939

Britain and France guarantee Poland

In response to the Czech invasion, Britain and France pledge to defend Poland if Hitler attacks. Appeasement is officially abandoned.

April – August 1939

Diplomatic negotiations intensify

Both the Western Allies and Germany approach Stalin with alliance proposals. Britain and France send a low-ranking delegation by slow boat — confirming Stalin’s belief they are not serious.

23 August 1939

Nazi-Soviet Pact signed

Molotov and Ribbentrop sign the non-aggression pact in Moscow. The secret protocol divides Poland and Eastern Europe into spheres of influence. The last barrier to Hitler’s invasion of Poland is removed.

1 September 1939

Germany invades Poland

With his eastern flank secured by the Pact, Hitler launches the invasion. World War Two begins.

Exam Tip

When analysing why Stalin signed the Nazi-Soviet Pact, demonstrate that it was a logical if cynical choice. The strongest answers will explain how Stalin had been alienated by the Western powers at Munich, distrusted their guarantees to Poland, and needed time to rebuild the Red Army after the purges. Avoid simply calling it “evil” — examiners reward answers that explain the reasoning behind historical decisions.

Conclusion

The invasion of Czechoslovakia killed appeasement and led directly to the guarantee to Poland. The Nazi-Soviet Pact removed the threat of Soviet opposition and gave Hitler the confidence to invade Poland, making the outbreak of World War Two a virtual certainty.

Key Takeaways

  • Appeasement died in Prague: Hitler’s invasion of the rest of Czechoslovakia in March 1939 proved his aims went far beyond self-determination — the Munich Agreement was worthless, and Britain and France finally committed to resisting further aggression with the guarantee to Poland.
  • The Pact was a cynical bargain on both sides: Hitler gained freedom to invade Poland without a two-front war; Stalin gained time to rearm and a buffer zone of territory — both knew the agreement was temporary, but each calculated it served their immediate interests.
  • Western failures pushed Stalin toward Hitler: Exclusion from Munich, a weak British negotiating effort, and distrust of the Poland guarantee all convinced Stalin that signing with Germany was his best option for survival — a decision that sealed Poland’s fate.
