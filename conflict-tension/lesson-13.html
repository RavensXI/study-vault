Lesson 13 of 15

The Nazi-Soviet Pact

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The End of Appeasement

Chamberlain’s promise of “peace for our time” lasted less than six months. In March 1939, Hitler invaded the rest of Czechoslovakia, tearing up the Munich Agreement. Appeasement was dead.

Key Fact

The Sudetenland had a German-speaking majority, so Hitler could claim self-determination. The rest of Czechoslovakia was not German. This invasion proved Hitler wanted empire, not just unity for German speakers.

Britain and France responded with a guarantee to Poland — if Hitler attacked, they would declare war. The era of giving in was over. The question was no longer whether war would come, but when.

Stalin’s Dilemma

Soviet leader Joseph Stalin was suspicious of both sides. He had alliance offers from the Western democracies and from Nazi Germany, but strong reasons to distrust each.

The Nazi-Soviet Pact

On 23 August 1939, the world was stunned. Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union — sworn ideological enemies — signed a Nazi-Soviet Pact. Also called the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, it was a non-aggression deal with devastating secret clauses. Nazi Foreign Minister Joachim von Ribbentrop flew to Moscow to meet Stalin and Soviet Foreign Minister Molotov.

Key Fact — The Secret Protocol

The Pact included a secret clause agreeing to divide Poland between Germany and the USSR. It also gave Stalin a sphere of influence over the Baltic states (Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania) and parts of Finland — a buffer zone against future German attack.

Why Hitler Signed

The Pact was a tactical masterstroke for Hitler. It gave him a green light to invade Poland without Soviet attack from the east. Germany could avoid a two-front war — the nightmare that had destroyed Germany in World War One. It also stopped Britain and France from forming an alliance with the USSR that could surround Germany. On top of this, the USSR promised one million tons of food, oil and raw materials plus electric equipment and trains — vital resources for the coming war.

Why Stalin Signed

Stalin’s decision was logical, if cynical. The Red Army was badly weakened — his purges had executed 81 out of 103 generals, leaving the military poorly led. The Pact gave him time to rearm. He used this time to move factories deeper into Russia, behind the Ural Mountains, preparing for when Hitler eventually betrayed him. He also gained territory — eastern Poland and influence over the Baltic states, creating a buffer zone. In return, Stalin sent Germany 30 warplanes and samples of other weapons.

Key Fact — Stalin’s Calculation

Stalin had been alienated at Munich. He did not trust the guarantee to Poland. Signing with Hitler was not an endorsement of Nazism — it was a survival strategy: gain time, gain territory, and let the capitalist nations fight each other first.

Timeline: The Road to War

March 1939

Hitler invades the rest of Czechoslovakia

German troops march into Prague. The Munich Agreement is torn up. Hitler’s aims clearly go beyond uniting German speakers.

March 1939

Britain and France guarantee Poland

They pledge to defend Poland if Hitler attacks. Appeasement is officially over.

April – August 1939

Diplomatic negotiations intensify

Both sides approach Stalin. Britain sends Drax by slow boat with no power to agree anything. Stalin sees this as proof they are not serious.

23 August 1939

Nazi-Soviet Pact signed

Molotov and Ribbentrop sign in Moscow. The secret protocol divides Poland and Eastern Europe. The last barrier to invasion is removed.

1 September 1939

Germany invades Poland from the west

With his eastern flank secured, Hitler launches the invasion. On 17 September, the USSR attacks from the east. War begins.

Exam Tip

When analysing why Stalin signed the Pact, show it was a logical if cynical choice. Explain how he was alienated at Munich, distrusted the guarantee to Poland, and needed time to rebuild the Red Army after the purges. Do not just call it “evil” — examiners reward answers that explain the reasoning behind decisions.

Conclusion

The invasion of Czechoslovakia killed appeasement and led to the guarantee to Poland. The Nazi-Soviet Pact removed Soviet opposition and gave Hitler the confidence to invade, making war almost certain.

Key Takeaways

  • Appeasement died in Prague: Hitler’s March 1939 invasion proved his aims went beyond self-determination. Britain and France finally committed to resisting him with the guarantee to Poland.
  • The Pact was a cynical bargain: Hitler avoided a two-front war and gained resources. Stalin gained time to rearm, territory, and a buffer zone. Both knew it was temporary.
  • Western failures pushed Stalin toward Hitler: Exclusion from Munich, the weak Drax mission, and distrust of the Poland guarantee convinced Stalin that signing with Germany was his best option — sealing Poland’s fate.
