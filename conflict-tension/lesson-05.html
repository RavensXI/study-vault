The League of Nations

The League of Nations was founded in 1920, with its headquarters in Geneva, Switzerland. It was the brainchild of President Woodrow Wilson, who believed an international organisation could prevent future wars through cooperation rather than conflict. However, the League was critically damaged from the outset when the USA refused to join.

Key Fact — The League’s Four Aims
  • Stop war through collective security
  • Improve lives — better working conditions, health, and social welfare
  • Achieve disarmament — reduce weapons to prevent future wars
  • Enforce the Treaty of Versailles — uphold the post-war settlement

Structure of the League

The League’s structure was designed to enable collective decision-making, but in practice its mechanisms made decisive action extremely difficult. Understanding the structure reveals why the League often failed to respond quickly to crises.

The Assembly

The Assembly was the League’s parliament, where every member nation had one vote. It met annually to discuss major issues and set the League’s budget. Crucially, decisions required unanimity — every member had to agree — making it extremely slow and difficult to reach decisions on controversial matters.

The Council

The Council was a smaller, more powerful body that met more frequently to deal with crises. It had permanent members — Britain, France, Italy, and Japan — each holding veto power, meaning any one of them could block any decision. Temporary members were elected by the Assembly to serve on a rotating basis.

Permanent Court of International Justice

Based at The Hague in the Netherlands, this court was designed to settle legal disputes between nations. However, it had no means of enforcing its rulings — nations could simply ignore its judgements without consequence.

The Secretariat

The Secretariat was the League’s civil service, responsible for administration, record-keeping, and preparing reports for the Assembly and Council. It carried out the day-to-day work of the organisation.

Commissions and Agencies

The League operated several specialist bodies that achieved some genuine success. The International Labour Organisation (ILO) worked to improve working conditions worldwide. The Health Committee combated disease. The Slavery Commission fought to end forced labour. These humanitarian efforts were among the League’s most positive achievements.

Powers: Collective Security in Practice

The League’s main tool for keeping peace was collective security — the idea that all members would act together against any aggressor. The Covenant of the League set out a three-step process for dealing with aggression:

Key Fact — Three Steps of Collective Security
  1. Moral Condemnation — the League would publicly condemn the aggressor and order them to stop
  2. Economic Sanctions — member nations would refuse to trade with the aggressor, damaging their economy
  3. Military Force — as a last resort, member nations would contribute armed forces to stop the aggressor

Fatal Flaws

The League’s collective security system suffered from a critical weakness: it had no army of its own. Every step of enforcement relied entirely on the goodwill and cooperation of member nations, who were often reluctant to commit troops or damage their own trade.

Absent USA: The world’s most powerful economy and military refused to join, removing the League’s strongest potential enforcer and undermining its credibility from day one.

Unanimity requirement: Both the Assembly and Council required unanimous agreement. Any single member could block action, making swift responses to crises virtually impossible.

Veto power: Permanent Council members could individually block any decision, meaning self-interested nations could prevent the League from acting against them or their allies.

No standing army: Military action required members to voluntarily contribute forces. In practice, no nation wanted to send its soldiers to fight in distant conflicts that did not directly threaten its own interests.

Sanctions were leaky: Non-member nations — including the USA — were not bound by League sanctions and could continue trading with aggressors, rendering economic penalties ineffective.

Exam Tip

Don’t just describe the League’s structure — explain how it was flawed. For example, argue that the combination of unanimous decision-making and veto power made decisive action virtually impossible. Linking structure to weakness demonstrates analytical thinking that examiners reward.

Conclusion

The League of Nations was founded with noble ambitions — to prevent war, improve lives, and create a new international order based on cooperation. Yet it was crippled from the beginning by structural weaknesses and the absence of the world’s most powerful nation.

Key Takeaways

  • The USA’s absence was the League’s greatest weakness: Without American economic and military power, the League lacked the ability to enforce its decisions — collective security was only as strong as its most powerful members, and the strongest refused to participate.
  • Structure guaranteed inaction: Unanimous voting, veto power, and no standing army meant the League could identify problems but rarely solve them — it was designed for consensus in a world that could not agree.
  • Ambition outstripped capability: The League tried to maintain peace, enforce treaties, achieve disarmament, and improve global welfare simultaneously, but lacked the authority, resources, or member commitment to accomplish any of these goals reliably.
