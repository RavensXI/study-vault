Responsibility for WWII

Who Was to Blame?

World War Two was not caused by one person or one event. It grew from twenty years of tension — flawed treaties, failed institutions, and bad decisions on all sides. The best exam answers show war had multiple, connected causes.

Argument 1: Hitler Was Responsible

This is the most direct argument. Hitler’s aims, set out in Mein Kampf (1925), could not be achieved without war. He wanted to overturn the Treaty of Versailles, unite all German speakers, seize Lebensraum in the east, and destroy Communism. The Hossbach Memorandum (1937) confirmed these plans. In a secret meeting with his generals, Hitler said Germany could only get the raw materials it needed by expanding east. He named Austria, Czechoslovakia, Poland, and Lithuania as targets, and said this must happen as soon as possible. War with Britain was to be avoided — but war itself was inevitable.

Hitler broke every agreement he made. He rearmed Germany, remilitarised the Rhineland, carried out Anschluss with Austria, seized the Sudetenland, invaded Czechoslovakia, and finally invaded Poland. He also built alliances — the Rome–Berlin Axis (1936) with Italy, the Anti-Comintern Pact (1936) with Japan, and the Pact of Steel (1939) with Italy — making Germany stronger at each step.

Each move was calculated. He tested Britain and France and found them weak. The invasion of Poland was the direct cause of war — Hitler planned it, manufactured the Gleiwitz pretext, and gave the order.

Historians debate whether Hitler had a master plan from the start (the “intentionalist” view) or was an opportunist who seized chances as they came (the “structuralist” view). The Hossbach Memorandum supports the intentionalist view. Either way, his actions were the main driver of war.

Argument 2: Chamberlain & Appeasement

Hitler was the aggressor — but the policy of appeasement by Britain and France encouraged him. By giving in again and again, the Western powers told Hitler that aggression would be rewarded and that they lacked the will to fight.

The Munich Agreement (1938) is the clearest example. Britain and France let Hitler take the Sudetenland without a fight, betraying Czechoslovakia. This gave Hitler the confidence to seize the rest of Czechoslovakia just months later.

Appeasement gave Hitler time to rearm. Germany’s military grew far faster than Britain’s. Each concession made the eventual war harder to win. Appeasement also alienated Stalin — being excluded from Munich pushed him toward signing the Nazi-Soviet Pact with Hitler instead.

Context matters, though. Britain was still recovering from World War One. The public feared another war. The military was not ready to fight in 1938. Chamberlain may have been wrong, but his reasoning was not irrational.

Argument 3: Stalin Was Responsible

The Nazi-Soviet Pact (August 1939) sealed Poland’s fate. By signing with Hitler, Stalin removed the threat of a two-front war — the one thing that might have stopped the invasion of Poland.

The Pact’s secret clause agreed to divide Poland between Germany and the USSR. Stalin did not just stand aside — he invaded Poland from the east on 17 September 1939.

Stalin acted out of self-interest. He gained territory, time to rearm, and a buffer zone of Baltic states. He calculated that letting the Western nations fight Germany would help the USSR.

However, Stalin’s defenders say he was pushed toward the Pact by Western failures. He had been excluded from Munich and distrusted the guarantee to Poland. He believed Britain and France wanted to redirect Hitler eastward. Without the Nazi-Soviet Pact, Hitler may never have risked invading Poland — he would have faced a war on two fronts.

Argument 4: Wider Factors

Beyond individual leaders, deeper factors created the conditions that made war possible — even likely.

Key Fact — Long-Term Causes
  • The Treaty of Versailles (1919) — created deep German resentment. The “Diktat,” War Guilt Clause, reparations, territorial losses, and military limits all fuelled a desire for revenge that Hitler exploited.
  • The League of Nations — failed to stop aggression in Manchuria (1931) and Abyssinia (1935). This proved collective security was an empty promise. Dictators learned they could act without punishment.
  • The Great Depression (1929) — mass unemployment and poverty created the conditions for extremism. Without the Depression, Hitler would probably never have risen to power. Many countries acted in self-interest rather than working together, and some turned to aggressive dictators who promised easy answers.

The Treaty of Versailles was a long-term cause. It did not make war certain, but it created resentment that extremists exploited. Hitler’s promise to overturn the Treaty was central to his appeal.

The League of Nations was meant to prevent wars through collective action. It failed — unable to stop Japan in Manchuria or Italy in Abyssinia. This showed Hitler that no one would stand up to aggression.

The Great Depression (1929) turned German resentment into revolution. Millions lost jobs and hope, making them willing to vote for extreme parties. Without the Depression, the Nazis might have stayed a fringe movement. Aggressive leaders across Europe used their militaries to grab the resources they wanted.

Exam Tip

This is a classic “who was to blame?” essay topic. The exam question will name one cause and ask “How far do you agree?” — e.g. appeasement, Hitler’s foreign policy, the Nazi-Soviet Pact, or German remilitarisation. Start with that given factor, then add two more causes to show balance. Always reach a clear judgement in your conclusion — explain why one cause matters more than the others. Examiners reward answers that weigh causes against each other, not just list them.

Conclusion

There was no single cause of World War Two. Hitler’s ambition drove Europe toward war, but he was helped by a flawed treaty, a weak League, appeasement that rewarded aggression, and a Soviet pact that removed the last barrier to invasion. The war was the result of collective failure over two decades.

Key Takeaways

  • Hitler bears the greatest responsibility: His aims could not be achieved without war. He broke every agreement, manufactured pretexts, and ordered the invasion of Poland.
  • He did not act alone: Appeasement emboldened him, the Nazi-Soviet Pact enabled him, and the failures of Versailles, the League, and the Depression brought him to power. These causes are connected — for example, appeasement alienated Stalin, pushing him toward the Pact with Hitler.
  • Think in layers: Short-term triggers (Poland invasion), medium-term policies (appeasement, the Pact), and long-term factors (Versailles, League failure, Depression) all interacted. Always weigh them against each other and reach a clear judgement.
