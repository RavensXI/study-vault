Appeasement & the Sudetenland

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

What Was Appeasement?

Appeasement meant giving in to an aggressor’s demands to avoid conflict. From 1937, it became the official policy of British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain, who believed many of Hitler’s grievances were fair and that satisfying them would keep the peace.

Why Was Appeasement Followed?

Appeasement was not cowardice. It was driven by powerful factors that made it seem the most sensible choice at the time.

Key Fact — Six Reasons for Appeasement
  • Fear of another war — The trauma of WWI was still vivid. Britain and France had lost 2.3 million soldiers combined. No leader wanted to repeat that.
  • The Treaty was seen as unfair — Many in Britain felt Germany had been treated too harshly at Versailles. Some of Hitler’s demands — like reuniting German-speakers — seemed reasonable.
  • Fear of Communism — Some saw Hitler as a useful barrier against Soviet communism. A strong Germany could act as a buffer against Stalin.
  • Economic problems — The Great Depression had hit Britain and France hard. Neither felt it could afford war.
  • American isolationism — The USA had retreated into isolationism. Britain knew it could not count on American support.
  • Buying time to rearm — Britain’s military was not ready for war. Some argue Chamberlain used appeasement to delay conflict while rebuilding the armed forces, especially the RAF.

Case Study 1: Anschluss with Austria (March 1938)

The Anschluss — the union of Germany and Austria — was forbidden by the Treaty of Versailles. Hitler had tried and failed in 1934, when Mussolini moved Italian troops to the Austrian border to stop him. By 1938, everything had changed: Italy was now Germany’s ally through the Rome-Berlin Axis. Mussolini told Hitler that “Austria did not interest him at all.”

Hitler encouraged Austrian Nazis to create unrest, making it look like the country was in crisis. He then summoned Chancellor Schuschnigg to a meeting and bullied him into giving Austrian Nazis key government posts.

When Schuschnigg tried to hold a vote on Austrian independence, Hitler threatened invasion. Schuschnigg was forced to resign. On 12 March 1938, German troops marched in and were met by cheering crowds.

A rigged vote then claimed 99.75% support for union with Germany. Britain and France only issued diplomatic protests. Churchill protested strongly, but the policy of appeasement prevented stronger action. Most people felt Austria was too far from Britain and too close to Germany for Britain to act.

The Anschluss gave Hitler control of Austria’s iron, steel, and engineering industries (useful for making weapons). He also gained the Austrian Central Bank’s funds and the country’s gold reserves.

Austria gave Germany a springboard for any future invasion of south-eastern Europe. Czechoslovakia’s western provinces were now virtually surrounded by Germany on three sides. The Czechs were horrified — and had little faith that Britain and France would actually help them.

Over 80,000 “enemies” were rounded up in the first two weeks of occupation, and new laws restricted Jews. Hitler had now taken over another country without firing a shot — and his actions were escalating.

Case Study 2: The Sudetenland Crisis (September 1938)

Emboldened by the Anschluss, Hitler turned to Czechoslovakia. The Sudetenland — the western border region — had about 3 million ethnic Germans. It also contained Czechoslovakia’s border fortifications, rich coal deposits, and major factories including the Skoda works (a huge arms factory). Losing it would leave Czechoslovakia defenceless.

Hitler used the same tactics as Austria. He encouraged the Sudeten German Party, led by Konrad Henlein, to demand self-government and claim German speakers were being persecuted. This led to riots and protests. Hitler told his generals to prepare to invade by 1 October 1938.

Chamberlain flew to Germany three times to negotiate. On 15 September at Berchtesgaden, Hitler demanded areas with over 50% German speakers. Chamberlain agreed and persuaded the Czechs to accept. On 22 September at Bad Godesberg, Hitler raised his demands to all of the Sudetenland and staged fake reports of Czech atrocities. Chamberlain refused. The Royal Navy was put on high alert. Finally, on 29 September, a conference was called at Munich.

Key Fact — The Munich Agreement

The Munich Agreement was signed on 29 September 1938 by Hitler, Chamberlain, Mussolini, and Daladier (France). Hitler got all his demands — the entire Sudetenland. In return, he promised he had “no further territorial ambitions.” Czechoslovakia and the USSR were not invited. Chamberlain returned to Britain declaring he had secured “peace for our time.” Hitler took the Sudetenland on 1 October 1938.

Britain: Huge celebrations. Crowds gathered at Buckingham Palace and Downing Street. Chamberlain was seen as a hero who had saved Europe from war. But critics like Winston Churchill attacked it, declaring: “You were given the choice between war and dishonour. You chose dishonour, and you will have war.”

Czechoslovakia: The Czechs were devastated. They lost 20% of their land, including valuable resources and industries. They felt betrayed — they had not even been invited to the conference that decided their fate. To make things worse, Hungary and Poland also began demanding parts of Czechoslovakia.

The USSR: Stalin was insulted he had not been invited. The USSR had an alliance with Czechoslovakia and would have been willing to act — but not without British and French support. Stalin suspected Britain and France were happy to let Hitler grow stronger in the east, so long as it did not threaten them. This pushed him towards a deal with Hitler.

Six months after Munich, Hitler broke his word. He encouraged Slovakia to break away from Czechoslovakia. The new Czech president, Hacha, travelled to Berlin in desperation. Hitler bullied and threatened him — the 66-year-old likely had a heart attack during the meeting. Hacha agreed to Germany taking over the rest of Czechoslovakia.

This was a turning point. For the first time, Hitler had taken over a country that was not mainly German-speaking. This was blatant aggression, not “uniting German speakers.” Britain dropped appeasement. Chamberlain pledged to defend Poland if Hitler attacked. Rearmament increased. The British public now supported action — they felt everything possible had been done to keep the peace.

Historians disagree about appeasement. In 1940, Guilty Men blamed Chamberlain but mainly blamed Hitler. In 1961, A.J.P. Taylor argued that Chamberlain’s misreading of the situation pushed Hitler into war.

By the late 1960s, “revisionist” historians like Dilks argued Britain was in a position of weakness, and appeasement was a realistic policy. By the 1990s, “counter-revisionists” like Parker and McDonough again blamed Chamberlain for missed opportunities and clung to appeasement when it clearly was not working.

Exam Tip

Do not dismiss appeasement as cowardly or stupid. Examiners reward answers that explain the context — WWI trauma, sympathy for German grievances, fear of communism, economic weakness, and no American support. Show you understand why it was followed before judging whether it was right. You can also mention the historian debate (Taylor vs Parker) for top marks.

Conclusion

Appeasement allowed Hitler to achieve his greatest ambitions without firing a shot. The Anschluss united Austria with Germany. Munich handed over the Sudetenland. Then in March 1939, Hitler broke his word and took the rest of Czechoslovakia — finally proving appeasement had failed. Britain pledged to defend Poland, and Europe moved towards war.

Key Takeaways

  • Appeasement had rational foundations: WWI trauma, sympathy for German grievances, economic weakness, fear of communism, and the need to rearm. It was not simple cowardice.
  • Each concession made the next crisis worse: The Anschluss went unopposed, emboldening Hitler over the Sudetenland. Munich showed that a democratic nation could be betrayed without its consent.
  • March 1939 was the turning point: Taking non-German Czechoslovakia proved Hitler could not be trusted. Britain dropped appeasement, the USSR lost faith in the West, and Stalin began looking for his own deal with Hitler.
