Appeasement & the Sudetenland

0:00 / 0:00

What Was Appeasement?

Appeasement was the policy of giving in to an aggressor’s demands in order to avoid conflict. From 1937, it became the official foreign policy of British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain, who believed that many of Hitler’s grievances were legitimate and that satisfying them would preserve peace in Europe.

Why Was Appeasement Followed?

Appeasement was not the result of cowardice or naivety. It was driven by a combination of powerful factors that made it seem like the most moral and pragmatic choice available in the late 1930s.

Key Fact — Six Reasons for Appeasement
  • Fear of another war — The trauma of World War One was still vivid. Britain and France had lost a combined 2.3 million soldiers. No leader wanted to repeat that slaughter.
  • The Treaty was seen as unfair — Many in Britain believed Germany had been treated too harshly at Versailles. Some of Hitler’s demands — such as reuniting German-speakers — seemed reasonable.
  • Fear of Communism — Hitler was seen by some as a useful barrier against the spread of Soviet communism. A strong Germany could serve as a buffer against Stalin.
  • Economic problems — The Great Depression had devastated the British and French economies. Neither country felt it could afford the enormous cost of war.
  • American isolationism — The USA had retreated into isolationism after World War One. Britain knew it could not count on American support and feared fighting a major war alone.
  • Buying time to rearm — Britain’s military was not ready for war. Some argue Chamberlain used appeasement to delay conflict while Britain rebuilt its armed forces, particularly the RAF.

Case Study 1: Anschluss with Austria (March 1938)

The Anschluss — the union of Germany and Austria — had been one of Hitler’s earliest ambitions and was explicitly forbidden by the Treaty of Versailles. In March 1938, he achieved it through a combination of internal subversion, diplomatic bullying, and military threat.

Hitler first encouraged Austrian Nazis to create unrest and instability, making it appear as though Austria was on the verge of civil war. He then summoned Austrian Chancellor Schuschnigg to a meeting and subjected him to intense pressure, demanding that Austrian Nazis be given key government positions.

When Schuschnigg tried to hold a plebiscite on Austrian independence, Hitler threatened invasion. Schuschnigg was forced to resign. On 12 March 1938, German troops marched into Austria and were met by cheering crowds. There was no military opposition whatsoever.

A rigged vote was subsequently held, claiming 99.75% support for union with Germany. Britain and France issued no more than diplomatic protests. The League of Nations was not even consulted.

Case Study 2: The Sudetenland Crisis (September 1938)

Emboldened by the Anschluss, Hitler turned his attention to Czechoslovakia. The Sudetenland — the western border region — was home to approximately 3 million ethnic Germans and contained Czechoslovakia’s vital border fortifications and much of its industrial capacity.

Hitler used the same tactics that had worked in Austria. He encouraged the Sudeten German Party, led by Konrad Henlein, to make increasingly extreme demands for self-government. When the Czechoslovak government attempted to negotiate, Hitler raised his demands further, creating a deliberately unresolvable crisis.

As war seemed imminent, Chamberlain flew to Germany three times in September 1938 to negotiate directly with Hitler. Each time, Hitler increased his demands. Finally, on 29 September 1938, a conference was called at Munich.

Key Fact — The Munich Agreement

The Munich Agreement was signed on 29 September 1938 by four leaders: Hitler, Chamberlain, Mussolini, and Daladier (France). It gave the Sudetenland to Germany. Crucially, Czechoslovakia and the USSR were not invited to the conference that decided their fate. Chamberlain returned to Britain and famously declared he had secured “peace for our time.”

Munich was controversial because it sacrificed a democratic nation — Czechoslovakia — without its consent. The Czech government was presented with the agreement as a fait accompli and told that if it refused, it would face Germany alone, without British or French support.

The USSR, which had a mutual defence treaty with Czechoslovakia, was deliberately excluded from the conference. This deepened Soviet suspicion that Britain and France were trying to direct Hitler’s aggression eastward — a suspicion that would later drive Stalin towards the Nazi-Soviet Pact.

Winston Churchill condemned the agreement in Parliament, declaring: “You were given the choice between war and dishonour. You chose dishonour, and you will have war.”

Exam Tip

Do not simply dismiss appeasement as cowardly or stupid. Examiners reward answers that explain the historical context — the trauma of WWI, genuine sympathy for German grievances, fear of communism, economic weakness, and the absence of American support. In 1938, appeasement seemed like both a moral and a pragmatic choice to many people. Show you understand why it was followed before you evaluate whether it was the right decision.

Conclusion

The policy of appeasement, born from the trauma of the First World War and sustained by a combination of fear, sympathy, and strategic calculation, allowed Hitler to achieve two of his greatest ambitions without firing a shot. The Anschluss united Austria with Germany, and the Munich Agreement handed over the Sudetenland — betraying a democratic nation, alienating the USSR, and convincing Hitler that the Western democracies would never stand up to him.

Key Takeaways

  • Appeasement had rational foundations: It was driven by WWI trauma, sympathy for German grievances, economic weakness, fear of communism, and the need to buy time for rearmament — not simply weakness or naivety.
  • Each concession made the next crisis worse: The Anschluss went unopposed, which emboldened Hitler over the Sudetenland. Munich then demonstrated that even a democratic nation’s territorial integrity could be bargained away without its consent.
  • Munich’s consequences extended beyond Czechoslovakia: Excluding the USSR from the conference deepened Soviet distrust of the Western powers and pushed Stalin towards an agreement with Hitler — a decision that would reshape the path to war.
