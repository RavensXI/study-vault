Locarno & the Kellogg-Briand Pact

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Growing Optimism in the Late 1920s

The mid-to-late 1920s saw growing optimism about peace. The Dawes Plan (1924) had stabilised Germany’s economy by providing US loans, ending the hyperinflation crisis. But it made Germany entirely dependent on American money — a weakness that would prove disastrous when the Depression hit. The biggest agreements of this period were made outside the League of Nations. The great powers trusted treaties between themselves more than they trusted the League’s collective security.

The Locarno Treaties (1925)

The Locarno Treaties were signed in October 1925 in Switzerland. For the first time since the war, Germany was voluntarily entering agreements rather than having terms forced on it.

The treaties created the “Spirit of Locarno” — a period of real warmth between former enemies. As a direct result, Germany joined the League of Nations in 1926.

But these agreements were made outside the League. This showed that the great powers trusted direct deals more than collective security. Germany’s refusal to guarantee its eastern borders hinted at future ambitions that would eventually shatter the peace.

The Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928)

The Kellogg-Briand Pact was signed in August 1928 — the peak of 1920s optimism. It was co-authored by US Secretary of State Frank Kellogg and French Foreign Minister Aristide Briand.

Key Fact — The Kellogg-Briand Pact
  • Signed by 62 nations, including Germany, France, and — importantly — the USA.
  • All signatories promised not to use war to settle disputes.
  • Fatal flaw: there was no enforcement mechanism. If a country broke the pact, nothing would happen.
  • It did not cover wars of self-defence — a loophole so big that any aggressor could claim they were defending themselves.

The USA — not even a League member — signed this pact. The most important diplomacy was happening outside Geneva, not within the League. If even peace agreements bypassed the League, what purpose did it serve?

The Disarmament Conference (1932–1934)

One of the League’s main aims was disarmament. Germany had been forced to disarm by the Treaty of Versailles. Other countries were supposed to follow — but none did. Britain wanted to cut spending but still had a huge empire to protect. France refused to reduce its weapons because it feared Germany.

In 1932, the League organised a World Disarmament Conference in Geneva. It was meant to be the next big step towards peace. Instead, it became a disaster.

Key Fact — The Disarmament Conference
  • Hitler pointed out that Germany was the only country that had actually disarmed — everyone else had broken their promise.
  • He demanded France reduce its army to Germany’s size. France refused.
  • Germany walked out of the conference — and then left the League of Nations (October 1933).
  • Behind the scenes, Hitler was secretly rearming Germany the whole time.
  • The conference collapsed with no agreement. France began to rearm and build border defences.

The failure of the Disarmament Conference was a turning point. The League had failed in one of its core missions. Countries now began rearming instead of disarming, and the optimism of the 1920s was well and truly over.

Timeline: From Optimism to Collapse (1924–1933)

1924

Dawes Plan

US loans stabilise Germany’s economy and reduce reparations payments. But Germany becomes dependent on American money.

October 1925

Locarno Treaties signed

Germany, France, and Belgium agree to respect their western borders. The Rhineland stays demilitarised. Britain and Italy act as guarantors.

September 1926

Germany joins the League of Nations

The goodwill from Locarno leads to Germany joining the League.

August 1928

Kellogg-Briand Pact signed

62 nations, including the USA, renounce war. But the pact has no enforcement and a huge self-defence loophole.

1932

Disarmament Conference begins

The League tries to get all nations to reduce their armies. Germany demands equal treatment. Talks stall.

October 1933

Germany leaves the League

Hitler walks out of the Disarmament Conference and withdraws Germany from the League. He secretly begins rearming.

Exam Tip

Locarno and Kellogg-Briand look like successes. A strong answer will argue they were actually signs of the League’s weakness — the key diplomacy happened outside the League, proving powerful nations did not trust collective security. Link this to the Disarmament Conference failure to show how optimism collapsed.

Conclusion

The 1920s agreements looked hopeful, but they all bypassed the League and lacked enforcement. When the Disarmament Conference collapsed and Germany walked out, the era of optimism was over.

Key Takeaways

  • Diplomacy bypassed the League: Locarno and Kellogg-Briand were negotiated outside the League, proving powerful nations did not trust collective security.
  • Germany’s eastern borders were left unguaranteed: Locarno secured western borders, but Germany refused to guarantee its borders with Poland and Czechoslovakia — a warning sign for the late 1930s.
  • The Disarmament Conference failed: The League could not deliver on one of its core aims. Hitler used the conference to claim victimhood, then left both the conference and the League while secretly rearming.
