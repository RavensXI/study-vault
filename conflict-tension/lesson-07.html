Locarno & the Kellogg-Briand Pact

Growing Optimism in the Late 1920s

The mid-to-late 1920s saw growing optimism about international peace. Remarkably, the most significant agreements of this period were made outside the League of Nations framework. While this seemed like good news at the time, it carried a troubling implication: the great powers trusted specific treaties negotiated between themselves more than they trusted the League’s system of collective security.

The Locarno Treaties (1925)

The Locarno Treaties were signed in October 1925 at Locarno, Switzerland. They represented a dramatic shift in European diplomacy — for the first time since the war, Germany was voluntarily entering into agreements rather than having terms imposed upon it.

Key Fact — The Locarno Treaties
  • Germany, France, and Belgium voluntarily promised to respect their shared western borders.
  • The demilitarised zone in the Rhineland was to remain permanently demilitarised.
  • Britain and Italy acted as guarantors — promising to take action if any signatory broke the agreement.
  • Crucially, Germany did NOT agree to respect its eastern borders with Poland and Czechoslovakia — a significant and ominous omission.

The treaties produced what became known as the “Spirit of Locarno” or the “Locarno Honeymoon” — a period of genuine warmth and cooperation between former enemies. As a direct result, Germany was invited to join the League of Nations in 1926, seemingly completing its return to the international community.

However, there was an important subtext. The fact that these agreements were made outside the League showed that the great powers trusted specific, negotiated treaties between themselves far more than they trusted the League’s collective security system. Furthermore, Germany’s refusal to guarantee its eastern borders hinted at future ambitions that would eventually shatter the peace.

The Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928)

The Kellogg-Briand Pact was signed in August 1928 and represented the high-water mark of 1920s optimism. It was co-authored by US Secretary of State Frank Kellogg and French Foreign Minister Aristide Briand.

Key Fact — The Kellogg-Briand Pact
  • Signed by 62 nations, including Germany, France, and — significantly — the USA.
  • All signatories promised not to use war as a means of settling international disputes.
  • Fatal flaw: there was absolutely no enforcement mechanism. If a country broke the pact, nothing would happen.
  • It did not cover wars of self-defence — a loophole so large that any aggressor could simply claim they were defending themselves.

The involvement of the USA — which was not even a member of the League of Nations — was both significant and revealing. It showed that the most important diplomacy of the era was happening outside Geneva, not within the League’s framework. If even peace agreements bypassed the League, what purpose did it truly serve?

Timeline: The Locarno Era (1925–1928)

October 1925

Locarno Treaties signed

Germany, France, and Belgium voluntarily agree to respect their shared western borders. The Rhineland is confirmed as a permanent demilitarised zone. Britain and Italy act as guarantors.

1925–1926

“Spirit of Locarno”

A period of diplomatic warmth and reconciliation. Former enemies cooperate openly. International optimism grows about lasting peace in Europe.

September 1926

Germany joins the League of Nations

As a direct result of the goodwill created by Locarno, Germany is invited to join the League, seemingly completing its return to the international community.

August 1928

Kellogg-Briand Pact signed

Sixty-two nations, including the USA, sign a pact renouncing war. Despite its symbolic importance, it has no enforcement mechanism and excludes wars of “self-defence.”

Exam Tip

Be prepared to explain the contradictory nature of these agreements. On the surface, Locarno and Kellogg-Briand look like peace successes. But a strong answer will argue they were actually signs of the League’s weakness — the most important diplomacy was happening outside the League, proving that powerful nations did not trust collective security to keep the peace.

Conclusion

The Locarno Treaties and the Kellogg-Briand Pact represented the high point of 1920s international cooperation. Yet their very existence outside the League of Nations framework highlighted a crucial, underlying problem that would prove fatal.

Key Takeaways

  • Diplomacy bypassed the League: The most significant peace agreements of the 1920s — Locarno and Kellogg-Briand — were negotiated outside the League, demonstrating that powerful nations trusted direct treaties between themselves more than the League’s collective security system.
  • Germany’s eastern borders were left unguaranteed: While Locarno secured the western borders, Germany deliberately refused to guarantee its borders with Poland and Czechoslovakia — a warning sign that would become catastrophically relevant in the late 1930s.
  • Promises without enforcement are meaningless: The Kellogg-Briand Pact was signed by 62 nations but had no enforcement mechanism and a self-defence loophole wide enough to drive an army through. Within a decade, signatories including Germany, Italy, and Japan would all launch wars of aggression.
