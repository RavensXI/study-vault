The Peacemakers & the 14 Points

0:00 / 0:00

The Big Three

The Treaty of Versailles was shaped by three leaders with very different goals. Understanding their clashing aims is key to explaining why the treaty ended up the way it did.

Key Fact

The “Big Three” were David Lloyd George (Britain), Georges Clemenceau (France), and Woodrow Wilson (USA). Germany was not invited. Although 32 nations attended the Paris Peace Conference from January 1919, and Italy made it a “Big Four,” Italy was virtually ignored — so in reality it was the Big Three who decided the treaty over six months of talks.

David Lloyd George (Britain)

David Lloyd George won re-election in 1918 using slogans like “Hang the Kaiser,” “Make Germany Pay,” and “squeeze the German lemon till the pips squeak.” Almost a million British soldiers had died, so the public demanded harsh punishment. Privately, though, Lloyd George knew that crippling Germany would hurt British trade — before the war, Germany had been one of Britain’s biggest trading partners. He was a practical deal-maker, caught between Clemenceau’s desire for revenge and Wilson’s idealism.

Lloyd George wanted to reduce the German Navy, which had threatened the British Empire. He also hoped to take over some German colonies. But he feared that pushing Germany too hard could drive it toward communism, as had happened in Russia in 1917.

He was suspicious of the League of Nations — he did not want it to have its own army, in case it gained power over Britain. He supported peace because it was good for business, but always looked for practical deals rather than grand ideals.

His middle-ground approach aimed to punish Germany enough to satisfy voters while keeping it strong enough to trade with — and to act as a buffer against the spread of Bolshevism from the east.

Georges Clemenceau (France)

Georges Clemenceau — nicknamed “The Tiger” — was 77 when the conference began. He had been just 29 when Germany invaded France in 1871, so in his lifetime Germany had invaded his homeland twice. His son was wounded in the war. He wanted revenge: high reparations, the return of Alsace-Lorraine, the Rhineland demilitarised, and ideally Germany broken up into smaller states.

Key Fact

France had suffered huge damage — much of the fighting on the Western Front took place on French soil. Over 1.4 million French soldiers were killed, and large areas of northern France were destroyed. Clemenceau wanted Germany to pay for all of it. He also wanted the League of Nations to have its own army to protect France from any future attack.

Woodrow Wilson (USA)

Woodrow Wilson was an idealist. The USA had only joined the war in 1917 and suffered around 100,000 casualties — far fewer than Britain or France — with no damage to American soil. Wilson had a poor opinion of empires and wanted a fair, “just” peace through his Fourteen Points. He worried that punishing Germany too harshly would only create a desire for revenge. However, American isolationism meant many at home wanted nothing to do with Europe’s problems.

Where the Big Three Disagreed

The Big Three clashed on almost every major issue:

Key Fact — Key Disagreements
  • Land: Clemenceau wanted to break Germany into smaller states. Wilson and Lloyd George blocked this, fearing it would cause future wars.
  • Reparations & trade: Clemenceau wanted to cripple Germany financially. Lloyd George wanted to keep Germany strong enough to buy British goods — Germany had been Britain’s second biggest trade partner before the war.
  • The League of Nations: Clemenceau wanted the League to have its own army to protect France. Lloyd George did not — he feared it would interfere with the British Empire. Wilson wanted an army to help keep the peace.
  • Disarmament: Wilson wanted all countries to disarm. Clemenceau only wanted Germany to disarm.
  • Self-determination: Wilson wanted every ethnic group to rule itself. Lloyd George feared this would threaten the British Empire. Both Britain and France used the idea to grab German colonies instead.

Wilson’s Fourteen Points

Published in January 1918, the Fourteen Points set out Wilson’s plan for lasting peace. The key ideas included:

Key Fact — The Fourteen Points (Summary)
  • 1. No secret treaties — all diplomacy to be open
  • 2. Free access to the seas in peace and war
  • 3. Free trade between countries
  • 4. Disarmament by all countries
  • 5. Colonies to have a say in their own future
  • 6–13. Specific territorial changes — Belgium independent, Alsace-Lorraine to France, self-determination in Eastern Europe, an independent Poland with sea access
  • 14. A League of Nations to settle disputes between countries

Wilson was widely seen as arrogant and too idealistic. Clemenceau mocked him: “Mr. Wilson bores me with his Fourteen Points; why, God Almighty has only Ten!” Wilson thought the USA had “won” the war and took the moral high ground, but he would not listen to others.

His biggest blind spot was self-determination. Eastern Europe’s ethnic groups were hopelessly mixed together — for example, Austria-Hungary alone had dozens of nationalities living side by side. Drawing neat borders along ethnic lines was impossible. Many Germans ended up living outside Germany, storing up problems for the future. Britain and France also had huge empires with many colonies, so self-determination threatened their own power too.

Reception of the Fourteen Points

When Wilson published his Fourteen Points in January 1918, Germany ignored them and kept fighting. But after defeat in November 1918, Germany accepted the Armistice expecting a fair peace based on those same points. The other leaders, however, had serious objections.

January 1918

Wilson publishes the Fourteen Points

Presented to the US Congress as a plan for peace. Germany ignores them and keeps fighting.

November 1918

Germany signs the Armistice

Germany surrenders expecting fair treatment based on the Fourteen Points.

January 1919

Paris Peace Conference begins

32 nations meet at Versailles. Clemenceau and Lloyd George challenge many of Wilson’s points. Germany is excluded.

June 1919

Treaty of Versailles signed

The final treaty is far harsher than Germany expected. Many of Wilson’s ideals are watered down or dropped.

Lloyd George rejected freedom of the seas because it threatened British naval power. Both he and Clemenceau knew self-determination threatened their own empires. Clemenceau saw the points as far too soft given the damage France had suffered.

Wilson arrived in Paris as a hero, but his position quickly weakened. Clemenceau was set on punishing Germany. Lloyd George needed to balance public demands for punishment with keeping trade alive.

Wilson gave up many of his ideals to secure the one thing he cared about most: the League of Nations. Ironically, the US Senate later refused to ratify the treaty, so America never joined the League.

Exam Tip

Abbreviate long terms after defining them once — e.g. write “Treaty of Versailles (ToV)” then use “ToV” throughout. Also, when explaining disagreements, always link each leader’s aim back to why they wanted it (e.g. Clemenceau’s demand for reparations came from France being invaded twice).

Conclusion

The Big Three each had different aims shaped by their own country’s experience of the war. Wilson’s Fourteen Points raised hopes of a fair peace, but Clemenceau and Lloyd George had other priorities. The result was a compromise that left nobody satisfied.

Key Takeaways

  • Every clause was a compromise: Clemenceau wanted revenge, Wilson wanted fairness, and Lloyd George wanted trade — each had to give ground, leaving all three unhappy.
  • Germany expected a fair peace but got a harsh one: Germany accepted the Armistice based on the Fourteen Points. The final treaty was far tougher, creating a lasting sense of betrayal.
  • Self-determination caused as many problems as it solved: Eastern Europe’s mixed ethnic groups made neat borders impossible, leaving millions of Germans outside Germany and storing up future conflict.
