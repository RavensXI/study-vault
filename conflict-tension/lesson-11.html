Remilitarisation of the Rhineland

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Hitler’s Boldest Gamble

On 7 March 1936, Hitler ordered German troops to march into the Rhineland. This was his boldest and most dangerous move yet — a direct violation of both the Treaty of Versailles and the Locarno Treaties of 1925.

What Was the Rhineland?

The Rhineland was Germany’s industrial heartland, located along the western border with France and Belgium. Under the Treaty of Versailles, it was designated a demilitarised zone — no German troops or fortifications were allowed there. This served as a vital buffer zone protecting France from a future German attack.

Key Fact — Why the Rhineland Mattered

Crucially, Germany had freely accepted the Rhineland’s demilitarised status in the Locarno Treaties of 1925. This meant Hitler could not use his usual argument that he was simply overturning an unfair Diktat imposed by the victors. Remilitarising the Rhineland was a violation of an agreement Germany had entered willingly.

The Gamble: 7 March 1936

Hitler sent a relatively small force of around 22,000 troops into the Rhineland. The German army was still weak — it was no match for the French military, which could have easily driven it back. Hitler himself acknowledged the enormous risk.

Key Fact — Hitler’s Own Admission

Hitler later admitted: “If the French had marched in, we would have had to withdraw with our tails between our legs.” He had given his generals standing orders to retreat immediately at the first sign of French military opposition.

Why Did Nobody Act?

Despite the blatant treaty violation, neither France, Britain, nor the League of Nations took any action to stop Hitler. Each had its own reasons for inaction, and together they amounted to a catastrophic failure.

France was in the best position to act but was paralysed by internal division. A general election was approaching, and no politician wanted to be responsible for starting a military confrontation. French military intelligence also significantly overestimated the size of the German force, believing it was far larger than it actually was.

Most importantly, France refused to act without British support. When Britain made clear it would not back military action, France felt it could not respond alone — despite having more than enough military strength to push the Germans out.

Many in Britain felt that Germany had the right to station troops in its own territory. The prevailing view was that Hitler was simply “marching into his own backyard” — a sentiment that reflected widespread sympathy for German grievances about the Treaty of Versailles.

The British public had no appetite for military confrontation over what seemed like a distant border dispute, and the government was not prepared to commit forces over what it privately considered an inevitable revision of an outdated treaty.

The League was still preoccupied with the ongoing Abyssinian Crisis and its failure to stop Mussolini’s invasion. It formally condemned Hitler’s action but was utterly powerless to enforce any response. The Rhineland crisis confirmed what Abyssinia had already demonstrated — the League could not stop determined aggressors.

Consequences of the Rhineland Remilitarisation

The failure to act over the Rhineland had profound and far-reaching consequences that shifted the balance of power in Europe decisively in Hitler’s favour.

Key Fact — Four Critical Consequences
  • Hitler’s prestige soared — His domestic position became virtually unchallengeable. German generals who had doubted him were silenced, and the German public celebrated.
  • The Treaty of Versailles was effectively dead — The Locarno Treaties were also proven worthless. If freely-agreed borders could be violated without consequence, no treaty had meaning.
  • Strategic disaster for France — Germany could now build the Siegfried Line of fortifications along the French border, making any future attack on Germany far more difficult and costly.
  • Hitler and Mussolini drew closer — Italy’s isolation after Abyssinia pushed Mussolini towards Hitler. By late 1936, they had formed the Rome-Berlin Axis, creating a dangerous alliance of fascist dictators.
Exam Tip

Explain why the Rhineland remilitarisation was a crucial turning point. It was arguably the last real opportunity to stop Hitler without a full-scale war. Had France acted, Germany’s weak army would have been forced to retreat, likely undermining Hitler’s authority. Instead, inaction emboldened him and made every subsequent crisis harder to manage.

Conclusion

The remilitarisation of the Rhineland was a calculated risk that paid off spectacularly for Hitler. It shattered the Treaty of Versailles’s remaining authority, proved the Locarno agreements worthless, and emboldened Hitler to pursue ever-more aggressive territorial demands. The failure of the Allies to respond when they still had overwhelming military superiority made it exponentially harder to contain Hitler in the years that followed.

Key Takeaways

  • A gamble, not a certainty: Hitler’s own generals feared the move and he ordered retreat at the first sign of resistance — this was not an unstoppable advance but a bluff that the Allies failed to call.
  • The last clear chance to stop Hitler cheaply: France had the military strength to push Germany back, and a French response would likely have humiliated Hitler and undermined his authority at home — but political division, overestimation of German strength, and British reluctance prevented action.
  • Consequences reshaped Europe: The Siegfried Line closed France’s window for easy intervention, the Rome-Berlin Axis created a fascist alliance, and Hitler learned that boldness would always be rewarded with Allied inaction.
