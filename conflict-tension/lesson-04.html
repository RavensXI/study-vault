The Wider Peace Settlement

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Beyond Versailles

The Treaty of Versailles dealt with Germany, but separate treaties were needed for each of Germany’s wartime allies. These settlements reshaped the map of Europe, broke up empires, and created new nations — but they also planted the seeds of future conflict.

September 1919

Treaty of St Germain — Austria

Broke up the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Austria lost land and its army was cut.

November 1919

Treaty of Neuilly — Bulgaria

Bulgaria lost territory and access to the Aegean Sea. Reparations set at £100 million.

June 1920

Treaty of Trianon — Hungary

Very harsh — Hungary lost over two-thirds of its territory.

August 1920

Treaty of Sèvres — Turkey

Broke up the Ottoman Empire. Middle Eastern lands were given to Allied powers as mandates.

July 1923

Treaty of Lausanne — Turkey (revised)

Replaced Sèvres after Turkey’s nationalist revolt. No reparations or military limits.

Key Fact — Time Pressure

The Big Three were under huge time pressure. After 1918, Allied armies began to demobilise (send soldiers home). The longer talks dragged on, the weaker the Allies’ bargaining position became — they had fewer troops to back up their demands.

Treaty of St Germain (Austria, 1919)

The Treaty of St Germain broke up the Austro-Hungarian Empire and imposed tough terms on the new, much smaller Austria.

Austria lost land to the new states of Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and Poland. Its army was cut to just 30,000 men.

Anschluss (union) with Germany was banned. This contradicted self-determination, since both countries were German-speaking.

Austria was left as a small, landlocked country with a broken economy, cut off from the industry and farmland it once relied on.

Treaty of Trianon (Hungary, 1920)

The Treaty of Trianon was the harshest of all the settlements. Hungary suffered huge territorial losses.

Hungary lost over two-thirds of its territory to Czechoslovakia, Romania, and Yugoslavia. About 3 million Hungarians ended up as minorities in foreign countries.

Its army was cut to 35,000 men. Hungary was left bitter and resentful, and it would push to overturn the settlement for decades.

Treaty of Neuilly (Bulgaria, 1919)

Bulgaria’s treaty was tough but less severe than Austria’s or Hungary’s.

Bulgaria lost land to Yugoslavia and Greece, and — crucially — lost its access to the Aegean Sea, which hurt trade.

The army was limited to 20,000 men, and Bulgaria was ordered to pay £100 million in reparations.

Treaty of Sèvres (Turkey, 1920) & Lausanne (1923)

The Treaty of Sèvres broke up the Ottoman Empire. Middle Eastern lands were handed to Allied powers as mandates. The Allies also took control of the Dardanelles Straits, a vital shipping route to the Black Sea. Turkey’s response was unique among the defeated nations.

Key Fact — Turkey’s Challenge

The harsh terms of Sèvres sparked a nationalist uprising led by Mustafa Kemal Atatürk. Turkish forces fought to reclaim lost territory, forcing the Allies to negotiate a replacement treaty. The Treaty of Lausanne (1923) was significantly fairer: Turkey regained most of its land, faced no military restrictions, and paid no reparations.

Turkey is the only defeated nation that fought back and overturned its treaty by force. This showed the peace settlement was fragile — when challenged, the Allies backed down.

New States & New Problems

The peace settlement created new nations, including Poland, Czechoslovakia, and Yugoslavia. This looked like self-determination in action — but the reality was far messier.

Key Fact — Problems with New States
  • Minority populations — millions of Germans, Hungarians, and other ethnic groups were placed under foreign rule, creating tensions
  • Economic weakness — new states inherited broken infrastructure and lacked established trade networks
  • Political instability — inexperienced governments struggled to manage diverse, sometimes hostile populations
Exam Tip

Show the Allies applied self-determination selectively. Millions of Germans ended up under Polish or Czech rule, and Austria was banned from joining Germany. Pointing out this hypocrisy shows strong analysis in exam answers.

Conclusion

The wider peace settlement spread the flaws of Versailles across all the defeated powers. Austria, Hungary, and Bulgaria were left weakened and bitter. New states were plagued by ethnic tensions. Turkey’s military challenge showed the whole settlement could be overturned.

Key Takeaways

  • Self-determination was applied inconsistently: Millions of Germans and Hungarians ended up as minorities in foreign states. Austria was banned from joining Germany despite both being German-speaking.
  • Turkey proved the settlement could be challenged: Atatürk’s revolt forced the Allies to replace Sèvres with the much softer Lausanne treaty — showing force could overturn the peace.
  • New states inherited old problems: Poland, Czechoslovakia, and Yugoslavia were born with ethnic divisions, weak economies, and inexperienced governments — creating flashpoints for future conflict.
