The Wider Peace Settlement

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Beyond Versailles

While the Treaty of Versailles dealt with Germany, separate treaties were needed for each of Germany’s wartime allies. These settlements reshaped the map of Europe, dismantling empires and creating new nations — but they also sowed the seeds of future instability.

September 1919

Treaty of St Germain — Austria

Dissolved the Austro-Hungarian Empire and imposed military and territorial penalties on Austria.

November 1919

Treaty of Neuilly — Bulgaria

Stripped Bulgaria of territory and access to the Aegean Sea, with £100 million in reparations.

June 1920

Treaty of Trianon — Hungary

Particularly harsh — Hungary lost over two-thirds of its territory.

August 1920

Treaty of Sèvres — Turkey

Dismantled the Ottoman Empire and distributed Middle Eastern territories as mandates.

July 1923

Treaty of Lausanne — Turkey (revised)

Replaced Sèvres after Turkey’s nationalist uprising. A fairer settlement with no reparations or military limits.

Treaty of St Germain (Austria, 1919)

The Treaty of St Germain formally dissolved the Austro-Hungarian Empire and imposed severe terms on the newly independent Austria.

Austria lost substantial territory to the newly created states of Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and Poland. Its army was limited to 30,000 men.

Anschluss with Germany was explicitly forbidden — a decision that contradicted the principle of self-determination, since both nations were predominantly German-speaking.

The result was a small, landlocked country with a shattered economy, stripped of the industrial and agricultural resources it had relied upon as part of the larger empire.

Treaty of Trianon (Hungary, 1920)

The Treaty of Trianon was particularly harsh. Hungary suffered some of the most severe territorial losses of any defeated nation.

Hungary lost over two-thirds of its territory to Czechoslovakia, Romania, and Yugoslavia. Approximately 3 million Hungarians found themselves living as minorities in foreign countries.

The army was limited to 35,000 men. The result was a bitter, resentful nation that would seek revision of the settlement for decades to come.

Treaty of Neuilly (Bulgaria, 1919)

Bulgaria’s treaty was harsh but less severe than those imposed on Austria and Hungary.

Bulgaria lost territory to Yugoslavia and Greece, and crucially lost its access to the Aegean Sea, damaging trade prospects.

The army was limited to 20,000 men, and Bulgaria was ordered to pay £100 million in reparations.

Treaty of Sèvres (Turkey, 1920) & Lausanne (1923)

The Treaty of Sèvres dismantled the Ottoman Empire, distributing Middle Eastern lands as mandates to Allied powers. However, Turkey’s response was unique among the defeated nations.

Key Fact — Turkey’s Challenge

The harsh terms of Sèvres sparked a nationalist uprising led by Mustafa Kemal Atatürk. Turkish forces fought to reclaim lost territory, forcing the Allies to negotiate a replacement treaty. The Treaty of Lausanne (1923) was significantly fairer: Turkey regained most of its land, faced no military restrictions, and paid no reparations.

Turkey remains the only example of a defeated nation successfully challenging and overturning its treaty terms by force. This demonstrated the fragility of the entire peace settlement — when challenged with determination, the Allies backed down.

New States & New Problems

The peace settlement created several new nations, including Poland, Czechoslovakia, and Yugoslavia. While this appeared to fulfil the principle of self-determination, the reality was far messier.

Key Fact — Problems with New States
  • Minority populations — millions of Germans, Hungarians, and other ethnic groups were placed under foreign rule, creating tensions
  • Economic weakness — new states inherited broken infrastructure and lacked established trade networks
  • Political instability — inexperienced governments struggled to manage diverse, sometimes hostile populations
Exam Tip

Critically evaluate the principle of self-determination. The Allies applied it selectively — millions of Germans were placed under Polish or Czech rule, and Austria was forbidden from uniting with Germany. Highlighting this hypocrisy demonstrates sophisticated analysis in your exam answers.

Conclusion

The wider peace settlement extended the flaws of Versailles across the defeated powers. Treaties left Austria, Hungary, and Bulgaria weakened and resentful, new states were plagued by ethnic tensions and economic fragility, and Turkey’s successful military challenge exposed the settlement’s vulnerability to resistance.

Key Takeaways

  • Self-determination was applied inconsistently: The principle that peoples should choose their own government was selectively enforced — millions of Germans and Hungarians ended up as minorities in foreign states, and Anschluss was forbidden despite popular desire for union.
  • Turkey proved the settlement could be challenged: Atatürk’s nationalist uprising forced the Allies to replace Sèvres with the far more lenient Lausanne treaty, demonstrating that the post-war order could be overturned by determined resistance.
  • New states inherited old problems: Poland, Czechoslovakia, and Yugoslavia were born with ethnic divisions, economic weakness, and political inexperience — creating potential flashpoints for future conflict rather than stable new democracies.
