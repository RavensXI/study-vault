Lesson 9 of 15

The Abyssinian Crisis

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Fatal Blow

If Manchuria wounded the League, Abyssinia killed it. This was not a distant conflict in Asia — it was a major European power invading a fellow League member, right on Europe’s doorstep. The League’s failure proved that collective security was dead.

Background: Mussolini’s Ambitions

Italy’s fascist dictator, Mussolini, dreamed of building a new Roman Empire. Abyssinia (modern-day Ethiopia) was a prime target. It was the only independent black country in Africa and sat between two Italian colonies (Eritrea and Italian Somaliland). It was rich in resources. Mussolini also wanted revenge for Italy’s humiliating defeat by Abyssinia at the Battle of Adwa in 1896.

Key Fact

In December 1934, Italian and Abyssinian troops clashed at the Wal-Wal oasis on the border with Italian Somaliland. This gave Mussolini his excuse. Emperor Haile Selassie appealed to the League for help. In June 1935, he spoke at the League in Geneva, warning of what would happen if they failed to act.

The League’s Response — and Failure

Britain and France faced a dilemma. They were worried about Hitler’s Germany and wanted to keep Mussolini as an ally against the Nazi threat. Protecting Abyssinia risked pushing Italy towards Germany. They chose self-interest over the League’s principles.

In April 1935, Britain, France, and Italy formed the Stresa Front — agreeing to oppose Hitler. But they deliberately avoided any mention of Abyssinia.

Mussolini took this silence as a green light. If Britain and France would not even raise the issue at a conference, they clearly would not stop him. He was right.

In October 1935, Italy launched a full invasion of Abyssinia, using tanks, aircraft, and even poison gas against poorly equipped Abyssinian forces. The League imposed economic sanctions — but they were deliberately weak:

  • The sanctions did NOT include oil, coal, or steel — the very resources Italy needed for war. Mussolini warned that banning oil would be an “act of war.”
  • The Suez Canal — controlled by Britain and the main route for Italian ships — was left open. Closing it would have crippled Italy’s supply lines.
  • Non-League members like the USA continued trading with Italy.

Britain also acted out of self-interest: a speech in the House of Commons warned that banning coal exports to Italy would cost 30,000 British miners their jobs. The sanctions were designed to look like action without actually hurting Italy.

The Hoare-Laval Pact (December 1935)

The worst moment came in December 1935. The Hoare-Laval Pact was drawn up in secret by British Foreign Secretary Samuel Hoare and French Prime Minister Pierre Laval.

Key Fact — The Hoare-Laval Pact

The plan would give Mussolini two-thirds of Abyssinia in exchange for stopping the war. When it was leaked to the press, there was a huge public outcry. Both Hoare and Laval were forced to resign. This was the ultimate betrayal — the League’s two leading members had secretly tried to reward an aggressor by carving up a fellow member state.

The Outcome

Italy conquered Abyssinia in May 1936 and then left the League. Emperor Haile Selassie went into exile and made a famous speech to the League Assembly: “It is us today. It will be you tomorrow.”

Rome-Berlin Axis formed

Mussolini allies with Hitler. Two aggressive dictators are now partners.

Exam Tip

If comparing Manchuria and Abyssinia, argue Abyssinia was more damaging. In Manchuria, the League could claim the crisis was too far away. In Abyssinia, a permanent Council member broke the Covenant — and Britain and France actively undermined the League through the Hoare-Laval Pact. This was not just failure; it was betrayal from within. For “Write an account” questions, show how the crisis escalated: Italy invaded, the League’s sanctions were useless, and then Hoare-Laval made it even worse.

Conclusion

The Abyssinian Crisis ended the League as a serious force. Britain and France put self-interest first, sanctions were deliberately useless, and the Hoare-Laval Pact proved the League’s own leaders did not believe in it. After Abyssinia, countries rearmed and formed their own alliances instead.

Key Takeaways

  • Self-interest over the League: Britain and France wanted Mussolini as an ally against Hitler and sacrificed Abyssinia to keep him. The Hoare-Laval Pact proved the League’s leaders did not believe in collective security.
  • Useless sanctions: Oil, coal, and steel were excluded. The Suez Canal stayed open. Even when the League acted, its actions were designed to fail.
  • Devastating knock-on effects: Mussolini allied with Hitler (Rome-Berlin Axis). Hitler remilitarised the Rhineland while the world was distracted. Countries lost faith, rearmed, and formed secret alliances. The path to war was wide open.
