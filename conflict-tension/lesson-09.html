Lesson 9 of 15

The Abyssinian Crisis

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Fatal Blow

If the Manchurian Crisis severely wounded the League of Nations, Abyssinia was the fatal blow. This was not a distant conflict in the Far East — it was a clear case of a large European nation invading a smaller League member state, right on Europe’s doorstep. The League’s failure to stop it proved beyond doubt that collective security was dead.

Background: Mussolini’s Ambitions

Italy’s fascist dictator, Mussolini, dreamed of building a new Roman Empire. Abyssinia (modern-day Ethiopia) was a tempting target: it sat between two existing Italian colonies in East Africa (Eritrea and Italian Somaliland), it was rich in natural resources, and conquering it would avenge Italy’s humiliating defeat at the Battle of Adwa in 1896.

Key Fact

In December 1934, a clash between Italian and Abyssinian troops at the Wal-Wal oasis on the border with Italian Somaliland gave Mussolini the pretext he needed. Emperor Haile Selassie appealed to the League of Nations for help, setting the stage for the League’s greatest failure.

The League’s Response — and Failure

Britain and France — the League’s most powerful members — faced a dilemma. They were growing increasingly worried about Hitler’s Germany and desperately wanted to keep Mussolini as an ally against the Nazi threat. Protecting Abyssinia risked pushing Italy towards Germany.

In April 1935, Britain, France, and Italy met at Stresa in northern Italy to form a united front against Hitler’s violations of the Treaty of Versailles. The resulting “Stresa Front” condemned German rearmament — but deliberately avoided any mention of Abyssinia.

Mussolini interpreted this silence as a green light. If Britain and France were not prepared to raise the issue even at a diplomatic conference, he concluded they would not act to stop him. He was right.

In October 1935, Italy launched a full-scale invasion of Abyssinia. The League responded by imposing economic sanctions on Italy — but the sanctions were deliberately weak:

  • The sanctions did NOT include oil, steel, or coal — the very resources Italy needed to wage war.
  • The Suez Canal — controlled by Britain and the main route for Italian ships to East Africa — was left open.
  • Non-League members like the USA continued trading with Italy.

The sanctions were, in effect, designed to look like action while ensuring they did not actually hurt Italy enough to provoke Mussolini into breaking with Britain and France.

The Hoare-Laval Pact (December 1935)

The most damaging episode of the entire crisis came in December 1935, when the Hoare-Laval Pact was drawn up in secret by British Foreign Secretary Samuel Hoare and French Prime Minister Pierre Laval.

Key Fact — The Hoare-Laval Pact

The plan proposed giving Mussolini two-thirds of Abyssinia in exchange for ending the war. When the details were leaked to the press, there was a massive public outcry. Both Hoare and Laval were forced to resign. The pact was the ultimate betrayal — two leading League members had secretly tried to reward an aggressor by carving up a fellow League member state.

The Outcome

Italy completed its conquest of Abyssinia in May 1936. Emperor Haile Selassie was forced into exile and made a famous, impassioned speech to the League Assembly, warning: “It is us today. It will be you tomorrow.”

Key Fact — Consequences
  • The League’s credibility was completely destroyed. No nation could trust it to protect them from aggression.
  • Mussolini, angered by the League’s half-hearted sanctions, was pushed towards Hitler. The two dictators formed the Rome-Berlin Axis in October 1936.
  • While the world was distracted by the Abyssinian crisis, Hitler remilitarised the Rhineland in March 1936 — a direct violation of the Treaty of Versailles and the Locarno Treaties — and nobody stopped him.

Hoare-Laval Pact leaked

March 1936

Hitler remilitarises the Rhineland

While the world is distracted by Abyssinia, Hitler sends troops into the demilitarised Rhineland. No one takes action to stop him.

May 1936

Italy conquers Abyssinia

Abyssinia falls. Haile Selassie goes into exile. The League’s credibility is destroyed.

October 1936

Rome-Berlin Axis formed

Mussolini, pushed away from Britain and France by the crisis, forms an alliance with Hitler’s Germany.

Exam Tip

If asked to compare the Manchurian and Abyssinian crises, argue that Abyssinia was more significant. In Manchuria, the League could claim the crisis was too far away. In Abyssinia, a major European League member invaded a fellow member state — and the League’s two leading powers (Britain and France) actively undermined its response through the Hoare-Laval Pact. This was not just failure; it was betrayal from within.

Conclusion

The Abyssinian Crisis was the final nail in the League’s coffin. It proved beyond doubt that Britain and France’s national interests would always come before collective security, and that the League was powerless to protect its own members from aggression. After Abyssinia, the League was irrelevant.

Key Takeaways

  • Britain and France chose self-interest over collective security: They wanted Mussolini as an ally against Hitler and were willing to sacrifice Abyssinia — a fellow League member — to keep him on side. The Hoare-Laval Pact was the ultimate proof that the League’s leading members did not believe in its principles.
  • Sanctions without teeth are meaningless: The League imposed sanctions but deliberately excluded the resources Italy actually needed (oil, steel, coal) and left the Suez Canal open. This demonstrated that even when the League acted, its actions were designed to be ineffective.
  • The crisis had devastating knock-on effects: It pushed Mussolini into alliance with Hitler (the Rome-Berlin Axis), distracted the world while Hitler remilitarised the Rhineland, and destroyed any remaining faith in the League. The path to World War Two was now wide open.
