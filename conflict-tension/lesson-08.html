Lesson 8 of 15

The Manchurian Crisis

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Great Depression

The Wall Street Crash of October 1929 triggered the worst economic crisis the world had seen. Its effects spread from the USA and wrecked economies everywhere, destroying the optimism of the 1920s almost overnight.

Key Fact — Global Impact of the Depression
  • Trade collapsed as countries turned to protectionism — putting up tariffs to protect their own industries. America stopped trading with much of the world, so the Depression spread.
  • Mass unemployment hit every major economy. In Germany, over 6 million were unemployed by 1932.
  • Rise of extremism: Desperate people supported leaders like Hitler and the Japanese military, who promised simple solutions — restore national pride and take over more land.

The Depression crippled the League. Countries turned inward. They were too busy dealing with their own problems — unemployment, poverty — to support the League. Nations refused to impose economic sanctions on aggressors because they feared hurting their own fragile economies. Britain and France slashed military spending, leaving them unable to back up the League with force.

The Manchurian Crisis (1931–1933)

This was the League’s first big crisis involving a major member. It ended in total failure and fatally damaged the League’s reputation.

Background: Japan and the Depression

Japan was an island nation dependent on trade, so the Depression hit it hard. The powerful Japanese military argued that the answer was territorial expansion — seizing resource-rich land in Asia. Their target was Manchuria, a region of north-eastern China rich in coal, iron, and farmland. China itself was unstable and divided, making it an easier target.

The Mukden Incident (September 1931)

In September 1931, the Japanese army staged an explosion on the South Manchuria Railway near Mukden. They blamed Chinese saboteurs and used this fake incident as an excuse for a full-scale invasion. By early 1932, Japan controlled the whole of Manchuria. In February 1932, Japan also bombed the Chinese city of Shanghai.

Key Fact

Japan renamed the territory “Manchukuo” and set up a puppet state — placing the former Chinese emperor Pu Yi on the throne as a figurehead. Japanese officials made all the real decisions.

The League’s Response

China appealed to the League. The response was painfully slow and completely useless:

The League sent the Lytton Commission to investigate. Lord Lytton travelled to China by boat, which delayed things even further. The report took over a year to produce — by which time Japan had tightened its grip on Manchuria.

The Lytton Report (October 1932) said Japan had acted unlawfully. The League voted 42 to 1 to accept the report — Japan was the only vote against.

Japan’s response was devastating. It walked out of the League (March 1933) and then invaded Jehol, another region of China. The League imposed no sanctions and no military action. Without the USA and USSR, economic sanctions would have been meaningless anyway — Japan’s main trade was with America. Only the USA or USSR had the resources to actually stop Japan, and neither was in the League.

Japan saw things differently. It wanted to build an empire, but felt Britain and France were blocking it. This seemed very hypocritical — Britain and France already had huge empires of their own. Japan felt it was unfair and insulting to be ordered around by the League. Britain also wanted to keep good relations with Japan and avoided taking strong action.

Why Did the League Fail?

Timeline: From Crash to Crisis (1929–1933)

Wall Street Crash

The US stock market collapses, triggering a worldwide depression. Trade falls and millions lose their jobs.

Depression deepens

Nations turn to protectionism. Japan’s economy is hit hard. The military pushes for expansion.

Mukden Incident

Japan stages an explosion on its own railway and blames China. It uses this as an excuse to invade Manchuria.

Exam Tip

For “Write an account” questions on Manchuria, show how the crisis got worse step by step: Japan staged the Mukden Incident, the League was too slow (Lytton), Japan ignored the ruling, walked out, and invaded Jehol. Explain why it was a crisis — a permanent Council member broke the Covenant, and the League could do nothing. Link to wider consequences: it set a precedent that encouraged Mussolini and Hitler.

Conclusion

The Manchurian Crisis proved a powerful League member could commit open aggression and face no real consequences. It set a precedent — the League could be ignored. Mussolini and Hitler watched closely.

Key Takeaways

  • The Depression killed cooperation: Countries turned inward and refused to impose sanctions or risk war. Without the USA and USSR, the League had no real power to act.
  • The League was too slow: The Lytton Commission took over a year. By the time the League voted, Manchuria was lost. Japan then invaded Jehol too, proving the League could not respond to fast-moving crises.
  • A green light for aggressors: Japan defied the League and got away with it. This encouraged Mussolini (Abyssinia, 1935) and Hitler (Rhineland, 1936). The pattern of unchecked aggression began here.
