The Manchurian Crisis

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Great Depression

The Wall Street Crash of October 1929 triggered the worst economic crisis the modern world had ever seen. Its effects rippled outward from the United States and devastated economies across the globe, destroying the fragile optimism of the “Locarno era” almost overnight.

Key Fact — Global Impact of the Depression
  • International trade collapsed as countries introduced protectionism — imposing tariffs and trade barriers to protect their own industries.
  • Mass unemployment hit every major economy. In Germany, unemployment reached over 6 million by 1932.
  • Rise of extremism: Economic desperation fuelled the rise of the Nazis in Germany and empowered the Japanese military, who demanded territorial expansion.

The Depression had a devastating impact on the League of Nations. International cooperation — the League’s lifeblood — evaporated as countries turned inward. Nations became reluctant to impose economic sanctions on aggressors because they feared damaging their own fragile economies. Britain and France, the League’s most important members, cut military spending drastically, leaving them unable and unwilling to back up the League’s decisions with force.

The Manchurian Crisis (1931–1933)

The Manchurian Crisis was the first major test of the League by one of its own leading members. It would prove to be a catastrophic failure that fatally undermined the League’s credibility.

Background: Japan and the Depression

Japan’s economy was devastated by the Depression. As an island nation dependent on trade, the collapse of international markets and the rise of protectionism hit Japan particularly hard. The Japanese military, which had enormous political influence, argued that territorial expansion in Asia was the solution — and their eyes turned to Manchuria, a resource-rich region of north-eastern China.

The Mukden Incident (September 1931)

In September 1931, the Japanese army staged an explosion on the South Manchuria Railway near the city of Mukden. They blamed Chinese saboteurs and used this fabricated incident as a pretext for launching a full-scale invasion of Manchuria. By early 1932, Japan had conquered the entire region.

Key Fact

Japan renamed the conquered territory “Manchukuo” and installed a puppet state — placing the last Chinese emperor, Pu Yi, on the throne as a figurehead while Japanese officials controlled everything behind the scenes.

The League’s Response

China appealed to the League for help. The League’s response was painfully slow and ultimately ineffective:

The League sent the Lytton Commission to investigate — but the commission took over a year to produce its report, during which time Japan continued to consolidate its hold on Manchuria.

The Lytton Report, published in October 1932, concluded that Japan had acted unlawfully. In February 1933, the League Assembly voted 42 to 1 to accept the report — Japan cast the only dissenting vote.

Japan’s response was devastating: it simply walked out of the League and formally withdrew in March 1933. The League imposed no economic sanctions and took no military action. Manchuria was too far away, and Britain and France — dealing with their own economic crises — were completely unwilling to risk a war in the Far East.

Exam Tip

The Manchurian Crisis is a topic that could be asked about specifically in the exam. It is also an excellent example to use in any question about the League’s failure — it demonstrates every weakness (slow response, no sanctions, no military action, powerful members acting with impunity). Learn the key dates and the sequence of events thoroughly.

Conclusion

The Manchurian Crisis proved that a powerful League member could commit blatant aggression and face no real consequences. It sent a clear message to Mussolini and Hitler: the League was a “paper tiger” — impressive on paper but powerless in practice.

Key Takeaways

  • The Depression destroyed international cooperation: The economic crisis made nations turn inward, unwilling to impose sanctions or risk military confrontation. The League’s entire system of collective security depended on cooperation that no longer existed.
  • The League was fatally slow: The Lytton Commission took over a year to report, during which Japan consolidated its conquest. By the time the League voted, Manchuria was already lost — proving the League could not respond to fast-moving crises.
  • A green light for future aggressors: Japan’s success in defying the League without consequence emboldened Mussolini (who would invade Abyssinia in 1935) and Hitler (who would begin remilitarising Germany). The 1930s pattern of unchecked aggression began here.
