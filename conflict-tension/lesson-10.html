Hitler’s Aims & Rearmament

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Hitler Becomes Chancellor

In January 1933, Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany. From the start, he pursued an aggressive foreign policy based on ideas from Mein Kampf. His goal was to overturn the post-war settlement, expand German territory, and make Germany the dominant power in Europe.

Hitler’s Foreign Policy Aims

Hitler’s foreign policy had four linked aims, all from Mein Kampf. Together, they made a clash with the Treaty of Versailles unavoidable.

First Steps: 1933–1935

Between 1933 and 1935, Hitler took careful steps to test the post-war settlement. Each move pushed his aims forward while avoiding a fight Germany was too weak to win.

October 1933

Withdrawal from the League of Nations

At the Disarmament Conference, Hitler demanded that other countries disarm to Germany’s level — or that Germany be allowed to rearm to match France. When France refused, he pulled Germany out of the Conference and the League, blaming Allied hypocrisy.

January 1934

Non-Aggression Pact with Poland

This looked peaceful, but Hitler was buying time to rearm. The pact also weakened the alliance between France and Poland, reducing the threat of a two-front war.

July 1934

Failed Anschluss with Austria

Hitler encouraged Austrian Nazis to stage a coup. They murdered Chancellor Dollfuss, but the attempt failed when Mussolini moved Italian troops to the Austrian border as a warning. Hitler backed down — he was not ready for a confrontation.

January 1935

Saar Plebiscite

The Saar — a German region rich in coal and steel, run by the League since 1919 — held a vote on its future. The result was 477,000 to 48,000 in favour of rejoining Germany (over 90%). This was a legal reversal of the Treaty and a huge propaganda victory for Hitler.

March 1935

Rearmament & Conscription Announced

Hitler openly announced conscription, plans for a 550,000-strong army, and the existence of the Luftwaffe (air force) — all direct violations of the Treaty of Versailles.

The Allied Response: Stresa Front to Naval Agreement

Hitler’s rearmament alarmed the Allies, but their response was weak and divided. In April 1935, Britain, France, and Italy formed the Stresa Front — condemning German rearmament and pledging to uphold the Treaty.

Key Fact — The Anglo-German Naval Agreement

Just two months later, Britain destroyed the Stresa Front. In June 1935, Britain signed the Anglo-German Naval Agreement, allowing Germany a navy up to 35% of the Royal Navy — including submarines. Britain did not consult France or Italy before signing. This shattered the united front and accepted German rearmament as a fact.

Britain believed German rearmament was happening anyway, so it was better to limit it through agreement. The government also felt some of the Treaty’s military terms had been too harsh.

The result was a diplomatic disaster. France was furious at not being consulted. Italy felt betrayed. The Stresa Front — the only united opposition to Hitler — collapsed within weeks. Hitler learned the Allies could be split apart.

Caution and Aggression: Hitler’s Strategy

Hitler’s early foreign policy mixed defiance with peaceful gestures. Each aggressive step — leaving the League, rearming, attempting Anschluss — was balanced by moves that looked reasonable, like the Polish pact and the Saar vote.

Hitler tested the Allies at each stage. When the 1934 Anschluss failed and Mussolini resisted, Hitler backed down at once. But when Britain signed the Naval Agreement, it showed the Allies would make deals rather than confront him.

This pattern — probe, retreat if resisted, advance if unchallenged — defined Hitler’s approach through the 1930s. Each success made the next gamble easier.

Historians disagree about Hitler’s foreign policy. A.J.P. Taylor argued Hitler was an opportunist who did not plan a war but took advantage of chances created by Britain and France’s mistakes.

Other historians, such as Hugh Trevor-Roper, argued Hitler had a long-term plan for conquest. German historian Andreas Hillgruber called it the Stufenplan — a step-by-step programme that led to war in 1939.

Exam Tip

Describe Hitler’s early foreign policy as a mix of caution and aggression. Show he calculated each move — retreating when opposed (1934 Anschluss) and pushing forward when the Allies showed weakness (rearmament, Naval Agreement). You can also use the historian debate: was Hitler a planner (Trevor-Roper) or an opportunist (Taylor)?

Conclusion

Between 1933 and 1935, Hitler began taking apart the Treaty of Versailles. He mixed open defiance with peaceful gestures that divided the Allied response. The Anglo-German Naval Agreement shattered the Stresa Front and convinced Hitler the democracies lacked the will to stop him.

Key Takeaways

  • Clear aims from the start: Abolishing the Treaty, Lebensraum, uniting German-speakers, and defeating communism were all in Mein Kampf. His actions after 1933 followed this blueprint.
  • Caution enabled aggression: “Peaceful” moves like the Polish pact and the Saar vote gave diplomatic cover for the aggressive steps that followed.
  • Allied disunity was his greatest asset: Britain signed the Naval Agreement without consulting France or Italy, destroying the Stresa Front and showing Hitler the democracies could be split apart.
