Lesson 10 of 15

Hitler’s Aims & Rearmament

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Hitler Becomes Chancellor

In January 1933, Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany. From the very start, he pursued a clear and aggressive foreign policy shaped by the ideas he had set out years earlier in Mein Kampf. His ultimate goal was to overturn the post-war settlement, expand German territory, and establish Germany as the dominant power in Europe.

Hitler’s Foreign Policy Aims

Hitler’s foreign policy rested on four interconnected ambitions, all rooted in the ideology of Mein Kampf. Together, they pointed towards an inevitable confrontation with the Treaty of Versailles and the European order it had created.

Key Fact — Hitler’s Four Aims
  • Abolish the Treaty of Versailles — This was his central and most popular aim. The Treaty was hated across Germany, and destroying it would unite the nation behind him.
  • Expand German territory — Lebensraum — Hitler believed Germany needed “living space” in Eastern Europe, to be taken from Poland and the USSR.
  • Unite all German-speaking people — He aimed to create a Greater Germany by absorbing German-speaking populations in Austria, Czechoslovakia, and Poland.
  • Defeat Communism — Hitler viewed the USSR as Germany’s ultimate enemy. Destroying communism and seizing Soviet land were inseparable goals.

First Steps: 1933–1935

Between 1933 and 1935, Hitler took a series of calculated steps to test the boundaries of the post-war settlement. Each move was designed to advance his aims while avoiding a direct military confrontation that Germany was not yet strong enough to win.

October 1933

Withdrawal from the League of Nations

At the Disarmament Conference, Hitler proposed that all nations should disarm to Germany’s level. When France refused, he used this as justification to pull Germany out of both the Conference and the League itself — presenting the move as a response to Allied hypocrisy.

January 1934

Non-Aggression Pact with Poland

A cunning diplomatic move. On the surface it looked peaceful and reasonable, but Hitler was actually buying time while Germany rearmed. The pact also weakened the alliance between France and Poland, reducing the threat of a two-front war.

July 1934

Failed Anschluss with Austria

Hitler encouraged Austrian Nazis to stage a coup. They murdered Chancellor Dollfuss, but the attempt failed when Mussolini — at that time protective of Austrian independence — moved Italian troops to the Austrian border as a warning.

January 1935

Saar Plebiscite

The people of the Saar — administered by the League of Nations since 1919 — voted overwhelmingly (over 90%) to rejoin Germany. This was a legitimate, democratic result and a major propaganda victory for Hitler, appearing to validate his claim that German-speaking peoples wanted to be united.

March 1935

Rearmament & Conscription Announced

At the “Freedom to Rearm” rally, Hitler openly announced the reintroduction of conscription, plans for an army of 550,000 men, and the existence of the Luftwaffe — all in direct violation of the Treaty of Versailles.

The Allied Response: Stresa Front to Naval Agreement

Hitler’s announcement of rearmament alarmed the Allied powers, but their response was fatally inconsistent. In April 1935, Britain, France, and Italy formed the Stresa Front — condemning German rearmament and pledging to uphold the Treaty of Versailles.

Key Fact — The Anglo-German Naval Agreement

Just two months after the Stresa Front was formed, Britain undermined it completely. In June 1935, Britain signed the Anglo-German Naval Agreement, allowing Germany to build a navy up to 35% of the size of the Royal Navy — including submarines. By negotiating directly with Hitler, Britain had effectively accepted German rearmament and shattered the united front it had just created.

Britain believed that since German rearmament was happening regardless, it was better to control and limit it through agreement than to try to prevent it. The British government also felt that some of the Treaty’s military restrictions had been unfairly harsh, and that allowing Germany a modest navy was a reasonable concession.

However, the decision was a diplomatic disaster. France was furious at not being consulted. Italy felt betrayed. The Stresa Front — the only unified opposition to Hitler — collapsed almost immediately. Hitler learned that the Allies could be divided and that bold action carried few consequences.

Caution and Aggression: Hitler’s Strategy

What made Hitler’s early foreign policy so effective was the way he combined defiance with peaceful gestures. Each aggressive step — leaving the League, rearming, attempting Anschluss — was balanced by actions that appeared reasonable or conciliatory, such as the Non-Aggression Pact with Poland and the Saar plebiscite.

Hitler tested the Allies’ willingness to act at each stage. When the failed Anschluss of 1934 showed that Mussolini was willing to resist, Hitler backed down immediately — he was not yet ready for confrontation. But when Britain signed the Naval Agreement, it proved that the Allies were willing to make concessions rather than confront him.

This pattern — probe, retreat if resisted, advance if unchallenged — would define Hitler’s approach throughout the 1930s. Each success made the next gamble easier, and each failure of Allied resolve made the next act of defiance bolder.

Exam Tip

Characterise Hitler’s early foreign policy as a deliberate mix of caution and aggression. In exam answers, show that he was not reckless — he calculated each move carefully, retreating when opposed (the failed 1934 Anschluss) and pressing forward when the Allies showed weakness (rearmament after the Stresa Front collapsed).

Conclusion

Between 1933 and 1935, Hitler began systematically dismantling the Treaty of Versailles. He combined open defiance — leaving the League, rearming, reintroducing conscription — with carefully calculated peaceful gestures that confused and divided the Allied response. The weak and inconsistent reaction of Britain and France, culminating in the Anglo-German Naval Agreement that shattered the Stresa Front, convinced Hitler that the democracies lacked the will to stop him.

Key Takeaways

  • Hitler’s aims were clear from the start: Abolishing the Treaty, expanding into Eastern Europe for Lebensraum, uniting German-speakers, and defeating communism were all laid out in Mein Kampf — his actions after 1933 followed this blueprint methodically.
  • Caution enabled aggression: Each “peaceful” move — the Polish pact, the Saar plebiscite — created diplomatic cover for the aggressive steps that followed, making it harder for the Allies to justify a strong response.
  • Allied disunity was Hitler’s greatest asset: The Anglo-German Naval Agreement proved that Britain would negotiate with Hitler rather than confront him, destroying the Stresa Front and teaching Hitler that the democracies could be divided and exploited.
