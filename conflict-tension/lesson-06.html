The League in the 1920s

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Mixed Fortunes for the League

The 1920s were a mixed decade for the League. It settled some disputes and did great humanitarian work. But it also exposed serious weaknesses — especially when powerful nations were involved.

The League’s Successes

The League resolved several territorial disputes in the early 1920s. This showed collective security could work — at least when the nations involved were small enough to accept the League’s rulings.

The Åland Islands sit in the Baltic Sea between Sweden and Finland. Both claimed the islands, and the dispute threatened to turn violent.

The League ruled in favour of Finland. Sweden was disappointed but accepted the decision. A genuine success — the League resolved a dispute without a shot being fired.

Upper Silesia was a valuable industrial region on the Germany–Poland border. Both nations claimed it, and the Treaty of Versailles had not settled the issue.

The League surveyed the area and organised a plebiscite (public vote). The result was close, so the League split the region based on how each area voted — making sure both sides kept good rail links and water supplies. Germany was unhappy but accepted the outcome. Another success.

In 1925, Greek soldiers were killed in a border clash with Bulgaria. Greece responded by invading Bulgarian territory. Bulgaria asked the League for help.

The League acted fast: it condemned Greece and ordered a withdrawal. Greece obeyed. On the surface, another success.

But Greece bitterly complained of double standards. Just two years earlier, Italy had invaded the Greek island of Corfu in very similar circumstances — and the League did nothing to stop Italy. The League was only tough on weaker nations. This complaint would prove prophetic.

Humanitarian Work

Beyond settling disputes, the League did hugely valuable humanitarian work through its specialist commissions. This is often overlooked but represents some of the League’s greatest achievements.

Key Fact — Humanitarian Achievements
  • Refugee Commission: Returned about 400,000 prisoners of war to their home countries after World War One.
  • Health Organisation: Led global campaigns against malaria and leprosy, saving countless lives.
  • International Labour Organisation (ILO): Improved working conditions worldwide, including banning lead paint and limiting child labour. In 1930 it helped Greece set up social insurance for the unemployed. However, its 1919 attempt to ban children under 14 from working was ignored by most members.
  • Slavery Commission: Freed about 200,000 slaves in Sierra Leone and fought forced labour across Africa and the Middle East.
  • Opium Board: Worked to reduce the drug trade worldwide.

The League’s Failures

The League’s failures in the 1920s were fewer than its successes, but far more serious. Each involved a powerful nation defying the League — exposing the fatal weakness at the heart of collective security.

Vilna (modern-day Vilnius) was Lithuania’s historic capital. It had a large Polish-speaking population, and in 1920 a Polish army seized it by force.

Lithuania appealed to the League. The League condemned Poland and ordered a withdrawal — but Poland refused. France saw Poland as a key ally against Germany and would not take real action. Without French support, the League was powerless.

A major failure. The League made a ruling, a member ignored it, and nothing happened. It showed the League’s sanctions and moral authority meant nothing if powerful members would not enforce them.

In August 1923, an Italian general and four of his team were murdered while surveying the Greek–Albanian border for the League. Italy’s leader, Mussolini, blamed Greece and demanded heavy compensation.

When Greece refused, Mussolini bombarded and occupied the Greek island of Corfu. Greece appealed to the League. The League condemned Italy, but Mussolini refused to accept the League’s authority.

The dispute was handed to the Conference of Ambassadors — a powerful group of countries (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) that met separately from the League. It sided with Italy, forcing Greece to apologise and pay up. Mussolini in particular used this body to bully the League into doing what he wanted.

A disastrous failure. It proved powerful nations could ignore the League and get their way through intimidation. The Conference of Ambassadors undermined the League’s authority — it showed the League was not the all-powerful body it was meant to be.

Exam Tip

Learn one success (e.g. Upper Silesia) and one failure (e.g. Corfu) in detail. Being able to explain what happened and why it mattered for the League will prepare you for any question on this topic. Compare the two to show the League only worked against weaker nations.

Conclusion

The 1920s showed the League could do good work with humanitarian projects and smaller disputes. But Vilna and Corfu exposed cracks that would widen badly in the 1930s.

Key Takeaways

  • Success with small nations, failure with powerful ones: The League settled disputes between weaker countries (Aaland Islands, Upper Silesia, Bulgaria). But when major powers like Italy or France were involved, no one enforced the rules against them.
  • Valuable humanitarian work: Commissions returned 400,000 POWs, fought diseases, freed 200,000 slaves in Sierra Leone, and improved working conditions — though some reforms (like the ILO’s child labour ban) were ignored by members.
  • Corfu set a dangerous precedent: Mussolini defied the League and the Conference of Ambassadors sided with him. This sent a clear signal: aggressive nations could bully their way to victory. Japan and Italy would exploit this lesson in the 1930s.
