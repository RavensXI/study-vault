Lesson 14 of 15

The Outbreak of War

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Final Target: Poland

With the Nazi-Soviet Pact securing his eastern flank, Hitler turned on Poland. He demanded the return of Danzig (a “Free City” under the League of Nations with a majority German population) and the end of the Polish Corridor — the strip of land separating East Prussia from the rest of Germany.

Poland stood firm. Unlike Czechoslovakia, Poland had a clear guarantee from Britain and France. After Hitler broke his promise over Czechoslovakia, they had committed to defending Poland. Hitler gambled they would back down again. This time, he was wrong.

The Gleiwitz Incident

On 31 August 1939, German SS troops staged a Gleiwitz Incident — a false flag operation. Nazis dressed as Polish soldiers attacked several radio and communications centres on the German border near Gleiwitz. They left behind the body of a concentration camp prisoner in a Polish uniform to make it look real.

Key Fact

The Gleiwitz Incident was a manufactured pretext. Hitler needed an excuse to claim Germany was acting in “self-defence.” Every detail was planned by the SS under orders from Berlin. The invasion had been prepared weeks in advance.

The Invasion of Poland

On 1 September 1939, Germany invaded Poland using Blitzkrieg — “Lightning War.” This new form of warfare combined speed, technology, and overwhelming force. Yet again, Hitler claimed Germany was acting in self-defence.

Blitzkrieg used coordinated air and ground attacks. Panzers (tanks) smashed through Polish defences in armoured columns, while Stukas (dive-bombers) destroyed communications and supply lines from the air.

The Polish army, though brave, was overwhelmed. Much of it relied on horse-mounted cavalry and outdated equipment — no match for German armour and air power. The speed of advance meant Poland could not organise an effective defence.

On 17 September, the Soviet Red Army invaded Poland from the east — as agreed in the Nazi-Soviet Pact’s secret clause. Caught between two armies, Poland was hopeless. Warsaw surrendered on 27 September. Poland was divided between Germany and the USSR.

Britain and France Declare War

On 3 September 1939, Britain and France both gave ultimatums to Germany: leave Poland or face war. They had promised to do this after Hitler broke his word over Czechoslovakia.

Germany ignored the threat. At 11:15 am on 3 September 1939, Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain addressed the nation by radio:

Key Fact — Chamberlain’s Broadcast

“I am speaking to you from the Cabinet Room at 10 Downing Street. This morning the British Ambassador in Berlin handed the German Government a final note stating that, unless we heard from them by 11 o’clock that they were prepared at once to withdraw their troops from Poland, a state of war would exist between us. I have to tell you now that no such undertaking has been received, and that consequently this country is at war with Germany.”

France declared war later that day. The guarantee to Poland had been honoured. After a decade of tension, failed diplomacy, and appeasement, Europe was at war again. It took just four weeks to defeat Poland.

Timeline: The Outbreak of War

The Gleiwitz Incident

SS troops dress as Polish soldiers and attack German border targets. Hitler uses this as a pretext to claim “self-defence.”

1 September 1939

Germany invades Poland

Blitzkrieg is unleashed. Panzers and Stukas devastate Polish defences.

3 September 1939

Britain and France declare war

Both give ultimatums: leave Poland or face war. Germany ignores them. At 11:15 am, Chamberlain announces Britain is at war. France follows that day.

17 September 1939

Soviet Union invades eastern Poland

The Red Army attacks from the east, as agreed in the Nazi-Soviet Pact. Poland is caught between two invaders.

27 September 1939

Warsaw surrenders

After four weeks, the Polish capital falls. Poland is divided between Germany and the USSR.

Exam Tip

When assessing who caused the war, focus on individuals’ roles. Hitler was the most direct cause — he planned the invasion, manufactured the pretext, and gave the order. But also show how the Nazi-Soviet Pact made it possible by removing the two-front war threat. The best answers weigh both immediate and enabling causes.

Conclusion

The invasion of Poland was the direct cause of World War Two. Blitzkrieg crushed Poland in four weeks. Britain and France honoured their guarantee, ending a decade of tension and appeasement with a declaration of war.

Key Takeaways

  • Gleiwitz was a calculated lie: Hitler manufactured a pretext to justify invasion. The attack on Poland was premeditated — key evidence when assessing blame for the war.
  • Blitzkrieg changed warfare: Panzers, Stukas, and motorised infantry overwhelmed Poland in four weeks, shocking the world with Germany’s military power.
  • Britain and France finally acted: The guarantee to Poland was honoured — but years of appeasement had let Hitler grow far stronger than he might have been.
