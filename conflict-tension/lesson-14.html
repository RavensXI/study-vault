The Outbreak of War

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Final Target: Poland

With the Nazi-Soviet Pact securing his eastern flank, Hitler was ready to turn on Poland. He demanded the return of Danzig (a “Free City” under the League of Nations with a majority German population) and the abolition of the Polish Corridor — the strip of land that separated East Prussia from the rest of Germany.

Poland stood firm. Unlike Czechoslovakia, Poland had a clear guarantee from Britain and France that they would come to its defence. Hitler gambled that — as with previous crises — the Western powers would back down. This time, he was wrong.

The Gleiwitz Incident

On 31 August 1939, German SS troops staged a Gleiwitz Incident — a false flag operation. German soldiers dressed in Polish uniforms attacked a German radio station near the border. They even left behind the body of a concentration camp prisoner dressed as a Polish soldier to make it look authentic.

Key Fact

The Gleiwitz Incident was a manufactured pretext — Hitler needed an excuse to present the invasion as a defensive response to “Polish aggression.” In reality, every detail was orchestrated by the SS under orders from Berlin. The invasion plan had been prepared weeks in advance.

The Invasion of Poland

On 1 September 1939, Germany launched a devastating invasion of Poland using Blitzkrieg — “Lightning War.” This was a new form of warfare that combined speed, technology, and overwhelming force.

Blitzkrieg relied on coordinated attacks from air and ground. Panzers (tanks) smashed through Polish defences in concentrated armoured columns, while Stukas (dive-bombers) destroyed communications, supply lines, and troop concentrations from the air.

The Polish army, though brave, was overwhelmed. Much of its forces relied on horse-mounted cavalry and outdated equipment, which stood no chance against German armoured divisions and air power. The speed of the German advance meant Poland could not organise an effective defence.

On 17 September, the Soviet Red Army invaded Poland from the east — honouring the secret clause of the Nazi-Soviet Pact. Caught between two invading armies, Poland’s position was hopeless. Warsaw surrendered on 27 September. Poland was divided between Germany and the USSR.

Key Fact — Poland’s Fate

Poland was crushed in just four weeks. The country ceased to exist as an independent state, divided between Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union exactly as the secret protocol of the Nazi-Soviet Pact had stipulated. It would not regain full independence until 1989.

Britain and France Declare War

On 2 September 1939, Britain and France issued an ultimatum to Germany: withdraw all forces from Poland by 11:00 am on 3 September, or Britain and France would declare war.

Hitler ignored the deadline. At 11:15 am on 3 September 1939, Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain addressed the nation by radio:

Key Fact — Chamberlain’s Broadcast

“I am speaking to you from the Cabinet Room at 10 Downing Street. This morning the British Ambassador in Berlin handed the German Government a final note stating that, unless we heard from them by 11 o’clock that they were prepared at once to withdraw their troops from Poland, a state of war would exist between us. I have to tell you now that no such undertaking has been received, and that consequently this country is at war with Germany.”

France declared war on Germany later the same day. The guarantee to Poland had been honoured. After a decade of tension, failed diplomacy, and appeasement, Europe was once again at war.

Timeline: The Outbreak of War

31 August 1939

The Gleiwitz Incident

German SS troops stage a false flag attack on a German radio station, dressing as Polish soldiers to manufacture a pretext for invasion.

1 September 1939

Germany invades Poland

Blitzkrieg is unleashed. Panzers and Stukas devastate Polish defences. Hitler claims it is a defensive response to “Polish aggression.”

2 September 1939

Britain and France issue ultimatum

Germany is given until 11:00 am on 3 September to withdraw from Poland or face war. Hitler ignores the demand.

3 September 1939

Britain and France declare war

At 11:15 am, Chamberlain announces Britain is at war with Germany. France follows later that day. World War Two has begun.

17 September 1939

Soviet Union invades eastern Poland

The Red Army crosses the Polish border from the east, fulfilling the secret clause of the Nazi-Soviet Pact. Poland is caught between two invaders.

27 September 1939

Warsaw surrenders

After four weeks of fighting, the Polish capital falls. Poland is divided between Germany and the USSR.

Exam Tip

When assessing responsibility for the outbreak of war, focus on key individuals’ roles. Hitler’s aggression was the primary and most direct cause — he planned the invasion, manufactured the pretext, and launched the attack. But also consider how Stalin’s decision to sign the Nazi-Soviet Pact made the invasion possible by removing the threat of a two-front war. The best answers weigh both immediate and enabling causes.

Conclusion

The invasion of Poland was the direct and immediate cause of World War Two. Blitzkrieg and the Soviet invasion from the east sealed Poland’s fate within four weeks. Britain and France honoured their guarantee, ending a decade of tension, failed collective security, and appeasement with a declaration of war.

Key Takeaways

  • The Gleiwitz Incident was a calculated lie: Hitler manufactured a pretext to justify the invasion, showing that the attack on Poland was premeditated aggression, not a defensive response — this is crucial evidence when assessing blame for the outbreak of war.
  • Blitzkrieg changed warfare: The coordinated use of Panzers, Stukas, and motorised infantry overwhelmed Poland in four weeks — a speed of conquest that shocked the world and demonstrated Germany’s terrifying military capability.
  • Britain and France finally acted: After years of appeasement and broken promises, the guarantee to Poland was honoured — but the delay in confronting Hitler had allowed him to grow far stronger than he might otherwise have been.
