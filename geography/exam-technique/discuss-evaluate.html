9 marks (+3 SPaG)

“Discuss…” / “To what extent…” / “Evaluate…”

Used in: Paper 1 & Paper 2

What the Examiner Wants

This is the highest-value question on the paper and the one that separates grade boundaries. The examiner wants you to weigh up both sides of an argument using detailed case study knowledge, then reach a supported conclusion. You also earn up to 3 marks for spelling, punctuation and grammar (SPaG), so write clearly and use geographical terminology correctly.

Level Descriptors

Level Marks What You Need
Level 3 7–9 Thorough and detailed discussion of both sides. Accurate, specific geographical knowledge with case study evidence. A clear, supported conclusion that weighs up the arguments.
Level 2 4–6 Some discussion of both sides with partial development. General knowledge used but may lack specific case study detail. Conclusion may be present but not well-supported.
Level 1 1–3 Basic or one-sided statements with limited geographical knowledge. Points listed without development. No conclusion or a vague one.

SPaG Marks

Marks What You Need
3 Accurate spelling, punctuation and grammar throughout. Geographical terminology used correctly and consistently.
2 Mostly accurate with occasional errors. Some geographical terminology used correctly.
1 Some accuracy but frequent errors. Limited use of geographical terminology.

Step-by-Step Formula

  1. 1
    Plan (2 minutes) — Jot down arguments FOR and AGAINST (or advantages/disadvantages). Note specific case study facts, statistics, and place names you will use.
  2. 2
    Paragraph 1: One side — Make 2–3 developed points supporting one side of the argument. Use specific case study evidence and chains of reasoning.
  3. 3
    Paragraph 2: The other side — Start with “However…” or “On the other hand…” Make 2–3 equally developed counter-arguments with evidence.
  4. 4
    Conclusion — Start with “Overall…” or “In conclusion…” State your judgement clearly and justify it by referring back to the strongest evidence. Do not sit on the fence — commit to a position.

Timing

Plan Side 1 Side 2 Conclude

About 15 minutes total. 2 minutes planning, 5 minutes on each side, 3 minutes on the conclusion. This is the question where time management matters most.

Three-part structure

Side 1: “There is strong evidence to suggest that [statement]. For example, [case study detail]. This shows that [developed reasoning]. Furthermore, [second point with evidence].”

Side 2: “However, it could also be argued that [counter-argument]. In [named place], [specific evidence]. This suggests that [reasoning]. Additionally, [second counter-point].”

Conclusion: “Overall, I believe that [clear judgement] because [strongest reason]. While [acknowledge the other side], the evidence from [case study] shows that [final justification].”

‘Soft engineering is always the best approach to managing coastal erosion.’ Do you agree? Use evidence to support your answer. [9 marks + 3 SPaG]

Level 3 (9/9 + 3 SPaG)

Soft engineering has several advantages over hard engineering when it comes to managing coastal erosion. Beach nourishment, for example, replaces sediment lost by longshore drift, creating a wider beach that absorbs wave energy and protects the cliffs behind it. At Lyme Regis in Dorset, sand was pumped onto the beach as part of a £22 million coastal protection scheme, and this has been effective at reducing wave impact on the seafront. Soft engineering also tends to look more natural and maintains the aesthetic appeal of the coastline, which is important for tourism-dependent areas.

However, soft engineering is not always the best option. Beach nourishment is temporary — storms can strip away imported sand within a single winter, meaning the process must be repeated regularly at significant cost. Hard engineering methods such as sea walls, while more expensive initially (£5,000–£10,000 per metre), provide a permanent physical barrier against wave attack. At Lyme Regis itself, a new sea wall was built alongside the beach nourishment because soft engineering alone was not sufficient to protect the town. Groynes trap sediment and slow longshore drift, extending the life of beaches far more reliably than nourishment alone.

Overall, I do not fully agree with the statement. While soft engineering is often more sustainable and environmentally sensitive, it is rarely sufficient on its own for areas where settlements and infrastructure are at serious risk. The most effective approach, as demonstrated at Lyme Regis, is to use an integrated strategy that combines both hard and soft engineering. The best method depends on the value of the land being protected, the budget available, and the severity of the erosion threat.

Examiner: 9/9 + 3 SPaG — Both sides thoroughly discussed with specific case study evidence (Lyme Regis, costs). Clear chains of reasoning. A strong, justified conclusion that doesn’t simply agree or disagree but evaluates. Geographical terminology used accurately throughout.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Only arguing one side — A one-sided answer cannot reach Level 3. Even if you strongly agree, you must discuss counter-arguments.
  • ❌
    No conclusion — The conclusion is where you demonstrate evaluation and judgement. Without it, you cap yourself at Level 2.
  • ❌
    Sitting on the fence — “Both sides have good points” is not a conclusion. Commit to a clear position and justify it.
  • ❌
    Ignoring SPaG — 3 marks are awarded for spelling, punctuation and grammar. Use full sentences, check your spelling of key terms, and write neatly.
