1 mark

“Define…” / “What is meant by…”

Used in: Paper 1 & Paper 2

What the Examiner Wants

The simplest question on the paper. The examiner wants a clear, precise definition of a geographical term in one sentence. Some 1-mark questions are multiple choice instead — circle the correct answer and move on.

Marking

Marks What You Need
1 A clear, accurate definition that shows understanding of the term
0 Incorrect, vague, or no definition given

Step-by-Step Formula

  1. 1
    Read the term carefully — Make sure you know exactly which term they want. Don’t confuse similar terms (e.g. “weather” vs “climate”, “erosion” vs “weathering”).
  2. 2
    Write one clear sentence — State what the term means using precise geographical language. Aim for 10–20 words.
  3. 3
    Move on immediately — This is worth 1 mark. Don’t waste time adding examples or extra detail. Spend no more than 1 minute.

Timing

Define — 1 min

One minute maximum. Write the definition and move on. Every extra second here is time stolen from higher-mark questions.

Standard definition

“[Term] is [what it means].”

“[Term] refers to [what it means].”

Process-type term

“[Term] is the process by which [what happens].”

What is meant by the term ‘natural hazard’? [1 mark]

Full mark

A natural hazard is a natural event that poses a risk to people and property.

Examiner: 1/1

Define the term ‘urbanisation’. [1 mark]

0 marks — too vague

When people live in cities.

Full mark

Urbanisation is the increasing proportion of a country’s population living in urban areas.

Examiner: “People live in cities” doesn’t show the idea of growth or increasing proportion, which is the key concept.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Being too vague — “It’s when rocks break down” is not enough for ‘weathering’. You need: “The breakdown of rock in situ (in place) by physical, chemical or biological processes.”
  • ❌
    Confusing similar terms — Erosion vs weathering, weather vs climate, immigration vs migration. Read the question carefully.
  • ❌
    Writing too much — One clear sentence is all you need. Save your time for the 6-mark and 9-mark questions.
