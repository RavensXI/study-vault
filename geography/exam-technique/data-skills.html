1–4 marks

Map, Graph & Data Skills

Used in: Paper 1 & Paper 2

What the Examiner Wants

AQA Geography exams include questions that test your ability to read and interpret geographical data. These can involve OS maps, graphs and charts (bar, line, pie, climate graphs), photographs, and statistical data. The examiner wants you to extract information accurately and describe patterns using evidence from the resource.

Common Data Skills Questions

Type Marks What You Do
OS map reading 1–4 Give grid references (4 or 6 figure), measure distance, identify features, describe relief
Graph/chart reading 1–3 Read values, identify trends, compare data sets, describe patterns
Calculations 1–2 Percentages, percentage change, mean, median, mode, range, interquartile range
Photo interpretation 2–4 Identify features, describe land use, explain processes visible in the photo

Key Skills to Practise

  1. 1
    Grid references — 4-figure: give the bottom-left corner of the square (along the corridor, up the stairs). 6-figure: estimate tenths within the square for greater precision. Always go along first, then up.
  2. 2
    Distance — Use the scale bar on the map. For curved routes, use a piece of string or paper edge to follow the path, then measure against the scale.
  3. 3
    Describing trends — Always state the general trend first (“overall increase”), then add specific data (“from 20% in 1990 to 65% in 2020”), then note any anomalies (“there was a slight dip in 2008”).
  4. 4
    Calculations — Always show your working. If asked for a percentage change: (new − old) ÷ old × 100. If your calculation is wrong but your method is correct, you can still earn method marks.

Timing

Data skills — 1 min per mark

The standard rule: roughly 1 minute per mark. A 3-mark graph question should take about 3 minutes. Don’t rush — read the resource carefully before writing.

  • Contour lines close together = steep slope. Far apart = gentle slope or flat land.
  • Learn common map symbols: PH (public house), Mus (museum), blue areas (water), green areas (woodland).
  • When describing relief, use compass directions: “The land rises steeply to the north-west, reaching 200m above sea level.”
  • For cross-sections, plot heights at regular intervals and join with a smooth line.
  • Always read the axes — check the units and scale before reading values.
  • Use data in your answer — “The temperature increased” scores less than “The temperature increased from 15°C in January to 28°C in July.”
  • For climate graphs: bars = rainfall (mm, left axis), line = temperature (°C, right axis).
  • When comparing two data sets, use connective phrases: “In contrast…”, “Similarly…”, “Whereas…”

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Not using data from the resource — If the question gives you a graph, table or map, you must reference specific values from it. Don’t just describe what you remember from class.
  • ❌
    Getting grid references backwards — Remember: along the corridor first, then up the stairs. Easting before northing.
  • ❌
    Not showing working in calculations — Even if your final answer is wrong, you can earn method marks if your working is correct. Always write out your calculation steps.
