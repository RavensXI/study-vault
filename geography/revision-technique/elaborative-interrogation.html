Elaborative Interrogation

Ask “why?” and “how?” to build chains of reasoning

What the Research Says

Elaborative interrogation means asking yourself “why is this true?” or “how does this work?” for every fact you learn. Instead of just memorising that tropical storms form over warm oceans, you ask WHY warm water is needed, which leads you to understand evaporation, rising air, condensation, and energy release. This deeper processing creates stronger, more connected memories — exactly what you need for 6-mark and 9-mark Geography questions.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Elaborative interrogation rated MODERATE utility — highly effective when combined with retrieval practice Best used alongside other techniques
Pressley et al. (1987) Students who asked “why?” during study recalled 72% more than those who simply read Questioning dramatically improves recall
Craik & Lockhart (1972) Levels of processing theory — deeper processing creates stronger memories than shallow repetition Understanding beats memorising

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Start with a fact — Take any Geography statement from your notes. For example: “Tropical storms only form over oceans with a surface temperature above 27°C.”
  2. 2
    Ask “why?” — Why does the ocean need to be 27°C? Because warm water evaporates rapidly, providing the moisture and energy the storm needs.
  3. 3
    Ask “what happens next?” — The warm, moist air rises rapidly. As it rises, it cools. Water vapour condenses, releasing latent heat. This heat energy fuels the storm further, creating a positive feedback loop.
  4. 4
    Build the chain — Keep asking “why?” and “so what?” until you reach the end of the process. Write the chain out as a connected sequence of because/therefore statements.
  5. 5
    Link to other topics — Can you connect this chain to something else? Tropical storms link to climate change (warmer oceans = more intense storms), which links to resource management and economic development.

One elaboration chain takes about 15 minutes. Do 2–3 per session. This technique is especially valuable for topics you find confusing — the “why?” questioning forces you to understand rather than just memorise.

The chain of reasoning

Fact: Tropical storms form over oceans with sea temperatures above 27°C.
↓ Why?
Because warm water evaporates rapidly, creating large amounts of warm, moist air above the ocean surface.
↓ What happens next?
This air rises rapidly because it is warm and less dense than the surrounding air (convection).
↓ Then what?
As it rises, it cools. Water vapour condenses to form towering cumulonimbus clouds.
↓ Why does that matter?
Condensation releases latent heat, which warms the surrounding air further, causing it to rise even faster — a positive feedback loop.
↓ So what?
This creates an area of very low pressure at the surface. Surrounding air rushes in. The Coriolis effect causes this air to spin (anticlockwise in the Northern Hemisphere).
↓ And the result?
A tropical storm forms with an eye (calm, descending air), an eye wall (fastest winds, heaviest rain), and spiral rain bands.

Why this helps in the exam: A 6-mark “explain the formation of tropical storms” question requires exactly this chain of reasoning. Each “why/because” step earns marks. Students who only memorise “they form over warm water” without the chain stop at Level 1.

Physical vs Human Geography link

Fact: The 2010 Haiti earthquake (magnitude 7.0) killed 316,000 people. The 2011 Christchurch earthquake (magnitude 6.3) killed 185.
↓ Why such a big difference?
Haiti is an LIC (GDP per capita ~$800). Buildings were poorly constructed — many made of concrete blocks without reinforcement.
↓ Why does that matter?
Unreinforced buildings collapse easily during shaking, trapping and killing people inside. New Zealand has strict building codes — steel-framed, flexible structures that absorb seismic energy.
↓ What about the response?
Haiti had limited emergency services — no national fire service, few hospitals with earthquake-resistant design. New Zealand had trained search-and-rescue teams deployed within hours.
↓ What about preparation?
New Zealand has earthquake drills, early warning systems, and public education. Haiti had none of these due to lack of government funding.
↓ So the conclusion is?
Vulnerability is determined by wealth, governance, and infrastructure — not just the physical event. This is why the same magnitude earthquake can have dramatically different impacts.

Exam link: This chain of reasoning is perfect for a 9-mark (+3 SPaG) “to what extent” question about the impact of tectonic hazards. It links physical and human geography, uses two specific case studies, and builds a sustained argument.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Stopping after one “why?” — One explanation is not a chain. Keep going until you reach 4–5 steps. The depth is the point.
  • ❌
    Only using this for Physical Geography — Elaborative interrogation works just as well for Human Geography. Why does urbanisation cause problems? Why does globalisation benefit some countries more than others?
  • ❌
    Not writing it down — Thinking through a chain in your head is less effective than writing it out. The act of writing forces clearer, more precise reasoning.
  • ❌
    Not linking to other topics — The best Geography answers connect ideas across topics. After building a chain, always ask: “What other topic does this link to?”
