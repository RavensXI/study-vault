Summarising

Knowledge Organisers

Condense each topic onto one page

What the Research Says

A knowledge organiser is a single-page summary of everything you need to know about one topic. It forces you to select, prioritise, and organise information — all of which strengthen understanding. The act of creating one from memory is itself a powerful retrieval exercise. For Geography, where every topic has key terms, processes, case study facts, and statistics, a well-made knowledge organiser gives you a complete revision tool on one sheet.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Dunlosky et al. (2013) Summarising rated LOW utility as a passive technique — but active summarisation from memory is far more effective Create from memory, not by copying
Chi et al. (1994) Self-explanation — students who organised and explained material to themselves learned more deeply Organising information aids understanding
Fiorella & Mayer (2016) Generative learning — creating your own summaries produces better retention than studying pre-made ones Making it yourself is the point

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Choose one topic — Pick a single topic such as “Tropical Rainforests” or “Tectonic Hazards”. One topic per page — no more.
  2. 2
    Create from memory first — Before opening any notes, write down everything you know about the topic. This is a brain dump in an organised format. Use sections: Key Terms, Processes, Case Study, Statistics, Diagram.
  3. 3
    Fill the sections — Key terms with definitions. Key processes explained in 1–2 sentences each. One case study with location, causes, effects (social, economic, environmental), and responses. 3–5 key statistics. One diagram or sketch map.
  4. 4
    Check and fill gaps — Open your notes and add anything you missed in a different colour. These gaps tell you what to focus on next time.
  5. 5
    Use it for retrieval — In future sessions, try to recreate the knowledge organiser from memory. Each time, you should need to check less.

One knowledge organiser takes about 25 minutes. Aim to create one per topic over the course of your revision. By exam time, you should have a complete set that you can recreate from memory.

Key Terms

Biodiversity — the variety of plant and animal species in an area. Nutrient cycling — the continuous recycling of nutrients between living organisms, soil, and biomass. Deforestation — the permanent removal of trees. Subsistence farming — farming to feed a family, not to sell. Selective logging — only cutting certain trees to reduce environmental damage.

Climate

Hot and wet all year. Average temperature: 26–28°C. Rainfall: over 2,000mm per year (some areas 3,000mm+). No distinct seasons. Found between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn.

Structure & Adaptations

Four layers: emergent (40m+), canopy (30m), under-canopy (15m), shrub layer (ground). Plant adaptations: drip tips (shed water), buttress roots (support in thin soil), lianas (climb to reach light). Animal adaptations: tree frogs (suction pads), toucans (large bills crack nuts), sloths (camouflage, slow metabolism).

Case Study: Amazon Deforestation

Location: Brazil, South America. Causes: cattle ranching (80% of cleared land), soya farming, logging, mining, road building (Trans-Amazonian Highway). Effects: loss of 17% of rainforest in last 50 years, increased CO₂ emissions, soil erosion, loss of indigenous communities, species extinction. Management: CITES (protects endangered species), debt-for-nature swaps, ecotourism, selective logging, international pressure (Paris Agreement).

Key Statistics

Rainforests cover 6% of Earth’s surface but contain 50% of species. Amazon produces 20% of world’s oxygen. 1.6 billion people depend on forests for their livelihood. Brazil lost 11,088 km² of forest in 2020.

Fit this on one page: Use a grid layout with boxes for each section. Include a small sketch of the rainforest layers on the right-hand side. The discipline of fitting everything onto a single A4 sheet forces you to select the most important information.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Copying straight from notes — If you just transcribe your textbook, you’re re-reading, not learning. Always create from memory first, then check.
  • ❌
    Trying to include everything — A knowledge organiser is not a complete set of notes. It’s the essential facts, key terms, and statistics that you need for the exam. If it spills onto two pages, cut the least important material.
  • ❌
    Forgetting case study details — Geography examiners reward specific named examples and statistics. Always include at least one case study with location, date, and 3–5 key numbers.
  • ❌
    Making it once and never returning — The real value is in recreating it from memory in future sessions. Use your knowledge organiser as a retrieval practice tool, not a decoration for your folder.
