Spatial memory

Sketch Maps

Draw maps and annotated diagrams from memory

What the Research Says

Geography is a spatial subject — where things are matters as much as what they are. Drawing a sketch map from memory forces you to encode location, distribution, and pattern all at once. You cannot draw the global distribution of tropical storms without understanding why they form between 5° and 30° north and south of the equator, over warm oceans, and never at the equator itself. The act of drawing builds connections that passive reading cannot.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Wills et al. (2006) Spatial encoding activates distinct memory pathways — people remember locations and spatial relationships through a separate system to verbal facts Drawing maps creates an additional memory trace
Uttal et al. (2013) Spatial training significantly improves STEM performance, and the gains transfer to new tasks Practising sketch maps improves wider Geography skills
Fernandes et al. (2018) Drawing information produces stronger memory than writing, visualising, or viewing images The physical act of drawing beats re-reading notes

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Choose a distribution or location — Pick something with a clear spatial pattern: global distribution of tropical storms, tectonic plate boundaries, UK weather hazards, or a specific case study location like the River Tees.
  2. 2
    Draw the outline from memory — Start with the basic shape. For a world map, sketch the continents roughly. For a local area, draw the coastline or river channel. It does not need to be perfect — proportions and relative positions matter more than artistic skill.
  3. 3
    Add key features and labels — Mark the locations, boundaries, or features. Use simple symbols: triangles for volcanoes, arrows for plate movement, shading for zones. Label each feature with a short name (3–5 words).
  4. 4
    Annotate with processes and explanations — This is where the marks are. Add short annotations explaining WHY features are where they are. “Warm ocean 27°C+ provides evaporation energy” next to the tropical storm zone. “Destructive margin — oceanic plate subducts beneath continental” at a plate boundary.
  5. 5
    Compare and correct — Open your notes or textbook and check. Use a different colour to add anything you missed. Pay attention to what you forgot — those are the gaps to target next time.

One sketch map takes about 20 minutes. Create 1–2 per revision session. Once you have built up a set, redraw them from memory on later days as a retrieval exercise. Aim to have sketch maps for every major distribution and process in the course.

Step 1: Draw the outline

Sketch a rough world map with the equator marked as a dashed line. Add the Tropics of Cancer (23.5°N) and Capricorn (23.5°S) as lighter dashed lines. Mark the continents roughly — you need North America, Central America, the Caribbean, Southeast Asia, the Indian subcontinent, East Africa, and northern Australia.

Step 2: Add the distribution zones

Shade the tropical storm formation zones: Atlantic — Caribbean Sea and Gulf of Mexico, curving northeast towards Europe. Pacific — two zones: west Pacific (Philippines, Japan) and east Pacific (off Mexico). Indian Ocean — Bay of Bengal and around Madagascar. Draw curved arrows showing the typical storm tracks moving away from the equator and curving poleward.

Step 3: Annotate with processes

Add these annotations: “Ocean temp must be 27°C+ to a depth of 60m” near a formation zone. “Coriolis effect deflects storms — too weak within 5° of equator, so none form there” near the equator. “Storms weaken over land — cut off from warm water energy source” where a track crosses land. “Season: June–November (Atlantic), May–November (Pacific)” in a corner box. Mark Typhoon Haiyan’s track across the Philippines with a star.

After checking — additions in a different colour

Missing from first attempt: no tropical storms form in the South Atlantic (water too cold due to the Benguela Current), the upper atmosphere wind shear needed to be low for storm formation, and the ITCZ seasonal shift explaining why storm seasons vary by hemisphere.

Why this works: In a 6-mark “describe the distribution” question, you can mentally picture this sketch map and describe the pattern systematically — latitude range, ocean basins, proximity to the equator, and notable gaps — rather than writing vague generalisations.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Worrying about artistic quality — Your sketch map does not need to be beautiful. A rough outline with correct relative positions and good annotations is far more useful than a perfectly drawn but unlabelled map.
  • ❌
    Labelling without explaining — Writing “tropical storms” on a zone is not enough. You need to annotate WHY they form there: “warm ocean 27°C+, Coriolis effect, low wind shear.” The annotations earn the marks, not the labels.
  • ❌
    Copying from a textbook — The whole point is to draw from memory first. If you copy, you bypass the retrieval effort that makes this technique effective. Close your notes, draw, then check.
  • ❌
    Only sketching distributions — Sketch maps work for processes too. A cross-section of a coastline, a long profile of a river, or a plan view of an urban area are all sketch maps. Use the format that fits the content.
