Case Study Cards

The facts that separate Level 2 from Level 3

Why Case Studies Matter

In AQA GCSE Geography, the highest marks go to answers that include specific named examples with real facts, dates, and statistics. A generic answer about “a tropical storm” stays at Level 2. An answer about Typhoon Haiyan, with specific details about wind speeds, death toll, and the response from the Philippines government, reaches Level 3. Case study cards give you a systematic way to learn and recall these details under pressure.

What Examiners Want

Level What It Looks Like Example
Level 1 (1–2 marks) Basic, generic statements with no real-world examples “Tropical storms cause damage and kill people.”
Level 2 (3–4 marks) Some explanation but vague or unnamed examples “A tropical storm in Asia destroyed buildings and caused flooding.”
Level 3 (5–6 marks) Detailed, named case study with specific facts and statistics “Typhoon Haiyan (2013) hit the Philippines with 315 km/h winds, killing 6,300 people and displacing 4 million.”

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Use one card per case study — Get a pack of index cards or cut A4 paper into quarters. One case study per card — no exceptions.
  2. 2
    Use a consistent structure — Every card follows the same format: Name & Location at the top, then Date, Causes, Effects (social, economic, environmental), Responses (immediate and long-term), and Key Statistics (3–5 numbers).
  3. 3
    Write from memory first — Before checking your notes, write down everything you can remember for each section. Then check and fill gaps in a different colour.
  4. 4
    Shuffle and test — Mix the cards up. Pick one at random, read the case study name, and try to recall all the details. Flip to check. Sort into piles: confident, unsure, forgotten.
  5. 5
    Prioritise the weak cards — Retest the “forgotten” pile first next session. Use the Leitner box system: correct answers move to a longer review cycle; wrong answers go back to daily review.

Create your cards over the first week or two of revision. After that, spend 10–15 minutes per session shuffling and testing yourself. You should have around 12–15 case study cards for the full AQA Geography course.

Front of card

TYPHOON HAIYAN — Philippines, November 2013

Back of card

Causes: Formed over warm Pacific Ocean (sea temp 27°C+). Category 5 super typhoon. Wind speeds up to 315 km/h. Storm surge up to 5 metres.

Social effects: 6,300 people killed. 4 million displaced. 600,000 homes destroyed. 14 million people affected.

Economic effects: $12 billion in damage. Rice and sugar crops destroyed. Tacloban city centre flattened.

Environmental effects: Coastal mangroves destroyed. Coral reefs damaged. Flooding contaminated water supplies.

Immediate responses: UN launched $301 million appeal. US aircraft carrier sent for aid delivery. Red Cross deployed emergency shelters.

Long-term responses: “Build Back Better” programme — typhoon-resistant buildings. Mangrove replanting for coastal protection. Improved early warning systems. Evacuation drills introduced.

Key stats: 315 km/h winds | 6,300 dead | 4 million displaced | $12 billion damage | 5m storm surge

Front of card

HAITI EARTHQUAKE — Port-au-Prince, Haiti, 12 January 2010

Back of card

Causes: Magnitude 7.0 earthquake. Conservative plate boundary (Caribbean and North American plates). Focus only 13 km deep (shallow = more damage). Haiti is an LIC — poor building standards, no earthquake-resistant design.

Social effects: 316,000 people killed. 1.5 million made homeless. 300,000 injured. 1 in 5 jobs lost.

Economic effects: $8 billion in damage. Port-au-Prince’s main port destroyed. Government buildings collapsed, including the presidential palace.

Environmental effects: Landslides blocked roads. Damaged water and sewage systems led to cholera outbreak (9,000+ deaths by 2012).

Immediate responses: $13.5 billion in international aid pledged. US military sent 10,000 troops. Search and rescue teams from 25+ countries.

Long-term responses: “Build Back Better” rebuilding programme. New building codes introduced. Earthquake-resistant schools built by NGOs. Still rebuilding years later due to slow aid distribution.

Key stats: Magnitude 7.0 | 316,000 dead | 1.5 million homeless | $8 billion damage | 13 km focus depth

Create one card for each. If your teacher has specified different case studies, use those instead — the method is the same.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Writing too much on each card — If you need more than one side of an index card, you’re including too much detail. Stick to bullet points and key statistics.
  • ❌
    Forgetting the structure — Every card should follow the same format: causes, effects (social, economic, environmental), responses (immediate, long-term), key stats. Consistency makes recall easier.
  • ❌
    Not including enough numbers — Examiners love specific statistics. “Many people died” is Level 1. “316,000 people were killed” is Level 3. Aim for 3–5 key statistics per card.
  • ❌
    Only testing the easy cards — It feels satisfying to recall Typhoon Haiyan perfectly. But if you keep avoiding Nigeria or Rio, those will be the ones that catch you out in the exam.
