Visual learning

Dual Coding

Combine words and visuals to remember more

What the Research Says

Your brain processes words and images through separate channels. When you encode information in both — reading about a concept AND drawing a diagram of it — you create two memory pathways instead of one. If one pathway fades, the other can still retrieve the information. Geography is naturally visual — maps, cross-sections, hydrographs, and annotated diagrams are already part of the subject. Use that to your advantage.

Evidence Base

Study Finding Impact
Paivio (1971) Dual coding theory — information stored in both verbal and visual memory is recalled better than either alone Foundational theory for visual learning
Mayer (2009) Multimedia learning principles — people learn better from words and pictures together than from words alone Words + visuals outperform words only
Clark & Paivio (1991) Dual coding consistently enhanced learning across different tasks, ages, and content areas Works for all subjects including Geography

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Pick a topic — Choose one topic or a key process. Read through it to refresh your memory.
  2. 2
    Identify the relationships — What kind of information is this? A physical process → annotated diagram or sequence. Spatial distribution → sketch map. Comparing two places → table. Causes and effects → flowchart. Changes over time → graph or timeline.
  3. 3
    Choose the right visual format — Match the format to the content. Waterfall formation needs a labelled cross-section. The hydrological cycle needs a cycle diagram. HIC vs LIC development needs a comparison table. Don’t force everything into a mind map.
  4. 4
    Draw it from memory — Close your notes and create the visual from what you remember. Use short labels (3–5 words max), arrows to show processes, and colour to group related features.
  5. 5
    Check and annotate — Open your notes and fill in anything you missed using a different colour. Add brief annotations explaining WHY each process happens.

One visual takes about 25 minutes to create properly. Create 1–2 per revision session. Your visuals become revision tools themselves — in later sessions, try to recreate them from memory as a retrieval practice exercise.

Step 1: Identify relationships — this is a process sequence

Waterfall formation involves differential erosion of hard and soft rock. A labelled cross-section diagram is the right format, showing the stages from initial overhang to eventual gorge.

Step 2: Cross-section drawn from memory

Stage 1: River flows over a band of hard rock overlying soft rock. Label: “resistant cap rock” on top, “less resistant rock” below.
↓
Stage 2: Soft rock erodes faster by hydraulic action and abrasion, creating an overhang. Plunge pool forms at the base. Arrow labelled “undercutting”.
↓
Stage 3: Overhang becomes unsupported and collapses. Fallen rocks add to abrasion in the plunge pool.
↓
Stage 4: Process repeats. Waterfall retreats upstream. Arrow showing direction of retreat, with a long narrow gorge left behind.

After checking — additions in a different colour

Missing from first attempt: specific erosion types at each stage (hydraulic action in the plunge pool, abrasion from swirling rocks), the splash-back erosion on the overhang from below, and the real-world example: High Force waterfall on the River Tees, where the Whin Sill (hard dolerite) overlies softer limestone and shale.

Why this works: Drawing the cross-section from memory forces you to understand the sequence of processes, not just memorise a definition. In the exam, you can mentally picture this diagram to structure a 6-mark “explain the formation” answer.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Too much text on visuals — If your diagram has full sentences, it’s just notes in boxes. Keep labels to 3–5 words and let the visual structure do the work.
  • ❌
    Copying instead of drawing from memory — The retrieval effort is essential. Copying a diagram from your textbook is just re-reading with extra steps.
  • ❌
    Using mind maps for everything — Mind maps are good for categories but poor for physical processes or spatial distributions. A cross-section must be a cross-section, not a mind map.
  • ❌
    Forgetting to annotate with processes — A diagram of a meander without labels like “erosion on outside bend” and “deposition on inside bend” is just a picture. The annotations are what earn marks.
