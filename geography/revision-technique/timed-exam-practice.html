Timed Exam Practice

Build speed, accuracy, and exam confidence

Why Timed Practice Matters

Knowing the content is only half the battle. In the exam, you need to recall it quickly, structure it clearly, and write it under strict time limits. Students who never practise under timed conditions often run out of time on the highest-value questions — the 9-mark (+3 SPaG) essays that carry the most marks. Timed practice builds the speed and discipline you need to perform on the day.

AQA Geography Paper Structure

Paper Content Time Marks
Paper 1 Living with the Physical Environment (natural hazards, physical landscapes, ecosystems) 1 hour 30 min 88 marks
Paper 2 Challenges in the Human Environment (urban, economic, resource management) 1 hour 30 min 88 marks
Paper 3 Geographical Applications (pre-release, fieldwork, map/data skills) 1 hour 15 min 76 marks

Rule of thumb: roughly 1 minute per mark. A 9-mark question should take about 12–13 minutes (including planning time). A 6-mark question should take about 8 minutes.

Step-by-Step Method

  1. 1
    Start with single questions — Don’t jump straight to full papers. Pick one 6-mark or 9-mark question, set a timer, and write your answer under timed conditions. No notes allowed.
  2. 2
    Plan before you write — Spend 1–2 minutes planning. For a 9-mark question: jot down 3 developed points, each with a specific example or statistic. This structure prevents waffling and keeps you on track.
  3. 3
    Self-mark against the mark scheme — Download AQA mark schemes from their website. Read the level descriptors carefully. Be honest — did you hit Level 3? What was missing? Highlight the specific phrases in the mark scheme that your answer did or didn’t include.
  4. 4
    Rewrite the weak parts — If your answer was Level 2, identify exactly what you needed for Level 3. Write the improved version. This is where the learning happens.
  5. 5
    Build to full papers — Once comfortable with single questions, attempt a full section under timed conditions. In the last 2 weeks before the exam, do at least 2 full timed papers for each of Paper 1, Paper 2, and Paper 3.

These timings include planning. For the 9-mark (+3 SPaG) questions, spend 2 minutes planning, 10 minutes writing, and 1 minute checking spelling, punctuation, and grammar. The SPaG marks are free marks — don’t throw them away by rushing.

Question: “To what extent are the effects of tropical storms linked to a country’s level of development?” [9 marks + 3 SPaG]

This is a judgement question. You need to argue both sides and reach a conclusion.

Level 2 answer (4–5 marks)

The effects of tropical storms are worse in poorer countries. When Typhoon Haiyan hit the Philippines in 2013, over 6,000 people died because the buildings were not strong enough. In comparison, hurricanes in the USA cause less deaths because they have better warning systems and stronger buildings. However, the USA still suffers a lot of economic damage. This shows that development affects the effects of tropical storms.

Level 3 answer (7–9 marks)

The effects of tropical storms are significantly linked to a country’s level of development, although the physical characteristics of the storm also play a role.

In LICs and NEEs, the effects are typically more severe in terms of loss of life. Typhoon Haiyan (2013) struck the Philippines, an NEE with a GDP per capita of around $2,800, killing 6,300 people and displacing 4 million. Buildings in Tacloban were poorly constructed and could not withstand the 315 km/h winds and 5-metre storm surge. The government had limited capacity to evacuate people in advance.

In contrast, when Hurricane Katrina hit the USA in 2005, a HIC with far greater resources, the death toll was 1,836. The economic damage, however, was $125 billion — far higher than Haiyan’s $12 billion — because of the high value of property and infrastructure destroyed. This shows that HICs suffer greater economic effects while LICs suffer greater human effects.

However, development is not the only factor. The physical intensity of the storm matters too. Haiyan was a Category 5 super typhoon, one of the strongest ever recorded, so even a HIC would have suffered significant damage. The location of impact (coastal vs inland), population density, and time of day also affect the severity.

Overall, I would argue that development is the most important factor because it determines a country’s capacity to prepare, respond, and recover. While physical factors influence the scale of the hazard, it is governance, infrastructure, and wealth that determine how many people are killed and how quickly the country recovers.

Key differences: The Level 3 answer uses two specific case studies with statistics, addresses both sides of the argument, considers alternative factors, and reaches a clear judgement. The Level 2 answer had the right idea but lacked specific detail and did not evaluate alternative factors.

  • AQA website — Search “AQA GCSE Geography past papers”. Free access to papers and mark schemes from previous years.
  • Your teacher — Ask for additional practice papers or specimen papers.
  • ExamBuilder (if your school has access) — AQA’s tool for creating custom question sets by topic.

Always use the mark scheme alongside the paper. Self-marking is only useful if you are comparing against the actual level descriptors, not just guessing your grade.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Spending too long on low-mark questions — A 1-mark “define” question should take 30 seconds, not 3 minutes. Save your time for the 9-mark questions where the most marks are available.
  • ❌
    Not planning 9-mark answers — Without a plan, you ramble. Two minutes of planning saves you from repeating yourself and ensures you cover both sides of the argument plus a conclusion.
  • ❌
    Ignoring SPaG marks — 3 marks for spelling, punctuation, and grammar on the 9-mark question. Use geographical terminology correctly (“constructive plate boundary” not “plates moving apart”). Write in full paragraphs. Check for basic spelling errors.
  • ❌
    Never doing a full timed paper — Practising individual questions is good, but the real challenge is sustaining concentration across 90 minutes. Do at least two full timed papers for each exam before the real thing.
