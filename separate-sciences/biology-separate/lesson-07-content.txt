# Decomposition, Food Security and Biotechnology

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Decomposition and Decay</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All living organisms eventually die. When they do, their bodies &mdash; along with waste products such as faeces and urine &mdash; are broken down by organisms called <dfn class="term" data-def="An organism such as a bacterium or fungus that breaks down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients back into the ecosystem.">decomposers</dfn>. The two main types of decomposer are bacteria and fungi. Both are <dfn class="term" data-def="An organism too small to see without a microscope, such as a bacterium or fungus.">microorganisms</dfn> &mdash; they are too small to see without a microscope.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Larger organisms called <dfn class="term" data-def="An organism that feeds on dead organic matter (detritus), starting the process of decay &mdash; examples include maggots, earthworms, and woodlice.">detritus feeders</dfn> (detritivores) also play a role. Maggots, earthworms, woodlice, and beetles eat dead organic matter and produce waste. Bacteria and fungi then break down everything &mdash; the dead organism, the detritivores, and their waste &mdash; releasing carbon dioxide, water, and mineral ions back into the environment.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4">Decomposers work by secreting <dfn class="term" data-def="A biological catalyst &mdash; a protein that speeds up chemical reactions without being used up.">enzymes</dfn> onto the dead organic material. These enzymes break down large molecules (such as carbohydrates and proteins) into smaller, soluble molecules that the decomposers can absorb and use as food. This process of <dfn class="term" data-def="The breakdown of dead organic matter by microorganisms, recycling nutrients such as carbon and nitrogen back into the soil and atmosphere.">decomposition</dfn> is essential because it recycles elements like carbon and nitrogen so they can be reused by living organisms.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Without decomposers, dead organisms and waste would pile up, and essential elements such as carbon and nitrogen would become locked away and unavailable to plants. The decay cycle ensures these nutrients are constantly recycled.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Factors Affecting the Rate of Decomposition</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Decomposition does not always happen at the same speed. Three main factors affect how quickly organic matter is broken down:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Temperature:</strong> Decomposers use enzymes, and enzymes work fastest at an optimum temperature (around 37&deg;C for many bacteria). Below this, particles move slowly and there are fewer successful collisions between enzyme and substrate. Above the optimum, the enzyme is <dfn class="term" data-def="When an enzyme loses its shape due to high temperature or extreme pH, so the substrate can no longer fit into the active site.">denatured</dfn> &mdash; its active site changes shape permanently, so the substrate no longer fits and the reaction stops.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Moisture:</strong> Water is needed for enzymes to be secreted and for the products of digestion to be absorbed. Dry conditions slow decomposition dramatically &mdash; this is why dried foods like ship&rsquo;s biscuits lasted for months on long sea voyages.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Oxygen availability:</strong> Most decomposers respire aerobically, so they need oxygen. More oxygen means faster decomposition. However, some microorganisms can respire anaerobically (without oxygen), producing methane and carbon dioxide &mdash; this is the basis of biogas production.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Compost and Biogas</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Gardeners and farmers make use of decomposition in two practical ways. <dfn class="term" data-def="Decomposed plant material added to soil to improve its quality by providing mineral ions such as nitrates.">Compost</dfn> is made from dead plant material (grass cuttings, leaves, vegetable peelings) that is broken down by decomposers in aerobic conditions. The resulting material is rich in mineral ions such as nitrates, which act as a natural fertiliser and improve soil quality for growing crops.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="A renewable fuel made mainly of methane, produced when microorganisms break down organic matter anaerobically in a sealed container.">Biogas</dfn> is produced when organic waste (manure, sewage, crop waste) is decomposed anaerobically inside a sealed container called a biogas generator. The main product is methane, which is flammable and can be used for heating, cooking, or generating electricity. Biogas is a renewable fuel because the organic waste can be continually replaced.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Food Security</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><dfn class="term" data-def="Having enough food to feed a population at all times &mdash; access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food.">Food security</dfn> means having enough food to feed a population. The global demand for food is rising, and several biological and environmental factors threaten our ability to meet it.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Threats to food security include: increasing birth rate, changing diets in developed countries (more meat consumption), new pests and pathogens affecting crops and livestock, environmental changes (climate change, drought, flooding), conflict and poverty, and the cost of agricultural inputs such as fertilisers and machinery.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n19">To address these threats, scientists and farmers are developing sustainable ways to produce more food without depleting natural resources.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Making Food Production More Efficient</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">At each stage of a food chain, biomass is lost through respiration (releasing energy for movement and body heat), excretion, and undigested material in faeces. Shorter food chains are more efficient because less energy is wasted between trophic levels. Eating less meat and more plant-based food reduces the number of stages in the food chain, making food production more efficient overall.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Sustainable Fisheries</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><dfn class="term" data-def="Managing food production in a way that meets current needs without damaging the environment or reducing the ability to produce food in the future.">Sustainable food production</dfn> means producing food in ways that meet current needs without harming the ability to produce food in the future. For fishing, this means managing fish stocks so they do not collapse.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Strategies include: setting <dfn class="term" data-def="A limit on the amount of fish that can be caught in a given area over a set period, used to prevent overfishing.">fishing quotas</dfn> to limit how many fish can be caught, controlling net mesh sizes so young fish can escape and breed, banning fishing during breeding seasons, and using fish farming (aquaculture) to rear fish in controlled environments. Fish farms manage feeding, water quality, and disease to maximise growth efficiently.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Biotechnology: Mycoprotein</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><dfn class="term" data-def="The use of living organisms or biological processes in industry, agriculture, and food production.">Biotechnology</dfn> offers solutions to food security. One key example is <dfn class="term" data-def="A protein-rich food made from the fungus Fusarium venenatum, grown in fermenters using aerobic conditions and glucose syrup &mdash; sold as Quorn.">mycoprotein</dfn>, a protein-rich food produced from the fungus <em>Fusarium venenatum</em>. It is grown in large fermenters under aerobic conditions, using glucose syrup as a food source. The fungal biomass is harvested, purified, and processed into products sold under the brand name Quorn&trade;.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">Mycoprotein is high in protein and fibre, low in saturated fat, and has a texture similar to chicken. It provides a sustainable alternative to meat because it can be produced quickly in controlled conditions, using far less land and water than animal farming. However, some people may be allergic to it, and it must be clearly labelled.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n31">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Mycoprotein production: <em>Fusarium venenatum</em> fungus &rarr; grown in fermenters with glucose syrup + oxygen &rarr; harvested, purified, and processed into Quorn&trade;. It is high in protein and fibre, low in fat, and suitable for vegetarians.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Biotechnology: GM Crops</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><dfn class="term" data-def="Genetically modified crops &mdash; plants whose DNA has been altered using genetic engineering to introduce desirable characteristics such as pest resistance or higher yield.">GM crops</dfn> (genetically modified crops) are plants whose DNA has been changed using genetic engineering. A gene for a desirable characteristic is cut from one organism using a restriction enzyme, inserted into a <dfn class="term" data-def="A small circular piece of DNA found in bacteria, often used as a vector to transfer genes in genetic engineering.">plasmid</dfn> (a small ring of bacterial DNA that acts as a vector), and transferred into the plant cells.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34">Examples of GM crop traits include: resistance to insect pests (reducing the need for chemical pesticides), resistance to herbicides (allowing farmers to spray weeds without harming the crop), improved nutritional content (such as Golden Rice, which contains beta-carotene to combat vitamin A deficiency), and increased crop yield.</p>

Crops can be engineered to grow in harsh conditions (drought, poor soil). Improved nutritional content can address malnutrition in developing countries.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n37">For 6-mark extended response questions on food security or biotechnology, structure your answer clearly: state the problem, explain the solution, and evaluate by giving both advantages and disadvantages. ## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n38">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n39">Decomposition is carried out by bacteria, fungi, and detritus feeders &mdash; it recycles carbon and nitrogen. The rate is affected by temperature, moisture, and oxygen availability.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40">Food security is threatened by rising populations, climate change, new pests, and conflict. Sustainable strategies include fishing quotas, efficient farming, and shorter food chains.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Biotechnology helps address food security: mycoprotein (Quorn) is made from <em>Fusarium venenatum</em> in fermenters; GM crops use genetic engineering to improve yield, pest resistance, or nutrition.</li></ul>
