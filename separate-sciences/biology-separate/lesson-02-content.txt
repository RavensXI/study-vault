# Monoclonal Antibodies

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Monoclonal Antibodies?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Your immune system produces <dfn class="term" data-def="Y-shaped proteins produced by lymphocytes that bind to specific antigens on the surface of pathogens.">antibodies</dfn> to fight pathogens. Each antibody is specific &mdash; it only binds to one type of <dfn class="term" data-def="A molecule on the surface of a pathogen that triggers an immune response.">antigen</dfn>. Normally, your body produces many different antibodies at once (polyclonal antibodies). But scientists have found a way to produce large quantities of a single, identical antibody. These are called <dfn class="term" data-def="Identical antibodies produced from a single clone of cells, designed to target one specific antigen.">monoclonal antibodies</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Monoclonal antibodies are incredibly useful because they are all identical and all target the same specific protein or cell. This makes them precise tools for diagnosis, research and treatment.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Monoclonal antibodies are produced from a single clone of cells. &ldquo;Mono&rdquo; means one and &ldquo;clonal&rdquo; means from a clone &mdash; so monoclonal antibodies are all identical copies that bind to one specific antigen.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">How Are Monoclonal Antibodies Produced?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Producing monoclonal antibodies involves combining two types of cell to create a <dfn class="term" data-def="A cell formed by fusing a lymphocyte with a tumour cell, capable of both producing antibodies and dividing indefinitely.">hybridoma</dfn>. Here is the step-by-step process:</p>

<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 1</div>
    <h4>Inject the antigen</h4><p>A mouse is injected with the specific antigen that you want to produce antibodies against. The mouse&rsquo;s immune system responds by producing <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of white blood cell that produces antibodies as part of the immune response.">lymphocytes</dfn> that make antibodies against that antigen.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 2</div>
    <h4>Extract lymphocytes</h4><p>The antibody-producing lymphocytes are extracted from the mouse. These cells can make the right antibody, but they cannot divide indefinitely on their own.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 3</div>
    <h4>Fuse with tumour cells</h4><p>The lymphocytes are fused with <dfn class="term" data-def="Cells that divide uncontrollably and can be used in hybridoma production because they divide rapidly and indefinitely.">tumour cells</dfn> (cancer cells). Detergent is added to break down the cell-surface membranes so the cells can fuse together. Tumour cells are used because they divide rapidly and do not stop dividing.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 4</div>
    <h4>Create hybridoma cells</h4><p>The fused cells are called hybridoma cells. They combine the lymphocyte&rsquo;s ability to produce a specific antibody with the tumour cell&rsquo;s ability to divide indefinitely.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Step 5</div>
    <h4>Select and clone</h4><p>The hybridoma cells are separated and tested. Only those producing the desired antibody are selected. These are then grown on a large scale in culture, and the monoclonal antibodies are collected from the growth medium.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">Uses of Monoclonal Antibodies</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">Monoclonal antibodies have several important applications in medicine and research. Because they bind to one specific antigen, they can be used to target particular molecules or cells with great precision.</p>

<h3 data-narration-id="n11">Pregnancy Testing</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n12">One of the most familiar uses of monoclonal antibodies is the <dfn class="term" data-def="A diagnostic test that uses monoclonal antibodies to detect the hormone HCG in urine, indicating pregnancy.">pregnancy test</dfn>. When a woman becomes pregnant, her body produces the hormone HCG (human chorionic gonadotrophin). This hormone is excreted in urine. The pregnancy test strip contains monoclonal antibodies that are designed to bind specifically to HCG.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">If HCG is present in the urine sample, it binds to the monoclonal antibodies on the strip. These antibody-HCG complexes move along the strip and bind to a second set of immobilised antibodies at the test line, producing a visible colour change. A positive result shows two lines; a negative result shows only one (the control line).</p>

<h3 data-narration-id="n14">Blood and Laboratory Testing</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Monoclonal antibodies are used in laboratory blood tests to measure levels of specific hormones, detect pathogens, or identify blood groups. Because they bind only to one target, they provide very accurate and reliable results. For example, they can detect the presence of a specific virus in a patient&rsquo;s blood sample.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Monoclonal antibodies are used in pregnancy tests to detect the hormone HCG, in laboratory blood tests to identify pathogens or measure hormone levels, and in research to locate specific molecules in cells.</p>
</div>

<h3 data-narration-id="n17">Research</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n18">In scientific research, monoclonal antibodies are used to identify and locate specific molecules within cells or tissues. Because each monoclonal antibody targets one specific protein, researchers can use them like molecular labels &mdash; attaching a fluorescent marker to the antibody and then tracking where the target molecule appears in the body or in a tissue sample.</p>

<div class="collapsible">

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n19">Cancer cells have specific antigens on their surface that normal cells do not. Scientists can design monoclonal antibodies to bind to these cancer-specific antigens. Once bound, the antibodies can:</p>
    <ul>
      <li data-narration-id="n20">Trigger the patient&rsquo;s own immune system to attack the cancer cells</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n21">Carry a radioactive substance, toxic drug or chemical directly to the cancer cell, killing it without harming surrounding healthy tissue (this is sometimes called a &ldquo;magic bullet&rdquo;)</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n22">Block chemical signals that tell the cancer cell to divide</li>
    </ul>
    <p data-narration-id="n23">This targeted approach causes fewer side effects than traditional chemotherapy, which kills all rapidly dividing cells &mdash; including healthy ones like hair follicle cells and cells lining the gut.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Problems and Ethical Issues</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Although monoclonal antibodies were initially expected to be a &ldquo;magic bullet&rdquo; for treating many diseases, they have created more side effects than expected. Some of the issues include:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Side effects:</strong> Monoclonal antibodies can cause unexpected immune reactions in patients. In 2006, a clinical trial of the monoclonal antibody TGN1412 caused six healthy volunteers to suffer multiple organ failure within minutes, likely because T cells overproduced chemicals that stimulated a massive immune response.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Animal use:</strong> Production involves using mice &mdash; both to produce antibodies and to create <dfn class="term" data-def="Cells that grow and divide uncontrollably, forming cancerous masses.">tumour</dfn> cells. Inducing cancer in mice raises ethical concerns, even with guidelines in place to minimise suffering.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Genetic engineering:</strong> Transgenic mice can be used (with a human gene inserted) so that they produce human antibodies rather than mouse antibodies. This avoids the need to &ldquo;humanise&rdquo; the antibodies, but raises separate ethical issues about genetic engineering.</li>
</ul>

  <p>Monoclonal antibodies have produced more side effects than expected. Their development and testing raises ethical issues about animal use, genetic engineering, and the safety of clinical trials.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n31">When describing the production of monoclonal antibodies, examiners want you to include <strong>all five steps</strong> in order: inject antigen into mouse &rarr; extract lymphocytes &rarr; fuse with tumour cells &rarr; form hybridoma &rarr; select, clone and collect. A common mistake is forgetting to explain <em>why</em> tumour cells are used (they divide indefinitely). For 6-mark extended response questions, always link the use of monoclonal antibodies back to their <strong>specificity</strong> &mdash; they work because each one binds to only one type of antigen.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n32">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n33">Monoclonal antibodies are identical antibodies produced from hybridoma cells (a lymphocyte fused with a tumour cell) that target one specific antigen.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n34">They are used in pregnancy tests (detecting HCG), laboratory blood tests, scientific research, and cancer treatment.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n35">Their production and use raises ethical issues including animal testing, side effects in clinical trials, and genetic engineering of transgenic mice.</li>
</ul>
