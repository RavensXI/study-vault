# The Brain, the Eye and Temperature Control

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Human Brain</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The organ that coordinates all voluntary and involuntary actions in the body, forming part of the central nervous system.">brain</dfn> is the most complex organ in the human body. It is made up of billions of interconnected neurones and is responsible for controlling everything from conscious thought to automatic body functions like breathing and heart rate.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Scientists have mapped the brain into distinct regions, each with specialised roles. Understanding what each part does helps doctors diagnose and treat neurological conditions. However, the brain is incredibly difficult to study because of its complexity &mdash; and because any investigation carries a risk of causing damage.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The brain controls complex behaviour and has different regions that carry out different functions. Scientists have been able to map these regions to particular functions by studying patients with brain damage, by electrically stimulating different parts of the brain, and by using MRI scanning techniques.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Regions of the Brain</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The brain has three main regions you need to know for your exam:</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The outer folded layer of the brain responsible for consciousness, intelligence, memory and language.">cerebral cortex</dfn> is the large, folded outer layer of the brain. It is responsible for consciousness, intelligence, memory and language. When you think, plan, remember facts or speak, it is your cerebral cortex at work. The folds increase its surface area, giving more space for neurones to carry out complex processing.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Cerebellum</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n8">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The region at the back of the brain that coordinates balance, posture and voluntary muscular movements.">cerebellum</dfn> sits at the back and underneath the cerebral cortex. It is responsible for the coordination of muscular activity, balance and posture. It does not initiate movement but ensures your muscles work together smoothly &mdash; for example, when you walk or catch a ball.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>The Medulla Oblongata</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n9">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The region at the base of the brain that controls unconscious activities such as breathing and heart rate.">medulla oblongata</dfn> (often called simply the medulla) is found at the base of the brain, where it connects to the spinal cord. It controls unconscious activities that keep you alive, such as breathing rate, heart rate and the movement of food through the digestive system (peristalsis). These activities happen automatically without you thinking about them.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n10">Studying the brain is challenging. Scientists cannot simply open up a living brain to observe it, and damage to brain tissue can have severe consequences. Techniques such as <dfn class="term" data-def="Magnetic resonance imaging &mdash; a non-invasive scanning technique that produces detailed images of brain structure and activity.">MRI</dfn> scanning allow researchers to observe which parts of the brain are active during different tasks without causing harm.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n11">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The treatment and investigation of brain disorders is difficult because the brain is so complex and delicate. Any damage to brain tissue can have significant and unpredictable consequences.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">The Structure of the Eye</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">The eye is a sense organ containing <dfn class="term" data-def="Cells that detect changes in the environment, such as light-sensitive cells in the retina.">receptors</dfn> that are sensitive to light. Light enters the eye and is focused onto the back of the eye, creating an image that the brain interprets.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n14">The key structures of the eye and their functions are:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Cornea</strong> &mdash; the transparent front layer that refracts (bends) light as it enters the eye. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The transparent front part of the eye that refracts incoming light.">cornea</dfn> does most of the eye&rsquo;s focusing.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Iris</strong> &mdash; the coloured part of the eye that controls the size of the pupil. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The coloured muscular structure that controls the diameter of the pupil to regulate the amount of light entering the eye.">iris</dfn> contains muscles that contract or relax to adjust how much light enters.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Pupil</strong> &mdash; the hole in the centre of the iris through which light passes. In bright light, the iris contracts and the pupil gets smaller. In dim light, the iris relaxes and the pupil gets larger.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Lens</strong> &mdash; a transparent, flexible structure behind the iris. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The transparent biconvex structure in the eye that changes shape to focus light onto the retina.">lens</dfn> fine-focuses light onto the retina by changing shape.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Retina</strong> &mdash; the light-sensitive layer at the back of the eye containing receptor cells. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The light-sensitive layer at the back of the eye containing rod and cone cells that generate nerve impulses.">retina</dfn> converts the focused light into electrical impulses.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Optic nerve</strong> &mdash; carries electrical impulses from the retina to the brain, where the image is processed.</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Accommodation &mdash; Focusing on Near and Distant Objects</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which the lens changes shape to focus light from objects at different distances onto the retina.">accommodation</dfn> is the process by which the eye changes the shape of the lens to focus on objects at different distances. Two structures work together to make this happen: the <dfn class="term" data-def="A ring of muscle surrounding the lens that contracts or relaxes to change the lens shape.">ciliary muscles</dfn> and the <dfn class="term" data-def="Fibres that connect the ciliary muscles to the lens, pulling it into shape.">suspensory ligaments</dfn>.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Focusing on a Distant Object</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n23">When you look at something far away, the ciliary muscles <strong>relax</strong>. This allows the suspensory ligaments to become <strong>taut</strong> (pulled tight). The taut ligaments pull on the lens, making it <strong>thin</strong>. A thin lens refracts light less, which is correct for distant objects where the light rays entering the eye are nearly parallel.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Focusing on a Near Object</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n24">When you look at something close up, the ciliary muscles <strong>contract</strong>. This releases tension on the suspensory ligaments, which become <strong>slack</strong> (loose). The lens springs back into a <strong>fat, rounded</strong> shape. A thicker lens refracts light more, bending the diverging light rays from a near object so they focus on the retina.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n25">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>To focus on a near object: ciliary muscles contract &rarr; suspensory ligaments go slack &rarr; lens becomes fat and round. To focus on a distant object: ciliary muscles relax &rarr; suspensory ligaments pull taut &rarr; lens becomes thin and flat. Many students get this the wrong way around in exams.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Thermoregulation &mdash; Controlling Body Temperature</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The human body must maintain a core temperature of approximately 37&deg;C. This is the optimum temperature for enzymes to work effectively. If body temperature rises too high or falls too low, enzymes can <dfn class="term" data-def="A permanent change in the shape of an enzyme&rsquo;s active site caused by excessive heat or extreme pH, preventing it from functioning.">denature</dfn> and vital chemical reactions will slow down or stop &mdash; which can be fatal.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A small region of the brain that acts as the body&rsquo;s thermostat, monitoring and regulating core body temperature.">thermoregulatory centre</dfn> in the brain (specifically the hypothalamus) monitors the temperature of the blood flowing through it. It also receives nerve impulses from temperature receptors in the skin. When body temperature deviates from 37&deg;C, the thermoregulatory centre triggers responses to bring it back to normal.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Response When the Body Is Too Hot</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n29">When body temperature rises above 37&deg;C, several mechanisms act to cool the body down:</p>
    <ul>
      <li data-narration-id="n30"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The widening of blood vessels near the skin surface to increase blood flow and heat loss by radiation.">vasodilation</dfn></strong> &mdash; blood vessels near the surface of the skin widen (dilate). This allows more blood to flow close to the skin surface, increasing the transfer of thermal energy to the environment by radiation.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Sweating</strong> &mdash; sweat glands release sweat onto the skin surface. As the water in sweat evaporates, it absorbs thermal energy from the skin, cooling the body down.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Hairs lie flat</strong> &mdash; erector muscles relax so body hairs lie flat against the skin. This reduces the layer of insulating air trapped next to the body, allowing more heat to escape.</li>
    </ul>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Response When the Body Is Too Cold</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n33">When body temperature drops below 37&deg;C, the body responds in the opposite way:</p>
    <ul>
      <li data-narration-id="n34"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The narrowing of blood vessels near the skin surface to reduce blood flow and minimise heat loss.">vasoconstriction</dfn></strong> &mdash; blood vessels near the skin surface narrow (constrict). Less blood flows near the surface, so less thermal energy is lost to the surroundings by radiation.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Shivering</strong> &mdash; muscles contract and relax rapidly and involuntarily. The metabolic reactions involved in muscle contraction require energy from respiration, which releases thermal energy as a by-product, warming the body.</li>
      <li data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Hairs stand up</strong> &mdash; erector muscles contract, pulling body hairs upright. This traps a thicker layer of insulating air close to the skin, reducing heat loss. This is sometimes called <dfn class="term" data-def="The involuntary erection of body hairs by contraction of erector muscles, trapping an insulating layer of air.">piloerection</dfn> and causes &ldquo;goosebumps.&rdquo;</li>
    </ul>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n37">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Thermoregulation is an example of a negative feedback mechanism. The thermoregulatory centre in the hypothalamus detects a change from the set point (37&deg;C) and triggers responses that counteract the change, returning body temperature to normal. This is essential because enzymes denature at high temperatures and work too slowly at low temperatures.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n38">A common 6-mark question asks you to describe and explain how the body responds when it is too hot or too cold. Structure your answer by naming each mechanism (vasodilation/vasoconstriction, sweating/shivering, hair position), then explaining <em>how</em> each mechanism increases or decreases heat loss. Always link back to the thermoregulatory centre in the hypothalamus detecting the temperature change. For accommodation questions, practise drawing a table comparing near vs distant object focusing &mdash; examiners love this clear format.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n39">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n40">The brain has three key regions: the cerebral cortex (consciousness and intelligence), the cerebellum (coordination and balance), and the medulla oblongata (unconscious functions like breathing and heart rate).</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n41">The eye focuses light using the cornea and lens. Accommodation is the process where ciliary muscles and suspensory ligaments change the lens shape to focus on near or distant objects.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n42">Body temperature is regulated by the thermoregulatory centre in the hypothalamus through vasodilation, vasoconstriction, sweating, shivering and changes in hair position &mdash; an example of negative feedback.</li>
</ul>
