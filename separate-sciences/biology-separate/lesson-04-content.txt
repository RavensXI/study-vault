# The Kidney, Water Balance and Plant Hormones

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Kidney and Its Role in Excretion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The body produces waste products that must be removed to prevent toxic build-up. This removal process is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The removal of metabolic waste products from the body, including urea, carbon dioxide, and excess water.">excretion</dfn>. The two main metabolic waste products are carbon dioxide (removed by the lungs) and <dfn class="term" data-def="A toxic nitrogen-containing waste produced when excess amino acids are broken down in the liver by deamination.">urea</dfn>, which is produced in the liver when excess amino acids are broken down in a process called <dfn class="term" data-def="The breakdown of excess amino acids in the liver, removing the amino group to produce ammonia, which is then converted to urea.">deamination</dfn>. Urea is then dissolved in the blood and carried to the kidneys for removal.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The kidneys perform two critical jobs: they filter waste products out of the blood, and they regulate the water and ion content of the body. Each kidney contains around one million tiny filtering units called <dfn class="term" data-def="The functional unit of the kidney containing a glomerulus and tubule system that filters blood and produces urine.">nephrons</dfn>.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The three main functions of the kidney are: <strong>filtration</strong> of the blood to remove waste, <strong>selective reabsorption</strong> of useful substances back into the blood, and <strong>excretion</strong> of urea, excess water, and ions as urine.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">How the Nephron Works</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Blood enters the kidney through the renal artery and flows into a tiny knot of capillaries called the <dfn class="term" data-def="A tiny ball of capillaries inside the Bowman&rsquo;s capsule where blood is filtered under high pressure.">glomerulus</dfn>, which sits inside a cup-shaped structure called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The cup-shaped end of a nephron that surrounds the glomerulus and collects the filtrate.">Bowman&rsquo;s capsule</dfn>. The blood arriving at the glomerulus is under high pressure because the blood vessel leaving the glomerulus (the efferent arteriole) is narrower than the one entering it (the afferent arteriole). This high pressure forces small molecules &mdash; water, glucose, urea, and ions &mdash; out of the blood and into the Bowman&rsquo;s capsule. Large molecules such as proteins and blood cells are too big to pass through and stay in the blood. This stage is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The process in the glomerulus where high blood pressure forces water, glucose, urea, and ions out of the blood into the Bowman&rsquo;s capsule.">ultrafiltration</dfn>.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n7">After filtration, the liquid (called filtrate) passes along the kidney tubules. Here, useful substances are reabsorbed back into the blood by <dfn class="term" data-def="The process in the kidney tubules where useful substances such as all glucose, some ions, and the right amount of water are taken back into the blood.">selective reabsorption</dfn>. <strong>All glucose</strong> is reabsorbed by active transport. <strong>The correct amount of water</strong> is reabsorbed &mdash; the amount depends on the body&rsquo;s needs and is controlled by the hormone ADH. <strong>Some ions</strong> (such as sodium) are selectively reabsorbed. Urea is <strong>not</strong> reabsorbed &mdash; it stays in the tubule and is excreted as part of urine.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8">If glucose appears in urine, it can be a sign of diabetes. In diabetes, blood glucose levels are too high for the kidneys to reabsorb all the glucose, so some passes into the urine.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">Water Balance and ADH</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">The water content of the blood must be carefully controlled. If cells gain or lose too much water by <dfn class="term" data-def="The movement of water molecules from a region of higher water concentration to a region of lower water concentration through a partially permeable membrane.">osmosis</dfn>, they cannot function properly &mdash; they may swell and burst, or shrink and become damaged. The regulation of water balance is part of <dfn class="term" data-def="The regulation of water and solute concentrations in the body fluids to maintain a stable internal environment.">osmoregulation</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Water balance is controlled by a negative feedback loop involving the hormone <dfn class="term" data-def="Antidiuretic hormone &mdash; released by the pituitary gland to increase water reabsorption in the kidney tubules when the blood is too concentrated.">ADH</dfn> (antidiuretic hormone). When the blood becomes <strong>too concentrated</strong> (for example, after sweating or not drinking enough), receptors in the <dfn class="term" data-def="The region of the brain that monitors blood water concentration and coordinates the release of ADH from the pituitary gland.">hypothalamus</dfn> detect this change. The hypothalamus signals the <dfn class="term" data-def="A small endocrine gland at the base of the brain that releases hormones including ADH, FSH, and LH.">pituitary gland</dfn> to release more ADH into the blood. ADH travels to the kidneys and causes the tubules to become more permeable to water, so <strong>more water is reabsorbed</strong> back into the blood. The result is a small volume of concentrated urine.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">When the blood is <strong>too dilute</strong> (for example, after drinking a lot of water), less ADH is released. The kidney tubules stay less permeable, so <strong>less water is reabsorbed</strong>. The result is a large volume of dilute urine. This is a classic example of <dfn class="term" data-def="A control mechanism where a change in a condition triggers a response that reverses the change, bringing the system back to its set point.">negative feedback</dfn> &mdash; the system automatically corrects itself to maintain a stable internal environment.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>More ADH = more water reabsorbed = small volume of concentrated urine.</strong> Less ADH = less water reabsorbed = large volume of dilute urine. The hypothalamus monitors blood concentration; the pituitary gland releases ADH.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Kidney Failure: Dialysis and Transplants</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">If both kidneys fail, waste products such as urea build up in the blood and water balance is lost. Without treatment, kidney failure is fatal. There are two options: <strong>kidney dialysis</strong> or a <strong>kidney transplant</strong>.</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Dialysis: How It Works</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n16">In <dfn class="term" data-def="A medical treatment that artificially filters the blood using a machine when the kidneys have failed.">dialysis</dfn>, the patient&rsquo;s blood is passed through a machine containing a partially permeable membrane. On the other side of the membrane flows <dfn class="term" data-def="A carefully prepared solution used in dialysis that contains the correct concentrations of glucose, ions, and water to control what moves out of the blood.">dialysis fluid</dfn>, which has the same concentration of glucose and useful ions as normal blood, but contains no urea. Urea and excess ions diffuse from the blood (high concentration) across the membrane into the dialysis fluid (low concentration) down a concentration gradient. The dialysis fluid flows in the <strong>opposite direction</strong> to the blood to maintain a steep concentration gradient along the whole length of the membrane. Glucose is not removed because its concentration in the dialysis fluid matches the blood.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Advantages:</strong> Dialysis keeps patients alive while waiting for a transplant and is widely available. <strong>Disadvantages:</strong> Treatment takes 3&ndash;4 hours, three times per week. Patients must follow a restricted diet. There is a risk of infection at the point where blood leaves and re-enters the body. It is expensive for the NHS in the long term.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Kidney Transplants: Benefits and Risks</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n18">A kidney transplant replaces a failed kidney with a healthy one from a donor. The donor can be a living relative or someone who has recently died (deceased donor). The donor kidney must be a close tissue match to reduce the risk of <dfn class="term" data-def="When the body&rsquo;s immune system recognises a transplanted organ as foreign and attacks it.">rejection</dfn>, where the recipient&rsquo;s immune system attacks the new kidney as if it were a foreign pathogen.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Advantages:</strong> A successful transplant frees the patient from dialysis and dietary restrictions, allowing a much more normal life. It is cheaper for the NHS in the long term. <strong>Disadvantages:</strong> There is a shortage of donor kidneys and long waiting lists. The patient must take <dfn class="term" data-def="Drugs that suppress the immune system to prevent rejection of a transplanted organ.">immunosuppressant</dfn> drugs for life to prevent rejection, which increases the risk of infections. There are also risks from the surgery itself.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Plant Hormones: Auxins and Tropisms</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Plants cannot move to find better conditions, but they can grow towards or away from stimuli such as light and gravity. These directional growth responses are called <dfn class="term" data-def="A directional growth response of a plant towards or away from a stimulus such as light or gravity.">tropisms</dfn>. Tropisms are controlled by plant hormones, particularly <dfn class="term" data-def="A plant hormone produced in the tips of shoots and roots that controls cell elongation and directional growth responses.">auxin</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Phototropism</strong> is the growth response to light. Shoots show <strong>positive phototropism</strong> &mdash; they grow towards the light. Auxin is produced in the tip of the shoot. When light hits one side, auxin moves to the <strong>shaded side</strong> of the shoot. This higher concentration of auxin on the shaded side causes cells there to elongate more, bending the shoot towards the light source.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In shoots, auxin <strong>promotes</strong> cell elongation (more auxin = more growth). In roots, auxin <strong>inhibits</strong> cell elongation (more auxin = less growth). This opposite effect explains why shoots grow towards light but roots grow downwards.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Gravitropism</strong> (also called geotropism) is the growth response to gravity. Roots show <strong>positive gravitropism</strong> &mdash; they grow downwards, towards gravity. When a root grows horizontally, auxin accumulates on the <strong>lower side</strong>. Because auxin <em>inhibits</em> cell elongation in roots, the lower side grows more slowly than the upper side, causing the root to bend downwards. Shoots show <strong>negative gravitropism</strong> &mdash; they grow upwards, away from gravity.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n25"><dfn class="term" data-def="A group of plant hormones that promote seed germination, stem elongation, and flowering.">Gibberellins</dfn> are plant hormones involved in seed germination, stem elongation, and flowering. They stimulate the breakdown of starch stores in seeds, providing energy for the embryo to begin growing. Gibberellins can also promote the growth of seedless fruit.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26"><dfn class="term" data-def="A gaseous plant hormone that promotes fruit ripening and controls leaf fall.">Ethene</dfn> is a gaseous hormone that promotes fruit ripening. It is used commercially to ripen bananas and tomatoes after they have been transported while still unripe. Plants also use auxins in weedkillers: synthetic auxins cause broad-leaved weeds to grow uncontrollably and die, while narrow-leaved grasses are less affected.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n28">For 6-mark extended response questions on the kidney, structure your answer clearly: describe ultrafiltration first (what is filtered and why), then selective reabsorption (what is reabsorbed and how), then link to ADH and water balance. Use key terms like &lsquo;concentration gradient&rsquo;, &lsquo;partially permeable membrane&rsquo;, and &lsquo;active transport&rsquo; to show the examiner you understand the underlying science. When comparing dialysis and transplants, always give advantages <em>and</em> disadvantages of both.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n29">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n30">The kidney filters blood by ultrafiltration in the glomerulus, then selectively reabsorbs glucose, water, and ions in the tubules &mdash; urea is excreted as urine.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">ADH controls water balance: more ADH means more water reabsorbed and concentrated urine; less ADH means dilute urine. This is an example of negative feedback.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Auxin causes shoots to grow towards light (positive phototropism) and roots to grow downwards (positive gravitropism) &mdash; its effect is opposite in shoots vs roots.</li></ul>
