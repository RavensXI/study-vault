# Trophic Levels and Transfer of Biomass

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Trophic Levels?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All organisms in an ecosystem can be placed into <dfn class="term" data-def="The stages in a food chain, numbered from level 1 (producers) upwards to top predators.">trophic levels</dfn> depending on how they get their food. The word &ldquo;trophic&rdquo; comes from the Greek word for feeding. Understanding trophic levels helps us see how energy and <dfn class="term" data-def="The total mass of living material in an organism or at a particular trophic level.">biomass</dfn> move through an ecosystem.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><strong>Trophic level 1 &mdash; Producers:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Organisms that make their own food by photosynthesis, such as green plants and algae.">Producers</dfn> are organisms that make their own food using energy from the Sun through photosynthesis. All green plants and algae are producers. They form the base of every food chain because they convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4"><strong>Trophic level 2 &mdash; Primary consumers:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Herbivores that eat producers &mdash; the first consumers in a food chain.">Primary consumers</dfn> are herbivores that eat producers. Examples include rabbits, caterpillars, and zooplankton. They obtain energy by digesting the biomass of the plants they eat.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n5"><strong>Trophic level 3 &mdash; Secondary consumers:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Carnivores or omnivores that eat primary consumers.">Secondary consumers</dfn> are carnivores or omnivores that eat primary consumers. Examples include frogs, small birds, and foxes.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6"><strong>Trophic level 4 &mdash; Tertiary consumers:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Top predators that eat secondary consumers and have no natural predators themselves.">Tertiary consumers</dfn> are top predators that eat secondary consumers. Examples include hawks, sharks, and lions. They sit at the top of the food chain and are rarely eaten by other animals.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Trophic levels always follow the same order: producers &rarr; primary consumers &rarr; secondary consumers &rarr; tertiary consumers. Each level depends on the one below it for energy.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Food Chains and Food Webs</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A diagram showing the transfer of energy from one organism to the next, starting with a producer.">food chain</dfn> is a simple diagram that shows the transfer of energy from one organism to the next in a single pathway. The arrows in a food chain always point in the direction that energy flows &mdash; from the organism being eaten to the organism doing the eating. For example: grass &rarr; rabbit &rarr; fox &rarr; eagle.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">In reality, most organisms eat more than one type of food and are eaten by more than one predator. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A network of interconnected food chains showing the complex feeding relationships in an ecosystem.">food web</dfn> shows these interconnected feeding relationships. Food webs give a more realistic picture of an ecosystem because they show how changes in one population can affect many other species.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Pyramids of Biomass</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A bar chart drawn to scale showing the biomass at each trophic level, with producers at the base.">pyramid of biomass</dfn> represents the total biomass at each trophic level in a food chain. Each bar is drawn to scale, with the producer level at the bottom and the top predator at the top. The width of each bar represents the amount of biomass at that level.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Pyramids of biomass almost always form a pyramid shape &mdash; wide at the bottom and narrow at the top. This is because biomass is lost at every trophic level, so there is always less biomass at higher levels than at lower ones.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Pyramids of biomass are more reliable than pyramids of numbers. A single oak tree (one organism) can support thousands of caterpillars, which would make a pyramid of numbers look inverted &mdash; but the pyramid of biomass still shows the tree has more biomass than all the caterpillars combined.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">How Is Biomass Lost Between Trophic Levels?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Not all the biomass from one trophic level is transferred to the next. In fact, a large proportion is lost at each stage. This is why food chains rarely have more than four or five trophic levels &mdash; by the time energy has passed through several levels, there is not enough biomass left to support another level of consumers.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Biomass and energy are lost between trophic levels in several ways:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Not all organisms are eaten:</strong> Some organisms die of old age, disease, or other causes before being consumed by the next trophic level. Their biomass passes to <dfn class="term" data-def="Organisms such as bacteria and fungi that break down dead organic matter, returning nutrients to the soil.">decomposers</dfn> instead.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Not all parts are digested:</strong> Some materials, such as bones, teeth, hair, feathers, and cellulose from plant cell walls, cannot be digested. These are egested as faeces and become available to decomposers rather than being absorbed by the consumer.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Energy is used in life processes:</strong> Organisms at every trophic level use energy from the biomass they consume for <dfn class="term" data-def="Chemical reactions in cells that break down glucose to release energy for life processes.">respiration</dfn>. This energy is used for movement, maintaining body temperature (especially in mammals and birds), growth, reproduction, and other metabolic processes. The energy released by respiration is transferred to the surroundings as heat and cannot be passed to the next trophic level.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Excretory products:</strong> Organisms produce waste products such as urea (in urine) and carbon dioxide. These contain energy and biomass that are lost from the food chain.</p>

This is sometimes called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The approximation that only about 10% of biomass at one trophic level is transferred to the next level.">10% rule</dfn>. For example, if producers contain 10,000 kJ of energy, primary consumers receive approximately 1,000 kJ, secondary consumers receive approximately 100 kJ, and tertiary consumers receive only about 10 kJ.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25">This explains why top predators are always rare &mdash; there simply is not enough energy reaching the top of the food chain to support large populations. It also explains why food chains rarely exceed four or five trophic levels.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Calculating Efficiency of Biomass Transfer</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The AQA specification requires you to calculate the efficiency of biomass transfer between trophic levels. The formula is:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Efficiency = (biomass transferred to the next level &divide; biomass at the previous level) &times; 100</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">For example, if producers contain 5,000 kg of biomass and primary consumers contain 600 kg, the efficiency of transfer is: (600 &divide; 5,000) &times; 100 = 12%.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n30">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Learn the efficiency formula: (biomass at next level &divide; biomass at previous level) &times; 100. You may also be asked to rearrange it to calculate how much biomass is transferred or how much was available at the previous level.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n36">Why Does This Matter for Food Security?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n37">Understanding biomass transfer has practical implications for feeding the human population. Because energy is lost at every trophic level, eating plants (trophic level 1) is a more efficient way to feed people than eating animals (trophic level 2 or higher). For example, the same area of farmland used to grow grain could feed far more people directly than if the grain were first fed to cattle and the cattle then eaten.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n38">This is one reason why some scientists argue that reducing meat consumption could help address global food security challenges. However, grazing animals can use land that is not suitable for growing crops, and animal products are important sources of essential nutrients.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n39">When answering questions about biomass transfer, always state <strong>where</strong> the biomass goes &mdash; respiration (released as heat), egestion (faeces), excretion (urine, CO&sub2;), and organisms that are not eaten. Simply saying &ldquo;energy is lost&rdquo; will not earn full marks &mdash; you must explain <strong>how</strong> it is lost. For calculation questions, show your working clearly and include the correct unit (%). If asked to &ldquo;explain&rdquo; why pyramids of biomass get smaller at each level, link your answer back to energy loss through respiration.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n40">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n41">Trophic levels describe an organism&rsquo;s position in a food chain: producers (level 1), primary consumers (level 2), secondary consumers (level 3), and tertiary consumers (level 4).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42">Biomass is lost between trophic levels through respiration (heat), egestion (faeces), excretion, and organisms not being eaten &mdash; so only about 10% passes to the next level.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n43">Pyramids of biomass show the total biomass at each trophic level and are always pyramid-shaped because biomass decreases at each level.</li></ul>
