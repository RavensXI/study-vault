# DNA Structure, Cloning and the History of Genetics

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Structure of DNA</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="Deoxyribonucleic acid &mdash; the molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the development, functioning, and reproduction of all living organisms.">DNA</dfn> (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that carries the genetic code in living organisms. It is found inside the <dfn class="term" data-def="The organelle in a eukaryotic cell that contains the cell&rsquo;s genetic material (DNA) organised into chromosomes.">nucleus</dfn> of cells, packaged into structures called chromosomes. DNA is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A large molecule made by joining many smaller molecules (monomers) together in a chain. DNA is a polymer of nucleotides.">polymer</dfn> &mdash; a long chain molecule made from many smaller repeating units called <dfn class="term" data-def="The repeating monomer unit of DNA, consisting of a sugar, a phosphate group, and one of four bases (A, T, C, or G).">nucleotides</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Each nucleotide consists of three parts: a sugar molecule, a phosphate group, and one of four bases. The four bases are <strong>adenine (A)</strong>, <strong>thymine (T)</strong>, <strong>cytosine (C)</strong>, and <strong>guanine (G)</strong>. Two strands of nucleotides wind around each other to form a shape called a <dfn class="term" data-def="The twisted ladder shape of a DNA molecule, formed by two strands of nucleotides wound around each other.">double helix</dfn>.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>DNA is a polymer made of two strands of <strong>nucleotides</strong> twisted into a <strong>double helix</strong>. The bases always pair in the same way: <strong>A with T</strong> and <strong>C with G</strong>. This is called <strong>complementary base pairing</strong>.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n5">The two strands are held together by weak hydrogen bonds between the bases. The bases always pair in a specific way: adenine (A) always pairs with thymine (T), and cytosine (C) always pairs with guanine (G). This is known as <dfn class="term" data-def="The specific pairing of bases in DNA where adenine always bonds with thymine, and cytosine always bonds with guanine.">complementary base pairing</dfn>. Because of this rule, if you know the sequence of bases on one strand, you can work out the sequence on the other strand.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Protein Synthesis</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A short section of DNA on a chromosome that codes for the sequence of amino acids needed to make a specific protein.">gene</dfn> is a small section of DNA that codes for a particular sequence of <dfn class="term" data-def="Small organic molecules that are the building blocks of proteins. There are 20 different amino acids used by human cells.">amino acids</dfn>, which are assembled to make a specific protein. Different proteins are made by arranging amino acids in different orders. Every three bases along a gene form a <dfn class="term" data-def="A sequence of three bases on DNA (or mRNA) that codes for one specific amino acid during protein synthesis.">triplet code</dfn> &mdash; each triplet codes for one amino acid.</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>How Proteins Are Made &mdash; Step by Step</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Step 1 &mdash; Transcription:</strong> In the nucleus, the two strands of DNA unzip. One strand is used as a template to make a molecule of <dfn class="term" data-def="Messenger RNA &mdash; a single-stranded copy of a gene that carries the genetic code from the nucleus to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm.">mRNA</dfn> (messenger RNA). The mRNA is a single-stranded copy of the gene. It carries the genetic code out of the nucleus and into the cytoplasm.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Step 2 &mdash; Translation:</strong> The mRNA attaches to a <dfn class="term" data-def="A small organelle in the cytoplasm where amino acids are assembled into proteins by reading the code on mRNA.">ribosome</dfn> in the cytoplasm. The ribosome reads the mRNA in groups of three bases (triplets). Each triplet codes for a specific amino acid. The amino acids are brought to the ribosome and joined together in the correct order to form a long chain &mdash; this chain folds into a protein.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n10">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A random change in the sequence of bases in DNA. Mutations can occur naturally during DNA replication or be caused by environmental factors such as radiation.">mutation</dfn> is a change in the sequence of bases in DNA. Mutations can occur naturally when DNA is copied before cell division, or they can be caused by environmental factors such as radiation or certain chemicals. If a mutation changes the triplet code, a different amino acid may be inserted, which could produce a different protein that may not function correctly. This is the basis of many inherited diseases.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Cloning</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A genetically identical copy of an organism, produced asexually from a single parent.">clone</dfn> is a genetically identical copy of an organism. Cloning occurs naturally in plants (e.g. strawberry runners) and in identical twins in animals. Scientists have also developed artificial cloning techniques.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Cloning in Plants: Cuttings and Tissue Culture</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Cuttings:</strong> An older and simpler method of plant cloning. A small section of a plant (usually a stem) is cut away and placed in damp compost. The cutting develops roots and grows into a new plant that is genetically identical to the parent. Rooting powder containing auxins can speed up root growth.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Tissue culture:</strong> A more modern technique. A small group of cells is taken from a parent plant and grown on a nutrient medium containing plant hormones under sterile conditions. <dfn class="term" data-def="A technique for cloning plants by growing small groups of cells on a sterile nutrient medium containing plant hormones.">Tissue culture</dfn> can produce many more clones than cuttings, is less likely to damage the original plant, and allows large numbers of identical plants to be produced quickly. It is used commercially to produce disease-resistant crop plants with high yields.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Cloning in Animals: Embryo Transplants and Adult Cell Cloning</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Embryo transplants:</strong> An embryo is produced through selective breeding of animals with desirable characteristics. Before the cells of the embryo become specialised, they are split apart. Each group of cells is transplanted into a host mother, where it develops into a genetically identical offspring. This allows multiple cloned offspring to be produced from a single embryo.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Adult cell cloning:</strong> The nucleus is removed from an unfertilised egg cell. The nucleus from an adult body cell (of the organism to be cloned) is inserted into the empty egg cell. An electric shock stimulates the egg cell to divide and develop into an embryo. The embryo is implanted into a host mother. The offspring is a clone of the adult that donated the body cell nucleus. Dolly the sheep, born in 1996, was the first mammal to be cloned from an adult cell.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In <strong>embryo transplants</strong>, cells are split from a developing embryo before they specialise. In <strong>adult cell cloning</strong>, the nucleus from an adult body cell replaces the nucleus in an egg cell. Both produce genetically identical offspring, but adult cell cloning does not involve sexual reproduction.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n18">Cloning has potential benefits: protecting endangered species from extinction, producing large numbers of crop plants or livestock with desirable characteristics, and possible medical applications. However, there are risks and concerns. Cloned organisms have reduced genetic diversity, making populations more vulnerable to disease. There are also ethical concerns about the use of embryos and the welfare of cloned animals, which may suffer health problems or have shortened lifespans.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">The History of Genetics</h2>

<div class="timeline" data-narration-id="n20">
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Mid-19th Century</div>
    <h4>Gregor Mendel&rsquo;s Pea Plant Experiments</h4><p><dfn class="term" data-def="An Austrian monk (1822&ndash;1884) who studied the inheritance of characteristics in pea plants and proposed the concept of &lsquo;units&rsquo; of inheritance, now known as genes.">Gregor Mendel</dfn> was an Austrian monk who carried out breeding experiments on pea plants. He studied traits such as flower colour, seed shape, plant height, and pod colour. Mendel cross-bred pure-breeding (homozygous) plants and observed how characteristics were passed to offspring. He proposed that characteristics are determined by &ldquo;units&rdquo; (now called genes) that are inherited in pairs &mdash; one from each parent.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">1858</div>
    <h4>Darwin and Wallace</h4><p><dfn class="term" data-def="English naturalist (1809&ndash;1882) who proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection after studying variation in species during his voyage on HMS Beagle.">Charles Darwin</dfn> and <dfn class="term" data-def="British naturalist (1823&ndash;1913) who independently developed a theory of evolution by natural selection at the same time as Darwin, and later pioneered the theory of speciation.">Alfred Russel Wallace</dfn> independently proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection. Darwin had spent decades collecting evidence, including studying finches on the Gal&aacute;pagos Islands. Wallace observed warning colouration in animals as supporting evidence. Their work was presented jointly at the Linnean Society in London in 1858.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Late 19th Century</div>
    <h4>Chromosomes Observed</h4><p>Scientists first observed the behaviour of chromosomes during cell division. However, Mendel&rsquo;s discoveries were not appreciated during his lifetime because no one understood the mechanism of inheritance.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Early 20th Century</div>
    <h4>Chromosomes Linked to Mendel&rsquo;s &ldquo;Units&rdquo;</h4><p>Scientists noticed that chromosomes and Mendel&rsquo;s &ldquo;units&rdquo; behaved in similar ways during cell division. This led to the understanding that Mendel&rsquo;s units of inheritance were carried on chromosomes.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="timeline-event">
    <div class="timeline-date">Mid-20th Century</div>
    <h4>DNA Structure Discovered</h4><p>Watson and Crick determined the double helix structure of DNA in 1953. Scientists also recognised that genes are sections of DNA that code for specific proteins, completing the link between Mendel&rsquo;s work and modern genetics.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Mendel&rsquo;s Contribution</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">Mendel&rsquo;s work was groundbreaking but went unrecognised during his lifetime because other scientists could not see how his &ldquo;units&rdquo; linked to physical structures in cells. It was only in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, after chromosomes and DNA were studied, that Mendel&rsquo;s ideas were rediscovered and their true significance understood. He is now known as the &ldquo;father of genetics.&rdquo;</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Why Were Mendel&rsquo;s Ideas Ignored?</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n23">Mendel published his results in 1866, but other scientists did not recognise their importance at the time. There were several reasons for this: Mendel worked in isolation as a monk and was not well connected in the scientific community; the concept of genes and chromosomes had not yet been discovered, so there was no known mechanism to explain his results; and his mathematical approach to biology was unusual for the time. It was not until 1900 that other scientists rediscovered his work and saw how it fitted with new discoveries about chromosomes.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Speciation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">Wallace pioneered the theory of <dfn class="term" data-def="The formation of a new species when populations of the same species become so different that they can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring.">speciation</dfn> &mdash; the process by which new species evolve from existing ones. Speciation begins when populations of the same species become separated, usually by a physical barrier such as a mountain range, river, or ocean. This is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The physical separation of populations of a species by a barrier such as a river, mountain range, or ocean, preventing them from interbreeding.">geographical isolation</dfn>.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Speciation occurs in four stages: <strong>geographical isolation</strong> separates two populations &rarr; different <strong>environmental conditions</strong> &rarr; <strong>mutations</strong> lead to new alleles and natural selection acts differently on each group &rarr; the populations become so different they can <strong>no longer interbreed</strong> to produce fertile offspring &mdash; they are now separate species.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n27">Once populations are isolated, they experience different environmental conditions. Random mutations produce new alleles in each population. Natural selection acts independently on each group, favouring different advantageous characteristics. Over a long period of time, the populations become so genetically different that they can no longer interbreed to produce fertile offspring. At this point, they have become two separate species.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n29">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n30">DNA is a polymer made of two strands of nucleotides twisted into a double helix, with complementary base pairing (A&ndash;T, C&ndash;G). Genes are sections of DNA where every three bases (a triplet) code for one amino acid during protein synthesis.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">Cloning produces genetically identical organisms. Plant cloning uses cuttings or tissue culture; animal cloning uses embryo transplants or adult cell cloning (where a body cell nucleus replaces an egg cell nucleus).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Mendel discovered &ldquo;units&rdquo; of inheritance through pea plant experiments, but his work was ignored until chromosomes and DNA were understood. Darwin and Wallace proposed natural selection, and Wallace pioneered the theory of speciation through geographical isolation.</li></ul>
