# Culturing Microorganisms and Plant Disease

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Culturing Microorganisms</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Bacteria are grown in laboratories for research, to test the effectiveness of antibiotics and disinfectants, and to study how diseases spread. To grow bacteria, scientists provide the nutrients and conditions they need to reproduce by <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which a bacterium divides into two identical daughter cells, doubling the population each generation.">binary fission</dfn> &mdash; a process where one bacterium splits into two identical cells.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Bacteria can be grown in a <dfn class="term" data-def="A liquid medium containing nutrients such as glucose, amino acids, and minerals that bacteria need to grow.">nutrient broth</dfn> (a liquid containing dissolved nutrients) or on <dfn class="term" data-def="A jelly-like growth medium made from seaweed extract, mixed with nutrients and poured into Petri dishes for growing bacteria.">agar</dfn> gel plates. Agar is a jelly-like substance extracted from seaweed. When mixed with nutrients and poured into a <dfn class="term" data-def="A shallow, lidded dish used to culture microorganisms on agar gel.">Petri dish</dfn>, it sets into a firm surface that bacteria can grow across, forming visible colonies.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Under ideal conditions, bacteria can divide by binary fission approximately every 20 minutes. Starting from one bacterium, this means there could be over 1 million bacteria after just 7 hours.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Aseptic Technique</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">When culturing bacteria, it is essential to prevent contamination by unwanted microorganisms. <dfn class="term" data-def="A set of procedures used to prevent contamination by unwanted microorganisms when handling cultures.">Aseptic technique</dfn> is the set of precautions taken to ensure that only the desired bacteria grow on the agar plate. Contamination could introduce harmful pathogens or produce unreliable results.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Steps in Aseptic Technique</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>1. Sterilise equipment:</strong> The Petri dish, agar, and all equipment must be sterilised before use. The <dfn class="term" data-def="A metal loop used to transfer bacteria from a culture to an agar plate, sterilised by heating in a Bunsen burner flame.">inoculating loop</dfn> is sterilised by passing it through a hot Bunsen burner flame until it glows red. This kills any bacteria already on the loop.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>2. Allow the loop to cool:</strong> After sterilising, the inoculating loop must be allowed to cool in the air without touching any surfaces. Placing it on the bench would pick up contaminants.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>3. Transfer the bacteria:</strong> The lid of the bacterial culture bottle is briefly removed and the neck of the bottle is passed through the Bunsen flame to kill any microorganisms around the opening. The cooled loop is dipped into the culture to pick up bacteria.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>4. Inoculate the agar plate:</strong> The lid of the Petri dish is lifted slightly &mdash; not removed completely &mdash; and the bacteria are spread in a zigzag pattern across the agar surface. The lid is replaced quickly to minimise exposure to airborne microorganisms.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>5. Seal and incubate:</strong> The Petri dish is secured with two pieces of tape (not sealed all the way round, to allow air in and prevent the growth of anaerobic pathogens). The plate is stored upside down to stop condensation dripping onto the colonies.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n12">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In school laboratories, agar plates are incubated at a maximum of 25&deg;C. This is because higher temperatures (such as 37&deg;C, used in industry) could encourage the growth of harmful human pathogens. At 25&deg;C, bacteria still grow but dangerous species are less likely to thrive.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n13">Investigating Antibiotics &mdash; Zones of Inhibition</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n14">One key application of culturing bacteria is the <strong>required practical</strong> that investigates the effect of antibiotics or disinfectants on bacterial growth. Different substances are compared by measuring how effectively they prevent bacteria from growing.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15">In this experiment, filter paper discs are soaked in different antibiotics or disinfectants and placed on an agar plate that has been inoculated with bacteria (a &ldquo;bacterial lawn&rdquo;). A control disc soaked in distilled water is also placed on the plate. After incubation, clear areas appear around the discs where the substance has killed or prevented bacterial growth. These clear areas are called <dfn class="term" data-def="The clear area around an antibiotic or disinfectant disc on an agar plate where bacteria have been killed or prevented from growing.">zones of inhibition</dfn>.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n16">The larger the zone of inhibition, the more effective the antibiotic or disinfectant is against that particular type of bacterium. However, a large zone does not mean the substance is effective against all bacteria &mdash; it may be useless against a different species.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">To calculate the area of a zone of inhibition, measure the diameter of the clear zone in mm, halve it to get the radius, and use the formula: <strong>area = &pi;r&sup2;</strong>. If the zone is not perfectly circular, measure the diameter in two directions and use the mean.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Worked Example: Calculating a Zone of Inhibition</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n18">A zone of inhibition has a diameter of 20 mm. The radius is 20 &divide; 2 = 10 mm. The area = &pi; &times; 10&sup2; = 3.14 &times; 100 = <strong>314 mm&sup2;</strong>.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19">If the diameters measured in two directions were 20 mm and 18 mm, the mean diameter = (20 + 18) &divide; 2 = 19 mm. Radius = 9.5 mm. Area = &pi; &times; 9.5&sup2; = 3.14 &times; 90.25 = <strong>283 mm&sup2;</strong>.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Plant Disease</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21">Plants can be infected by a range of pathogens including viruses, bacteria, fungi, and insects. <dfn class="term" data-def="A condition that disrupts the normal functioning of a plant, caused by pathogens, environmental stress, or mineral deficiency.">Plant disease</dfn> can reduce crop yields and threaten food security, so detecting and identifying diseases early is critical.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Examples of Plant Diseases</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV):</strong> A virus that causes a distinctive mosaic pattern of discolouration on the leaves. This reduces the amount of chlorophyll available for photosynthesis, stunting the plant&rsquo;s growth.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Rose black spot:</strong> A fungal disease that causes purple or black spots on leaves. Infected leaves turn yellow and drop early, reducing photosynthesis. It spreads through water and wind.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Aphids:</strong> <dfn class="term" data-def="Small sap-sucking insects that feed on plant phloem, weakening the plant and sometimes transmitting viral diseases.">Aphids</dfn> are small insects that feed on plant sap from the phloem. They weaken the plant by removing sugars and amino acids, and can act as vectors, transmitting viral diseases from one plant to another.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Detecting Plant Disease</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Plant diseases can be detected in several ways. Gardeners, farmers, and scientists look for visible signs such as stunted growth, spots or lesions on leaves, areas of decay or rot, malformed stems or leaves, discolouration, and the presence of pests. These symptoms help narrow down the possible cause.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">To confirm a diagnosis, scientists can use <strong>identification keys</strong> (dichotomous keys that guide users through a series of yes/no questions), <strong>testing kits</strong> that detect specific pathogens using monoclonal antibodies, or <strong>laboratory analysis</strong> including DNA testing or culturing the pathogen.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Ion Deficiency in Plants</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Sometimes what looks like disease is actually caused by a mineral <dfn class="term" data-def="A shortage of essential mineral ions in the soil, causing visible symptoms in plants such as yellowing leaves or stunted growth.">ion deficiency</dfn>. Plants absorb mineral ions from the soil through their roots by active transport, and a shortage of key minerals produces distinctive symptoms.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Nitrate deficiency:</strong> Nitrate ions are needed to make amino acids and proteins. Without enough nitrate, the plant cannot grow properly and shows <strong>stunted growth</strong> and <strong>yellowing of older leaves</strong> (chlorosis). <strong>Magnesium deficiency:</strong> Magnesium ions are needed to make chlorophyll. Without enough magnesium, leaves turn <strong>yellow</strong> because chlorophyll cannot be produced, reducing photosynthesis.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n31">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Nitrate is needed for protein synthesis (making amino acids), so deficiency causes stunted growth. Magnesium is needed for chlorophyll production, so deficiency causes yellow leaves. Learn these two mineral deficiencies &mdash; they are commonly examined.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Plant Defence Responses</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Plants cannot run away from threats, so they have evolved a range of physical, chemical, and mechanical defences to protect themselves against pathogens and herbivores.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Plant Defence Mechanisms</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Physical defences:</strong> Cellulose cell walls act as a barrier to pathogens. Tough, waxy cuticles on leaf surfaces prevent water loss and pathogen entry. Layers of dead cells on the outside of stems (bark) form a protective shield.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Chemical defences:</strong> Some plants produce <strong>antibacterial chemicals</strong> that kill invading pathogens &mdash; for example, mint and witch hazel produce antimicrobial substances. Others produce <strong>poisons</strong> to deter herbivores, such as the toxins in foxgloves (digitalis) and deadly nightshade. Some plants also produce chemicals that taste bitter or cause irritation.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Mechanical defences:</strong> Thorns and spines (e.g. on roses and cacti) deter animals from eating the plant. Leaves that droop or curl when touched (e.g. the mimosa plant) may deter insects. Some plants have hairy stems or leaves that make it difficult for insects to land or feed.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n37">In AQA Biology exams, questions on culturing microorganisms often ask you to <strong>describe</strong> the aseptic technique step by step or <strong>explain</strong> why each step is necessary. Always link each step to preventing contamination. For zone of inhibition calculations, show your working clearly &mdash; write out the formula, substitute the values, and give units (mm&sup2;). Plant disease questions may ask you to compare physical, chemical, and mechanical defences, so learn at least one example of each type.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n38">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n39">Bacteria are cultured on agar plates using aseptic technique to prevent contamination. In schools, plates are incubated at a maximum of 25&deg;C to avoid growing harmful pathogens.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40">Zones of inhibition show how effective an antibiotic or disinfectant is &mdash; a larger clear area means greater effectiveness against that specific bacterium. Area = &pi;r&sup2;.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Plants can be affected by pathogens (TMV, rose black spot, aphids) and mineral deficiencies (nitrate for stunted growth, magnesium for yellow leaves). They defend themselves through physical, chemical, and mechanical adaptations.</li></ul>
