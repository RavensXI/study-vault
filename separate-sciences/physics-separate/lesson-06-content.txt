# Lenses, Visible Light and Black Body Radiation

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Lenses and Refraction</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A curved piece of transparent material (usually glass or plastic) that refracts light to form an image.">lens</dfn> forms an image by refracting light. When light passes from air into glass it slows down, and when it leaves the glass it speeds up again. The curved surfaces of a lens bend the light in a controlled way, either bringing rays together or spreading them apart.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">There are two key terms you must know for ray diagrams. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The point where parallel rays of light converge (convex) or appear to diverge from (concave) after passing through a lens.">principal focus</dfn> (also called the focal point, F) is the point where parallel rays of light either converge or appear to diverge from after passing through the lens. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The distance between the centre of a lens and its principal focus.">focal length</dfn> is the distance from the centre of the lens to the principal focus.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">Convex (Converging) Lenses</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A lens that is thickest at the centre and refracts parallel rays of light inwards so they meet at the principal focus &mdash; also called a converging lens.">convex lens</dfn> is thickest at the centre. It refracts parallel rays of light inwards so that they converge and meet at the principal focus on the other side of the lens. This is why convex lenses are also called converging lenses.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The type of image a convex lens produces depends on where the object is placed relative to the focal point. If the object is beyond the focal point (further from the lens than F), the lens produces a <dfn class="term" data-def="An image formed where light rays actually converge &mdash; it can be projected onto a screen.">real image</dfn>. A real image is formed where the refracted rays actually meet, and it can be captured on a screen. Real images are always inverted (upside down). Cameras and projectors use convex lenses in this way.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">If the object is placed between the focal point and the lens (closer than F), the refracted rays diverge. They appear to come from a point on the same side as the object, producing a <dfn class="term" data-def="An image formed where light rays appear to come from but do not actually meet &mdash; it cannot be projected onto a screen.">virtual image</dfn>. A virtual image is upright, magnified, and cannot be projected onto a screen. This is how a magnifying glass works.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n8">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Convex lens images:</strong> Object beyond F &rarr; real, inverted image (can be on a screen). Object between F and the lens &rarr; virtual, upright, magnified image (magnifying glass).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n9">Concave (Diverging) Lenses</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n10">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A lens that is thinnest at the centre and refracts parallel rays of light outwards so they appear to diverge from the principal focus &mdash; also called a diverging lens.">concave lens</dfn> is thinnest at the centre. It refracts parallel rays of light outwards so they spread apart. If you trace the diverging rays back, they appear to come from a single point on the same side as the incoming light &mdash; this is the principal focus of a concave lens.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11">A concave lens always produces a virtual image, no matter where the object is placed. The image is always upright, smaller than the object, and on the same side as the object. Concave lenses are used in spectacles to correct short-sightedness (myopia).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Magnification</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><dfn class="term" data-def="The ratio of image height to object height &mdash; it has no units.">Magnification</dfn> tells you how much bigger or smaller the image is compared to the object. It is calculated using the equation:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>magnification = image height &divide; object height</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Magnification is a ratio, so it has no units. Both heights must be measured in the same unit (mm or cm). A magnification greater than 1 means the image is larger than the object; less than 1 means it is smaller.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n18">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Magnification equation:</strong> magnification = image height &divide; object height. This is a ratio with no units. A magnification of 5 means the image is 5 times larger than the real object.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Visible Light and Colour</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Visible light is a small part of the <dfn class="term" data-def="The continuous range of electromagnetic waves ordered by wavelength and frequency &mdash; from radio waves (longest) to gamma rays (shortest).">electromagnetic spectrum</dfn>. It is the only part that human eyes can detect. The visible spectrum ranges from red (longest wavelength, about 700&thinsp;nm) to violet (shortest wavelength, about 400&thinsp;nm), with the colours blending smoothly into each other &mdash; they do not have sharp boundaries.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">The colour of an opaque object depends on which wavelengths of light it reflects and which it absorbs. A red jumper reflects red light and absorbs all other colours. A white object reflects all wavelengths equally, while a black object absorbs all wavelengths.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n22">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A transparent material that absorbs certain wavelengths of light and transmits others, so only selected colours pass through.">colour filter</dfn> works by absorbing certain wavelengths of light and transmitting others. A red filter absorbs all colours except red, so only red light passes through. A blue filter transmits only blue light.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23">When you view a coloured object under coloured light, the object can only reflect the colours present in the light. A red shirt under red light still looks red (it reflects the red light). But a red shirt under blue light looks black &mdash; there is no red light to reflect, and it absorbs the blue light.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Transparent</strong> objects transmit all light through them (e.g. clear glass). <strong>Translucent</strong> objects transmit some light but scatter it, so you cannot see clearly through them (e.g. frosted glass). <strong>Opaque</strong> objects do not transmit any light.</p>

All the reflected rays travel in the same direction, producing a clear reflected image.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26"><dfn class="term" data-def="Reflection of light from a rough surface where rays scatter in many directions &mdash; no clear image is formed.">Diffuse reflection</dfn> occurs when light hits a rough surface (such as paper or fabric). The reflected rays scatter in many different directions, so no clear image is formed. Most objects around us cause diffuse reflection.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Black Body Radiation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28">All objects, no matter what their temperature, continuously emit and absorb <dfn class="term" data-def="Electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths longer than visible light but shorter than microwaves &mdash; associated with thermal energy transfer.">infrared radiation</dfn>. The hotter an object is, the more infrared radiation it emits in a given time. This is why you can feel the warmth radiating from a hot radiator or a bonfire even without touching it.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An idealised object that absorbs all electromagnetic radiation that falls on it, reflecting and transmitting none &mdash; it is also the best possible emitter of radiation.">perfect black body</dfn> is defined as an object that absorbs all of the radiation incident upon it. It does not reflect or transmit any radiation. Because a good absorber is also a good emitter, a perfect black body is also the best possible emitter of radiation at any given temperature.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n30">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A perfect black body absorbs <strong>all</strong> radiation falling on it and emits radiation at the maximum possible rate for its temperature. Dark, matt surfaces are better absorbers and emitters than light, shiny surfaces.</p>
</div>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n31">As the temperature of an object increases, the intensity of radiation emitted at every wavelength increases. However, the intensity of shorter wavelengths increases more rapidly. This is why very hot objects (such as stars) shift from glowing red to orange, yellow, white, and eventually blue-white as they get hotter.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n32">An object is in <dfn class="term" data-def="The state reached when an object absorbs radiation at the same rate as it emits radiation, so its temperature remains constant.">thermal equilibrium</dfn> when the rate at which it absorbs radiation equals the rate at which it emits radiation &mdash; its temperature stays constant. If an object absorbs radiation faster than it emits it, the temperature rises. If it emits faster than it absorbs, the temperature falls.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33">The temperature of the Earth depends on the balance between energy absorbed from the Sun, energy reflected back into space, and energy emitted by the Earth into space. Changes to the atmosphere (such as increased greenhouse gases) can alter this balance and affect the Earth&rsquo;s temperature.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">Questions on lenses often ask you to draw or interpret ray diagrams &mdash; practise these until you can do them quickly and accurately. For magnification calculations, always show your working and state that magnification has no units. Black body radiation questions may ask you to explain why a dark surface cools faster than a shiny one, or to describe how the Earth&rsquo;s temperature is maintained &mdash; link your answer to the balance between absorption and emission of radiation.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">Convex lenses converge light and can produce real (inverted) or virtual (upright, magnified) images depending on object position. Concave lenses diverge light and always produce virtual, upright, smaller images.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">The colour of an opaque object depends on which wavelengths it reflects &mdash; colour filters absorb certain wavelengths and transmit others.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">All objects emit and absorb infrared radiation. A perfect black body absorbs all incident radiation and is the best emitter. The Earth&rsquo;s temperature depends on the balance between absorbed and emitted radiation.</li></ul>
