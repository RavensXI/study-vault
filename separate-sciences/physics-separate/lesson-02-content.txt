# Gas Pressure and Pressure in Fluids

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Gas Pressure and the Particle Model</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Gases are made up of tiny particles that move rapidly in random directions. When these particles collide with the walls of their container, they exert a force on the surface. The total force per unit area created by billions of these collisions is what we measure as <dfn class="term" data-def="The force per unit area exerted by gas particles colliding with the walls of their container, measured in pascals (Pa).">gas pressure</dfn>. This links to AQA spec 4.3.3.2 and is a key concept for understanding how gases behave when conditions change.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Pressure is measured in <dfn class="term" data-def="The SI unit of pressure, equal to one newton per square metre (1 Pa = 1 N/m&sup2;).">pascals</dfn> (Pa), where 1 Pa = 1 N/m&sup2;. You may also see kilopascals (kPa) used, where 1 kPa = 1000 Pa.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Gas pressure is caused by particles colliding with the walls of their container. The more frequent or forceful the collisions, the higher the pressure.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">The Effect of Temperature on Gas Pressure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">When a gas is heated in a sealed container (constant volume), the particles gain <dfn class="term" data-def="The energy stored in the movement of particles &mdash; increasing temperature increases the kinetic energy of gas particles.">kinetic energy</dfn> and move faster. Faster-moving particles hit the container walls more frequently and with greater force. Both effects increase the gas pressure.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">This explains everyday observations. For example, an aerosol can warns against heating because the increased pressure inside the sealed container could cause it to explode. Conversely, if a gas is cooled, its particles slow down, collisions become less frequent and less forceful, and the pressure drops.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Gas Pressure and Volume &mdash; Boyle&rsquo;s Law</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">For a fixed mass of gas at a constant temperature, increasing the <dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of three-dimensional space occupied by a substance, measured in cubic metres (m&sup3;) or cubic centimetres (cm&sup3;).">volume</dfn> of the container means the particles have more space to travel before hitting the walls. Collisions become less frequent, so the pressure decreases. Decreasing the volume has the opposite effect &mdash; particles are closer together, collisions are more frequent, and the pressure increases.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">This inverse relationship is described by <dfn class="term" data-def="The law stating that for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, pressure multiplied by volume is constant: pV = constant.">Boyle&rsquo;s law</dfn>:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact &mdash; Boyle&rsquo;s Law</div>
  <p><strong>pV = constant</strong> (at constant temperature). If pressure increases, volume decreases proportionally, and vice versa. The equation to use is: <strong>p<sub>1</sub>V<sub>1</sub> = p<sub>2</sub>V<sub>2</sub></strong>.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n14">A gas occupies a volume of 0.040 m&sup3; at a pressure of 200 kPa. The gas is compressed at constant temperature until its volume is 0.010 m&sup3;. Calculate the new pressure.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Step 1:</strong> Write out Boyle&rsquo;s law: p<sub>1</sub>V<sub>1</sub> = p<sub>2</sub>V<sub>2</sub></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Step 2:</strong> Substitute: 200 &times; 0.040 = p<sub>2</sub> &times; 0.010</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Step 3:</strong> Solve: p<sub>2</sub> = (200 &times; 0.040) &divide; 0.010 = 8.0 &divide; 0.010 = <strong>800 kPa</strong></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18">The volume decreased by a factor of 4, so the pressure increased by a factor of 4 &mdash; this confirms the inverse relationship.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Pressure in Fluids</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that can flow &mdash; both liquids and gases are fluids.">fluid</dfn> is any substance that can flow &mdash; this includes both liquids and gases. A fluid at rest exerts pressure on any surface in contact with it. This pressure acts in all directions at any given point in the fluid.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">The pressure in a fluid increases with depth. This is because the deeper you go, the greater the weight of the column of fluid above you. The equation for calculating pressure due to a column of liquid is:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact &mdash; Pressure in a Column of Liquid</div>
  <p><strong>p = h &times; &rho; &times; g</strong>, where p = pressure in pascals (Pa), h = height of the column in metres (m), &rho; = <dfn class="term" data-def="Mass per unit volume of a substance, measured in kg/m&sup3;. Denser fluids create greater pressure at the same depth.">density</dfn> of the liquid in kg/m&sup3;, and g = gravitational field strength in N/kg (on Earth, g = 9.8 N/kg).</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n23">This equation tells us three factors that affect pressure in a liquid: the <strong>depth</strong> (height of the column), the <strong>density</strong> of the liquid, and the <strong>gravitational field strength</strong>. A denser liquid (such as mercury compared to water) produces a higher pressure at the same depth. Similarly, a greater depth means a taller column of liquid pressing down, creating more pressure.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n26"><strong>Step 2:</strong> Substitute: p = 2.5 &times; 1000 &times; 9.8</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Step 3:</strong> Calculate: p = <strong>24,500 Pa</strong> (or 24.5 kPa)</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Atmospheric Pressure</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><dfn class="term" data-def="The pressure exerted by the weight of the atmosphere above a surface &mdash; approximately 101,325 Pa (about 101 kPa) at sea level.">Atmospheric pressure</dfn> is the pressure exerted by the weight of the air above a surface. At sea level, atmospheric pressure is approximately <strong>101 kPa</strong> (about 101,325 Pa). The atmosphere is a thin layer of gas attracted to the Earth by gravity.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30">As altitude increases, atmospheric pressure decreases. This is because at higher altitudes there is a shorter column of air above you, so there is less weight of air pressing down. The density of the atmosphere also decreases with altitude because gravity pulls gas molecules closer to the Earth&rsquo;s surface, meaning fewer molecules are present at higher altitudes.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n31">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude because there is less air above to exert a downward force. At sea level it is about 101 kPa; on a mountaintop it is significantly lower.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n32">You can think of atmospheric pressure as the result of the same p = h&rho;g relationship but applied to the column of air above you. The atmosphere does not have a clear boundary &mdash; it gradually thins out into space. This is why there is no sudden edge where atmospheric pressure drops to zero.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n33">Upthrust and Floating</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><dfn class="term" data-def="The upward force exerted on an object submerged in a fluid, caused by the difference in pressure between the top and bottom of the object.">Upthrust</dfn> is an upward force that acts on any object placed in a fluid. It is caused by the pressure difference between the top and bottom of the object. Because pressure increases with depth, the bottom of the object experiences a greater pressure (and therefore a greater force) than the top.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35">The result is a net upward force &mdash; the upthrust. Whether an object sinks or floats depends on how the upthrust compares to the object&rsquo;s weight:</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Sinking vs Floating &mdash; The Role of Density</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n36">If the weight of the object is <strong>greater</strong> than the upthrust, the object sinks. This happens when the object is denser than the fluid it is placed in. If the weight is <strong>equal</strong> to the upthrust, the object floats. This happens when the object displaces a volume of fluid whose weight matches its own weight.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n37">For example, a steel ship floats despite steel being denser than water because the hull is shaped to displace a large volume of water. The upthrust from this displaced water is enough to support the ship&rsquo;s weight. A solid steel ball, however, sinks because it displaces a much smaller volume of water relative to its weight.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>How Upthrust Links to Pressure Difference</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n38">Consider a cube submerged in water. The pressure on the bottom face is greater than the pressure on the top face because the bottom is deeper. Since pressure = force &divide; area, and the top and bottom faces have the same area, the force on the bottom face is greater than the force on the top face. This difference in force is the upthrust.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n39">The sideways pressures on the left and right (and front and back) faces are equal and opposite, so they cancel out. Only the vertical pressure difference produces a net force &mdash; the upthrust acting upward.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n40">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>An object floats when the upthrust equals its weight. An object sinks when its weight exceeds the upthrust. Upthrust is caused by the pressure difference between the top and bottom of the object in a fluid.</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n41">For calculation questions using p = h&rho;g, always check your units &mdash; h must be in metres, &rho; in kg/m&sup3;, and g in N/kg to get pressure in pascals. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about gas pressure, always refer to <strong>particles</strong>, <strong>collisions</strong>, and <strong>force</strong> &mdash; the examiners want you to use the particle model, not just say &ldquo;pressure goes up.&rdquo; For Boyle&rsquo;s law calculations, write out p<sub>1</sub>V<sub>1</sub> = p<sub>2</sub>V<sub>2</sub> and substitute carefully before rearranging.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n42">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n43">Gas pressure is caused by particle collisions with container walls &mdash; increasing temperature or decreasing volume increases collision frequency and force, raising pressure. <strong>Boyle&rsquo;s law: pV = constant</strong> at constant temperature.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44">Pressure in a liquid increases with depth and depends on density and gravitational field strength: <strong>p = h&rho;g</strong>. Atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude because there is less air above.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n45">Upthrust is the upward force on an object in a fluid, caused by the pressure difference between its top and bottom. An object floats when upthrust equals weight; it sinks when weight exceeds upthrust.</li></ul>
