# Electromagnetic Induction and Transformers

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Generator Effect</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The induction of a potential difference (and current in a complete circuit) when a conductor moves through a magnetic field or when a magnetic field changes around a conductor.">generator effect</dfn> is the reverse of the motor effect. Instead of using a current to produce motion, motion is used to produce a current. When a wire or coil moves through a magnetic field &mdash; or when a magnetic field moves past a stationary conductor &mdash; a <dfn class="term" data-def="A voltage produced across a conductor when it experiences a changing magnetic field.">potential difference</dfn> is induced across the ends of the conductor. If the conductor is part of a complete circuit, this induced potential difference drives a current.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">This process is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The process of generating a potential difference across a conductor by changing the magnetic field around it or moving the conductor through a field.">electromagnetic induction</dfn>. It is the principle behind all generators, whether they power a bicycle light or a national power station.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>An induced potential difference is produced when there is <strong>relative movement</strong> between a conductor and a magnetic field. It does not matter which one moves &mdash; what matters is that the <dfn class="term" data-def="Lines representing the strength and direction of a magnetic field &mdash; the closer together the lines, the stronger the field.">magnetic field lines</dfn> being &ldquo;cut&rdquo; by the conductor are changing.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Factors Affecting the Induced Potential Difference</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The size of the induced potential difference can be increased in three ways:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>1. Increasing the speed of movement</strong> &mdash; moving the conductor or magnet faster means more field lines are cut per second, producing a larger voltage. <strong>2. Increasing the strength of the magnetic field</strong> &mdash; a stronger field means more flux is available to be cut. <strong>3. Increasing the number of turns on the coil</strong> &mdash; each turn of wire contributes its own induced voltage, so more turns means a higher total output.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n8">The <strong>direction</strong> of the induced current can be reversed by either reversing the direction of movement or reversing the polarity of the magnetic field. If both are reversed at the same time, the current direction stays the same.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Alternators and Dynamos</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of generator that produces alternating current (a.c.) by rotating a coil inside a magnetic field, using slip rings and brushes to connect the coil to an external circuit.">alternator</dfn> is a generator that produces <dfn class="term" data-def="Electric current that repeatedly reverses direction, typically at a frequency of 50 Hz in the UK mains supply.">alternating current (a.c.)</dfn>. Inside an alternator, a coil of wire rotates between the poles of a magnet. As the coil spins, each side of the coil alternately moves up and then down through the field, so the direction of the induced current reverses every half-turn. The coil is connected to the external circuit by <dfn class="term" data-def="Two continuous metal rings attached to the ends of a rotating coil in an alternator, allowing smooth electrical connection via brushes.">slip rings</dfn> and carbon brushes, which maintain a continuous but reversing connection.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A type of generator that produces direct current (d.c.) by using a split-ring commutator to reverse the connections every half-turn, so current always flows in one direction in the external circuit.">dynamo</dfn> produces <dfn class="term" data-def="Electric current that flows in one direction only.">direct current (d.c.)</dfn>. Its construction is almost identical to an alternator, but instead of slip rings it uses a <dfn class="term" data-def="A split ring of metal attached to a rotating coil that swaps the connections every half-turn, converting the alternating output of the coil into direct current.">split-ring commutator</dfn>. The commutator swaps the connections to the external circuit every half-turn, so although the current in the coil reverses, the current in the external circuit always flows in the same direction.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Alternator = slip rings = a.c. output.</strong> <strong>Dynamo = split-ring commutator = d.c. output.</strong> This is the key difference the exam tests. Alternators are used in power stations and cars; dynamos are used in some bicycle lights.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n14">Microphones and Loudspeakers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n15">Microphones and loudspeakers are practical applications of electromagnetic induction and the motor effect working in reverse.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">In a <strong>moving-coil microphone</strong>, sound waves hit a diaphragm, causing it to vibrate. Attached to the diaphragm is a coil of wire that sits inside a permanent magnet&rsquo;s field. As the diaphragm vibrates, the coil moves back and forth within the magnetic field, and an alternating potential difference is induced across the coil. The frequency of this a.c. signal matches the frequency of the incoming sound wave, which is how sound is converted into an electrical signal.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n17">A <strong>loudspeaker</strong> works in the opposite way. An alternating current is fed into a coil of wire (the voice coil) that sits in a permanent magnetic field. The changing current causes the coil to experience a force that alternates in direction (the motor effect), making the coil vibrate back and forth. The coil is attached to a cone, which pushes and pulls the surrounding air to produce sound waves. The frequency of the a.c. signal determines the pitch of the sound produced.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Transformers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A device that changes the potential difference of an alternating current supply, consisting of a primary coil and a secondary coil wound on an iron core.">transformer</dfn> is a device that changes the potential difference (voltage) of an a.c. supply. Transformers only work with alternating current because they rely on a continuously changing magnetic field.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">A transformer consists of two coils of insulated wire &mdash; the <dfn class="term" data-def="The input coil of a transformer, connected to the a.c. supply.">primary coil</dfn> and the <dfn class="term" data-def="The output coil of a transformer, from which the changed voltage is obtained.">secondary coil</dfn> &mdash; wound around a shared <dfn class="term" data-def="A loop of soft iron that links the primary and secondary coils in a transformer, carrying the changing magnetic field between them.">iron core</dfn>. The a.c. in the primary coil creates a changing magnetic field in the iron core. This changing field passes through the secondary coil and induces an alternating potential difference across it. The iron core is made of soft iron because it magnetises and demagnetises easily, efficiently transferring the changing field.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n24">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Transformers only work with <strong>a.c.</strong> (not d.c.) because a direct current produces a steady magnetic field, which does not change and therefore cannot induce a potential difference in the secondary coil.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n25">Step-Up and Step-Down Transformers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n26">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A transformer where the secondary coil has more turns than the primary coil, producing a higher output voltage than the input voltage.">step-up transformer</dfn> increases the potential difference. It has more turns on the secondary coil than on the primary coil. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A transformer where the secondary coil has fewer turns than the primary coil, producing a lower output voltage than the input voltage.">step-down transformer</dfn> decreases the potential difference. It has fewer turns on the secondary coil than on the primary coil.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The relationship between the voltages and the number of turns is given by the <dfn class="term" data-def="The ratio of the number of turns on the secondary coil to the number of turns on the primary coil in a transformer, which equals the ratio of secondary voltage to primary voltage.">turns ratio</dfn> equation:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>V<sub>p</sub> / V<sub>s</sub> = n<sub>p</sub> / n<sub>s</sub></strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">Where V<sub>p</sub> is the potential difference across the primary coil, V<sub>s</sub> is the potential difference across the secondary coil, n<sub>p</sub> is the number of turns on the primary coil, and n<sub>s</sub> is the number of turns on the secondary coil.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n34">Transformers in the National Grid</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n35">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A nationwide network of cables and transformers that links power stations to consumers, transmitting electricity at high voltage and low current to reduce energy losses.">National Grid</dfn> uses step-up and step-down transformers to transmit electricity efficiently across the country. Power stations generate electricity at around 25,000&thinsp;V. Step-up transformers at the power station increase this to 400,000&thinsp;V for transmission through overhead cables. Near homes and businesses, step-down transformers reduce the voltage back to 230&thinsp;V for safe domestic use.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n36">Transmitting at high voltage means the current is kept low (since power = voltage &times; current, and power stays the same). A lower current means less energy is wasted as heat in the cables, making the system more efficient. Without transformers, huge amounts of energy would be lost during transmission.</p>

<div class="collapsible">

    <span>Why High Voltage Reduces Energy Loss</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n37">Power transferred = voltage &times; current (P = V &times; I). For a fixed power, increasing voltage decreases current. The power wasted as heat in the cables = current&sup2; &times; resistance (P = I&sup2; &times; R). Since wasted power depends on current <em>squared</em>, even a small reduction in current dramatically reduces energy loss. This is why the National Grid transmits at 400,000&thinsp;V &mdash; the current is very small, so very little energy is lost as heat.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n38">Transformer calculation questions are very common in the exam. Always start by writing out the equation V<sub>p</sub> / V<sub>s</sub> = n<sub>p</sub> / n<sub>s</sub>, then substitute and rearrange. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions about the National Grid, link three ideas in a chain: step-up transformer increases voltage &rarr; this decreases current &rarr; less energy wasted as heat (P = I&sup2;R). For questions comparing alternators and dynamos, always name the specific component that differs: slip rings vs split-ring commutator.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n39">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n40">Electromagnetic induction occurs when a conductor moves through a magnetic field (or vice versa), inducing a potential difference &mdash; increased by faster movement, stronger fields, or more coil turns.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Alternators use slip rings to produce a.c.; dynamos use a split-ring commutator to produce d.c. Microphones use the generator effect; loudspeakers use the motor effect.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42">Transformers change a.c. voltage using the turns ratio V<sub>p</sub>/V<sub>s</sub> = n<sub>p</sub>/n<sub>s</sub>. The National Grid uses step-up transformers (high V, low I) to reduce energy loss in transmission cables.</li></ul>
