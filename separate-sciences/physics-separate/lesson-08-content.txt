# Space Physics: The Solar System and Beyond

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Our Solar System</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Our <dfn class="term" data-def="The Sun and all the objects that orbit it, including eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids and comets.">solar system</dfn> consists of the Sun at its centre, orbited by eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids and comets. The Sun is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A medium-sized star in the stable phase of its life cycle, fusing hydrogen into helium in its core.">main sequence star</dfn> &mdash; a medium-sized star that has been fusing hydrogen into helium for about 4.6 billion years. Everything in the solar system is held in orbit by the Sun&rsquo;s gravitational pull.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The eight planets, in order from the Sun, are Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. The first four are <strong>rocky planets</strong> (smaller, solid surfaces), while the outer four are <strong>gas giants</strong> (much larger, mostly gas and liquid). Beyond Neptune lies a region of dwarf planets including Pluto.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>The solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing cloud of dust and gas called a <strong>nebula</strong>. Gravity pulled material together to form the Sun, while leftover material clumped together to form the planets and other objects.</p>
</div>
<h3 data-narration-id="n5">Orbits in the Solar System</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n6">All planets orbit the Sun in slightly elliptical (oval-shaped) paths. Moons orbit planets in a similar way. <dfn class="term" data-def="The force of attraction between any two objects with mass. It holds planets in orbit around the Sun.">gravity</dfn> provides the centripetal force needed to keep an object moving in a curved path &mdash; without it, a planet would fly off in a straight line. The closer a planet is to the Sun, the stronger the gravitational pull and the faster it orbits.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n7">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An object that orbits a planet or star. Natural satellites include moons; artificial satellites are human-made objects such as the International Space Station.">satellite</dfn> is any object that orbits a planet or star. Satellites can be natural (like the Moon) or artificial (like the International Space Station). Artificial satellites orbit Earth at different heights depending on their purpose.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Natural satellites</strong> are objects that orbit planets due to gravity alone. The Moon is Earth&rsquo;s only natural satellite, but Jupiter has at least 95 known moons.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Artificial satellites</strong> are human-made objects placed into orbit for specific purposes. Communications satellites sit in <dfn class="term" data-def="An orbit where a satellite takes exactly 24 hours to complete one revolution, so it stays above the same point on Earth&rsquo;s surface.">geostationary orbit</dfn> (high altitude, 24-hour period, stays above the same point). Weather satellites use low polar orbits to scan the entire Earth.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n10">The Life Cycle of Stars</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Stars do not last forever. They are born, they live, and they die &mdash; and the way a star dies depends on its mass.</p>
<h3 data-narration-id="n12">Birth of a Star</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n13">All stars begin as a <dfn class="term" data-def="A large cloud of dust and gas in space from which stars are born when gravity causes the cloud to collapse.">nebula</dfn> &mdash; a vast cloud of hydrogen gas and dust. Gravity pulls the gas together, and as the cloud contracts it heats up. When the core reaches around 15 million &deg;C, <dfn class="term" data-def="The process by which small atomic nuclei combine to form a larger nucleus, releasing enormous amounts of energy. This powers stars.">nuclear fusion</dfn> begins &mdash; hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium, releasing enormous energy. At this stage the young star is called a <dfn class="term" data-def="A young, contracting star that has not yet begun nuclear fusion in its core.">protostar</dfn>. Once fusion is stable, it becomes a main sequence star.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>A main sequence star is in a stable phase where the outward pressure from nuclear fusion is <strong>balanced</strong> by the inward pull of gravity. This balance is called <strong>hydrostatic equilibrium</strong> and can last for billions of years. Our Sun is currently a main sequence star.</p>
</div>
<!-- DIAGRAM -->
<h3 data-narration-id="n15">Death of a Star &mdash; Sun-Sized Stars</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n16">When a Sun-sized star runs out of hydrogen fuel, the core contracts while the outer layers expand and cool, turning it into a <dfn class="term" data-def="A large, cool star formed when a main sequence star exhausts its hydrogen fuel and its outer layers expand.">red giant</dfn>. The red giant sheds its outer layers into space, forming a planetary nebula. The remaining core collapses to form a <dfn class="term" data-def="The hot, dense remnant core of a Sun-sized star after it has shed its outer layers. About the size of Earth but extremely dense.">white dwarf</dfn> &mdash; an incredibly dense remnant about the size of Earth that slowly cools and fades.</p>
<h3 data-narration-id="n17">Death of a Star &mdash; Massive Stars</h3>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Stars much larger than the Sun follow a more dramatic path. After the main sequence phase, a massive star swells into a <dfn class="term" data-def="An extremely large, cool star formed when a massive star exhausts its hydrogen fuel. Much bigger than a red giant.">red supergiant</dfn>. When the core runs out of fuel, it collapses violently, triggering an explosion called a <dfn class="term" data-def="A massive explosion that occurs when a large star collapses at the end of its life, briefly outshining an entire galaxy.">supernova</dfn>. A supernova briefly outshines an entire galaxy and blasts heavy elements (iron, gold, uranium) into space.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">What remains after the supernova depends on the original mass of the star:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n20">
<li><strong>Neutron star</strong> &mdash; if the remaining core is between about 1.4 and 3 solar masses, it collapses into a <dfn class="term" data-def="An extremely dense remnant of a massive star after a supernova, composed almost entirely of neutrons. A teaspoon would weigh about a billion tonnes.">neutron star</dfn>. Neutron stars are incredibly dense &mdash; a teaspoon of neutron star material would weigh about a billion tonnes on Earth.</li>
<li><strong>Black hole</strong> &mdash; if the remaining core is above about 3 solar masses, gravity is so strong that nothing can stop the collapse. The core forms a <dfn class="term" data-def="A region of space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it.">black hole</dfn>, a region of space where gravity is so intense that not even light can escape.</li>
</ul>

<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Sun-sized stars:</strong> Nebula &rarr; Protostar &rarr; Main sequence star &rarr; Red giant &rarr; Planetary nebula &rarr; White dwarf. The process is relatively gentle &mdash; no explosion occurs. The white dwarf slowly cools and fades over billions of years.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Massive stars:</strong> Nebula &rarr; Protostar &rarr; Main sequence star &rarr; Red supergiant &rarr; Supernova &rarr; Neutron star or Black hole. The supernova is one of the most energetic events in the universe. The heavy elements scattered by supernovae eventually form new stars, planets and even living things &mdash; we are literally made of stardust.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Elements heavier than iron can only be created during a <strong>supernova</strong>. Every atom of gold, silver and uranium on Earth was forged in the explosion of a massive star billions of years ago.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n24">Red-Shift and the Expanding Universe</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n25">When astronomers analysed the <dfn class="term" data-def="The pattern of dark lines seen when light from a star is split into a spectrum, caused by elements in the star absorbing specific wavelengths.">absorption spectra</dfn> of distant galaxies, they found the dark lines were shifted towards the red end of the spectrum. This effect is called <dfn class="term" data-def="The shift of light from a galaxy towards longer (redder) wavelengths, indicating the galaxy is moving away from the observer.">red-shift</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n26">Red-shift happens because the galaxy is moving <strong>away</strong> from us. As the galaxy recedes, the light waves are stretched out, increasing their wavelength and shifting them towards the red end of the spectrum. This is similar to how the pitch of an ambulance siren drops as it drives away from you (the <dfn class="term" data-def="The change in frequency or wavelength of a wave as the source moves towards or away from the observer.">Doppler effect</dfn>).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27">The American astronomer Edwin Hubble made a crucial discovery: <strong>the further away a galaxy is, the greater its red-shift</strong>. This means more distant galaxies are moving away from us faster. This relationship is known as <strong>Hubble&rsquo;s Law</strong> and it provided the first observational evidence that the universe is expanding.</p>
<div class="collapsible">

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n28">If all galaxies are moving away from us, and the most distant ones are moving fastest, the universe must be expanding in every direction. It is not that galaxies are flying through space &mdash; space itself is stretching, carrying galaxies with it. Imagine dots drawn on a balloon: as the balloon inflates, every dot moves away from every other dot, and the dots furthest apart move away from each other fastest.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29">If the universe is expanding now, it must have been smaller in the past. Winding the clock backwards, all the matter and energy in the universe would have been concentrated in an incredibly small, hot, dense point &mdash; the starting point for the <strong>Big Bang</strong> theory.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n30">The Big Bang Theory</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The scientific theory that the universe began from an extremely hot, dense point approximately 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding ever since.">Big Bang theory</dfn> states that the universe began approximately 13.8 billion years ago from an extremely hot, dense point. Space itself began to expand rapidly, and as it cooled, matter formed &mdash; first subatomic particles, then atoms, then stars and galaxies.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32">Two key pieces of evidence support the Big Bang theory:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n33">
<li><strong>Red-shift of galaxies</strong> &mdash; as described above, the observation that all distant galaxies are moving away from us (and the further away, the faster) shows the universe is expanding. Running this expansion backwards points to a single origin.</li>
<li><strong>Cosmic microwave background radiation (CMBR)</strong> &mdash; in 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson detected faint microwave radiation coming from every direction in space. This is the leftover thermal radiation from the Big Bang itself, now cooled to about &minus;270&deg;C (just 2.7 K above absolute zero). The CMBR is the strongest evidence for the Big Bang because no other theory explains its existence.</li>
</ul>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n34">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>The two main pieces of evidence for the Big Bang are <strong>red-shift</strong> (showing the universe is expanding) and <strong>cosmic microwave background radiation</strong> (the afterglow of the Big Bang, detected from every direction in space).</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n35">Dark Matter and Dark Energy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36">Scientists have discovered that the visible matter we can see &mdash; stars, planets, gas clouds &mdash; makes up only about <strong>5%</strong> of the total mass-energy of the universe. The rest is made of two mysterious components.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37"><dfn class="term" data-def="Invisible matter that does not emit or absorb light but exerts a gravitational pull. It is thought to make up about 27% of the universe.">Dark matter</dfn> accounts for about 27% of the universe. It cannot be seen because it does not emit, absorb or reflect light. Scientists know it exists because galaxies rotate faster than expected from their visible mass &mdash; unseen mass must be providing extra gravitational pull.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n38"><dfn class="term" data-def="A mysterious force that is causing the expansion of the universe to accelerate. It is thought to make up about 68% of the universe.">Dark energy</dfn> makes up about 68% of the universe. In the late 1990s, scientists studying distant supernovae discovered the expansion of the universe is <strong>accelerating</strong>. Dark energy is the name given to whatever force is driving this acceleration, but its true nature remains unknown.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n39">Dark matter and dark energy are two of the biggest unsolved problems in physics. Dark matter holds galaxies together but has never been directly detected. Dark energy is pushing the universe apart at an increasing rate, yet its true nature is unknown. Together, they make up about 95% of the universe &mdash; we can only directly observe about 5% of everything that exists.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n40">Space physics questions often ask you to <strong>describe</strong> the life cycle of a star or <strong>explain</strong> the evidence for the Big Bang. For star life cycle questions, always state the starting point (nebula) and make sure you describe <strong>both pathways</strong> &mdash; Sun-sized stars and massive stars &mdash; unless the question specifies one. For Big Bang evidence, name <strong>both</strong> red-shift and CMBR, and explain what each one shows. A common mistake is describing red-shift without linking it to the expansion of the universe &mdash; always make the connection explicit.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n41">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n42"><strong>Solar system structure</strong> &mdash; the Sun is a main sequence star orbited by eight planets, with gravity providing the centripetal force for all orbital motion.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n43"><strong>Star life cycles</strong> &mdash; all stars begin as nebulae; Sun-sized stars end as white dwarfs, while massive stars explode as supernovae, leaving neutron stars or black holes.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44"><strong>Expanding universe</strong> &mdash; red-shift and cosmic microwave background radiation are the two key pieces of evidence for the Big Bang, and dark energy is causing the expansion to accelerate.</li></ul>
