# Moments, Levers, Gears and Momentum Changes

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Moments and the Turning Effect of Forces</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">When you push a door open, you apply a force at a distance from the hinge. The door swings because your push creates a <dfn class="term" data-def="The turning effect of a force about a pivot, calculated as force multiplied by the perpendicular distance from the pivot.">moment</dfn>. A moment is the turning effect of a force around a fixed point called a <dfn class="term" data-def="The fixed point around which rotation occurs, such as a hinge or fulcrum.">pivot</dfn>.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>The moment of a force is calculated using: <strong>moment (Nm) = force (N) &times; distance from pivot (m)</strong>, or <strong>M = F &times; d</strong>. The distance must be measured perpendicular to the direction of the force.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n4">The unit of a moment is the newton metre (Nm). A larger force or a greater distance from the pivot both produce a larger moment. This is why it is easier to open a door by pushing near the handle (far from the hinge) than by pushing close to the hinge.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Balancing Moments &mdash; The Principle of Moments</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">When an object is balanced (in <dfn class="term" data-def="The state in which the total clockwise moments about a pivot equal the total anticlockwise moments, so the object does not rotate.">equilibrium</dfn>), the total clockwise moments about the pivot equal the total anticlockwise moments. This is the <strong>principle of moments</strong>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n10">On a balanced see-saw, if a heavier child sits closer to the pivot and a lighter child sits further away, the moments can be equal. You can use this principle to find an unknown force or distance:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>F<sub>1</sub> &times; d<sub>1</sub> = F<sub>2</sub> &times; d<sub>2</sub></strong></p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Levers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A rigid bar that rotates around a pivot and is used to multiply the effect of a force.">lever</dfn> is a simple machine that multiplies the effect of a force. It consists of a rigid bar that turns around a pivot. By applying a small effort force at a large distance from the pivot, you can move a heavy load that is close to the pivot.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Everyday examples include crowbars, wheelbarrows, scissors, and spanners. A builder using a crowbar applies a force at the long end to lift a heavy floorboard at the short end &mdash; the long lever arm means a smaller force is needed to create the same moment.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Gears</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A toothed wheel that interlocks with another toothed wheel to transmit and modify turning forces.">gear</dfn> is a toothed wheel that meshes with another toothed wheel. Gears transmit the turning effect of a force from one part of a machine to another.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>When a small gear drives a large gear, the large gear turns more slowly but with a greater moment (turning force). When a large gear drives a small gear, the small gear turns faster but with a smaller moment. Gears that mesh always turn in <strong>opposite directions</strong>.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Gears are used in bicycles, cars and clocks. A low gear on a bicycle uses a large rear gear driven by the pedals, giving a greater turning force at the wheel &mdash; useful for climbing hills. A high gear uses a smaller rear gear, giving less force but more speed on flat roads.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Momentum</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><dfn class="term" data-def="A quantity describing how difficult it is to stop a moving object, calculated as mass multiplied by velocity (p = mv).">Momentum</dfn> is a measure of how difficult it is to stop a moving object. It depends on both mass and velocity:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>momentum (kg m/s) = mass (kg) &times; velocity (m/s)</strong>, or <strong>p = m &times; v</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n22">A heavy lorry travelling at 3 m/s has a momentum of 10,000 &times; 3 = 30,000 kg m/s. A bullet has much less mass, but its very high velocity gives it a large momentum too. Momentum is a <dfn class="term" data-def="A quantity that has both magnitude (size) and direction.">vector</dfn> quantity &mdash; it has both size and direction.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Force and the Rate of Change of Momentum</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24">When a force acts on an object, the object&rsquo;s momentum changes. The faster the momentum changes, the greater the force involved. This relationship is expressed as:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>force (N) = change in momentum (kg m/s) &divide; time (s)</strong>, or <strong>F = &Delta;p / t</strong></p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n26">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>A fast change in momentum produces a large force, which can cause serious injury. A slow change in momentum produces a smaller force. This is why safety features are designed to <strong>increase the time</strong> over which momentum changes during a collision.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n27">A person of mass 71 kg is in a car travelling at 25 m/s. The car crashes and stops.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Step 3 &mdash; Without crumple zones</strong> (crash time 0.35 s): F = 1,775 &divide; 0.35 = <strong>5,071 N</strong></p>
<p data-narration-id="n31">The crumple zones reduce the force on the person by about 35% because the collision time is longer.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">Safety Features in Vehicles</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33">Modern vehicles include several features that protect occupants by increasing the time over which momentum changes, thereby reducing the force experienced:</p>
<ul data-narration-id="n34">
<li><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Areas at the front and rear of a vehicle designed to deform and crumple during a collision, absorbing kinetic energy and increasing the collision time.">Crumple zones</dfn></strong> &mdash; the front and rear of the car are designed to crumple and deform during a collision. This absorbs kinetic energy and extends the time of impact.</li>
<li><strong>Seatbelts</strong> &mdash; stretch slightly during a crash, increasing the time it takes for the person to decelerate and spreading the force across the chest.</li>
<li><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Inflatable cushions that deploy rapidly during a collision to increase the time over which a person decelerates, reducing the force on the body.">Air bags</dfn></strong> &mdash; inflate rapidly and then deflate as the person pushes into them, increasing the time of deceleration and cushioning the impact on the head and chest.</li>
</ul>

<p data-narration-id="n35">The change in momentum in a crash is fixed &mdash; the person goes from moving to stationary. Since F = &Delta;p / t, if the time (t) increases, the force (F) must decrease for the same change in momentum (&Delta;p). Every safety feature works on this principle: absorb the impact over a longer period to reduce the peak force on the body.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n36">In calculation questions on moments, always check units &mdash; convert centimetres to metres before substituting. For questions on vehicle safety, explain <em>how</em> the feature increases the time of impact <em>and</em> state that this reduces the force (F = &Delta;p / t). Examiners want both parts for full marks. If asked to &ldquo;compare&rdquo; two situations, calculate both values and write a concluding sentence stating which is greater and why.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n37">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n38">A moment is the turning effect of a force: M = F &times; d. Levers and gears use moments to multiply forces.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">Momentum (p = mv) describes how hard it is to stop a moving object. Force equals the rate of change of momentum: F = &Delta;p / t.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n40">Vehicle safety features (crumple zones, seatbelts, air bags) increase the collision time to reduce the force on occupants.</li>
</ul>
