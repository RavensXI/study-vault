# Static Electricity and Electric Fields

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Is Static Electricity?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">All matter is made of atoms, and atoms contain positively charged <dfn class="term" data-def="Tiny particles in the nucleus of an atom that carry a positive electrical charge.">protons</dfn> and negatively charged <dfn class="term" data-def="Tiny particles that orbit the nucleus of an atom and carry a negative electrical charge.">electrons</dfn>. Normally, the number of protons equals the number of electrons, so the overall charge is zero. <dfn class="term" data-def="A build-up of electrical charge on the surface of an object, caused by the transfer of electrons through friction.">static electricity</dfn> occurs when electrons are transferred from one material to another, giving each material an overall charge.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n3">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Only <strong>electrons</strong> move when objects become charged &mdash; protons are fixed inside the nucleus and cannot transfer between materials. An object that gains electrons becomes negatively charged; an object that loses electrons becomes positively charged.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n4">How Does Static Charge Build Up?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5">Static charge builds up when two <dfn class="term" data-def="A material that does not allow electrical charge to flow through it easily, such as plastic, rubber or glass.">insulating</dfn> materials are rubbed together. The friction causes electrons to transfer from one surface to the other. For example, when a polythene rod is rubbed with a woollen cloth, electrons move from the wool onto the rod. The rod gains extra electrons and becomes negatively charged, while the cloth loses electrons and is left with an equal positive charge.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The opposite happens with an acetate rod: when rubbed with a cloth, electrons move from the rod onto the cloth. The rod loses electrons and becomes positively charged, and the cloth gains a negative charge.</p>

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n7">Conductors, like metals, allow electrons to flow freely through them. If charge builds up on a conductor, the electrons simply flow away. Insulators do not allow charge to flow, so the electrons that are transferred by friction stay on the surface and build up. This is why you get a shock when you touch a metal door handle after walking on a nylon carpet &mdash; the charge that has built up on you is suddenly discharged through the conductor.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n8">Attraction and Repulsion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n9">Once objects are charged, they exert <dfn class="term" data-def="A force that acts between two objects without them touching, such as the electrostatic force between charged objects.">non-contact forces</dfn> on each other. Two objects carrying the same type of charge (both positive or both negative) repel each other. Two objects carrying different types of charge (one positive and one negative) attract each other.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Like charges repel; unlike charges attract. A charged object can also attract small uncharged objects (such as pieces of paper) because it causes the electrons on the surface of the uncharged object to rearrange &mdash; a process called <dfn class="term" data-def="The movement of charge within an object caused by the presence of a nearby charged object, without direct contact.">electrostatic induction</dfn>.</p>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n11">The Van de Graaff Generator</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A machine that produces a large static charge on a metal dome by transferring electrons using a moving belt.">Van de Graaff generator</dfn> is a device used to demonstrate static electricity. A moving rubber belt carries electrons away from the metal dome. The dome loses electrons and builds up a large positive charge. Because the dome is insulated from the ground, the charge cannot escape.</p>

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n13">A rubber belt moves continuously inside the generator. At the bottom, friction between the belt and a roller transfers electrons onto the belt. As the belt moves upward, a metal comb near the dome removes electrons from the dome surface. The electrons travel down through a wire and are neutralised at the base. This leaves the dome with a growing positive charge.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">When a person touches the dome, the charge spreads across their body. Because each strand of hair receives the same positive charge, the hairs repel each other and stand on end. If the charge on the dome becomes large enough, the <dfn class="term" data-def="The voltage between a charged object and the earth, which increases as more charge builds up.">potential difference</dfn> between the dome and the earth becomes so great that a spark jumps across the gap &mdash; this is called <dfn class="term" data-def="A sudden flow of charge through the air between a charged object and the earth (or another object), caused by a large potential difference.">sparking</dfn>.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Dangers of Static Electricity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">Static electricity can be dangerous in certain situations. When fuelling an aircraft or filling a car with petrol, friction between the flowing fuel and the pipe can cause a build-up of charge. If a spark occurs near the fuel vapour, it could cause an explosion or fire. Lightning is another example &mdash; the friction between ice crystals and water droplets inside storm clouds causes a massive build-up of charge, and the resulting spark (lightning bolt) can be lethal.</p>

<div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
<p data-narration-id="n17"><dfn class="term" data-def="Connecting a charged object to the ground using a conductor, so that the excess charge flows safely to earth.">earthing</dfn> is the main way to prevent dangerous static discharge. A conducting strap or wire connects the object to the ground. This provides a path for electrons to flow safely to or from the earth, preventing charge from building up. Fuel tankers, for example, have earthing straps that touch the ground to discharge any static before the fuel is transferred.</p>
</div></div>
</div>
<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Uses of Static Electricity</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Static electricity is not always a hazard &mdash; it has several practical applications. In spray painting, the paint droplets are given a charge and the object being painted is given the opposite charge (or is earthed). The droplets are attracted evenly to the surface, producing a smooth, even coat with less waste. The same principle is used in crop sprayers to ensure pesticide reaches all parts of a plant.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">In photocopiers and laser printers, a drum is given a pattern of static charge that matches the image. Toner powder (which carries the opposite charge) sticks to the charged areas and is then transferred onto paper. Electrostatic precipitators in power station chimneys use charged plates to attract soot and ash particles out of waste gases before they reach the atmosphere.</p>
<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Electric Fields</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">A charged object creates an <dfn class="term" data-def="The region around a charged object where another charged object would experience a force.">electric field</dfn> in the space around it. Any other charged object placed in this field will experience a non-contact force &mdash; either attraction or repulsion. The electric field is strongest close to the charged object and gets weaker with distance.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
<div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
<p>Electric field lines are drawn with arrows showing the direction a positive charge would move. Field lines point <strong>away from</strong> positive charges and <strong>towards</strong> negative charges. The closer together the field lines, the stronger the field at that point.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n24">Between two oppositely charged parallel plates, the electric field is <strong>uniform</strong> &mdash; the field lines are equally spaced and parallel. Between two point charges (one positive, one negative), the field lines curve from the positive charge towards the negative charge. Between two like charges, the field lines repel and curve away from each other.</p>

<p data-narration-id="n25">The concept of an electric field explains how charged objects exert forces on each other without touching. When a charged object is placed in another object&rsquo;s electric field, the field exerts a force on it. The closer the objects, the stronger the force. This is why sparking occurs when the potential difference is high enough &mdash; the electric field between the charged object and a nearby earthed conductor is strong enough to ionise the air molecules, creating a conducting path for the charge to flow through.</p>
</div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n26">When answering questions on static electricity, always state which particles move (electrons, not protons) and in which direction. For &ldquo;Explain&rdquo; questions, link cause to effect clearly: friction causes electron transfer &rarr; one object gains electrons &rarr; becomes negatively charged. When drawing electric field lines, remember three rules: lines never cross, arrows point away from positive and towards negative, and closer lines mean a stronger field. For extended response questions, structure your answer with a clear opening statement, supporting points with scientific vocabulary, and a concluding link back to the question.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n27">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
<li data-narration-id="n28">Static charge is caused by the transfer of electrons between insulating materials through friction &mdash; only electrons move, and the material gaining electrons becomes negative while the one losing electrons becomes positive.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n29">Static electricity can be dangerous (sparks near fuel, lightning) but is prevented by earthing, and has useful applications including spray painting, photocopiers, and electrostatic precipitators.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30">An electric field surrounds every charged object &mdash; field lines show direction (away from positive, towards negative) and density of lines shows field strength.</li>
</ul>
