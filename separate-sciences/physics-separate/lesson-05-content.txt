# Reflection, Sound Waves and Waves for Detection

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">The Law of Reflection</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">When a wave hits a boundary between two different materials, it can be <dfn class="term" data-def="The bouncing back of a wave when it hits a boundary between two different materials.">reflected</dfn>, transmitted, or absorbed. Reflection is when the wave bounces back from the surface. This applies to all types of waves &mdash; light, sound, and water waves all follow the same rules.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">To describe reflection precisely, scientists use a set of standard terms. The <dfn class="term" data-def="An imaginary line drawn at 90 degrees (perpendicular) to a reflecting surface at the point where the incoming ray hits.">normal</dfn> is an imaginary line drawn at 90&deg; (perpendicular) to the reflecting surface at the point where the wave hits. The incoming wave is called the <dfn class="term" data-def="The ray of light or wave approaching a reflecting or refracting surface.">incident ray</dfn>, and the wave that bounces back is the <dfn class="term" data-def="The ray of light or wave that bounces back from a reflecting surface.">reflected ray</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The angle between the incident ray and the normal line at the point of reflection.">angle of incidence</dfn> is measured between the incident ray and the normal. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The angle between the reflected ray and the normal line at the point of reflection.">angle of reflection</dfn> is measured between the reflected ray and the normal. Both angles are always measured from the normal &mdash; not from the surface of the mirror.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The <strong>law of reflection</strong> states that the angle of incidence always equals the angle of reflection. This is true for every type of wave at every surface.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n6">In the exam, you may need to construct ray diagrams. Draw the mirror as a straight line, then draw the normal at 90&deg; to the mirror at the point where the ray hits. Mark the incident ray coming in and the reflected ray going out. Use a protractor to check that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.</p>

The reflected rays are all parallel, so a clear image is formed. This is why you can see your reflection in a mirror or in still water.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Rough surfaces</strong> scatter reflected waves in all directions. This is called diffuse reflection. Each tiny part of the surface still obeys the law of reflection, but because the surface is uneven, the reflected rays go in many different directions. No clear image is formed.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9">An image formed in a plane mirror is <strong>virtual</strong> (cannot be projected onto a screen), <strong>upright</strong> (not upside down), and <strong>laterally inverted</strong> (left and right are swapped). This is why the word &ldquo;AMBULANCE&rdquo; is written backwards on the front of ambulances &mdash; so it reads correctly in a driver&rsquo;s rear-view mirror.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Sound Waves</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11">Sound is produced when an object vibrates. These vibrations travel through a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) as a <dfn class="term" data-def="A wave in which the oscillations are parallel to the direction of energy transfer, with areas of compression and rarefaction.">longitudinal wave</dfn>. In a longitudinal wave, the particles vibrate back and forth parallel to the direction the wave travels, creating areas of compression (high pressure) and rarefaction (low pressure).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Sound waves are <dfn class="term" data-def="A wave that requires a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel through and cannot travel through a vacuum.">mechanical waves</dfn> &mdash; they need a medium to travel through. Sound cannot travel through a vacuum. This can be demonstrated using a bell jar experiment: as air is pumped out, the sound of a ringing bell fades until it becomes inaudible, even though the bell is still vibrating.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Sound travels at different speeds through different materials. It travels fastest through solids (particles are closest together), slower through liquids, and slowest through gases. In air at room temperature, the speed of sound is approximately 330&thinsp;m/s.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The range of normal human hearing is <strong>20&thinsp;Hz to 20,000&thinsp;Hz</strong> (20&thinsp;kHz). Sounds below 20&thinsp;Hz are called infrasound. Sounds above 20,000&thinsp;Hz are called <strong>ultrasound</strong>.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n15">When sound waves enter the ear, they cause the eardrum to vibrate. These vibrations are passed through the tiny ear bones to the inner ear, where they are converted into electrical signals sent to the brain. The eardrum and ear bones can only vibrate at certain rates, which is why our hearing is limited to the 20&thinsp;Hz&ndash;20,000&thinsp;Hz range.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n16">The <strong>pitch</strong> of a sound depends on its frequency &mdash; a higher frequency means a higher pitch. The <strong>loudness</strong> of a sound depends on its amplitude &mdash; a larger amplitude means a louder sound. On an oscilloscope trace, higher pitch appears as waves closer together, and louder sound appears as taller waves.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n17">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A reflection of sound that occurs when sound waves bounce off a hard surface and return to the listener.">echo</dfn> is a reflection of sound. When sound waves hit a hard, smooth surface (such as a cliff face or the wall of a cave), they bounce back and can be heard again after a short delay. Soft, rough surfaces absorb sound rather than reflecting it &mdash; this is why cinemas have padded walls and curtains to prevent echoes that would spoil the film audio.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18">Echo calculations are common in exams. Remember that the sound wave travels <strong>there and back</strong>, so the distance to the reflecting surface is half the total distance the sound travels. Use the equation: distance = speed &times; time, then divide by 2.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Ultrasound and Its Uses</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20"><dfn class="term" data-def="Sound waves with frequencies above 20,000 Hz (above the range of human hearing), used in medical imaging, sonar, and industrial applications.">Ultrasound</dfn> refers to sound waves with frequencies above 20,000&thinsp;Hz &mdash; beyond the upper limit of human hearing. Despite being inaudible to humans, ultrasound has many practical applications because of the way it reflects at boundaries between materials of different densities.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n21">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>When ultrasound waves reach a boundary between two media with different densities, they are <strong>partly reflected</strong> back. A detector measures the time between the ultrasound pulse leaving the source and the reflection arriving back. This time is used to calculate the depth of the boundary.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>Medical imaging:</strong> Ultrasound is widely used in pre-natal scanning to check that a foetus is developing normally. The ultrasound waves are partly reflected at the boundaries between different tissues (skin, muscle, bone, fluid). A computer combines many readings to build a detailed image. Ultrasound is preferred over X-rays for this purpose because it is non-invasive, considered safe (it does not cause cell damage like ionising radiation), and provides real-time images.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>Echo sounding and <dfn class="term" data-def="Sound Navigation and Ranging &mdash; a technique that uses pulses of sound (usually ultrasound) to measure distances underwater by timing the echo.">SONAR</dfn></strong> (Sound Navigation and Ranging): Ships and submarines send pulses of ultrasound towards the sea floor. The pulses reflect off the bottom and return to a detector on the vessel. By measuring the time taken for the pulse to return and knowing the speed of sound in water, the depth can be calculated. SONAR is also used to detect submarines, shoals of fish, and underwater obstacles. Bats and dolphins use a natural version of this &mdash; they emit ultrasound and listen for the echoes to navigate and locate prey.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n26">Seismic Waves and the Structure of the Earth</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n27">Earthquakes produce <dfn class="term" data-def="Waves of energy that travel through the Earth, produced by earthquakes or explosions. The two main types are P-waves and S-waves.">seismic waves</dfn> that travel through the Earth. By studying how these waves travel, scientists have been able to work out the internal structure of the Earth &mdash; something that cannot be observed directly. There are two main types of seismic wave.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Primary waves &mdash; longitudinal seismic waves that travel through both solids and liquids. They are the fastest seismic waves and arrive first at a detector.">P-waves</dfn></strong> (primary waves) are longitudinal waves. They cause the ground to vibrate in the same direction the wave is travelling. P-waves can travel through <strong>both solids and liquids</strong>, so they can pass through all layers of the Earth &mdash; the crust, mantle, outer core, and inner core. They are the fastest seismic waves and arrive at detection stations first.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Secondary waves &mdash; transverse seismic waves that can only travel through solids. They are slower than P-waves and cannot pass through the liquid outer core.">S-waves</dfn></strong> (secondary waves) are transverse waves. They cause the ground to vibrate at right angles to the direction of travel. S-waves can <strong>only travel through solids</strong> &mdash; they cannot pass through liquids. This means S-waves are blocked by the Earth&rsquo;s liquid outer core.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n30">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The fact that S-waves cannot travel through the outer core proves it is <strong>liquid</strong>. P-waves can travel through the outer core but change direction at the boundaries between layers. Together, the behaviour of P-waves and S-waves provides evidence that the Earth has a <strong>solid inner core</strong>, a <strong>liquid outer core</strong>, and a <strong>solid mantle</strong> (which behaves like a solid despite being able to flow very slowly).</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n31">Both types of seismic wave change speed as they travel deeper into the Earth because the density of the rock increases. This causes the waves to follow curved paths through the mantle. When they reach a boundary between layers of different density or state, they change direction suddenly &mdash; this is refraction. By mapping where P-waves and S-waves are detected on the Earth&rsquo;s surface, scientists can work out the size and state of each internal layer.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n32">After an earthquake, seismometers around the world detect the arriving waves. There is a large &ldquo;shadow zone&rdquo; on the far side of the Earth where no S-waves are detected at all. This shadow zone shows that the outer core is liquid, because S-waves cannot pass through liquid. P-waves are also refracted at the core&ndash;mantle boundary, creating their own shadow zone, but they do eventually reach the far side after being bent through the core.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n33">These observations allowed scientists to discover that the Earth has a thin crust (10&ndash;100&thinsp;km thick), a thick mantle that behaves as a solid, a liquid outer core made mainly of iron and nickel, and a solid inner core. Without seismic wave studies, this internal structure would remain unknown.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n34">In AQA Physics questions on waves for detection and exploration, always state the <strong>type of wave</strong> (longitudinal or transverse), the <strong>medium</strong> it can or cannot travel through, and the <strong>evidence</strong> this provides. For echo and SONAR calculations, remember the sound travels there and back &mdash; divide the total distance by 2. When drawing ray diagrams, label the normal, incident ray, reflected ray, and both angles carefully using a protractor.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n35">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n36">The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection &mdash; both measured from the normal. Smooth surfaces give specular reflection; rough surfaces scatter waves.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n37">Sound is a longitudinal, mechanical wave. Human hearing ranges from 20&thinsp;Hz to 20,000&thinsp;Hz. Ultrasound (above 20&thinsp;kHz) is used in medical imaging and SONAR by detecting reflected pulses.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Seismic P-waves (longitudinal, travel through solids and liquids) and S-waves (transverse, solids only) provide evidence for the Earth&rsquo;s layered structure &mdash; including proof that the outer core is liquid.</li></ul>
