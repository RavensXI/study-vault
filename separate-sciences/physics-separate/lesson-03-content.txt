# Nuclear Radiation: Uses, Fission and Fusion

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Background Radiation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">We are all exposed to <dfn class="term" data-def="Low-level nuclear radiation that exists all around us from natural and artificial sources.">background radiation</dfn> every day. It comes from both natural and artificial (man-made) sources and is usually at a low enough level that it poses very little risk to health. However, the level of background radiation is not the same everywhere &mdash; it varies depending on location and lifestyle.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><strong>Natural sources</strong> account for roughly 85% of background radiation. The main natural sources are:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n4"><strong>Radon gas</strong> &mdash; the largest single contributor. <dfn class="term" data-def="A naturally occurring radioactive gas produced by the decay of uranium in rocks and soil, particularly granite.">Radon</dfn> is an alpha-emitting gas produced by the decay of uranium in rocks and soil. It seeps into buildings, especially in areas with granite bedrock (such as Cornwall and Devon). <strong>Rocks and soil</strong> &mdash; contain naturally radioactive isotopes such as uranium and potassium-40. <strong>Cosmic rays</strong> &mdash; high-energy radiation from space. People at higher altitudes receive a larger dose. <strong>Food and drink</strong> &mdash; small amounts of radioactive potassium-40 and carbon-14 are present in the food we eat.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Radon gas is the largest source of background radiation in the UK. It is an alpha emitter that seeps into buildings from underground, especially in areas with granite rock. Exposure can increase the risk of lung cancer.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n6"><strong>Artificial sources</strong> make up the remaining 15% and include medical procedures (X-rays, CT scans, radiotherapy), nuclear weapons testing fallout, and nuclear power station discharges. Of these, medical uses are by far the largest artificial contributor.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n7">Uses of Nuclear Radiation in Medicine</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n8">Radioactive isotopes have several important applications in medicine. How useful a particular isotope is depends on two key factors: its <dfn class="term" data-def="The time taken for the number of radioactive nuclei (or the activity) of a sample to halve.">half-life</dfn> and the type of radiation it emits.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n9">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A radioactive isotope introduced into the body so that its path can be tracked using a detector, allowing doctors to monitor organ function.">medical tracer</dfn> is a radioactive substance that is injected into or swallowed by a patient. A detector outside the body tracks the tracer as it moves through the body, producing an image of the organ being investigated. Technetium-99m is the most widely used medical tracer because it emits gamma radiation (which can pass through the body to reach the detector) and has a short half-life of about 6 hours (so it does not stay radioactive in the body for long).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10">For a tracer to be useful, it must have a half-life that is long enough for doctors to carry out the scan but short enough to minimise the patient&rsquo;s radiation exposure. It must also emit gamma or beta radiation &mdash; alpha would be absorbed before leaving the body.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Treatment of cancer:</strong> Gamma radiation can be used to destroy cancerous tumours. In <dfn class="term" data-def="A medical procedure that uses focused beams of gamma radiation from multiple angles to destroy a brain tumour while minimising damage to surrounding healthy tissue.">gamma knife surgery</dfn>, multiple beams of gamma radiation are focused from different angles so they all converge on the tumour. Each individual beam is weak enough to pass through healthy tissue without causing much damage, but where all the beams meet at the tumour the combined dose is strong enough to destroy the cancerous cells. Radioactive implants (brachytherapy) can also be placed inside or next to a tumour to deliver a targeted dose of radiation.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Uses of Nuclear Radiation in Industry</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Beyond medicine, radioactive isotopes are used in several industrial applications:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Thickness monitoring:</strong> Beta radiation is used to check the thickness of materials such as paper or metal sheets. A beta source is placed on one side and a detector on the other. If the material is too thick, less radiation reaches the detector; if too thin, more gets through. Alpha would be stopped by any solid, and gamma would pass through unaffected by small thickness changes, so beta is ideal.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Sterilisation:</strong> Gamma radiation is used to sterilise medical equipment and food. Gamma rays kill bacteria without heating the product or leaving residue &mdash; useful for plastic surgical instruments that would melt if heated.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n16">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The choice of radioactive source depends on the application. Tracers need gamma emitters with short half-lives. Thickness monitors need beta emitters. Sterilisation needs gamma emitters with long half-lives. Always justify your choice of radiation type and half-life in exam answers.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Nuclear Fission</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><dfn class="term" data-def="The splitting of a large, unstable nucleus (such as uranium-235 or plutonium-239) into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy, neutrons, and gamma radiation.">Nuclear fission</dfn> is the splitting of a large, unstable nucleus into two smaller nuclei. This process releases a huge amount of energy. The main fissile materials used are uranium-235 and plutonium-239.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19">Spontaneous fission is extremely rare. Usually, fission is induced by firing a slow-moving neutron at a uranium-235 nucleus. The nucleus absorbs the neutron, becomes unstable, and splits into two smaller &ldquo;daughter&rdquo; nuclei, releasing two or three neutrons and a large amount of energy (as kinetic energy of the fragments and gamma radiation).</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<p data-narration-id="n20">The energy released in fission is enormous compared to chemical reactions. Burning coal produces about 2.5 MWh of electricity per tonne of coal burned. <dfn class="term" data-def="The splitting of uranium nuclei in a reactor to release energy &mdash; 2.5 g of uranium can produce the same energy as 1 tonne of coal.">Nuclear fission of uranium</dfn> can yield the same 2.5 MWh from just 2.5 grams of uranium. Einstein&rsquo;s equation E&nbsp;=&nbsp;mc&sup2; explains why: even a tiny loss of mass produces a vast amount of energy because it is multiplied by the speed of light squared.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Chain Reactions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A self-sustaining series of fission reactions where neutrons released by one fission event go on to cause further fission events.">chain reaction</dfn> occurs when the neutrons released by one fission event go on to be absorbed by other uranium nuclei, causing them to split as well. Each fission produces two or three neutrons, and each of those can trigger another fission &mdash; so the number of reactions can increase rapidly.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n23">In an uncontrolled chain reaction, the energy is released almost instantaneously &mdash; this is the principle behind a nuclear weapon. In a nuclear power station, the chain reaction must be carefully controlled so that, on average, exactly one neutron from each fission goes on to cause one further fission. This keeps the reaction steady and the energy output constant.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n24">A nuclear reactor contains several key components:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Fuel rods</strong> &mdash; contain the fissile material (uranium-235 or plutonium-239). These are where fission takes place.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="Rods made of boron or cadmium that are raised or lowered into the reactor core to absorb neutrons and control the rate of the chain reaction.">Control rods</dfn></strong> &mdash; made from materials that absorb neutrons, such as boron or cadmium. They are raised or lowered into the reactor core to control the rate of the chain reaction. Lowering the rods absorbs more neutrons, slowing the reaction. Raising them allows more neutrons to cause fission, speeding it up. In an emergency, the rods drop fully into the core to shut the reaction down.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="A material (usually water or graphite) in a nuclear reactor that slows down fast-moving neutrons so they can be absorbed by fuel nuclei and cause fission.">Moderator</dfn></strong> &mdash; usually water or graphite. Its job is to slow down the fast-moving neutrons released during fission. Slow (thermal) neutrons are much more likely to be absorbed by uranium-235 nuclei and cause further fission than fast neutrons.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Coolant</strong> &mdash; a fluid (usually water or carbon dioxide gas) pumped through the reactor core. It absorbs the heat energy produced by fission and carries it to a heat exchanger, where it turns water into steam. The steam then drives turbines connected to generators to produce electricity.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n29">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In a nuclear reactor: <strong>fuel rods</strong> contain uranium or plutonium, <strong>control rods</strong> (boron/cadmium) absorb neutrons to control the chain reaction, the <strong>moderator</strong> (water/graphite) slows neutrons down, and the <strong>coolant</strong> transfers heat to generate steam for turbines.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n30">Nuclear Fusion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><dfn class="term" data-def="The joining of two small, light nuclei to form a larger, heavier nucleus, releasing a large amount of energy.">Nuclear fusion</dfn> is the opposite of fission. Instead of splitting a large nucleus, fusion involves joining two small, light nuclei together to form a larger, heavier nucleus. This process also releases a large amount of energy &mdash; even more per kilogram of fuel than fission &mdash; because some of the mass of the original nuclei is converted into energy (again, E&nbsp;=&nbsp;mc&sup2;).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32">Fusion is the process that powers the Sun and all other stars. In the Sun&rsquo;s core, hydrogen nuclei fuse together under extreme temperature and pressure to form helium. The energy released is what makes the Sun shine and keeps it hot.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Why Is Fusion So Difficult on Earth?</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n33">For fusion to occur, two nuclei must get close enough together for the <dfn class="term" data-def="A fundamental force of nature that acts over very short distances to hold protons and neutrons together in the nucleus, overcoming the electrostatic repulsion between protons.">strong nuclear force</dfn> to pull them together. But because both nuclei are positively charged, they repel each other (electrostatic repulsion). To overcome this repulsion, the nuclei must be travelling at extremely high speeds &mdash; which means temperatures of millions of degrees Celsius.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34">At these temperatures, matter exists as a <dfn class="term" data-def="A superheated state of matter in which electrons are stripped from atoms, leaving a gas of free-moving ions and electrons.">plasma</dfn> (a gas of ions and free electrons). Containing this plasma is one of the biggest engineering challenges in science. No solid container can withstand such temperatures, so researchers use powerful magnetic fields to hold the plasma in place (a device called a tokamak). As of 2026, scientists have achieved fusion in laboratories but have not yet built a reactor that produces more energy than it consumes &mdash; making commercial fusion power a goal for the future rather than a current reality.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n35">Star Formation and the Life Cycle of Stars</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n36">Nuclear fusion is central to the life cycle of stars. A star begins as a cloud of hydrogen gas and dust called a <dfn class="term" data-def="A large cloud of gas (mostly hydrogen) and dust in space from which stars are formed by gravitational collapse.">nebula</dfn>. Gravity pulls the gas together, increasing temperature and pressure until hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium. The star enters its &ldquo;main sequence&rdquo; phase, where the outward pressure from fusion balances the inward pull of gravity, keeping it stable.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n37">Our Sun is a main sequence star and has been for about 4.6 billion years. When a star eventually runs out of hydrogen fuel, its fate depends on its size &mdash; but the key point for GCSE is that fusion is the energy source that powers all stars.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n38">When answering questions on nuclear radiation, always be specific about the type of radiation (alpha, beta, or gamma) and explain <em>why</em> that type is suitable for the application. For fission and fusion questions, know the key differences: fission splits large nuclei, fusion joins small ones. Both release energy via E&nbsp;=&nbsp;mc&sup2;. For extended response questions, structure your answer clearly &mdash; define the process, explain how it works, and describe the conditions required.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n39">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n40">Background radiation comes mainly from natural sources (radon gas is the largest contributor). Medical procedures are the biggest artificial source.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Nuclear fission splits large nuclei (uranium-235) in a chain reaction controlled by control rods and a moderator in a nuclear reactor. Fusion joins small nuclei (hydrogen) and powers the Sun, but requires extreme temperatures.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42">Radioactive isotopes are used in medicine (tracers, cancer treatment) and industry (thickness monitoring, sterilisation) &mdash; the choice of isotope depends on its half-life and the type of radiation it emits.</li></ul>
