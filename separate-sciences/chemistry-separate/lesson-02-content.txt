# Yield, Atom Economy, Molar Concentrations and Gas Volumes

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Percentage Yield</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In any chemical reaction, the amount of product you actually make is almost always less than the amount you expected. The <dfn class="term" data-def="The maximum mass of product that could be formed from a given amount of reactants, calculated from a balanced equation.">theoretical yield</dfn> is the maximum mass of product calculated from the balanced equation, but the <dfn class="term" data-def="The actual mass of product obtained from a chemical reaction.">actual yield</dfn> is whatever you collect at the end of the experiment.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">To compare the two, chemists calculate the <dfn class="term" data-def="The proportion of the theoretical yield that is actually obtained, expressed as a percentage.">percentage yield</dfn> using this equation:</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Percentage yield = (actual yield &divide; theoretical yield) &times; 100</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n5">A percentage yield of 100% would mean you collected every gram of product the equation predicted. In practice this never happens because:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n6">The reaction may be <strong>reversible</strong>, so not all reactants convert to products.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n7">Some product is always <strong>lost during transfer</strong> &mdash; for example, liquid left behind on glassware or solid stuck to filter paper.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n8">Unexpected <strong>side reactions</strong> may form different products from those intended.</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Atom Economy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><dfn class="term" data-def="The proportion of the total relative formula mass of all products that is made up by the desired product, expressed as a percentage.">atom economy</dfn> measures how much of the total mass of reactants ends up as the useful product. It is a way of judging how wasteful a reaction is before you even carry it out.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Atom economy = (M<sub>r</sub> of desired product &divide; total M<sub>r</sub> of all products) &times; 100</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n14">A high atom economy means most atoms from the reactants end up in the useful product, generating less waste. Industries prefer reactions with high atom economy because they are more <strong>sustainable</strong> and more <strong>cost-effective</strong>.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Worked Example &mdash; Atom Economy</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n15">Calculate the atom economy for making hydrogen by reacting zinc with hydrochloric acid:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16">Zn + 2HCl &rarr; ZnCl<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17">M<sub>r</sub> of desired product (H<sub>2</sub>) = 2. Total M<sub>r</sub> of all products = ZnCl<sub>2</sub> (136) + H<sub>2</sub> (2) = 138.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18">Atom economy = (2 &divide; 138) &times; 100 = <strong>1.4%</strong></p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19">This extremely low atom economy shows that this method wastes almost all the reactant mass as zinc chloride &mdash; an unwanted by-product.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Reactions that produce only one product, such as addition reactions, have an atom economy of 100%. Reactions that produce several products, where only one is useful, have a low atom economy.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n21">Molar Concentrations</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n22">You already know that <dfn class="term" data-def="The amount of solute dissolved in a given volume of solution, measured in g/dm&sup3; or mol/dm&sup3;.">concentration</dfn> can be measured in g/dm&sup3;. For more advanced calculations, chemists express concentration in <strong>mol/dm&sup3;</strong> (moles per cubic decimetre). This tells you how many moles of solute are dissolved in each dm&sup3; of solution.</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Concentration (mol/dm&sup3;) = moles of solute &divide; volume of solution (dm&sup3;)</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n24">To convert between units, remember: 1 dm&sup3; = 1000 cm&sup3;. So if a volume is given in cm&sup3;, divide by 1000 to get dm&sup3;.</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Worked Example &mdash; Molar Concentration</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n25">A student dissolves 0.5 mol of sodium hydroxide in 250 cm&sup3; of water. Calculate the concentration in mol/dm&sup3;.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26">Volume in dm&sup3; = 250 &divide; 1000 = 0.25 dm&sup3;</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n27">Concentration = 0.5 &divide; 0.25 = <strong>2 mol/dm&sup3;</strong></p>
  </div></div>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n28">You can also rearrange the equation to find moles or volume:</p>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n29">Moles = concentration &times; volume (in dm&sup3;)</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n30">Volume (dm&sup3;) = moles &divide; concentration</li>
</ul>

<h2 data-narration-id="n31">Molar Volume of Gases</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n32">Equal numbers of moles of any gas occupy the same volume at the same temperature and pressure. At <dfn class="term" data-def="Room temperature and pressure: 20&deg;C (293 K) and 1 atmosphere (101.3 kPa).">room temperature and pressure (RTP)</dfn>, the <dfn class="term" data-def="The volume occupied by one mole of any gas at a specified temperature and pressure; at RTP it is 24 dm&sup3;.">molar volume</dfn> of any gas is <strong>24 dm&sup3;</strong> (or 24,000 cm&sup3;).</p>
<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n33">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Volume of gas (dm&sup3;) = moles &times; 24. This works for <em>any</em> gas at RTP &mdash; hydrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide or any other.</p>
</div>
<p data-narration-id="n34">You can rearrange to find moles from a volume: moles = volume (dm&sup3;) &divide; 24.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Yield vs Atom Economy &mdash; What&rsquo;s the Difference?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40"><dfn class="term" data-def="A measure of the efficiency of a reaction based on how much of the reactants become useful product, compared to the theoretical maximum.">percentage yield</dfn> tells you how efficiently the reaction was <em>carried out</em> in practice &mdash; it compares what you actually got to what you should have got. <dfn class="term" data-def="A theoretical measure of the proportion of reactant atoms that end up in the desired product.">atom economy</dfn> tells you how efficient the reaction <em>itself</em> is by design &mdash; whether the equation produces waste products or not.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n41">A reaction can have a high atom economy but a low percentage yield (if the method was poorly carried out), or a low atom economy but a high percentage yield (if you collected nearly all the useful product, but the reaction still creates lots of waste).</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n42">In calculation questions, always <strong>show your working</strong> clearly &mdash; you can earn method marks even if your final answer is wrong. For atom economy, make sure you divide by the M<sub>r</sub> of <em>all products</em>, not all reactants. For gas volume questions, check whether the volume is given in cm&sup3; or dm&sup3; before using 24 or 24,000. Label every answer with the correct unit.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n43">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul>
  <li data-narration-id="n44">Percentage yield compares actual yield to theoretical yield &mdash; it is always less than 100% because of losses, reversibility, or side reactions.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n45">Atom economy compares the M<sub>r</sub> of the desired product to the total M<sub>r</sub> of all products &mdash; higher is more sustainable.</li>
  <li data-narration-id="n46">Concentration in mol/dm&sup3; = moles &divide; volume (dm&sup3;). One mole of any gas occupies 24 dm&sup3; at RTP.</li>
</ul>
