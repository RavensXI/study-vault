# Titrations, Chemical Cells and Fuel Cells

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Titrations</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A technique used to find the exact volume of acid needed to neutralise a known volume of alkali, or vice versa.">titration</dfn> is a technique used to find the precise concentration of an acid or alkali. It works by reacting an acid with an alkali of known concentration and measuring the exact volume needed to achieve <dfn class="term" data-def="The point in a titration where the acid has exactly neutralised the alkali, shown by a colour change in the indicator.">neutralisation</dfn>. This is one of the most important required practicals in GCSE Chemistry (spec ref 4.4.2.5).</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n3">Titration Equipment</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n4">A titration uses specialised glassware to achieve accurate results. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A glass tube with a tap at the bottom and a graduated scale, used to add precise volumes of liquid.">burette</dfn> is a long graduated tube with a tap at the bottom, used to deliver the acid in a controlled way. A <dfn class="term" data-def="A glass tube designed to measure and transfer an exact fixed volume of liquid.">pipette</dfn> is used to measure a precise, fixed volume of the alkali &mdash; typically 25.0 cm&sup3;. The alkali is placed in a conical flask on a white tile so colour changes are easy to see.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>A burette measures the volume of acid added. A pipette measures the fixed volume of alkali. An <dfn class="term" data-def="A chemical that changes colour at the end point of a titration, showing when neutralisation has occurred.">indicator</dfn> shows when neutralisation is complete.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n6">Carrying Out a Titration</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n7">Follow these steps to carry out an acid&ndash;alkali titration:</p>
<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Step-by-Step Titration Method</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n8">1. Use the pipette to add exactly 25.0 cm&sup3; of alkali (e.g. sodium hydroxide) into a clean conical flask. 2. Add 2&ndash;3 drops of a suitable indicator, such as phenolphthalein (pink in alkali, colourless in acid). 3. Fill the burette with acid of unknown concentration. Record the initial burette reading. 4. Perform a <dfn class="term" data-def="A quick, approximate titration used to find the rough volume of acid needed, before carrying out accurate titrations.">rough titration</dfn> first: add the acid 1 cm&sup3; at a time whilst swirling the flask, until the indicator changes colour. This gives an approximate volume. 5. Record the final burette reading and calculate the titre (final &minus; initial reading). 6. Repeat, but this time add the acid quickly until you are about 5 cm&sup3; below your rough titre, then add it dropwise until the colour change occurs. 7. Repeat until you obtain at least two <dfn class="term" data-def="Titration results that are within 0.10 cm&sup3; of each other, showing they are reliable and repeatable.">concordant results</dfn> &mdash; results within 0.10 cm&sup3; of each other.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n9">The <dfn class="term" data-def="The exact point in a titration at which the indicator changes colour, showing that neutralisation is complete.">end point</dfn> is the exact moment the indicator permanently changes colour &mdash; for phenolphthalein, this is when the solution goes from pink to colourless. The mean of the concordant results gives the accurate titre, which is used in calculations. The rough titre is always excluded from the mean.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Concordant results are within 0.10 cm&sup3; of each other. Calculate the mean of the concordant results only &mdash; always discard the rough titre.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Titration Calculations</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Once you have the accurate titre, you can calculate the unknown concentration using the formula:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">M&#8321;V&#8321; = M&#8322;V&#8322;</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14">Where M&#8321; is the concentration of the alkali, V&#8321; is the volume of the alkali, M&#8322; is the concentration of the acid, and V&#8322; is the mean titre (volume of acid). If three values are known, you can rearrange to find the fourth. For example, if 20.0 cm&sup3; of 0.10 mol/dm&sup3; NaOH is neutralised by 13.3 cm&sup3; of HCl, the concentration of HCl = (0.10 &times; 20.0) &divide; 13.3 = 0.15 mol/dm&sup3;.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n15">Chemical Cells and Batteries</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n16">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A device containing two different metals (electrodes) in an electrolyte that produces a voltage by converting chemical energy into electrical energy.">chemical cell</dfn> produces electricity from a chemical reaction. When two different metals are placed into an <dfn class="term" data-def="A liquid or solution that conducts electricity because it contains free-moving ions.">electrolyte</dfn> (an electrically conducting liquid) and connected by a wire, the more reactive metal loses electrons more readily. These electrons flow through the wire to the less reactive metal, creating an electric current.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Factors Affecting Voltage in a Chemical Cell</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n17">The voltage (potential difference) produced by a chemical cell depends on: the types of metals used as electrodes &mdash; the greater the difference in reactivity between the two metals, the higher the voltage; and the electrolyte used. For example, a cell with magnesium and copper electrodes will produce a higher voltage than one with zinc and copper, because magnesium is much more reactive than zinc. A <dfn class="term" data-def="Two or more chemical cells connected together in series to provide a higher voltage.">battery</dfn> is simply two or more cells connected in series.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n18">In non-rechargeable cells, the chemical reactions are irreversible &mdash; once the reactants are used up, the cell stops working. In <dfn class="term" data-def="A cell or battery in which the chemical reactions can be reversed by passing an electric current through it, restoring the original reactants.">rechargeable batteries</dfn>, the reactions can be reversed by connecting to an external power supply, allowing the battery to be used again. Alkaline batteries (like AA batteries) are non-rechargeable, while lithium-ion batteries in phones and laptops are rechargeable.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Hydrogen Fuel Cells</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">A <dfn class="term" data-def="An electrochemical cell that produces electricity by reacting hydrogen with oxygen, with water as the only waste product.">hydrogen fuel cell</dfn> is an electrochemical device that generates electricity by combining hydrogen and oxygen. Unlike a battery, it does not run out &mdash; as long as hydrogen and oxygen are supplied, it keeps producing electricity.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>How a Hydrogen Fuel Cell Works</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n21">Hydrogen gas enters at the anode (negative electrode) and is oxidised, losing electrons. These electrons flow through an external circuit to the cathode (positive electrode), creating a current. At the cathode, oxygen reacts with the electrons and hydrogen ions to form water. The overall reaction is: hydrogen + oxygen &rarr; water (2H&#8322; + O&#8322; &rarr; 2H&#8322;O). The only waste product is water, making fuel cells very clean.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22">Higher tier: At the anode, the half equation is 2H&#8322; &rarr; 4H&#8314; + 4e&#8315;. At the cathode, the half equation is O&#8322; + 4H&#8314; + 4e&#8315; &rarr; 2H&#8322;O.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n23">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The only waste product from a hydrogen fuel cell is water. This makes them much cleaner than fossil fuel engines, which produce carbon dioxide and other pollutants.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Fuel Cells vs Rechargeable Batteries</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n24">Advantages of hydrogen fuel cells: the only waste product is water (no CO&#8322;); they do not run out as long as fuel is supplied; and they are more efficient than combustion engines. Disadvantages: hydrogen is difficult and expensive to store and transport safely (it is highly flammable); producing hydrogen itself often requires energy from fossil fuels (unless from electrolysis using renewable energy); the infrastructure for hydrogen refuelling is limited; and fuel cells are currently expensive to manufacture.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25">By contrast, rechargeable batteries can be charged from the mains (convenient), but they eventually degrade and need replacing; their production uses finite resources (lithium, cobalt); and charging from fossil-fuel electricity still produces emissions indirectly.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n26">Titration questions often ask you to describe the method in a set order &mdash; practise writing out the steps without prompts. For &ldquo;Explain&rdquo; questions on chemical cells, always link the reactivity difference between the metals to the voltage produced. Fuel cell questions frequently ask you to &ldquo;compare&rdquo; or &ldquo;evaluate&rdquo; &mdash; make sure you give both advantages and disadvantages with clear scientific reasoning, not just a list.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n27">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n28">A titration uses a burette, pipette, and indicator to find the exact volume of acid needed to neutralise an alkali. Concordant results (within 0.10 cm&sup3;) are averaged to give the accurate titre.</li><li data-narration-id="n29">Chemical cells produce electricity when two different metals in an electrolyte are connected &mdash; the greater the reactivity difference, the higher the voltage. A battery is two or more cells joined together.</li><li data-narration-id="n30">Hydrogen fuel cells produce electricity from hydrogen and oxygen with water as the only waste product, but storing hydrogen safely and building infrastructure remain challenges.</li></ul>
