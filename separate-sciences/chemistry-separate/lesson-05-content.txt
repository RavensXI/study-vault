# Polymers: Addition, Condensation and Natural

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">What Are Polymers?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A very large molecule made by joining many small monomer molecules together in a repeating pattern.">polymer</dfn> is a very large molecule formed when many small molecules called <dfn class="term" data-def="A small, reactive molecule that joins with others to form a polymer chain.">monomers</dfn> bond together in a repeating pattern. The word &ldquo;polymer&rdquo; comes from the Greek for &ldquo;many parts.&rdquo; Polymers can be synthetic (made by humans) or naturally occurring. Everyday synthetic polymers include poly(ethene), PVC, and PTFE, while natural polymers include proteins, starch, and DNA.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">The process of joining monomers together is called <dfn class="term" data-def="A chemical reaction in which many monomer molecules join together to form a polymer.">polymerisation</dfn>. There are two main types of polymerisation you need to know for GCSE Chemistry: addition polymerisation and condensation polymerisation. Each works in a different way and produces a different type of polymer.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n4">Addition Polymerisation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n5"><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of polymerisation in which unsaturated monomers (alkenes) open their double bonds and join together, producing only the polymer with no other product.">addition polymerisation</dfn> occurs when many unsaturated monomer molecules (alkenes) join together. The carbon&ndash;carbon double bond (C=C) in each monomer &ldquo;opens up,&rdquo; allowing the monomers to link into a long chain. No other substance is produced &mdash; the only product is the polymer itself.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The simplest example is poly(ethene). Ethene (C&#8322;H&#8324;) is the monomer. Under high pressure and with a catalyst, thousands of ethene molecules open their double bonds and bond together to form a long-chain polymer. The repeating unit of poly(ethene) looks the same as the monomer but with single bonds on each side instead of a double bond.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n7">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>In addition polymerisation, the only product is the polymer. No atoms are lost or gained &mdash; the polymer has the same empirical formula as the monomer. The letter <em>n</em> in the equation represents a very large number of monomer molecules joining together.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n10">Condensation Polymerisation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n11"><dfn class="term" data-def="A type of polymerisation in which monomers with two functional groups join together, forming a polymer and releasing a small molecule (usually water) as a by-product.">condensation polymerisation</dfn> is different from addition polymerisation in two important ways. First, the monomers have two <dfn class="term" data-def="A specific group of atoms within a molecule that determines how that molecule reacts.">functional groups</dfn> &mdash; one on each end of the molecule. Second, a small molecule (usually water) is released every time two monomers join together. This by-product is the reason it is called &ldquo;condensation&rdquo; polymerisation.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Condensation polymers include polyesters and polyamides. To form a <dfn class="term" data-def="A condensation polymer formed when dicarboxylic acid monomers react with diol monomers, releasing water.">polyester</dfn>, a dicarboxylic acid reacts with a diol. The &ndash;COOH group on the acid reacts with the &ndash;OH group on the alcohol, forming an ester link (&ndash;COO&ndash;) and releasing a molecule of water (H&#8322;O) each time.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The crucial difference: addition polymerisation produces only the polymer, while condensation polymerisation produces the polymer <em>and</em> a small molecule by-product (usually water). This is the most common comparison question in the exam.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">Naturally Occurring Polymers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17">Many of the most important molecules in living organisms are polymers. You need to know three key examples for the exam: proteins, starch, and DNA. Each is built from smaller monomer units through condensation polymerisation.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n21">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>All three naturally occurring polymers &mdash; proteins, starch, and DNA &mdash; are formed by condensation polymerisation. In every case, monomers join together and water molecules are released as by-products.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">Comparing the Two Types of Polymerisation</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">Exam questions frequently ask you to compare addition and condensation polymerisation. The table below summarises the key differences.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>Addition polymerisation:</strong> monomers are alkenes with C=C double bonds; only one product (the polymer); no by-product; examples include poly(ethene), poly(propene), PVC. <strong>Condensation polymerisation:</strong> monomers have two functional groups (e.g. &ndash;OH and &ndash;COOH); two products (the polymer and a small molecule, usually water); examples include polyesters, nylon, proteins, starch, DNA.</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n27">The most common exam question on this topic asks you to <strong>compare</strong> addition and condensation polymerisation. Always state three differences: (1) the type of monomer used, (2) whether a by-product is formed, and (3) give a named example of each. For 6-mark extended response questions, link your answer to naturally occurring polymers such as proteins and DNA to show breadth of knowledge. Remember &mdash; if water is produced, it is condensation polymerisation.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n28">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n29">Addition polymerisation joins alkene monomers by opening C=C double bonds, producing only the polymer with no by-product.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n30">Condensation polymerisation joins monomers with two functional groups, producing the polymer and a small molecule (usually water).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">Proteins (from amino acids), starch (from glucose), and DNA (from nucleotides) are all naturally occurring condensation polymers.</li></ul>
