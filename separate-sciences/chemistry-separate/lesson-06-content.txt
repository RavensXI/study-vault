# Chemical Tests for Ions

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Identifying Ions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">In chemistry, you often need to identify which ions are present in an unknown substance. <dfn class="term" data-def="An atom or group of atoms that has gained or lost electrons, giving it a positive or negative charge.">Ions</dfn> are charged particles formed when atoms gain or lose electrons. Metal atoms lose electrons to form <dfn class="term" data-def="A positively charged ion formed when a metal atom loses one or more electrons.">cations</dfn> (positive ions), while non-metal atoms gain electrons to form <dfn class="term" data-def="A negatively charged ion formed when a non-metal atom gains one or more electrons.">anions</dfn> (negative ions). There are reliable chemical tests for identifying both types, and you need to know them all for your exam.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n3">Flame Tests</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n4">A <dfn class="term" data-def="A test in which a sample is held in a Bunsen burner flame to identify metal ions by the characteristic colour they produce.">flame test</dfn> is used to identify certain metal ions. When compounds containing these metals are heated in a flame, the metal ions emit light of a characteristic colour. To carry out a flame test, you dip a clean nichrome wire loop into hydrochloric acid, then into the sample, and hold it in the blue flame of a Bunsen burner.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>You must memorise these flame test colours: <strong>lithium &mdash; crimson</strong>, <strong>sodium &mdash; yellow</strong>, <strong>potassium &mdash; lilac</strong>, <strong>calcium &mdash; orange-red</strong>, <strong>copper &mdash; green</strong>. The wire must be cleaned with hydrochloric acid between tests to avoid contamination.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Flame Test Colours in Detail</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n6"><strong>Lithium (Li&sup1;&#8314;):</strong> Produces a crimson (deep red) flame. Lithium compounds are used in batteries and fireworks &mdash; the red colour in fireworks often comes from lithium salts.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Sodium (Na&sup1;&#8314;):</strong> Produces a strong yellow flame. Sodium is so common as a contaminant that its yellow colour can mask other flame colours, which is why the wire must be thoroughly cleaned.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Potassium (K&sup1;&#8314;):</strong> Produces a lilac (pale purple) flame. This can be hard to see if sodium is also present, as the yellow colour dominates.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Calcium (Ca&sup2;&#8314;):</strong> Produces an orange-red flame. This is distinct from lithium&rsquo;s crimson &mdash; calcium&rsquo;s colour has more orange in it.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Copper (Cu&sup2;&#8314;):</strong> Produces a green flame. Copper compounds are also responsible for the green colour in some fireworks.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Testing with Sodium Hydroxide Solution</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Some metal ions cannot be identified by flame tests. Instead, you add a few drops of <dfn class="term" data-def="A strong alkali (NaOH) used as a reagent to test for metal ions. It reacts with metal ion solutions to form insoluble metal hydroxides that appear as coloured precipitates.">sodium hydroxide solution</dfn> (NaOH) to a solution of the unknown compound. If certain metal ions are present, an insoluble metal hydroxide forms as a coloured <dfn class="term" data-def="An insoluble solid that forms when two solutions are mixed together. It separates from the solution and can be seen as a cloudy suspension or solid settling out.">precipitate</dfn>.</p>

<!-- DIAGRAM -->

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n13">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Sodium hydroxide precipitate colours: <strong>aluminium &mdash; white (dissolves in excess NaOH)</strong>, <strong>calcium &mdash; white (does not dissolve in excess)</strong>, <strong>magnesium &mdash; white (does not dissolve in excess)</strong>, <strong>copper(II) &mdash; blue</strong>, <strong>iron(II) &mdash; green</strong>, <strong>iron(III) &mdash; brown</strong>.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Sodium Hydroxide Test Results</span>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Aluminium ions (Al&sup3;&#8314;):</strong> Form a white precipitate of aluminium hydroxide, Al(OH)&sub3;. Crucially, this precipitate <em>dissolves</em> when excess NaOH is added. This distinguishes aluminium from calcium and magnesium, which also give white precipitates but do not dissolve in excess.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Calcium ions (Ca&sup2;&#8314;):</strong> Form a white precipitate of calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)&sub2;. This does <em>not</em> dissolve in excess NaOH.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Magnesium ions (Mg&sup2;&#8314;):</strong> Form a white precipitate of magnesium hydroxide, Mg(OH)&sub2;. This does <em>not</em> dissolve in excess NaOH. It is distinguished from calcium by flame test (calcium gives orange-red; magnesium gives no flame colour).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n17"><strong>Copper(II) ions (Cu&sup2;&#8314;):</strong> Form a blue precipitate of copper(II) hydroxide, Cu(OH)&sub2;.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n18"><strong>Iron(II) ions (Fe&sup2;&#8314;):</strong> Form a green precipitate of iron(II) hydroxide, Fe(OH)&sub2;. Sometimes called a &ldquo;sludgy green&rdquo; or &ldquo;dirty green&rdquo; colour.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n19"><strong>Iron(III) ions (Fe&sup3;&#8314;):</strong> Form a brown precipitate of iron(III) hydroxide, Fe(OH)&sub3;. This is a distinctive rusty brown colour.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n20">Testing for Carbonate Ions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n21"><dfn class="term" data-def="A negative ion with the formula CO&#8323;&sup2;&macr; found in compounds like calcium carbonate. Carbonates react with dilute acids to produce carbon dioxide gas.">Carbonate</dfn> ions (CO&sub3;&sup2;&macr;) are tested by adding dilute hydrochloric acid to the substance. If carbonates are present, the mixture fizzes and produces carbon dioxide gas. You confirm that the gas is carbon dioxide by passing it through <dfn class="term" data-def="A solution of calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)&#8322;, in water. It turns milky (cloudy white) when carbon dioxide is bubbled through it, confirming the presence of CO&#8322;.">limewater</dfn> (calcium hydroxide solution) &mdash; if the limewater turns milky (cloudy white), carbon dioxide is confirmed.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The test for carbonates: add dilute acid &rarr; fizzing observed &rarr; gas turns limewater milky. The word equation is: carbonate + acid &rarr; salt + water + carbon dioxide. For example: CaCO&sub3; + 2HCl &rarr; CaCl&sub2; + H&sub2;O + CO&sub2;</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n23">Testing for Halide Ions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n24"><dfn class="term" data-def="Negative ions formed by Group 7 elements (halogens): chloride (Cl&macr;), bromide (Br&macr;), and iodide (I&macr;). They are tested using acidified silver nitrate solution.">Halide</dfn> ions &mdash; chloride (Cl&macr;), bromide (Br&macr;), and iodide (I&macr;) &mdash; are identified by adding dilute nitric acid followed by <dfn class="term" data-def="A solution of AgNO&#8323; used as a reagent to test for halide ions. It reacts with halides to form insoluble silver halide precipitates of distinctive colours.">silver nitrate solution</dfn> (AgNO&sub3;). The nitric acid is added first to remove any interfering carbonate or sulfite ions that could give false positives.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n28">Testing for Sulfate Ions</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><dfn class="term" data-def="A negative ion with the formula SO&#8324;&sup2;&macr;. Sulfate ions are tested using acidified barium chloride solution, which forms a white precipitate of barium sulfate.">Sulfate</dfn> ions (SO&sub4;&sup2;&macr;) are identified by first adding dilute hydrochloric acid to remove any carbonate ions, then adding <dfn class="term" data-def="A solution of BaCl&#8322; used as a reagent to test for sulfate ions. It reacts with sulfates to form an insoluble white precipitate of barium sulfate.">barium chloride solution</dfn> (BaCl&sub2;). If sulfate ions are present, a white precipitate of barium sulfate (BaSO&sub4;) forms. The ionic equation is: Ba&sup2;&#8314; + SO&sub4;&sup2;&macr; &rarr; BaSO&sub4;</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n30">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>To test for sulfates: add dilute HCl, then barium chloride solution. A <strong>white precipitate</strong> of barium sulfate confirms sulfate ions are present. The HCl is added first to prevent false positives from carbonates.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n31">Flame Emission Spectroscopy</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><dfn class="term" data-def="An instrumental technique that identifies metal ions by analysing the specific wavelengths of light emitted when a sample is heated in a flame. It produces a line spectrum unique to each element.">Flame emission spectroscopy</dfn> is an instrumental method of analysis that works on the same principle as flame tests but is far more accurate and sensitive. A sample is introduced into a flame, and the light emitted is passed through a spectroscope, which separates it into individual wavelengths. Each element produces a unique <dfn class="term" data-def="A series of coloured lines at specific positions on a spectrum, unique to each element. Used to identify elements and determine their concentration in a sample.">line spectrum</dfn> &mdash; like a chemical fingerprint.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n33"><strong>Sensitivity:</strong> Instrumental methods can detect very small quantities of an element &mdash; even trace amounts that chemical tests would miss. This makes them essential in forensic science, environmental monitoring, and quality control.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Speed and accuracy:</strong> Machines analyse samples much faster than manual chemical tests and produce quantitative results (exact concentrations), not just qualitative ones (present or absent). They are also more reliable because they remove the subjectivity of judging colours by eye.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n35"><strong>Multiple elements:</strong> Flame emission spectroscopy can identify several different metal ions in a single sample. It can also measure their concentrations by comparing the intensity of emitted light against reference samples of known concentration.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n36"><strong>Small sample size:</strong> Only a very small amount of sample is needed for analysis, which is useful when the sample is rare, expensive, or limited in quantity.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n39">Practical Tips for Ion Tests</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n40">When carrying out chemical tests for ions in a practical or answering exam questions about them, remember these key points. Always clean equipment between tests to avoid contamination. The order of reagent addition matters &mdash; adding nitric acid before silver nitrate for halides, and hydrochloric acid before barium chloride for sulfates. When describing observations, use precise colour terms: &ldquo;white precipitate&rdquo; rather than &ldquo;it went white,&rdquo; and &ldquo;the limewater turned milky&rdquo; rather than &ldquo;it changed.&rdquo;</p>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n41">Ion test questions are very common on Paper 2. Learn the tests as a table &mdash; ion, reagent, observation &mdash; and practise recalling them without notes. When asked to <strong>describe</strong> a test, state the reagent added <em>and</em> the expected observation. For &ldquo;explain&rdquo; questions, include the ionic equation and name the precipitate formed. A common mistake is confusing the halide precipitate colours &mdash; remember <strong>W-C-Y</strong> (white, cream, yellow) for chloride, bromide, iodide. If asked to compare flame tests with flame emission spectroscopy, always mention that instrumental methods are more <strong>sensitive</strong>, <strong>accurate</strong>, and can measure <strong>concentrations</strong>.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n42">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n43">Flame tests identify metal ions by colour: lithium (crimson), sodium (yellow), potassium (lilac), calcium (orange-red), copper (green). Sodium hydroxide solution produces coloured precipitates for aluminium (white, dissolves in excess), calcium and magnesium (white), copper(II) (blue), iron(II) (green), and iron(III) (brown).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n44">Carbonates fizz with dilute acid and produce CO&sub2; (confirmed by turning limewater milky). Halides are tested with acidified silver nitrate: chloride (white), bromide (cream), iodide (yellow). Sulfates give a white precipitate with acidified barium chloride.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n45">Flame emission spectroscopy is an instrumental method that is more sensitive, accurate, and can measure concentrations &mdash; but the equipment is expensive and needs specialist operation.</li></ul>
