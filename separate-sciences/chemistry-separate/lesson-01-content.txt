# Transition Metals and Nanoparticles

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Transition Metals in the Periodic Table</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">The <dfn class="term" data-def="A block of metallic elements found in the central section of the periodic table, between Groups 2 and 3.">transition metals</dfn> sit in the central block of the periodic table, between Group 2 and Group 3. Common examples include iron (Fe), copper (Cu), nickel (Ni), chromium (Cr), cobalt (Co), platinum (Pt), and gold (Au). Unlike the metals in Group 1, transition metals are much harder, stronger, denser, and have much higher melting points &mdash; making them far more useful as structural and engineering materials.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3">Transition metals are good conductors of heat and electricity, which is why copper is used for electrical wiring and iron is the main component in steel for buildings and bridges. They are also much less reactive than Group 1 metals, so they do not react vigorously with water or oxygen at room temperature.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Transition metals have higher melting points, higher densities, and are harder and stronger than Group 1 metals. They are also much less reactive &mdash; they do not react vigorously with water or oxygen.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Comparing Transition Metals with Group 1</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">The AQA spec requires you to compare transition metals directly with <dfn class="term" data-def="The elements in the first column of the periodic table (lithium, sodium, potassium, rubidium, caesium, francium), which are soft, low-density metals that react vigorously with water.">Group 1 metals</dfn> (the alkali metals: lithium, sodium, potassium). The differences arise because transition metals have more electrons available for bonding and their metallic bonds are stronger.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Hardness:</strong> Transition metals are hard and tough (iron is used in construction). Group 1 metals are so soft they can be cut with a knife.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Density:</strong> Transition metals are dense (iron = 7.9 g/cm&sup3;). Group 1 metals have low densities &mdash; lithium, sodium, and potassium all float on water.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Melting point:</strong> Transition metals have high melting points (iron melts at 1538 &deg;C, copper at 1085 &deg;C). Group 1 metals melt at much lower temperatures (sodium melts at just 98 &deg;C).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Reactivity:</strong> Transition metals react slowly or not at all with water and oxygen. Group 1 metals react vigorously &mdash; potassium bursts into lilac flames when placed on water.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Special Properties of Transition Metals</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12">Two properties of transition metals are frequently tested in the exam: their ability to act as <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up itself. It provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy.">catalysts</dfn> and their tendency to form <dfn class="term" data-def="Chemical compounds that absorb certain wavelengths of visible light, giving them a distinctive colour rather than appearing white or grey.">coloured compounds</dfn>.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13"><strong>Catalysts:</strong> Many transition metals and their compounds are used as catalysts in industrial reactions. Iron is the catalyst in the Haber process (making ammonia), nickel is used in the hydrogenation of vegetable oils to make margarine, and platinum is used in catalytic converters in car exhausts to reduce harmful emissions. Catalysts speed up reactions without being used up themselves, lowering the <dfn class="term" data-def="The minimum amount of energy that reacting particles must have in order for a successful collision to occur.">activation energy</dfn> needed for the reaction to occur.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n14"><strong>Coloured compounds:</strong> Unlike Group 1 metals, which form white or colourless compounds, transition metals form compounds with distinctive colours. Copper sulfate is blue, iron(II) compounds are green, iron(III) compounds are orange-brown, and potassium dichromate is orange. These colours are useful in identifying unknown substances during chemical analysis.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n15">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Transition metals can form ions with different charges (e.g. Fe&sup2;&#8314; and Fe&sup3;&#8314;), which is why iron forms both green iron(II) compounds and orange-brown iron(III) compounds. Group 1 metals only form ions with a +1 charge.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n16">What Are Nanoparticles?</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n17"><dfn class="term" data-def="Particles that have dimensions between 1 and 100 nanometres (nm), containing a few hundred to a few thousand atoms.">Nanoparticles</dfn> are extremely small structures with dimensions between 1 and 100 nanometres (nm). A single nanometre is one billionth of a metre (1 nm = 1 &times; 10&#8315;&sup9; m). To put this in perspective, a human hair is around 80,000 nm wide, so nanoparticles are roughly a thousand times smaller. They typically contain a few hundred to a few thousand atoms.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n18">Nanoparticles are much smaller than <dfn class="term" data-def="Particles between 1 &micro;m and 2.5 &micro;m (fine particles) or larger than 2.5 &micro;m but smaller than about 0.01 m (coarse particles, also called dust).">fine particles</dfn> (1 &micro;m &ndash; 2.5 &micro;m) and <dfn class="term" data-def="Particles with diameters larger than 2.5 &micro;m and up to around 10 mm, commonly known as dust.">coarse particles</dfn> (larger than 2.5 &micro;m, often called dust). The AQA spec expects you to understand the size scale: coarse &gt; fine &gt; nanoparticles.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n19">Surface Area to Volume Ratio</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n20">The key reason nanoparticles behave differently from the same material in bulk is their very high <dfn class="term" data-def="The ratio comparing the total surface area of a particle to its volume. As particles get smaller, this ratio increases dramatically.">surface area to volume ratio</dfn>. As a particle gets smaller, its surface area increases relative to its volume. This means a much greater proportion of the atoms are on the surface, where they can interact with other substances.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n21">You can demonstrate this with a simple cube calculation. A cube with sides of 1 cm has a surface area of 6 cm&sup2; and a volume of 1 cm&sup3;, giving a ratio of 6:1. If you cut that same cube into smaller cubes with sides of 0.1 cm, the total surface area increases to 60 cm&sup2; while the total volume stays at 1 cm&sup3; &mdash; a ratio of 60:1. At the nanoscale, this ratio becomes enormous.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n22">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>As particle size decreases, the surface area to volume ratio increases. This is why nanoparticles have different properties from the same material in bulk &mdash; more atoms are exposed on the surface, making them more reactive and more effective as catalysts, coatings, and drug delivery agents.</p>
</div>

    <p data-narration-id="n24">Surface area = 6 &times; (2 &times; 2) = 24 cm&sup2;. Volume = 2 &times; 2 &times; 2 = 8 cm&sup3;. Ratio = 24 &divide; 8 = <strong>3:1</strong>.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Now a cube with sides of 0.5 cm.</strong> Surface area = 6 &times; (0.5 &times; 0.5) = 1.5 cm&sup2;. Volume = 0.5 &times; 0.5 &times; 0.5 = 0.125 cm&sup3;. Ratio = 1.5 &divide; 0.125 = <strong>12:1</strong>.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26">The smaller cube has a surface area to volume ratio four times greater than the larger one. At the nanoscale, this ratio is thousands of times larger still.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n27">Uses of Nanoparticles</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n28">Because of their high surface area to volume ratio, nanoparticles have properties that make them useful in a range of applications. The AQA spec highlights several key uses.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Sunscreens:</strong> Titanium dioxide and zinc oxide nanoparticles are used in sunscreens. In bulk form these chemicals are white and leave a visible white layer on the skin. As nanoparticles they are transparent &mdash; they still absorb and reflect harmful UV radiation but cannot be seen, making the product more cosmetically appealing.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n30"><strong>Catalysts:</strong> Nanoparticles of metals such as gold and platinum can act as highly efficient catalysts because their enormous surface area provides many more active sites for reactions to take place. Less material is needed compared to using the bulk metal, reducing costs.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n31"><strong>Medicine:</strong> Nanoparticles can be used for targeted drug delivery &mdash; carrying medication directly to diseased cells (such as cancer cells) while minimising side effects to healthy tissue. Silver nanoparticles have antibacterial properties and are used in wound dressings and medical equipment coatings.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n32"><strong>Electronics and coatings:</strong> Nanoparticles are used in electronics to build smaller, more powerful components. They are also used in self-cleaning glass coatings and antibacterial surfaces.</p>

    <p data-narration-id="n33">Nanoparticles are so small that they may be able to enter cells and pass through membranes that larger particles cannot cross, including the blood&ndash;brain barrier. There are concerns that inhaling nanoparticles could damage the lungs in a similar way to asbestos fibres. Because nanoparticles are a relatively new technology, their long-term effects on human health and the environment are not yet fully understood. Scientists continue to research whether nanoparticles in sunscreens, food packaging, and other consumer products pose any risk when absorbed through the skin or ingested.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n34">The exam may ask you to <strong>evaluate</strong> the use of nanoparticles &mdash; meaning you need to weigh up the benefits (better sunscreens, targeted medicine, efficient catalysts) against the potential risks (unknown long-term health effects, environmental concerns).</p>

</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n35">When comparing transition metals with Group 1 metals, always state the specific property and give an example for each side &mdash; for instance, &ldquo;iron has a melting point of 1538 &deg;C compared with sodium at 98 &deg;C.&rdquo; For nanoparticle questions, the key phrase is &ldquo;high surface area to volume ratio&rdquo; &mdash; examiners expect to see this linked to why properties change at the nanoscale. In &ldquo;evaluate&rdquo; questions about nanoparticles, always cover both benefits and risks to access the top marks.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n36">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n37">Transition metals are harder, denser, and have higher melting points than Group 1 metals. They also form coloured compounds and act as catalysts (e.g. iron in the Haber process).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n38">Nanoparticles (1&ndash;100 nm) have a very high surface area to volume ratio, which gives them different properties from the same material in bulk &mdash; used in sunscreens, catalysts, medicine, and electronics.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n39">The long-term health and environmental effects of nanoparticles are not yet fully understood &mdash; the exam expects you to evaluate both the benefits and the risks.</li></ul>
