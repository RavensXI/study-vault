# Alkenes, Alcohols and Carboxylic Acids

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Functional Groups</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2">Organic chemistry is the study of carbon compounds. Carbon atoms can form four covalent bonds, which allows them to build chains and rings of almost unlimited size. The properties of an organic molecule depend on its <dfn class="term" data-def="An atom or group of atoms within a molecule that determines the chemical properties of that molecule.">functional group</dfn> &mdash; the specific atom or group of atoms within the molecule that controls how it reacts. Alkenes, alcohols, and carboxylic acids are three important homologous series, each defined by a different functional group.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n3">Alkenes</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n4"><dfn class="term" data-def="A homologous series of hydrocarbons that contain a carbon&ndash;carbon double bond (C=C) as their functional group, with the general formula C&#8345;H&#8322;&#8345;.">Alkenes</dfn> are hydrocarbons that contain at least one <dfn class="term" data-def="A covalent bond in which two pairs of electrons are shared between two carbon atoms, written as C=C &mdash; the functional group of alkenes.">carbon&ndash;carbon double bond</dfn> (C=C). This double bond is their functional group. The general formula for alkenes is C&#8345;H&#8322;&#8345;, where <em>n</em> is the number of carbon atoms. The simplest alkene is ethene (C&#8322;H&#8324;), followed by propene (C&#8323;H&#8326;) and butene (C&#8324;H&#8328;).</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n5">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Alkenes are <strong>unsaturated</strong> hydrocarbons because they contain a C=C double bond. Alkanes are <strong>saturated</strong> because they contain only single C&ndash;C bonds. This single difference in bonding gives the two families very different reactivity.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n6">Because alkenes have a double bond, they are more reactive than alkanes. The double bond can &ldquo;open up&rdquo; and allow other atoms to add across it. This type of reaction is called an <dfn class="term" data-def="A reaction in which atoms or groups add across a double bond in an unsaturated molecule, producing a single saturated product with no other substance formed.">addition reaction</dfn>.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Addition of hydrogen (hydrogenation):</strong> Alkenes react with hydrogen gas in the presence of a nickel catalyst at about 150&thinsp;&deg;C. The hydrogen atoms add across the double bond, converting the alkene into an alkane. For example: ethene + hydrogen &rarr; ethane (C&#8322;H&#8324; + H&#8322; &rarr; C&#8322;H&#8326;). This reaction is used industrially to harden vegetable oils into margarine.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Addition of water (hydration):</strong> Alkenes react with steam in the presence of a phosphoric acid catalyst at high temperature and pressure. This produces an alcohol. For example: ethene + steam &rarr; ethanol (C&#8322;H&#8324; + H&#8322;O &rarr; C&#8322;H&#8325;OH). This is the industrial method for making ethanol.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Addition of halogens:</strong> Alkenes react rapidly with halogens such as bromine. Ethene decolourises <dfn class="term" data-def="An orange solution of bromine dissolved in water, used as a chemical test for unsaturation &mdash; it turns colourless when shaken with an alkene.">bromine water</dfn> from orange to colourless, forming a colourless dibromo compound. This is the standard test to distinguish alkenes from alkanes &mdash; alkanes do not decolourise bromine water because they have no double bond to react with.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n10">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p><strong>Test for alkenes:</strong> Shake the substance with bromine water. If the bromine water changes from <strong>orange to colourless</strong>, the substance is an alkene (unsaturated). If it stays orange, the substance is saturated (an alkane).</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n11">Alcohols</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n12"><dfn class="term" data-def="A homologous series of organic compounds that contain the hydroxyl functional group (&ndash;OH) attached to a carbon atom.">Alcohols</dfn> are a homologous series of organic compounds whose functional group is the hydroxyl group (&ndash;OH). The general formula for alcohols is C&#8345;H&#8322;&#8345;&#8330;&#8321;OH. The first four members are <dfn class="term" data-def="The simplest alcohol, with the formula CH&#8323;OH &mdash; toxic, used as a solvent and fuel.">methanol</dfn> (CH&#8323;OH), <dfn class="term" data-def="An alcohol with the formula C&#8322;H&#8325;OH &mdash; the alcohol found in alcoholic drinks, also used as a solvent and fuel.">ethanol</dfn> (C&#8322;H&#8325;OH), propanol (C&#8323;H&#8327;OH), and butanol (C&#8324;H&#8329;OH).</p>
<p data-narration-id="n13">Alcohols dissolve in water to form neutral solutions &mdash; they do not produce H&#8314; ions, so they are not acidic. They react with sodium metal to produce hydrogen gas and a sodium alkoxide. Alcohols also burn in air, releasing energy, which makes them useful as fuels. Ethanol is the alcohol in alcoholic drinks and is widely used as a solvent and as a fuel (often blended with petrol).</p>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">

    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n14">There are two ways to make ethanol on an industrial scale:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Hydration of ethene:</strong> Ethene (from cracking crude oil) reacts with steam using a phosphoric acid catalyst at 300&thinsp;&deg;C and 60&ndash;70 atm. This is a continuous process that produces pure ethanol quickly, but it uses a non-renewable feedstock (crude oil).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong><dfn class="term" data-def="The anaerobic breakdown of sugars by yeast enzymes to produce ethanol and carbon dioxide, used to make alcoholic drinks and biofuel.">Fermentation</dfn>:</strong> Yeast converts sugars (e.g. glucose from crops) into ethanol and carbon dioxide at about 25&ndash;35&thinsp;&deg;C in the absence of oxygen: glucose &rarr; ethanol + carbon dioxide (C&#8326;H&#8321;&#8322;O&#8326; &rarr; 2C&#8322;H&#8325;OH + 2CO&#8322;). Fermentation uses a renewable feedstock (sugar from plants) and requires low temperatures, so energy costs are low. However, the process is slow (batch process), and the ethanol produced is impure &mdash; it must be separated from the mixture by fractional distillation.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n17">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Hydration of ethene is faster and gives purer ethanol but uses a <strong>non-renewable</strong> feedstock. Fermentation is slower and gives impure ethanol but uses a <strong>renewable</strong> feedstock and lower temperatures. Exam questions often ask you to compare these two methods.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n18">Carboxylic Acids</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><dfn class="term" data-def="A homologous series of organic compounds containing the &ndash;COOH functional group, which makes them weak acids.">Carboxylic acids</dfn> contain the functional group &ndash;COOH (also written as &ndash;CO&#8322;H). This group contains both a C=O double bond and an O&ndash;H bond. The first members of the series are methanoic acid (HCOOH), <dfn class="term" data-def="The carboxylic acid with the formula CH&#8323;COOH &mdash; the acid found in vinegar, also known as acetic acid.">ethanoic acid</dfn> (CH&#8323;COOH &mdash; found in vinegar), propanoic acid, and butanoic acid.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n20">Carboxylic acids dissolve in water to produce <dfn class="term" data-def="Acids that only partially ionise in water, releasing a low concentration of H&#8314; ions and giving a pH of roughly 3&ndash;6.">weak acidic</dfn> solutions. They ionise only partially &mdash; only a small proportion of the acid molecules release H&#8314; ions at any one time. This means a solution of ethanoic acid has a higher pH (is less acidic) than a solution of hydrochloric acid of the same concentration.</p>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n21">Carboxylic acids show the typical reactions of acids, but they react more slowly because they are weak acids:</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n22"><strong>With metal carbonates:</strong> Carboxylic acids react with carbonates to produce a salt, water, and carbon dioxide. For example: ethanoic acid + sodium carbonate &rarr; sodium ethanoate + water + carbon dioxide (2CH&#8323;COOH + Na&#8322;CO&#8323; &rarr; 2CH&#8323;COONa + H&#8322;O + CO&#8322;).</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n23"><strong>With water:</strong> Carboxylic acids dissolve in water and partially dissociate, producing H&#8314; ions. The solution is acidic but weakly so.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n24"><strong>With alcohols:</strong> Carboxylic acids react with alcohols in the presence of an acid catalyst to form <dfn class="term" data-def="A compound formed when a carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol in the presence of an acid catalyst &mdash; esters often have pleasant, fruity smells.">esters</dfn>. For example: ethanoic acid + ethanol &rarr; ethyl ethanoate + water. Esters often have pleasant, fruity smells and are used in flavourings, perfumes, and solvents.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Comparing Strong and Weak Acids</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n25"><strong>Strong acids</strong> (e.g. hydrochloric acid, sulfuric acid) completely ionise in water &mdash; every acid molecule releases H&#8314; ions. <strong>Weak acids</strong> (e.g. ethanoic acid, citric acid) only partially ionise &mdash; most of the acid molecules remain intact in solution. At the same concentration, a strong acid has a lower pH, reacts faster, and conducts electricity better than a weak acid because it has a higher concentration of H&#8314; ions.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n26">Do not confuse acid <em>strength</em> with <em>concentration</em>. Strength refers to the degree of ionisation. Concentration refers to how much acid is dissolved per unit volume of solution. A concentrated weak acid (e.g. concentrated ethanoic acid) can still have a higher pH than a dilute strong acid.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n27">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Carboxylic acids are <strong>weak acids</strong> because they only partially ionise in water. At the same concentration, ethanoic acid has a higher pH, reacts more slowly, and produces less gas with carbonates than hydrochloric acid (a strong acid).</p>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n28">When answering questions about organic chemistry, always identify the <strong>functional group</strong> first &mdash; it determines the reactions. For &ldquo;compare&rdquo; questions on making ethanol, structure your answer around the feedstock (renewable vs non-renewable), the conditions (temperature, catalyst), the rate (fast continuous vs slow batch), and the purity of the product. For the bromine water test, state the colour change <em>and</em> what it tells you about the bonding.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n29">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n30">Alkenes contain a C=C double bond, making them unsaturated. They undergo addition reactions and decolourise bromine water from orange to colourless.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n31">Alcohols contain the &ndash;OH functional group. Ethanol can be made by hydration of ethene (fast, non-renewable) or fermentation of sugars (slow, renewable).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n32">Carboxylic acids contain the &ndash;COOH group and are weak acids &mdash; they partially ionise in water and react with carbonates and alcohols (forming esters).</li></ul>
