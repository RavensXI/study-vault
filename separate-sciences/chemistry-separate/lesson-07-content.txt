# Materials, Corrosion, Haber Process and Fertilisers

<h2 data-narration-id="n1">Corrosion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n2"><dfn class="term" data-def="The gradual destruction of a metal by chemical reaction with substances in its environment, such as oxygen and water.">Corrosion</dfn> is the destruction of a metal caused by reactions with substances in the environment. Most metals corrode when exposed to air and water over time. The most familiar example is the rusting of iron and steel.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n3"><dfn class="term" data-def="The corrosion of iron or steel in the presence of both oxygen and water, forming hydrated iron(III) oxide.">Rusting</dfn> is the corrosion of iron. It only happens when iron is in contact with both oxygen and water. The chemical name for rust is <dfn class="term" data-def="The chemical name for rust &mdash; a flaky, orange-brown compound formed when iron reacts with oxygen and water.">hydrated iron(III) oxide</dfn>. Unlike the oxide layers on aluminium, rust is soft and flaky, so it falls away from the surface and exposes fresh metal underneath. This means rusting continues until all the iron has corroded.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n4">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Iron requires <strong>both</strong> oxygen <strong>and</strong> water to rust. Remove either one and rusting does not occur. This is the basis for all rust prevention methods.</p>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n5">Preventing Corrosion</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n6">Since rusting needs oxygen and water, prevention methods work by creating a barrier between the iron and these reactants, or by using a more reactive metal to protect the iron.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Barrier Methods of Rust Prevention</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n7"><strong>Painting:</strong> A layer of paint stops oxygen and water reaching the iron surface. Used on bridges, cars, and railings. If the paint is scratched or chips off, the iron underneath will rust.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n8"><strong>Oiling or greasing:</strong> A thin coat of oil or grease creates a waterproof barrier. Used on moving parts such as bicycle chains and machine components, where paint would crack or wear off.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n9"><strong>Electroplating:</strong> Coating the iron with a thin layer of another metal (such as tin, chromium, or nickel) using electrolysis. Tin cans are steel coated with tin. If the coating is breached, the iron underneath can still rust.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n10"><strong>Galvanising:</strong> Coating iron or steel with a layer of <dfn class="term" data-def="A corrosion-resistant bluish-white metal used to coat iron and steel in the galvanising process.">zinc</dfn>. Zinc acts as a barrier, but it also provides <dfn class="term" data-def="A method of rust prevention where a more reactive metal (such as zinc or magnesium) is placed in contact with iron, corroding preferentially to protect the iron.">sacrificial protection</dfn> &mdash; even if the coating is scratched, the zinc corrodes instead of the iron because zinc is more reactive.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n11"><strong>Sacrificial protection</strong> uses a block of a more reactive metal (usually zinc or magnesium) attached to or near the iron structure. The more reactive metal oxidises preferentially, donating electrons to the iron and preventing it from reacting. This method is used on ships&rsquo; hulls, underground pipes, and offshore oil rigs. The sacrificial block must be replaced periodically as it is gradually consumed.</p>

<h2 data-narration-id="n12">Alloys</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n13">An <dfn class="term" data-def="A mixture of a metal with one or more other elements, usually other metals or carbon, designed to improve properties such as strength or corrosion resistance.">alloy</dfn> is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (usually other metals, but sometimes non-metals like carbon). Alloys are made because pure metals are often too soft for practical use. In a pure metal, the atoms are arranged in regular layers that can slide over each other easily. In an alloy, atoms of different sizes disrupt the regular layers, making it harder for them to slide. This makes alloys harder and stronger than pure metals.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n14">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>Most metals in everyday use are alloys, not pure metals. The different-sized atoms in an alloy disrupt the regular arrangement of layers, preventing them from sliding and making the alloy harder.</p>
</div>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Examples of Common Alloys</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n15"><strong>Steel</strong> is an alloy of iron and carbon (0.1&ndash;2% carbon). Low-carbon steel is easy to shape and used in car bodies. High-carbon steel is harder and used in cutting tools. <strong>Stainless steel</strong> contains chromium (and often nickel) and resists corrosion &mdash; used in cutlery, surgical instruments, and kitchen sinks.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n16"><strong>Bronze</strong> is an alloy of copper and tin &mdash; hard and resistant to corrosion, used for statues and ship propellers. <strong>Brass</strong> is copper and zinc &mdash; harder than pure copper, used in musical instruments and plumbing fittings. <strong>Gold alloys</strong> are gold mixed with silver, copper, or zinc to make them harder &mdash; pure gold (24 carat) is too soft for jewellery.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n17">Ceramics, Polymers, and Composites</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n18"><dfn class="term" data-def="Non-metallic, inorganic solid materials made by heating and cooling, such as clay pottery, glass, and brick &mdash; typically hard, brittle, and resistant to heat.">Ceramics</dfn> are non-metallic solids made by heating. Examples include clay pottery, bricks, and glass. Ceramics are very hard, resistant to heat and corrosion, but they are brittle &mdash; they shatter rather than bend when force is applied.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n19"><dfn class="term" data-def="Materials made from two or more different materials combined together to produce a material with improved properties, where the original materials remain distinct.">Composites</dfn> are materials made by combining two or more different materials. Each component keeps its own properties, but together the composite has improved characteristics that neither material has alone. The two parts are a <strong>matrix</strong> (the background material that binds everything together) and a <strong>reinforcement</strong> (the material that adds strength or stiffness).</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Examples of Composite Materials</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>

  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n20"><strong>Concrete:</strong> Gravel or crusite (reinforcement) held together by cement (matrix). Strong in compression but weak in tension. <strong>Reinforced concrete:</strong> Steel bars or mesh (reinforcement) embedded in concrete (matrix) &mdash; strong in both compression and tension, used in buildings and bridges.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n21"><strong>Fibreglass (GRP):</strong> Glass fibres (reinforcement) set in a polymer resin (matrix). Lightweight and strong, used in boat hulls, car body panels, and insulation. <strong>Carbon fibre reinforced polymer:</strong> Carbon fibres in a polymer resin. Extremely strong and lightweight, used in aircraft, racing cars, and tennis rackets.</p>

</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n22">The Haber Process</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n23">The <dfn class="term" data-def="An industrial process for manufacturing ammonia by reacting nitrogen and hydrogen at high temperature and pressure in the presence of an iron catalyst.">Haber process</dfn> is the industrial method for making <dfn class="term" data-def="A compound with the formula NH&#8323;, made from nitrogen and hydrogen &mdash; used to manufacture fertilisers, explosives, and cleaning products.">ammonia</dfn> (NH&#8323;). Ammonia is a vital chemical because it is used to make fertilisers, nitric acid, and cleaning products. Without the Haber process, there would not be enough food to feed the world&rsquo;s population.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n24">The raw materials are <strong>nitrogen</strong> (obtained from the air by fractional distillation of liquid air) and <strong>hydrogen</strong> (obtained from natural gas or the cracking of hydrocarbons). The balanced equation is:</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n25">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>N&#8322;(g) + 3H&#8322;(g) &#8652; 2NH&#8323;(g)</p>
  <p>This reaction is <strong>reversible</strong> &mdash; it can go in both directions. The yield of ammonia is only about 15%, so unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen are recycled back through the reactor.</p>
</div>

<p data-narration-id="n26">The conditions used in the Haber process represent a compromise between rate and yield:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n27"><strong>Temperature:</strong> About 450&thinsp;&deg;C. The forward reaction is exothermic, so a lower temperature would give a higher yield. However, too low a temperature makes the reaction too slow. 450&thinsp;&deg;C is a compromise &mdash; fast enough to be economical, but the yield is only about 15%.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n28"><strong>Pressure:</strong> About 200 atmospheres. There are 4 molecules of gas on the left (1 N&#8322; + 3 H&#8322;) and only 2 on the right (2 NH&#8323;). High pressure shifts the equilibrium towards the side with fewer gas molecules &mdash; the products. Higher pressures would give even higher yields, but they are expensive and dangerous.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n29"><strong>Catalyst:</strong> An <dfn class="term" data-def="A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being used up &mdash; the Haber process uses an iron catalyst.">iron catalyst</dfn> is used. It speeds up the reaction so equilibrium is reached faster. The catalyst does not change the position of equilibrium or the yield &mdash; it just helps the system get there more quickly.</p>

<div class="collapsible">
  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Why Are the Conditions a Compromise?</span>
    <svg class="collapsible-icon" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2"><polyline points="6 9 12 15 18 9"/></svg>
  </button>
  <div class="collapsible-content"><div class="collapsible-inner">
    <p data-narration-id="n30">The forward reaction is exothermic, so Le Chatelier&rsquo;s principle says a lower temperature would shift equilibrium to the right and increase yield. But at low temperatures, the particles have less kinetic energy, fewer successful collisions occur, and the rate of reaction is too slow to be practical. The chosen temperature (450&thinsp;&deg;C) gives a reasonable rate with an acceptable yield. The unreacted gases are recycled to maximise overall conversion.</p>
    <p data-narration-id="n31">Similarly, very high pressures would increase yield further, but the cost of building and maintaining equipment that can withstand extreme pressures, along with increased safety risks, means 200 atmospheres is the economically practical compromise.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

<h2 data-narration-id="n32">NPK Fertilisers</h2>
<p data-narration-id="n33"><dfn class="term" data-def="Fertilisers containing the three essential elements for plant growth: nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K).">NPK fertilisers</dfn> contain three essential elements that plants need for healthy growth:</p>
<p data-narration-id="n34"><strong>Nitrogen (N)</strong> &mdash; promotes leaf and stem growth. <strong>Phosphorus (P)</strong> &mdash; promotes root development and helps with energy transfer. <strong>Potassium (K)</strong> &mdash; helps with disease resistance, flower and fruit development, and overall plant health.</p>
<p data-narration-id="n35">Without these minerals, crops grow poorly. Intensive farming removes these elements from the soil faster than natural processes can replace them, so farmers add fertilisers to maintain crop yields and feed growing populations.</p>

<div class="key-fact" data-narration-id="n36">
  <div class="key-fact-label">Key Fact</div>
  <p>The nitrogen in NPK fertilisers comes from ammonia (via the Haber process). Ammonia can be reacted with nitric acid to produce ammonium nitrate (NH&#8324;NO&#8323;), a widely used nitrogen fertiliser. Phosphorus comes from phosphate rock, and potassium comes from potassium chloride or potassium sulfate mined from the ground.</p>
</div>

  <button class="collapsible-toggle" aria-expanded="false">
    <span>Making NPK Fertilisers in the Lab</span>

    <p data-narration-id="n37">In the laboratory, NPK fertilisers can be made by mixing the correct proportions of compounds containing nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. For example, mixing ammonium sulfate (provides N), potassium phosphate (provides P and K), and potassium chloride (provides K) in the right ratio produces a balanced NPK fertiliser. Industrially, <dfn class="term" data-def="A salt formed by reacting ammonia with nitric acid &mdash; formula NH&#8324;NO&#8323;, widely used as a nitrogen fertiliser.">ammonium nitrate</dfn> is produced by neutralising ammonia solution with nitric acid: NH&#8323; + HNO&#8323; &rarr; NH&#8324;NO&#8323;.</p>
  </div></div>
</div>

## Exam Tip

<p data-narration-id="n38">Questions on the Haber process often ask you to explain why specific conditions are used. Always link your answer to Le Chatelier&rsquo;s principle and the idea of compromise &mdash; state the effect on both rate and yield, then explain why the chosen condition is a balance between the two. For &ldquo;compare and explain&rdquo; questions on corrosion prevention, name the method, state whether it uses a barrier or sacrificial protection, and explain how it works at the particle or reactivity level.</p>

## Conclusion

<h3 data-narration-id="n39">Key Takeaways</h3>
<ul><li data-narration-id="n40">Iron rusts when exposed to both oxygen and water &mdash; prevention methods use barriers (paint, oil, electroplating) or sacrificial protection (zinc/magnesium corroding instead of the iron).</li>
<li data-narration-id="n41">Alloys are harder than pure metals because different-sized atoms disrupt the regular layers and prevent them from sliding. Composites combine a matrix and reinforcement for improved properties.</li>
<li data-narration-id="n42">The Haber process makes ammonia (N&#8322; + 3H&#8322; &#8652; 2NH&#8323;) at 450&thinsp;&deg;C, 200 atm, with an iron catalyst &mdash; conditions are a compromise between rate and yield. Ammonia is used to make NPK fertilisers.</li></ul>
