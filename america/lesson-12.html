Civil Rights: King, Malcolm X & Black Power

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Sit-In Movement

On 1 February 1960, four Black college students — Ezell Blair Jr., David Richmond, Franklin McCain, and Joseph McNeil — sat at a whites-only lunch counter at Woolworth’s in Greensboro, North Carolina, and asked to be served. When refused, they stayed seated. This simple act sparked the sit-in movement.

Key Fact — The Greensboro Sit-Ins

Within two months, sit-ins had spread to 54 cities across 9 states. By the end of 1960, around 70,000 people had taken part. Woolworth’s in Greensboro desegregated its lunch counter on 25 July 1960. The movement inspired the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC), founded in April 1960.

The Freedom Rides (1961)

In 1960, the Supreme Court ruled in Boynton v. Virginia that segregation on interstate buses and in bus terminals was illegal. Southern states ignored this. To challenge their defiance, the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE) organised the Freedom Rides.

On 4 May 1961, 13 riders — Black and white — set off from Washington, D.C., on two buses heading through the Deep South. They sat in integrated groups and used whites-only facilities at bus stations.

In Anniston, Alabama, one bus was firebombed. In Birmingham, riders were savagely beaten by Klansmen while police stood by. Commissioner Bull Connor had allegedly given the mob 15 minutes to attack before officers stepped in.

New volunteers — many from SNCC — replaced the original riders. In Montgomery, riders were attacked again. Attorney General Robert Kennedy sent 400 federal marshals to protect them.

In September 1961, the Interstate Commerce Commission issued strict new rules banning segregation in interstate travel — and this time enforcement followed.

The Albany Movement (1961–62)

Not every campaign was a success. In Albany, Georgia (1961–62), King and the SCLC joined a broad local protest against segregation. But the police chief, Laurie Pritchett, studied King’s tactics. Instead of using violent force, Pritchett quietly arrested protesters and avoided dramatic confrontations. Without shocking images for the media, the campaign attracted little national attention. Albany is seen as a failure for King — but he learned from it. He realised he needed opponents who would react with visible brutality to generate the media coverage the movement needed.

Birmingham, Alabama (1963)

In spring 1963, King and the SCLC launched a major campaign in Birmingham, Alabama — widely seen as the most segregated city in America. King chose Birmingham deliberately. He knew its racist police commissioner, Bull Connor, would respond with extreme violence, creating the media coverage that Albany had lacked.

Key Fact — Birmingham Campaign

On 2 May 1963, over 1,000 Black schoolchildren marched through Birmingham in the Children’s Crusade. Bull Connor ordered police to attack with fire hoses, police dogs, and batons. TV cameras broadcast the shocking images worldwide. President Kennedy said the footage made him “sick” and began drafting civil rights legislation.

While jailed during the campaign, King wrote his famous “Letter from Birmingham Jail,” defending non-violent direct action: “Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere.”

The March on Washington (1963)

On 28 August 1963, around 250,000 people gathered at the Lincoln Memorial for the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom. The crowd was notably interracial — around 60,000 were white.

Key Fact — “I Have a Dream”

King delivered his legendary “I Have a Dream” speech: “I have a dream that my four little children will one day live in a nation where they will not be judged by the color of their skin but by the content of their character.” It was broadcast live on television and became one of the most famous speeches in history.

The march put huge pressure on Congress to pass civil rights legislation. But weeks later, a bomb killed four young girls — Addie Mae Collins, Cynthia Wesley, Carole Robertson, and Carol Denise McNair — at the 16th Street Baptist Church in Birmingham on 15 September 1963. The horror showed the struggle was far from over.

The Civil Rights Act (1964)

Kennedy introduced a civil rights bill in June 1963, but it stalled in Congress. After Kennedy’s assassination on 22 November 1963, President Lyndon B. Johnson used his political skill — and the nation’s grief — to push it through.

Key Fact — Civil Rights Act 1964

Signed on 2 July 1964, the Act:

  • Banned discrimination based on race, colour, religion, sex, or national origin in employment and public places
  • Outlawed segregation in schools, restaurants, hotels, theatres, and all public facilities
  • Set up the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to enforce workplace anti-discrimination laws
  • Let the federal government withhold funding from institutions that practised discrimination

In 1964, King was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for his non-violent leadership of the civil rights movement — global recognition of the movement’s moral power.

Selma and the Voting Rights Act (1965)

Despite the Civil Rights Act, Black voters in the South still faced huge obstacles. In Selma, Alabama, only 2% of eligible Black citizens were registered to vote due to intimidation, literacy tests, and deliberate obstruction.

In early 1965, King and the SCLC launched a voter registration drive in Selma. On 7 March 1965, around 600 marchers set out from Selma to Montgomery. At the Edmund Pettus Bridge, state troopers attacked them with tear gas, clubs, and mounted charges.

This became “Bloody Sunday.” TV footage of peaceful marchers being beaten built overwhelming public support for voting rights.

A third march, with federal protection, completed the 54-mile journey on 25 March 1965. 25,000 marchers arrived in Montgomery.

Key Fact — Voting Rights Act 1965

Signed on 6 August 1965, the Act:

  • Banned literacy tests and other tools used to block Black voters
  • Allowed federal registrars to register voters where discrimination was found
  • Required states with a history of discrimination to get federal approval before changing voting laws

The impact was dramatic: within a year, 250,000 new Black voters registered. In Mississippi, Black voter registration rose from 6.7% to 59.8% by 1968.

Malcolm X and Black Power

Not all African Americans backed King’s non-violence and integration. Malcolm X was the most prominent alternative voice.

Malcolm X was a minister of the Nation of Islam, led by Elijah Muhammad. He rejected integration and non-violence, calling instead for Black nationalism, self-defence, and racial pride. His famous phrase was “by any means necessary.” He called King’s March on Washington the “Farce on Washington.”

Malcolm X appealed especially to young Black Americans in Northern cities, where legal segregation did not exist but poverty, unemployment, and police brutality fuelled deep frustration. He argued that Black people should build their own businesses, schools, and communities.

In 1964, he left the Nation of Islam and made a pilgrimage to Mecca, which led him to adopt a more inclusive worldview. He was assassinated on 21 February 1965 in New York. His autobiography, published after his death, became hugely influential.

The Rise of Black Power

By the mid-1960s, many younger activists were frustrated with the slow pace of change and the continued violence against Black communities. The Black Power movement emerged, rejecting non-violence in favour of racial pride and self-determination.

Key Fact — Black Power
  • Stokely Carmichael, the new leader of SNCC, popularised the slogan “Black Power” in 1966. He expelled white members from SNCC, marking a clear shift from integration to Black separatism
  • The Black Panther Party, founded in October 1966 by Huey Newton and Bobby Seale in Oakland, California, openly carried firearms, ran armed patrols to monitor police, and organised community programmes including free breakfasts for children
  • At the 1968 Mexico City Olympics, sprinters Tommie Smith and John Carlos raised black-gloved fists on the medal podium during the national anthem — a globally televised moment of Black Power protest that became one of the most iconic images of the era
  • Black Power divided opinion — it inspired racial pride but alarmed white Americans and split the civil rights movement

The FBI and the Civil Rights Movement

FBI director J. Edgar Hoover was deeply hostile to both King and the Black Panthers. The FBI ran a secret programme called COINTELPRO to spy on, infiltrate, and discredit civil rights leaders. Hoover called King the “most dangerous Negro in America” and tried to destroy his reputation through surveillance and leaked recordings. The FBI also targeted the Black Panthers, raiding their offices and arresting their leaders. This government hostility shows how threatening the movement was to the American establishment.

Urban Riots and King’s Assassination

Racial violence was not only a Southern problem. Through the mid-1960s, major riots erupted in Black neighbourhoods in Northern and Western cities, driven by poverty, poor housing, unemployment, and police brutality. The Watts riot in Los Angeles (1965) lasted six days — 34 people died. Detroit and Newark both saw devastating riots in 1967. These were not direct responses to Southern segregation but to the grinding inequality that Black Americans faced everywhere.

On 4 April 1968, Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated by James Earl Ray in Memphis, Tennessee, where he had gone to support striking sanitation workers. King was just 39 years old.

His death triggered riots in over 100 cities, with the worst violence in Washington, D.C., Chicago, and Baltimore. The tragedy sped up the passage of the Civil Rights Act of 1968 (the Fair Housing Act), banning discrimination in the sale, rental, and financing of housing.

Exam Tip

Exam questions often ask you to evaluate the significance of different events or individuals. Compare multiple factors — was the March on Washington more important than Selma? Were legal changes or direct action more significant? Why did King fail at Albany but succeed at Birmingham? Always use specific evidence and reach a balanced judgement. Mentioning divisions within the movement (King vs. Malcolm X, non-violence vs. Black Power) shows top-level understanding.

Conclusion

The 1960s were the most dramatic decade of the civil rights movement. Through courage and sacrifice, activists won landmark laws — the Civil Rights Act (1964) and Voting Rights Act (1965) — that dismantled legal segregation and secured voting rights. But the decade also revealed deep divisions. Black Power challenged King’s non-violence. Urban riots from Watts to Detroit showed that Northern inequality was just as explosive. The FBI worked to destroy both King and the Black Panthers. The assassinations of JFK, Malcolm X, and MLK showed that the legal battle may have been won, but genuine equality remained far off.

Key Takeaways

  • Non-violent direct action — sit-ins, Freedom Rides, and marches — exposed segregation’s brutality and built public support through media coverage. King learned from Albany’s failure to succeed at Birmingham.
  • The Civil Rights Act (1964) banned racial discrimination in public places and employment. King won the Nobel Peace Prize the same year.
  • The Voting Rights Act (1965) removed barriers to Black voting, transforming Southern politics.
  • Malcolm X and Black Power offered an alternative vision of racial pride and self-determination, reflecting frustrations in Northern cities where riots (Watts, Detroit, Newark) showed deep inequality.
  • King’s assassination (1968) triggered riots in over 100 cities and showed the violence that still underpinned American racism.
