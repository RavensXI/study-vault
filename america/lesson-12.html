Lesson 12 of 15

The Vietnam War & Protest

Overlay:

Why Did the USA Get Involved in Vietnam?

America’s involvement in Vietnam was driven by the Domino Theory — the Cold War belief that if one country fell to communism, its neighbours would follow like a row of dominoes. After China became communist in 1949 and the Korean War (1950–1953), the US was determined to prevent the spread of communism in Southeast Asia.

Key Fact — Escalation Timeline
  • 1954: France defeated at Dien Bien Phu; Vietnam divided into communist North (led by Ho Chi Minh) and non-communist South
  • 1955–1961: The US provided military advisers and financial aid to South Vietnam to prevent a communist takeover
  • 1961: President Kennedy increased the number of military advisers to 16,000
  • 1963: South Vietnamese President Diem was overthrown in a military coup tacitly supported by the US

The Gulf of Tonkin (1964)

The turning point came in August 1964. President Lyndon B. Johnson claimed that North Vietnamese patrol boats had attacked the US destroyer USS Maddox in the Gulf of Tonkin on 2 August 1964, and that a second attack occurred on 4 August. The second attack is now widely believed to have never happened.

Congress passed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution on 7 August 1964, giving Johnson the authority to take “all necessary measures” to prevent further aggression. This effectively gave the president a blank cheque to wage war without a formal declaration from Congress.

Escalation of the War

From 1965, the US dramatically escalated its involvement in Vietnam.

Operation Rolling Thunder (1965–1968): A massive bombing campaign against North Vietnam. The US dropped more bombs on Vietnam than were used by all sides in the entire Second World War — over 7 million tons. Despite this, it failed to break North Vietnam’s will to fight.

Ground troops: The first US combat troops arrived in March 1965. By 1968, there were over 500,000 American soldiers in Vietnam.

Search and destroy: US forces used search-and-destroy missions, sending patrols into the jungle to find and eliminate Viet Cong fighters. These missions were often ineffective and led to high civilian casualties.

Chemical warfare: The US used Agent Orange to destroy jungle cover and napalm (a burning gel) in attacks that caused horrific injuries to soldiers and civilians alike.

The Draft

The US military used conscription (the draft) to fill its ranks. Men aged 18–25 were required to register, and those selected had to serve. However, the draft was deeply unfair:

  • Wealthy young men could obtain college deferments to avoid service
  • African Americans and working-class men were disproportionately drafted — in the early years of the war, Black soldiers made up 25% of combat deaths despite being only 11% of the population
  • The average age of an American soldier in Vietnam was just 19

The Tet Offensive (1968)

The Tet Offensive was the turning point of the war. On 30 January 1968, during the Vietnamese New Year celebrations, over 80,000 Viet Cong and North Vietnamese fighters launched coordinated surprise attacks on more than 100 cities and towns across South Vietnam, including the US Embassy in Saigon.

Key Fact — Impact of the Tet Offensive

Militarily, Tet was a defeat for the communists — they suffered massive casualties and failed to hold any major cities. However, it was a devastating political and psychological blow to the United States:

  • The US government had been telling the public the war was being won — Tet proved this was a lie
  • Trusted TV news anchor Walter Cronkite declared on air that the war was unwinnable, prompting President Johnson to say, “If I’ve lost Cronkite, I’ve lost Middle America”
  • Johnson’s approval rating plummeted to 36%
  • On 31 March 1968, Johnson announced he would not seek re-election

The My Lai Massacre (1968)

On 16 March 1968, US soldiers from Charlie Company entered the village of My Lai and massacred between 347 and 504 unarmed civilians, including women, children, and elderly people. The atrocity was covered up by the military for over a year before being exposed by journalist Seymour Hersh in November 1969.

Only one officer, Lieutenant William Calley, was convicted. He was sentenced to life imprisonment but served just three and a half years under house arrest. The massacre became a symbol of the moral horror of the war and further fuelled anti-war sentiment.

The Anti-War Movement

Opposition to the Vietnam War grew steadily through the 1960s, becoming one of the largest protest movements in American history.

Television coverage: Vietnam was the first “television war.” Nightly news broadcasts brought graphic footage of combat, civilian casualties, and body bags into American living rooms. The famous photograph of a naked girl, Kim Phuc, fleeing a napalm attack became one of the most iconic images of the 20th century.

Rising casualties: By 1968, over 300 Americans were dying every week. The total US death toll reached 58,220 by the end of the war.

The draft: Compulsory military service meant the war directly affected millions of American families. Over 125,000 young men fled to Canada or other countries to avoid the draft.

Cost: The war cost over $120 billion, diverting money from domestic programmes. Johnson’s “Great Society” anti-poverty programmes suffered as a result.

Moral concerns: The use of napalm, Agent Orange, and tactics like search-and-destroy raised serious moral questions. The My Lai massacre confirmed many people’s worst fears about the conduct of the war.

Key Protests

Key Fact — Major Anti-War Events
  • 1965: The first major anti-war “teach-in” at the University of Michigan; 25,000 marched in Washington, D.C.
  • 1967: Over 100,000 marched on the Pentagon
  • 1968: Violent clashes between protesters and police outside the Democratic National Convention in Chicago were broadcast on live television
  • 1969: The Moratorium March in Washington attracted over 250,000 protesters
  • 1970: At Kent State University in Ohio, National Guard troops shot and killed four unarmed students protesting the US invasion of Cambodia (4 May 1970). The shootings triggered a nationwide student strike involving over 4 million students at 450 universities.

The End of US Involvement

President Richard Nixon, elected in 1968 on a promise to end the war, pursued a policy of Vietnamisation — gradually withdrawing American troops while building up the South Vietnamese army to fight on its own. US troop numbers fell from 500,000 in 1968 to 24,000 by 1972. A ceasefire was signed in the Paris Peace Accords in January 1973, and the last US troops left Vietnam in March 1973.

Exam Tip

A common question asks about the role of the media in turning public opinion against the Vietnam War. To achieve top marks, consider multiple factors that fuelled opposition — the draft, rising casualties, cost, moral concerns, and the credibility gap — rather than attributing the change solely to television coverage. The best answers will weigh up which factor was most significant and explain why, using specific evidence such as the Tet Offensive, My Lai, and Kent State.

Conclusion

The Vietnam War was the most divisive conflict in American history since the Civil War. What began as an effort to contain communism escalated into a devastating war that killed over 58,000 Americans and millions of Vietnamese. The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution gave the president sweeping powers, the draft fell disproportionately on the working class and minorities, and the Tet Offensive shattered public confidence in the government’s honesty. The anti-war movement, fuelled by television coverage and moral outrage, grew into a mass protest that fundamentally changed American politics and society. The war created a “credibility gap” between the government and the public that would have lasting consequences.

Key Takeaways

  • The Domino Theory drove US involvement, but the war proved far more difficult than expected due to guerrilla tactics and the determination of the North Vietnamese.
  • The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964) gave the president authority to escalate without a formal declaration of war, based on a likely fabricated second attack.
  • The Tet Offensive (1968) was the psychological turning point, shattering the government’s claim that the war was being won.
  • The anti-war movement was driven by multiple factors: television coverage, the unfair draft, rising casualties, cost, and moral outrage over atrocities like My Lai.
  • Kent State (1970) showed that the war was tearing America apart, as the government turned its firepower on its own citizens.
