Lesson 10 of 15

Civil Rights: The 1960s

Overlay:

The Sit-In Movement

On 1 February 1960, four Black college students — Ezell Blair Jr., David Richmond, Franklin McCain, and Joseph McNeil — sat down at a whites-only lunch counter at Woolworth’s in Greensboro, North Carolina, and politely asked to be served. When refused, they remained seated. This simple act of defiance sparked the sit-in movement.

Key Fact — The Greensboro Sit-Ins

Within two months, the sit-in movement had spread to 54 cities across 9 states. By the end of 1960, an estimated 70,000 people had participated in sit-ins. The Woolworth’s store in Greensboro desegregated its lunch counter on 25 July 1960. The movement inspired the creation of the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC) in April 1960.

The Freedom Rides (1961)

In 1960, the Supreme Court had ruled in Boynton v. Virginia that segregation on interstate buses and in bus terminals was illegal. However, Southern states continued to ignore this ruling. To test and challenge this defiance, the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE) organised the Freedom Rides.

On 4 May 1961, 13 riders — both Black and white — set off from Washington, D.C., on two buses heading through the Deep South. They deliberately sat in integrated groups to challenge segregated seating and used whites-only facilities at bus stations.

In Anniston, Alabama, one bus was firebombed by a white mob. In Birmingham, Alabama, riders were savagely beaten by Klansmen while local police stood by. Police Commissioner Bull Connor had allegedly given the mob 15 minutes to attack before officers intervened.

Despite the violence, new volunteers — many from SNCC — replaced the original riders. In Montgomery, riders were again attacked, and Attorney General Robert Kennedy sent 400 federal marshals to protect them.

The crisis forced the federal government to act. In September 1961, the Interstate Commerce Commission issued strict new regulations banning segregation in interstate travel facilities, and this time enforcement followed.

Birmingham, Alabama (1963)

In spring 1963, Martin Luther King Jr. and the SCLC launched a major campaign in Birmingham, Alabama — widely considered the most segregated city in America. King deliberately chose Birmingham because he knew the city’s notoriously racist police commissioner, Bull Connor, would respond with extreme violence, generating media coverage that would shock the nation.

Key Fact — Birmingham Campaign

On 2 May 1963, over 1,000 Black schoolchildren marched through Birmingham in what became known as the Children’s Crusade. Bull Connor ordered police to attack them with fire hoses, police dogs, and batons. Television cameras broadcast the shocking images across America and around the world. President John F. Kennedy said the images made him “sick,” and he began drafting civil rights legislation.

While imprisoned during the campaign, King wrote his famous “Letter from Birmingham Jail,” defending non-violent direct action and arguing that “injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere.”

The March on Washington (1963)

On 28 August 1963, approximately 250,000 people gathered at the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C., for the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom. It was one of the largest political rallies in American history, and the crowd was notably interracial — around 60,000 of the marchers were white.

Key Fact — “I Have a Dream”

The highlight of the march was Martin Luther King Jr.’s legendary “I Have a Dream” speech. Standing before the Lincoln Memorial, King declared: “I have a dream that my four little children will one day live in a nation where they will not be judged by the color of their skin but by the content of their character.” The speech was broadcast live on television to millions and became one of the most famous speeches in history.

The March on Washington put enormous pressure on Congress to pass civil rights legislation. However, tragedy struck just weeks later when a bomb killed four young girls — Addie Mae Collins, Cynthia Wesley, Carole Robertson, and Carol Denise McNair — at the 16th Street Baptist Church in Birmingham on 15 September 1963, a brutal reminder that the struggle was far from over.

The Civil Rights Act (1964)

President Kennedy had introduced a civil rights bill in June 1963, but it was stalled in Congress. After Kennedy’s assassination on 22 November 1963, his successor, President Lyndon B. Johnson, used his considerable political skill — and the nation’s grief — to push the bill through.

Key Fact — Civil Rights Act 1964

Signed on 2 July 1964, the Civil Rights Act:

  • Banned discrimination based on race, colour, religion, sex, or national origin in employment and public places
  • Outlawed segregation in schools, restaurants, hotels, theatres, and all public facilities
  • Established the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to enforce anti-discrimination laws in the workplace
  • Gave the federal government power to withhold funding from institutions that practised discrimination

Selma and the Voting Rights Act (1965)

Despite the Civil Rights Act, Black voters in the South still faced massive obstacles. In Selma, Alabama, only 2% of eligible Black citizens were registered to vote, due to intimidation, literacy tests, and deliberate bureaucratic obstruction.

In early 1965, King and the SCLC launched a voter registration campaign in Selma. On 7 March 1965, around 600 marchers set out to walk from Selma to Montgomery, the state capital. As they crossed the Edmund Pettus Bridge, they were met by state troopers who attacked them with tear gas, clubs, and mounted horseback charges.

This day became known as “Bloody Sunday.” Television footage of peaceful marchers being beaten was broadcast across America, generating overwhelming public support for voting rights legislation.

A third march, now with federal protection, successfully completed the 54-mile journey on 25 March 1965, with 25,000 marchers arriving in Montgomery.

Key Fact — Voting Rights Act 1965

Signed on 6 August 1965, the Voting Rights Act:

  • Banned literacy tests and other devices used to prevent Black people from voting
  • Allowed federal registrars to register voters in areas where discrimination was found
  • Required states with a history of discrimination to get federal approval before changing voting laws

The impact was dramatic: within a year, 250,000 new Black voters were registered. In Mississippi, Black voter registration rose from 6.7% to 59.8% by 1968.

Malcolm X and Black Power

Not all African Americans supported King’s philosophy of non-violence and integration. Malcolm X emerged as the most prominent alternative voice in the civil rights movement.

Malcolm X was a minister of the Nation of Islam, led by Elijah Muhammad. He rejected integration and non-violence, arguing instead for Black nationalism, self-defence, and racial pride. He described King’s March on Washington as the “Farce on Washington” and criticised non-violent protesters for allowing themselves to be beaten.

Malcolm X appealed particularly to young Black Americans in Northern cities, where legal segregation did not exist but poverty, unemployment, and police brutality created deep frustration. He argued that Black people should build their own businesses, schools, and communities rather than seeking acceptance from white America.

In 1964, Malcolm X left the Nation of Islam and made a pilgrimage to Mecca, which led him to adopt a more inclusive worldview. He was assassinated on 21 February 1965 while speaking in New York. His autobiography, published after his death, became hugely influential.

The Rise of Black Power

By the mid-1960s, many younger activists were frustrated with the slow pace of change and the continued violence against Black communities. The Black Power movement emerged, rejecting non-violence and integration in favour of racial pride and self-determination.

Key Fact — Black Power
  • Stokely Carmichael, the new leader of SNCC, popularised the slogan “Black Power” in 1966
  • The Black Panther Party, founded in October 1966 by Huey Newton and Bobby Seale in Oakland, California, openly carried firearms, organised armed patrols to monitor police brutality, and ran community programmes including free breakfast for children
  • At the 1968 Mexico City Olympics, sprinters Tommie Smith and John Carlos raised Black Power salutes during the medal ceremony, becoming an iconic image of the movement
  • Black Power divided opinion — it inspired racial pride but alarmed white Americans and created tensions within the civil rights movement

The Assassination of Martin Luther King Jr.

On 4 April 1968, Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated by James Earl Ray in Memphis, Tennessee, where he had gone to support striking sanitation workers. King was just 39 years old.

His death triggered riots in over 100 American cities, with the worst violence in Washington, D.C., Chicago, and Baltimore. The tragedy underscored the anger and frustration felt by Black Americans and accelerated the passage of the Civil Rights Act of 1968 (the Fair Housing Act), which banned discrimination in the sale, rental, and financing of housing.

Exam Tip

A common exam question asks you to evaluate the significance of different events or individuals in the civil rights movement. To achieve top marks, you should compare multiple factors — for example, was the March on Washington more important than Selma? Were legal changes (Civil Rights Act) or direct action (sit-ins, marches) more significant? Always support your argument with specific evidence and reach a balanced judgement.

Conclusion

The 1960s were the most dramatic and consequential decade of the civil rights movement. Through extraordinary courage and sacrifice, the movement achieved landmark legislation — the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 — that dismantled the legal framework of segregation and secured voting rights for millions. However, the decade also revealed deep divisions within the movement itself, as Black Power challenged King’s philosophy of non-violence and integration. The assassinations of JFK, Malcolm X, and Martin Luther King Jr., and the riots that followed, showed that while the legal battle had been largely won, the deeper struggle for genuine equality was far from over.

Key Takeaways

  • Non-violent direct action — sit-ins, Freedom Rides, and marches — was devastatingly effective at exposing the brutality of segregation and generating public support through media coverage.
  • The Civil Rights Act (1964) banned racial discrimination in public places and employment, ending legal segregation.
  • The Voting Rights Act (1965) removed the barriers that had prevented Black Americans from voting, transforming Southern politics.
  • Malcolm X and Black Power offered an alternative vision emphasising racial pride and self-determination, reflecting the frustrations of Black Americans in Northern cities.
  • King’s assassination (1968) was a devastating blow that triggered nationwide riots and symbolised the violence that had always underpinned American racism.
