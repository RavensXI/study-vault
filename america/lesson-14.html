Lesson 14 of 15

Race & Inequality in the 1970s

Overlay:

De Facto Segregation in the North

The Civil Rights Act (1964) and Voting Rights Act (1965) had dismantled de jure (legal) segregation in the South. However, de facto segregation — segregation in practice, if not in law — was deeply entrenched in Northern and Western cities. This proved far harder to tackle than legal segregation, because it was caused by economic inequality, housing discrimination, and deeply rooted social patterns rather than specific laws that could be overturned.

Key Fact — Northern Segregation

In Northern cities, Black Americans were concentrated in inner-city ghettos — overcrowded, under-resourced neighbourhoods with poor housing, high crime, inadequate schools, and few job opportunities. This was caused by:

  • Redlining: Banks and insurance companies refused to lend money or provide services in predominantly Black neighbourhoods, trapping residents in poverty
  • White flight: As Black families moved into neighbourhoods, white families moved to the suburbs, taking businesses, tax revenue, and investment with them
  • Restrictive covenants: Property deeds included clauses preventing the sale of homes to Black buyers, maintaining racially segregated housing patterns
  • Employment discrimination: Despite the Civil Rights Act, Black workers still faced discrimination in hiring and were disproportionately concentrated in low-paid jobs

The Urban Riots of the 1960s

The frustrations of life in Northern ghettos had already erupted in devastating urban riots during the mid-1960s. These riots continued to shape attitudes in the early 1970s:

  • Watts, Los Angeles (1965): Six days of rioting left 34 dead, over 1,000 injured, and caused $40 million in damage
  • Detroit (1967): Five days of rioting killed 43 people and required the deployment of federal troops and tanks
  • Nationwide riots (1968): Following MLK’s assassination, riots erupted in over 100 cities

The Kerner Commission, established by President Johnson to investigate the riots, concluded in 1968 that America was “moving toward two societies, one black, one white — separate and unequal.” It blamed white racism for the conditions that caused the riots and recommended massive investment in housing, education, and jobs. Most of its recommendations were never implemented.

The Busing Controversy

Because residential segregation meant that Black and white children attended different neighbourhood schools, the Supreme Court ruled in Swann v. Charlotte-Mecklenburg (1971) that busing — transporting students to schools outside their local area — could be used to achieve racial integration.

The most notorious conflict over busing occurred in Boston, Massachusetts, beginning in 1974. When a federal judge ordered the busing of Black students to schools in the predominantly white, working-class neighbourhood of South Boston, the response was ferocious:

  • White parents organised massive boycotts, keeping thousands of children out of school
  • Black students arriving at South Boston High School were met with mobs throwing rocks and bottles
  • A famous photograph showed a white man using an American flag as a weapon to attack a Black bystander — an image that shocked the nation
  • Violence continued for several years, requiring the presence of hundreds of police officers

The Boston crisis demonstrated that racial hostility was not limited to the South. Northern white communities could be just as resistant to integration as Southern ones, especially when it directly affected their children’s schools.

Economic Inequality

Despite legal gains, the economic gap between Black and white Americans remained vast in the early 1970s.

Key Fact — The Economic Divide
  • In 1970, the median income of Black families was 61% of the median income of white families
  • Black unemployment was consistently twice the rate of white unemployment
  • Over 30% of Black Americans lived below the poverty line, compared to around 10% of white Americans
  • Black Americans were disproportionately affected by the economic recession of the early 1970s, as they were often the “last hired, first fired”
  • The poverty rate for Black children was over 40%

The Legacy of the Black Panthers

By the early 1970s, the Black Panther Party had been severely weakened by the FBI’s COINTELPRO programme, which used infiltration, surveillance, disinformation, and outright assassination to destroy the organisation. Key leaders were killed, imprisoned, or driven into exile:

  • Fred Hampton, the charismatic 21-year-old head of the Chicago chapter, was shot and killed by police in a pre-dawn raid on 4 December 1969
  • Huey Newton was imprisoned, and Bobby Seale faced criminal charges
  • Eldridge Cleaver fled to Algeria to avoid prosecution

Despite their destruction, the Black Panthers left a lasting legacy. Their community programmes — particularly the Free Breakfast for Children programme, which served up to 10,000 children daily — were later adopted as models by the federal government. Their emphasis on Black pride, self-determination, and community empowerment continued to influence Black political thought.

Affirmative Action

To address the continuing effects of centuries of discrimination, the government introduced affirmative action — policies designed to increase representation of minorities and women in employment, education, and government contracts.

Arguments in favour:

  • Simply removing discriminatory laws was not enough — centuries of oppression had created structural disadvantages that required active intervention to address
  • Affirmative action helped increase Black enrolment in universities and professional schools
  • It opened doors in industries and institutions that had been effectively closed to minorities
  • President Nixon’s Philadelphia Plan (1969) required federal contractors to set goals for hiring minority workers

Arguments against:

  • Critics called it “reverse discrimination” — giving advantages to minorities at the expense of white Americans
  • Some argued it undermined the achievements of successful Black Americans by suggesting they needed special help
  • White working-class Americans, who had not personally benefited from historical privilege, resented being disadvantaged by policies designed to help others
  • The policy created deep political divisions that persisted for decades

Continuing Challenges

By the early 1970s, the civil rights movement had achieved remarkable legal and political victories. However, the deeper problems of poverty, housing segregation, educational inequality, and institutional racism proved far more resistant to change. The optimism of the early 1960s had given way to a more sober recognition that changing laws was only the first step in a much longer struggle for genuine equality.

Key Fact — Progress and Limitations by 1973
  • Political gains: Black voter registration in the South had transformed dramatically — the number of Black elected officials nationwide rose from fewer than 100 in 1964 to over 2,600 by 1973
  • Education: Black college enrolment increased significantly, though achievement gaps persisted
  • But: De facto segregation in housing and schools remained widespread, the economic gap was vast, and many Black communities still faced poverty, crime, and police brutality
Exam Tip

A key exam theme is the difference between de jure and de facto segregation. Make sure you can explain why legal changes alone were not enough to achieve genuine equality. The Civil Rights Act could outlaw segregation in law, but it could not fix housing discrimination, poverty, or deeply embedded racism in Northern cities. Top answers will explain why the problems of the 1970s proved harder to solve than the legal segregation of the South, using specific examples like busing, redlining, and economic statistics.

Conclusion

The 1970s revealed the limits of the civil rights legislation of the 1960s. While legal segregation had been dismantled, de facto segregation in housing, education, and employment proved far more resistant to change. Northern cities were deeply divided along racial lines, and the busing controversy showed that racism was a national problem, not just a Southern one. The economic gap between Black and white Americans remained vast, with Black families earning barely 60% of white family income. Affirmative action attempted to address structural inequality but sparked fierce controversy. The Black Panthers had been destroyed by government repression, and the optimism of the civil rights era had given way to a sobering recognition that the struggle for genuine equality was far from over.

Key Takeaways

  • De facto segregation in the North proved harder to tackle than legal segregation in the South, because it was caused by economic inequality, housing discrimination, and social patterns rather than specific laws.
  • The busing controversy showed that Northern white communities could be just as resistant to integration as Southern ones.
  • Economic inequality remained stark: Black family income was 61% of white income, Black unemployment was double the white rate, and over 30% of Black Americans lived in poverty.
  • Affirmative action attempted to address structural disadvantage but created political divisions that persist to this day.
  • The Kerner Commission’s warning of “two societies, one black, one white — separate and unequal” remained alarmingly accurate.
