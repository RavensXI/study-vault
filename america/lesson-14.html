The Feminist Movement

The Women’s Liberation Movement

The civil rights movement inspired other groups to fight for their rights. The women’s liberation movement — sometimes called “second-wave feminism” — became one of the most powerful social movements of the 1960s and 1970s. Women had won the right to vote in 1920 with the 19th Amendment, but they still faced deep inequality in employment, pay, education, and the law.

The Roots of Discontent

The seeds of the movement were planted during World War Two. Around 6 million women worked in factories during the war, gaining independence and skills. But when the soldiers returned, most women were sacked and pushed back into domestic roles. This created a sense of frustration that would build for two decades.

The Problem with No Name

In 1963, Betty Friedan — a psychologist and journalist — published The Feminine Mystique. The book challenged the idea that women could only find fulfilment through being housewives and mothers. Friedan described the deep frustration felt by millions of educated, middle-class women trapped in domestic roles as “the problem that has no name.”

The book sold over 3 million copies. It struck a chord with women across the country who felt unfulfilled but had been told they should be grateful for their comfortable lives. Friedan argued that society was wasting the talents of half its population by confining women to the home.

The Fight for Equal Pay

One of the central demands was equal pay for equal work. The pay gap was huge, and it was entirely legal for employers to pay women less simply because of their sex.

The Equal Pay Act (1963)

President Kennedy signed the Equal Pay Act in June 1963. It made it illegal to pay men and women different wages for the same work. This was the first federal law to tackle sex-based pay discrimination.

However, the Act had serious limitations:

  • It only covered jobs that were exactly the same — employers got around it by giving men and women slightly different job titles
  • Enforcement was weak — the government lacked resources to investigate millions of employers
  • It did not address the deeper problem: women were concentrated in low-paid jobs like teaching, nursing, and secretarial work, while better-paid professions stayed male-dominated
  • By 1973, women still earned only about 57 cents for every dollar earned by men — the gap had actually widened slightly

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964)

A more powerful tool came with Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964), which banned employment discrimination based on sex as well as race. Ironically, a Southern congressman, Howard W. Smith, added the word “sex” hoping it would make the bill fail — but it passed anyway.

Title VII created the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to handle discrimination complaints. But the EEOC did not take sex discrimination seriously at first — its director called the sex provision a “fluke” and the agency focused almost entirely on race. This failure to enforce the law was a key reason Betty Friedan and others formed a new organisation to fight for women’s rights.

The National Organization for Women (NOW)

In 1966, Betty Friedan and 27 others founded the National Organization for Women (NOW). It became the largest and most influential feminist organisation in America. NOW was professional and well-organised, with around 40,000 mainly middle-class members by the mid-1970s. It fought for change through the courts, lobbying Congress, and public campaigns.

Key campaigns:

  • Pressured the EEOC to enforce Title VII on sex discrimination — filing lawsuits and protesting outside EEOC offices
  • Campaigned to end sex-segregated job adverts (“Help Wanted — Male” / “Female”). By 1968, the EEOC ruled these were illegal
  • Pushed for affordable childcare so mothers could work
  • Campaigned for reproductive rights, including access to the contraceptive pill — which allowed women to plan when to have families — and abortion
  • Led the fight for the Equal Rights Amendment

By 1971, NOW had 15,000 members, growing to over 40,000 by the mid-1970s. Its tactics were inspired by the civil rights movement: marches, court cases, and lobbying.

The Women’s Liberation Movement — A More Radical Wing

Alongside NOW, a more radical wing emerged: the Women’s Liberation Movement (“Women’s Lib”). These were mostly younger women who saw NOW as too cautious. They used confrontational tactics — burning bras, disrupting the Miss World pageant, and staging dramatic protests to grab media attention. While NOW worked through courts and Congress, Women’s Lib aimed to shock society into change.

Gloria Steinem and Ms. Magazine

While Friedan was the movement’s intellectual founder, Gloria Steinem became its most visible public face. She used her media skills to bring feminist ideas to a mass audience.

In January 1972, Steinem co-founded Ms. magazine — the first mainstream magazine run by women, for women, with a feminist perspective. The preview issue sold all 300,000 copies in eight days. Ms. covered domestic violence, sexual harassment, pay inequality, and reproductive rights — topics other magazines ignored. It proved there was a huge audience of women hungry for content that took their concerns seriously.

Steinem also testified before Congress for the Equal Rights Amendment and co-founded the National Women’s Political Caucus (1971), which worked to elect more women to office.

Key Legislative and Legal Victories

1963

Equal Pay Act

First federal law requiring equal pay for equal work. Limited in scope but an important first step.

1964

Civil Rights Act — Title VII

Banned employment discrimination based on sex. Created the EEOC to handle complaints.

1966

NOW Founded

Betty Friedan and others founded the National Organization for Women to campaign for women’s equality.

1971

Reed v. Reed — Supreme Court

The Supreme Court ruled for the first time that the 14th Amendment’s Equal Protection Clause applied to sex discrimination.

1972

Title IX & ERA Passed by Congress

Title IX banned sex discrimination in education. The Equal Rights Amendment was passed by Congress and sent to the states for ratification.

1973

Roe v. Wade

The Supreme Court ruled that women had a constitutional right to abortion in the early stages of pregnancy.

Reed v. Reed (1971)

In 1971, the Supreme Court heard Reed v. Reed. Sally Reed challenged an Idaho law that automatically gave preference to men over women as estate administrators. The law stated: “males must be preferred to females.”

The Supreme Court ruled unanimously that this violated the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment. It was the first time the Court had struck down a law for sex discrimination. This opened the door to further legal challenges and was used as a precedent in many later cases.

Title IX (1972)

Title IX (1972) banned sex discrimination in any educational programme receiving federal funding. Its impact was dramatic:

  • Universities could no longer use quotas to limit female students
  • Schools had to offer equal sports opportunities — girls in high school sports rose from 300,000 in 1972 to over 2 million by 1978
  • More women entered law, medicine, and business courses — female law school enrolment jumped from 4% to 26% in a decade

Roe v. Wade (1973)

In January 1973, the Supreme Court delivered one of the most controversial rulings in American history. In Roe v. Wade, the court ruled 7–2 that women had a constitutional right to abortion in early pregnancy, based on the right to privacy under the 14th Amendment.

Key Fact — Roe v. Wade

The ruling set a framework based on the three trimesters of pregnancy:

  • First trimester: The decision was left to the woman and her doctor
  • Second trimester: States could regulate abortion to protect the mother’s health
  • Third trimester: States could ban abortion, as the foetus was viable (able to survive outside the womb)

Feminists celebrated this as a victory for women’s control over their own bodies. Opponents — especially religious conservatives — condemned it. The ruling triggered a powerful anti-abortion movement that shaped American politics for decades.

Other Key Gains for Women

Two further changes transformed women’s lives in this period:

  • The contraceptive pill — approved in 1960 and widely available by the late 1960s — gave women control over when to have children. This opened up career and education opportunities that had been almost impossible before
  • No-fault divorce laws — introduced by several states from 1969 — made it easier for women to leave unhappy marriages without having to prove their husband was at fault

The Equal Rights Amendment (ERA)

The most ambitious goal of the feminist movement was the Equal Rights Amendment (ERA). It would have added a simple statement to the Constitution: “Equality of rights under the law shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of sex.”

The ERA was first proposed in 1923, shortly after women won the vote. It was reintroduced in every session of Congress for nearly 50 years before finally passing in March 1972. It then needed to be ratified by 38 of the 50 states to become part of the Constitution.

At first, the ERA seemed certain to succeed. Within the first year, 30 states ratified it. Then the process stalled dramatically.

Phyllis Schlafly and the STOP ERA Campaign

The most effective opponent was Phyllis Schlafly, a conservative activist from Illinois. Schlafly was both a housewife and a qualified lawyer — and had once run for Congress herself and lost, which was ironic given her argument that women belonged in the home. In 1972, she founded the STOP ERA campaign (STOP stood for “Stop Taking Our Privileges”).

Schlafly argued the ERA would:

  • Force women into military combat — a powerful argument during Vietnam, when the draft was deeply unpopular
  • End separate toilets and changing rooms for men and women
  • Remove protections women already had in divorce law, such as alimony and child custody
  • Undermine the traditional family by suggesting motherhood was not valued
  • Lead to taxpayer-funded abortions and same-sex marriage

She mobilised conservative women, many of them religious Christians, who felt feminism attacked their way of life. Her supporters baked bread and jams for state legislators with notes saying: “From the breadmakers to the breadwinners.”

The campaign was remarkably successful. The ERA stalled at 35 states — three short of the 38 needed. Despite a deadline extension to 1982, no further states ratified it. Five states even voted to rescind (take back) their ratification.

Key Fact — Why the ERA Failed

The ERA’s failure showed the limits of the feminist movement:

  • Schlafly’s campaign frightened many women who feared losing protections they already had
  • The movement was seen as representing educated, middle-class, white women — not working-class or minority women
  • Opposition from religious conservatives in Southern and rural states proved impossible to overcome
  • The broader conservative backlash of the late 1970s shifted the political mood to the right

Impact and Limitations of the Feminist Movement

Legal victories: The Equal Pay Act, Title VII, Title IX, Reed v. Reed, and Roe v. Wade gave women far greater legal protection. Sex discrimination in employment and education was now illegal.

Changed attitudes: The idea that women should have the same opportunities as men became widely accepted, even if reality lagged behind.

Workforce: By 1973, over 44% of women were in paid work, up from around 34% in 1950.

Education: Title IX opened universities and professional schools to women. Female enrolment surged.

Politics: The National Women’s Political Caucus (1971) helped elect more women to office. Women’s issues became a permanent part of political debate.

The pay gap persisted: Women in 1973 still earned only about 57 cents for every dollar earned by men. Black women earned around 49 cents and Hispanic women around 45 cents.

The ERA failed: Without a constitutional amendment, women’s rights depended on individual laws and court rulings that could be changed or overturned.

Limited diversity: The movement was dominated by white, middle-class women. Working-class, Black, and Hispanic women often felt it did not represent them.

Conservative backlash: Schlafly’s campaign showed that many women actively opposed feminism. The religious right became a major political force partly in response to feminist gains.

Workplace reality: Even where discrimination was illegal, women faced subtle barriers. They were passed over for promotion (the “glass ceiling”), faced sexual harassment, and were still expected to manage childcare and housework alongside paid work.

Exam Tip

The examiner wants a balanced assessment. Acknowledge the legal victories (Equal Pay Act, Title VII, Title IX, Roe v. Wade, Reed v. Reed) but explain why these did not fully solve gender inequality. Discuss the opposition — especially Schlafly and STOP ERA. Top marks go to answers that explain why the ERA failed and what this reveals about the limits of social change. Mention the two wings of the movement: NOW (professional, court cases) and Women’s Lib (radical, confrontational).

Conclusion

The feminist movement achieved real progress. Friedan’s The Feminine Mystique and NOW sparked a national campaign for equality, while the more radical Women’s Lib wing grabbed public attention. The Equal Pay Act, Title VII, Title IX, the contraceptive pill, and no-fault divorce laws all changed women’s lives. The Supreme Court’s rulings in Reed v. Reed and Roe v. Wade set important constitutional protections. Yet limits remained. The pay gap barely narrowed, 95% of managers were still men, and Schlafly’s campaign defeated the ERA. By 1973, the legal framework for equality was largely in place, but genuine equality in everyday life was still a distant goal.

Key Takeaways

  • Friedan’s The Feminine Mystique (1963) and NOW (1966) launched the movement. NOW fought through courts and lobbying; the Women’s Lib wing used radical protest.
  • Reed v. Reed (1971) was the first Supreme Court ruling to strike down a law for sex discrimination, using the 14th Amendment.
  • Roe v. Wade (1973) gave women a right to abortion but triggered a powerful conservative backlash that shaped politics for decades.
