The Impact of World War Two

0:00 / 0:00

1930s Popular Culture

Before looking at the war, it is important to understand American culture during the 1930s Depression. Despite widespread poverty, popular culture boomed — much of it was escapism, helping people forget their troubles.

Jazz remained hugely popular. Big band swing music filled dance halls across the country. In cinema, ‘talkies’ (films with sound) had replaced silent films from 1928 onwards. Hollywood produced musicals, comedies, and adventure films that drew huge audiences. By the end of the 1930s, the first colour films appeared, including The Wizard of Oz (1939). Comic books also emerged in this period — Superman first appeared in 1938. Radio remained the main source of home entertainment, with comedy shows, dramas, and music reaching millions. All of this culture gave Americans a way to escape the misery of the Depression, even if only for a few hours.

From Neutrality to War

When war broke out in Europe in September 1939, most Americans wanted to stay out of it. However, Roosevelt gradually moved the USA towards supporting the Allies.

In November 1939, Roosevelt introduced the Cash and Carry Plan. This allowed Britain and France to buy American weapons, as long as they paid cash and carried the goods in their own ships. It was an important stepping stone between neutrality and full involvement. Roosevelt also gave Britain 50 old warships in exchange for the right to use British military bases — another step closer to war.

Pearl Harbor

The United States entered World War Two on 7 December 1941, after Japan’s surprise attack on the US naval base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. The attack killed over 2,400 Americans and destroyed much of the Pacific fleet. President Roosevelt called it ‘a date which will live in infamy’ and Congress declared war the next day.

The war ended the Great Depression, reshaped the role of women and African Americans, and turned the USA into the world’s leading superpower.

Economic Recovery Through War Production

After a decade of Depression, wartime production created a huge economic boom. Factories that had stood idle were now running around the clock, producing weapons, vehicles, ships, and aircraft.

Even before Pearl Harbor, the war economy was already working. By 1941, unemployment had fallen to 5 million as factories geared up to supply the Allies. The US government spent over $300 billion on the war effort between 1941 and 1945 — far more than anything the New Deal had attempted. This finally achieved full employment.

The War Production Miracle

American factories switched to war materials at astonishing speed. The Ford Willow Run plant in Michigan went from making cars to building B-24 Liberator bombers — at its peak, one every 63 minutes. Shipbuilder Henry J. Kaiser cut Liberty Ship construction time from 230 days to just 42 days.

By 1945, American factories had produced 300,000 aircraft, 86,000 tanks, 2.5 million trucks, and 6,500 naval vessels.

Cash and Carry, Lend-Lease, and Global Dominance

The Cash and Carry Plan (1939) was followed by the Lend-Lease Act, signed on 11 March 1941. Lend-Lease let the USA supply weapons, food, oil, and equipment to Britain, the Soviet Union, China, and other Allies without requiring immediate payment.

Lend-Lease totalled $50.1 billion (over $700 billion today). Britain received $31.4 billion; the Soviet Union received $11.3 billion. These programmes kept the Allies fighting and boosted the American economy — factories producing Lend-Lease goods employed millions of workers.

By 1945, the USA held two-thirds of the world’s gold reserves and produced half of the world’s manufactured goods. Average wages rose by 44% during the war. Personal savings jumped from $6.3 billion (1940) to $37 billion (1945).

The Experience of African Americans During WWII

World War Two was a turning point for African Americans. The war created new jobs, challenged racial attitudes, and planted the seeds of the civil rights movement. However, segregation and discrimination continued throughout the war.

The Double V Campaign

In February 1942, the Pittsburgh Courier — a leading Black newspaper — launched the ‘Double V’ campaign. The two ‘V’s stood for victory abroad (against fascism) and victory at home (against racial discrimination). The campaign highlighted the contradiction of fighting for freedom overseas while Black Americans were denied basic rights at home.

The Double V campaign gained huge support. It helped politicise African Americans and raised awareness of racial injustice among the wider public.

A. Philip Randolph and Executive Order 8802

A. Philip Randolph, head of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters (a Black trade union), threatened to organise a March on Washington of 100,000 African Americans in January 1941 to protest racial discrimination in defence industries.

To prevent the march, Roosevelt issued Executive Order 8802 on 25 June 1941 — the first federal order banning racial discrimination since Reconstruction. It banned discrimination in defence industries and set up the Fair Employment Practices Committee (FEPC).

Key Fact — Executive Order 8802

This was the first presidential executive order on race since the Civil War era. African Americans in defence industries rose from 3% (1942) to 8% (1945). Over one million Black workers gained factory jobs. However, African Americans were typically paid half of what white workers earned for the same roles. The FEPC had limited enforcement powers — it could investigate but not force employers to comply.

African Americans in the Military

Over one million African Americans served in the US military during WWII. The armed forces remained strictly segregated — Black soldiers served in separate units, often under white officers, and were frequently given non-combat roles like loading cargo.

Despite this, some Black units won distinction. The Tuskegee Airmen — the first African American fighter pilots — flew over 15,000 missions. The 761st Tank Battalion fought in the Battle of the Bulge and earned a Presidential Unit Citation. These achievements undermined racist claims that Black soldiers were inferior.

The Great Migration Accelerates

The war sped up the Great Migration of African Americans from the rural South to northern and western cities. Between 1940 and 1945, about 1.5 million Black Americans moved to cities like Detroit, Chicago, Los Angeles, and Pittsburgh for factory work.

This brought new opportunities but also tension. In June 1943, a race riot in Detroit lasted three days and left 34 people dead (25 of them African American). White workers often resented Black newcomers competing for jobs and housing.

The Experience of Women During WWII

With 16 million men in the armed forces, women were urgently needed to fill their jobs. The scale of female employment was unprecedented.

‘Rosie the Riveter’

The government launched a propaganda campaign to recruit women. The iconic ‘Rosie the Riveter’ — a strong woman in overalls flexing her bicep with the slogan ‘We Can Do It!’ — became the symbol of women’s wartime contribution.

Key Fact — Women in the Workforce

Working women rose from 12 million (1940) to 18 million (1945). By 1944, women made up 37% of the workforce. Over 300,000 women served in military roles such as the Women’s Army Corps (WAC) and WAVES. The WASPs (Women Airforce Service Pilots) were trained to deliver aircraft to war zones, freeing up male pilots for combat. Women worked as welders, riveters, electricians, and crane operators — jobs previously seen as impossible for women.

Limitations for Women

Women still faced real inequality. They earned only 65% of what men earned for the same work. Childcare was a major problem — the government funded some day nurseries, but provision was poor. Women were expected to go back to domestic roles once the war ended.

After 1945, 4 million women left the workforce to make way for returning soldiers. Surveys showed 75% of wartime women workers wanted to keep their jobs. The experience of wartime work changed women’s expectations permanently and laid the groundwork for the later feminist movement.

Japanese American Internment

One of the darkest episodes of the war was the forced internment of Japanese Americans. After Pearl Harbor, anti-Japanese hysteria swept the country. In February 1942, Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066, authorising the removal of all people of Japanese ancestry from the West Coast.

About 120,000 Japanese Americans — two-thirds of them US citizens — were forced into ten remote internment camps. Families had only days to sell their homes and possessions, often for a fraction of their value. They lost an estimated $400 million in property.

Camps were surrounded by barbed wire and armed guards. Families lived in cramped barracks with shared facilities. Despite this, internees organised schools, newspapers, and community activities.

No Japanese American was ever found guilty of spying or sabotage. The internment was driven by racial prejudice, wartime hysteria, and economic jealousy. Japanese Americans in Hawaii were not interned, even though they made up a third of the population there.

In 1988, President Reagan signed the Civil Liberties Act, formally apologising and awarding $20,000 to each surviving internee.

The GI Bill (1944)

The GI Bill (officially the Servicemen’s Readjustment Act), signed on 22 June 1944, helped 16 million returning veterans readjust to civilian life. It provided:

  • College or vocational education — the government paid tuition and a living allowance. By 1956, nearly 8 million veterans had used this.
  • Low-cost home loans — no deposit required, fuelling the post-war suburban boom.
  • Unemployment insurance — $20 per week for up to a year (nicknamed the ‘52-20 Club’).

The GI Bill is often credited with creating the American middle class. However, many African American veterans could not benefit equally — racist banks denied them home loans, and white universities (especially in the South) refused to admit them.

Exam Tip

When answering questions about WWII’s impact, cover economic, social, and racial dimensions. Use statistics: unemployment fell from 14.6% to 1.2%; women workers rose from 12 million to 18 million. Show how the war created both progress and contradictions for African Americans (Executive Order 8802 versus continued segregation and half pay). A top answer links these themes — war production drew women and African Americans into the workforce, raising expectations that fuelled post-war demands for equality.

Conclusion

World War Two transformed the United States. Massive government spending ended the Depression, and America emerged as the world’s dominant superpower. Millions of women entered the workforce, and African Americans gained new jobs while challenging segregation. Yet deep contradictions remained: Japanese Americans were interned, the military stayed segregated, African Americans were paid half of white wages, and women were pushed back into domestic roles after 1945. The rising expectations the war created would shape the decades that followed.

Key Takeaways

  • Economic transformation: Cash and Carry (1939) and Lend-Lease ($50.1 billion) supported the Allies and boosted American industry. Unemployment dropped from 14.6% to 1.2%. The USA emerged holding two-thirds of the world’s gold reserves.
  • Seeds of civil rights: The Double V campaign, Executive Order 8802, and over one million Black soldiers’ service challenged discrimination — but African Americans still faced segregation and were paid half of white wages.
  • Women’s changing role: Six million women entered the workforce. WASPs delivered aircraft to war zones. Although most women were pushed out after 1945, their experience changed expectations permanently.
