Lesson 11 of 15

Other Rights Movements

Overlay:

The Women’s Liberation Movement

The civil rights movement inspired other groups to campaign for their rights. The women’s liberation movement — sometimes called “second-wave feminism” — became one of the most significant social movements of the 1960s and 1970s.

The Problem with No Name

In 1963, journalist Betty Friedan published The Feminine Mystique, a groundbreaking book that challenged the idea that women could only find fulfilment through being housewives and mothers. Friedan described the deep dissatisfaction felt by millions of educated, middle-class women trapped in domestic roles as “the problem that has no name.”

Key Fact — Women’s Inequality in the 1960s
  • In 1960, women earned on average only 60 cents for every dollar earned by men doing the same job
  • Many jobs were openly advertised as “men only” or “women only,” with higher-paid positions reserved for men
  • Women could be legally fired for becoming pregnant
  • Women found it difficult to get credit cards, mortgages, or bank loans without a male co-signer
  • Very few women held positions in politics, law, medicine, or business

Key Achievements

Equal Pay Act (1963): This federal law required employers to pay men and women equally for the same work. While it was a landmark step, enforcement was weak and a significant gender pay gap persisted.

Civil Rights Act (1964): Title VII of the Act banned discrimination based on sex as well as race in employment. Ironically, the word “sex” was added by a Southern congressman who hoped it would make the bill so controversial it would fail — but the bill passed regardless.

National Organization for Women (NOW): Founded in 1966 by Betty Friedan and others, NOW became the leading women’s rights organisation. It campaigned for equal pay, equal opportunities in education and employment, and reproductive rights. By 1971, NOW had 15,000 members.

Title IX (1972): This legislation banned sex discrimination in any educational programme receiving federal funding, dramatically increasing opportunities for women in higher education and school sports.

Roe v. Wade (1973): In this landmark Supreme Court case, the court ruled that women had a constitutional right to an abortion in the early stages of pregnancy, based on the right to privacy. This was one of the most controversial rulings in American history and remains deeply divisive.

Limitations

Despite these gains, the women’s movement had significant limitations. The proposed Equal Rights Amendment (ERA), which would have guaranteed equal rights under the Constitution regardless of sex, passed Congress in 1972 but failed to be ratified by enough states. Conservative opponents, led by Phyllis Schlafly, argued that it would undermine family values and force women into military service. The pay gap persisted, and women remained underrepresented in positions of power.

Native American Rights

Native Americans were the poorest and most disadvantaged minority group in the United States. By the 1960s, they faced appalling conditions: life expectancy was 20 years below the national average, unemployment on reservations exceeded 50%, and the suicide rate was twice the national average.

Background: Termination Policy

Since the late 1940s, the US government had pursued a policy of termination — ending the special status of Native American tribes, dissolving reservations, and encouraging assimilation into mainstream American society. This policy was devastating, leading to the loss of tribal lands, cultural destruction, and increased poverty.

The Red Power Movement

Alcatraz Occupation (1969–1971): In November 1969, a group of Native American activists occupied the abandoned federal prison on Alcatraz Island in San Francisco Bay, claiming the land under an old treaty. The occupation lasted 19 months and drew national attention to Native American issues.

American Indian Movement (AIM): Founded in 1968 in Minneapolis, the American Indian Movement (AIM) was established to fight police brutality against Native Americans in cities and to campaign for treaty rights. AIM was modelled in part on the Black Panthers, combining community organising with confrontational protest.

Trail of Broken Treaties (1972): AIM organised a march on Washington, D.C., and occupied the Bureau of Indian Affairs building for six days, demanding that the government honour broken treaties.

Wounded Knee (1973): In February 1973, around 200 AIM members occupied the town of Wounded Knee on the Pine Ridge Indian Reservation in South Dakota. The site held deep symbolic significance — it was where the US Army had massacred around 300 Lakota Sioux in 1890. The occupation lasted 71 days and resulted in an armed standoff with federal forces. Two Native Americans were killed and a federal marshal was seriously wounded.

Key Fact — Native American Gains

The Red Power movement achieved some significant results:

  • The Indian Education Act (1972) gave tribes greater control over their children’s education
  • The Indian Self-Determination Act (1975) ended the termination policy and gave tribes more control over federal programmes on reservations
  • Several land and treaty claims were settled, returning some lands to tribal ownership
  • However, poverty, poor health, and social problems on reservations remained severe

Hispanic and Latino Rights

Hispanic Americans — particularly Mexican Americans (Chicanos) — faced widespread discrimination, low wages, and poor working conditions. Many worked as migrant farm workers in California and the Southwest, enduring backbreaking labour for poverty wages with no legal protections.

Cesar Chavez and the United Farm Workers

Cesar Chavez became the most prominent leader of the Hispanic rights movement. In 1962, he co-founded the National Farm Workers Association (later the United Farm Workers, or UFW), with fellow activist Dolores Huerta.

In 1965, the UFW launched the Delano Grape Strike, demanding better pay and conditions for grape pickers. When the strike alone proved insufficient, Chavez organised a nationwide consumer boycott of grapes. The boycott was remarkably successful — an estimated 17 million Americans stopped buying grapes.

Chavez, inspired by Gandhi and King, insisted on non-violent methods. In 1968, he undertook a 25-day hunger strike to draw attention to the cause and reaffirm the commitment to non-violence. Senator Robert F. Kennedy visited Chavez to show his support.

In 1970, the grape growers finally agreed to negotiate, granting workers better wages, benefits, and union recognition. It was one of the most significant labour victories in American history.

The Chicano Movement

Beyond labour rights, a broader Chicano Movement emerged in the late 1960s, campaigning for better education, political representation, and cultural pride. Key events included:

  • The 1968 East Los Angeles school walkouts, where over 10,000 Mexican American students walked out of high schools to protest inferior education
  • The formation of La Raza Unida, a Chicano political party, in 1970
  • Growing pride in Mexican American heritage and culture, with “Chicano” becoming a term of empowerment rather than an insult
Exam Tip

When writing about other rights movements, examiners look for connections to the Black civil rights movement. Show how groups like women, Native Americans, and Hispanic Americans were inspired by and modelled their tactics on the civil rights movement — using non-violent protest, boycotts, and legal challenges. Also consider the differences: each group faced distinct forms of discrimination and had specific goals. A top answer will compare the extent of change achieved by different movements.

Conclusion

The civil rights movement inspired a wave of activism among other disadvantaged groups in American society. The women’s liberation movement achieved significant legislative gains including the Equal Pay Act and Title IX, and the landmark Roe v. Wade ruling, though the ERA failed and deep inequalities persisted. Native Americans, through organisations like AIM and dramatic actions at Alcatraz and Wounded Knee, forced the government to end the destructive termination policy and grant greater self-determination. Hispanic Americans, led by Cesar Chavez and the UFW, won important labour rights through non-violent boycotts and strikes. Each movement demonstrated that the struggle for equality in America extended far beyond the issue of Black civil rights.

Key Takeaways

  • Betty Friedan’s The Feminine Mystique (1963) and the founding of NOW (1966) launched the women’s liberation movement, achieving major legislation but falling short of the ERA.
  • Roe v. Wade (1973) established a constitutional right to abortion, becoming one of the most significant and controversial Supreme Court rulings in American history.
  • AIM and Wounded Knee (1973) brought national attention to the desperate conditions faced by Native Americans and helped end the termination policy.
  • Cesar Chavez and the UFW used non-violent boycotts to win better conditions for migrant farm workers, with the Delano Grape Boycott becoming a model of effective protest.
  • All these movements were inspired by the Black civil rights movement and used similar tactics of non-violent direct action, legal challenges, and media attention.
