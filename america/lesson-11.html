Civil Rights: Segregation & Early Campaigns

0:00 / 0:00

Segregation and Jim Crow Laws

By the 1940s, African Americans — especially in the Southern states — faced racial discrimination enforced by law. The Jim Crow laws had been in place since the late 19th century. They created a system of legal segregation that touched every part of daily life.

Key Fact — Jim Crow Segregation

Jim Crow laws separated Black and white Americans in:

  • Schools — separate (and usually inferior) schools for Black children
  • Transport — separate seating on buses, trains, and waiting rooms
  • Public facilities — separate drinking fountains, restrooms, and parks
  • Restaurants and theatres — Black people were barred or forced into designated areas
  • Voting — literacy tests, poll taxes, and grandfather clauses stopped Black Americans from voting

In 1896, the Supreme Court ruling in Plessy v. Ferguson established the doctrine of “separate but equal,” declaring segregation constitutional as long as facilities were equal. In reality, facilities for Black Americans were almost always vastly inferior — schools underfunded, hospitals poorly equipped, opportunities severely limited.

Violence and Intimidation

Segregation was enforced not just by law but by violence and terror. The Ku Klux Klan (KKK) terrorised Black communities through lynchings, bombings, and arson. Between 1882 and 1968, over 4,700 people were lynched in the United States, the vast majority Black men. Police and courts often turned a blind eye, and killers were rarely punished.

The Murder of Emmett Till (1955)

Emmett Till was a 14-year-old Black boy from Chicago visiting relatives in Mississippi in the summer of 1955. He allegedly whistled at a white woman, Carolyn Bryant, in a grocery store. Days later, her husband Roy Bryant and his half-brother J.W. Milam kidnapped Emmett from his great-uncle’s home. They beat him brutally, shot him, and dumped his body in the Tallahatchie River.

Key Fact — Emmett Till

Emmett’s mother, Mamie Till, insisted on an open-casket funeral so the world could see what had been done to her son. Photos of his mutilated body were published in Jet magazine and shocked the nation. Despite overwhelming evidence, the all-white jury acquitted Bryant and Milam in just 67 minutes. The case became a catalyst for the civil rights movement — thousands of Americans, Black and white, were outraged and galvanised into action.

The Impact of World War Two

World War Two (1939–1945) was a turning point for civil rights. The war exposed the hypocrisy of fighting for freedom abroad while tolerating racial oppression at home.

Military service: Over 1 million African Americans served in WW2, though in segregated units. The Tuskegee Airmen — an all-Black fighter pilot squadron — excelled in combat and challenged racist assumptions.

The Double V Campaign: The Pittsburgh Courier launched the “Double V” campaign in 1942 — victory against fascism abroad and victory against racism at home.

Economic opportunities: The wartime boom drew hundreds of thousands of Black Americans to Northern and Western factories during the Great Migration. Even in the North, however, Black workers were usually given the lowest-paid jobs and were the first to be laid off during downturns. In 1941, A. Philip Randolph threatened a march on Washington to demand fair employment. Roosevelt responded with Executive Order 8802, banning racial discrimination in the defence industry — the first federal civil rights action since Reconstruction.

Truman desegregates the military: In 1948, President Truman signed Executive Order 9981, desegregating the US armed forces. This was a major early federal civil rights action, showing the growing political pressure for change.

Returning veterans: Black veterans came home expecting the freedoms they had fought for. Instead, they faced the same Jim Crow discrimination. This injustice radicalised many and swelled the ranks of civil rights groups.

The NAACP and Legal Challenges

The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP), founded in 1909, was the leading civil rights organisation. For decades, the NAACP campaigned for the federal government to pass anti-lynching laws and protect Black voting rights. But the government refused to act — it did not want a fight with powerful Southern politicians over “states’ rights.” The NAACP’s strategy therefore shifted towards using the legal system to challenge segregation, bringing carefully chosen test cases to court.

The NAACP’s chief lawyer, Thurgood Marshall, led a series of legal victories in the 1940s and 1950s that chipped away at “separate but equal.” Marshall later became the first African American Supreme Court Justice in 1967.

Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

The biggest legal victory came on 17 May 1954, when the Supreme Court ruled unanimously in Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka.

Key Fact — Brown v. Board of Education

The case was brought by Oliver Brown on behalf of his daughter Linda, denied entry to her local all-white school in Topeka, Kansas. She had to travel a long way to reach a segregated Black school. Chief Justice Earl Warren declared that “separate educational facilities are inherently unequal” — violating the 14th Amendment. This overturned Plessy v. Ferguson after 58 years.

Significance and Limitations

Brown was a landmark moment. It established that segregation was unconstitutional and gave the movement huge moral and legal force. But the ruling had serious limits:

  • The Supreme Court set no deadline, saying only that desegregation should happen “with all deliberate speed”
  • Many Southern states refused to comply — over 100 Southern congressmen signed the “Southern Manifesto” pledging resistance
  • White Citizens’ Councils formed across the South to fight integration. They had around 250,000 members and used economic pressure — such as sacking workers and refusing loans — to punish anyone who supported desegregation
  • By 1957, not a single Black child attended an integrated school in Virginia, Alabama, Mississippi, Georgia, Louisiana, or South Carolina
  • The ruling only applied to schools, not other areas of segregation

The Little Rock Nine (1957)

Fierce resistance to desegregation was shown in September 1957 at Central High School in Little Rock, Arkansas.

Nine Black students — the Little Rock Nine — tried to enrol at the all-white Central High School. Governor Orval Faubus ordered the Arkansas National Guard to block them, directly defying the federal government.

When Elizabeth Eckford arrived alone on the first day, a screaming white mob surrounded her. Photos of the 15-year-old being harassed were published worldwide, causing international outrage.

After weeks of crisis, President Eisenhower sent 1,000 soldiers from the 101st Airborne Division to escort the students into school. It was the first time since Reconstruction that a president had used federal troops to protect Black Americans’ rights.

The nine students endured a year of harassment, physical attacks, and verbal abuse. Their courage showed both the bravery needed to challenge segregation and the depth of white resistance.

Key Fact — Significance of Little Rock

Little Rock showed that: (1) Southern states would use force to resist desegregation; (2) the federal government could be pressured into acting; and (3) media coverage — especially television — was a powerful weapon for civil rights, as images of white hatred shocked moderate Americans and the world.

James Meredith and the University of Mississippi (1962)

In 1962, James Meredith became the first Black student to enrol at the University of Mississippi (‘Ole Miss’). The state governor tried to block him. President Kennedy sent federal marshals to enforce Meredith’s right to attend. Violent riots broke out on campus — 2 people were killed and hundreds injured. Meredith attended classes under armed guard. The crisis showed that even after Brown, every step towards integration could provoke deadly resistance.

The Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–1956)

While the NAACP fought segregation in the courts, a new form of protest emerged: direct action. The spark was the Montgomery Bus Boycott.

Claudette Colvin and Rosa Parks

Nine months before Rosa Parks, a 15-year-old girl named Claudette Colvin refused to give up her seat on a Montgomery bus and was arrested. The NAACP chose not to build their case around Colvin because she was a pregnant teenager. This shows that the Parks case was a deliberate, strategic choice by civil rights leaders — not a random act.

On 1 December 1955, Rosa Parks, a 42-year-old seamstress and NAACP secretary, was arrested in Montgomery, Alabama, for refusing to give up her seat to a white passenger. Parks was an experienced activist who had trained at the Highlander Folk School, a centre for civil rights education.

The Boycott

Montgomery’s Black community launched a total boycott of the city’s bus system. The Montgomery Bus Boycott lasted 381 days.

Key Fact — The Montgomery Bus Boycott
  • Around 40,000 Black commuters walked, carpooled, or cycled instead of riding segregated buses
  • Black passengers made up 75% of bus riders, so the boycott devastated the bus company financially
  • Protesters faced intimidation: MLK’s home was bombed, and many boycotters were arrested or sacked
  • On 13 November 1956, the Supreme Court ruled in Browder v. Gayle that bus segregation was unconstitutional
  • The boycott ended on 20 December 1956 when Montgomery’s buses were officially desegregated

The Emergence of Martin Luther King Jr.

The boycott made a young Baptist minister famous: Dr Martin Luther King Jr., aged just 26, was elected president of the Montgomery Improvement Association (MIA) to lead the boycott.

King was inspired by Mahatma Gandhi and championed non-violent direct action — confronting injustice through peaceful protest. His powerful speeches and moral authority made him the movement’s most prominent leader. In 1957, he helped found the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC) to coordinate campaigns across the South.

Eisenhower and Federal Action

President Eisenhower was a reluctant supporter of civil rights. He privately doubted the Brown ruling and was slow to act. But the Little Rock crisis forced his hand. In 1957, he signed the Civil Rights Act of 1957 — the first federal civil rights law since 1875. It set up a Civil Rights Commission and gave the government some power to protect voting rights, though it was weakened by compromise in Congress and had limited real impact.

Exam Tip

Examiners want to see you understand both the legal and direct-action approaches. The NAACP won court cases (Brown v. Board), while the Montgomery Bus Boycott showed the power of mass protest. A top answer explains how these strategies worked together — legal victories gave moral authority, while direct action created the public pressure to enforce change. Remember to mention Emmett Till as a catalyst and the role of media in building support.

Conclusion

The 1940s and 1950s laid the foundations of the civil rights movement. WW2 raised expectations and exposed American hypocrisy. Truman desegregated the military in 1948. The NAACP won the landmark Brown v. Board ruling. Emmett Till’s murder in 1955 shocked the nation into action. The Montgomery Bus Boycott proved that mass non-violent protest worked and launched MLK as a leader. But progress was painfully slow — White Citizens’ Councils and violent resistance showed how deep opposition ran. The stage was set for the confrontations of the 1960s.

Key Takeaways

  • Jim Crow enforced legal segregation across the South, backed by violence from the KKK and economic pressure from White Citizens’ Councils.
  • Emmett Till’s murder (1955) and his mother’s open-casket funeral galvanised the civil rights movement.
  • Brown v. Board (1954) declared school segregation unconstitutional, but Southern resistance meant real change was painfully slow.
  • The Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56) proved mass non-violent protest could win, and launched MLK as a national leader.
  • Little Rock (1957) and Ole Miss (1962) showed both the depth of white resistance and the power of federal intervention when media coverage forced action.
