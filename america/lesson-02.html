Lesson 2 of 15

Prohibition & Organised Crime

Overlay:

What Was Prohibition?

Prohibition was the ban on the manufacture, sale, and transportation of alcoholic drinks in the United States. It was introduced by the 18th Amendment to the US Constitution in January 1919 and came into effect on 17 January 1920. The Volstead Act (October 1919) defined the details of the law, making it illegal to produce, sell, or transport any drink containing more than 0.5% alcohol.

Key Fact — The Legal Framework

The 18th Amendment (1919) made Prohibition part of the Constitution. The Volstead Act (1919) provided the enforcement rules. Together, they banned the manufacture, sale, and transportation of ‘intoxicating liquors’ — but notably, it was never illegal to drink alcohol. People could still consume it; they just could not legally obtain it.

Why Was Prohibition Introduced?

The campaign for Prohibition had been growing for decades, driven by several powerful groups and arguments:

The Temperance Movement

Groups such as the Anti-Saloon League (founded 1893) and the Women’s Christian Temperance Union (WCTU) had campaigned against alcohol for years. They argued that alcohol destroyed families, caused domestic violence, and led to poverty. These organisations were well-funded, politically influential, and highly organised.

Religious and Moral Arguments

Many Protestant Christians, particularly in rural areas and the South, saw alcohol as sinful and immoral. They called saloons ‘dens of vice’ and believed banning alcohol would create a more moral, God-fearing society. The campaign for Prohibition was often linked to wider moral reform movements.

Industrial Efficiency

Industrialists like Henry Ford and John D. Rockefeller supported Prohibition because they believed sober workers would be more productive and reliable. Fewer workplace accidents and less absenteeism meant higher profits.

Anti-German Sentiment

During World War One, many of America’s largest breweries were owned by German-Americans (such as Anheuser-Busch and Pabst). Anti-German feeling during the war made it politically easier to target the brewing industry. Grain used for brewing could be redirected to the war effort.

How Was Prohibition Enforced?

Enforcement was the responsibility of Prohibition agents, but from the start the task was almost impossible:

  • Only around 1,500 Prohibition agents were employed to police the entire country — a vast territory with 18,700 miles of coastline and land borders.
  • Agents were poorly paid (around $2,500 per year), making many vulnerable to bribery.
  • In the first four years alone, 1 in 12 agents were dismissed for corruption.
  • Many state and local police forces were unwilling or unable to enforce the law, especially in large cities.
Key Fact — Eliot Ness

Eliot Ness was a young Prohibition agent in Chicago who led a team known as ‘The Untouchables’ because they refused to accept bribes. They raided Al Capone’s breweries and distilleries, but Ness’s team was the exception rather than the rule — widespread corruption undermined enforcement across the country.

Breaking the Law: Bootlegging and Speakeasies

Rather than stopping people from drinking, Prohibition drove the alcohol trade underground and created a massive illegal industry:

Bootlegging

Bootleggers were people who illegally manufactured, transported, or sold alcohol. Alcohol was smuggled across the border from Canada and Mexico, or brought in by ship from the Caribbean (a practice called ‘rum running’). Others produced ‘moonshine’ — homemade spirits often brewed in bathtubs or hidden stills. Some of this was extremely dangerous; badly made alcohol caused blindness and even death. An estimated 10,000 people died from drinking poisonous bootleg liquor during Prohibition.

Speakeasies

Speakeasies were illegal bars and drinking clubs that sprang up across America. By 1930, there were an estimated 200,000 speakeasies in the United States — in New York City alone, there may have been 30,000, more than double the number of legal bars before Prohibition. The name ‘speakeasy’ came from the practice of speaking quietly (‘easy’) about the location to avoid detection. Many were hidden behind secret doors, in basements, or disguised as shops.

The Rise of Organised Crime

Prohibition created an enormous opportunity for organised crime. Criminal gangs took control of the illegal alcohol trade, making vast fortunes and wielding enormous power.

Al Capone

Alphonse ‘Al’ Capone was the most notorious gangster of the Prohibition era. Based in Chicago, he controlled a criminal empire that earned an estimated $60 million a year from bootlegging alone (equivalent to over $1 billion today). Capone also ran gambling dens, brothels, and protection rackets.

Key Fact — Al Capone’s Power

Capone controlled much of Chicago through a combination of bribery and violence. He had judges, police officers, and politicians on his payroll. It is estimated he bribed officials with around $75 million during his criminal career. Despite being responsible for numerous murders, Capone was eventually convicted in 1931 — not for murder or bootlegging, but for tax evasion. He was sentenced to 11 years in federal prison.

The St Valentine’s Day Massacre

The most infamous act of gang violence during Prohibition was the St Valentine’s Day Massacre on 14 February 1929. Gunmen, believed to be working for Al Capone, dressed as police officers and executed seven members of the rival Bugs Moran gang in a garage on Chicago’s North Clark Street. The massacre shocked the nation and highlighted the extreme violence that Prohibition had unleashed. Although Capone was widely believed to have ordered the killings, he was in Florida at the time and was never charged.

Gang Wars and Violence

Chicago was not the only city affected. Gang warfare over control of the bootlegging trade caused hundreds of murders across American cities. In Chicago alone, there were over 500 gang-related murders between 1927 and 1930. Corruption was rampant — in many cities, the police and politicians were on the gangsters’ payroll.

Why Did Prohibition Fail?

By the late 1920s, it was clear that Prohibition was not working. Several factors explain its failure:

Impossible to enforce: The USA was simply too large. With thousands of miles of borders and coastline, and only 1,500 agents, there was no way to stop the flow of illegal alcohol.

Widespread disregard for the law: Millions of ordinary Americans continued to drink. Many saw Prohibition as an unreasonable restriction on their personal freedom. Even some politicians and judges drank illegally.

Corruption: Low-paid Prohibition agents, police officers, judges, and politicians were easily bribed by gangsters. In some cities, the criminal underworld and the authorities were virtually indistinguishable.

Organised crime: Rather than eliminating alcohol, Prohibition handed control of the drinks trade to violent criminals who made enormous profits. The gangs became more powerful and more dangerous as a result.

Loss of tax revenue: Before Prohibition, the government had collected significant tax revenue from the legal alcohol industry. This was now lost, while the cost of enforcement drained public funds.

The Great Depression: After the Wall Street Crash of 1929, ending Prohibition became an economic argument too — legalising alcohol would create jobs and generate tax revenue that the struggling government desperately needed.

The End of Prohibition

In the 1932 presidential election, Franklin D. Roosevelt campaigned on a promise to end Prohibition. After his landslide victory, the 21st Amendment was ratified on 5 December 1933, repealing the 18th Amendment and ending Prohibition after nearly 14 years. It remains the only time a Constitutional Amendment has been repealed by another Amendment.

Key Fact — The 21st Amendment

The 21st Amendment (1933) repealed Prohibition. Roosevelt reportedly said: ‘I think this would be a good time for a beer.’ The end of Prohibition created jobs, generated tax revenue, and removed a major source of income from organised crime — though criminal gangs simply moved into other illegal activities such as drugs and gambling.

Exam Tip

Questions about Prohibition often ask you to explain why it was introduced or why it failed. Make sure you can give multiple reasons for each, with specific evidence. For a top-level answer, explain how the reasons are linked — for example, the impossibility of enforcement led to widespread law-breaking, which created the opportunity for organised crime, which in turn corrupted the very officials meant to enforce the law.

Conclusion

Prohibition was one of the most dramatic social experiments in American history, and it is widely regarded as a failure. While it was introduced with genuine moral intentions, it proved impossible to enforce, led to the rise of powerful criminal organisations, corrupted law enforcement, and was widely ignored by the American public. Rather than creating a sober, moral society, Prohibition demonstrated the limits of using legislation to change deep-rooted social behaviour.

Key Takeaways

  • Good intentions, disastrous results: Prohibition was supported by sincere moral, religious, and economic arguments, but its consequences — organised crime, corruption, and widespread law-breaking — were far worse than the problems it sought to solve.
  • Al Capone and organised crime: The illegal alcohol trade made gangsters like Capone incredibly wealthy and powerful, with criminal empires that corrupted police, judges, and politicians.
  • The 21st Amendment: Prohibition was repealed in 1933, the only time a Constitutional Amendment has been undone. Its failure showed that laws are only effective when they have broad public support.
