Lesson 6 of 15

Life in the Great Depression

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

The Scale of the Depression

The Great Depression was the worst economic crisis in American history, lasting from 1929 to the late 1930s. After the Wall Street Crash, the economy went into a devastating downward spiral. Businesses closed, banks failed, and millions lost their jobs, homes, and savings.

Many ordinary Americans who had never bought shares still lost everything. Banks had been gambling with their customers’ deposits on the stock market. When the market crashed, the banks collapsed — and innocent savers’ money went with them. There was no government insurance to protect deposits.

Key Fact — Depression in Numbers

By 1933, around 13 million Americans were unemployed (25% of the workforce). Industrial production had fallen nearly 50%. Over 5,000 banks had collapsed. National income dropped from $87 billion (1929) to $40 billion (1933). Around 1–2 million were homeless.

Unemployment and Poverty

Mass unemployment was the most devastating effect. With no welfare system, no unemployment insurance, and no safety net, losing your job meant losing everything.

The Human Cost

  • By 1933, one in four workers was unemployed. In some cities it was far worse — 80% in Toledo, Ohio and 50% in Cleveland.
  • Those who kept jobs saw wages cut by an average of 60% between 1929 and 1933.
  • 250,000 families had their homes repossessed in 1932 alone.
  • Millions went hungry. Breadlines and soup kitchens appeared in every major city, run by charities — not the government.
  • Suicide rates rose by 30% between 1929 and 1932.

Hoovervilles

Homeless Americans built makeshift shelters on waste ground, in parks, and on town edges. These shanty towns were called ‘Hoovervilles’ — a bitter, mocking reference to President Herbert Hoover, whom many blamed for the crisis.

Key Fact — Hoovervilles

Hoovervilles were built from scrap wood, tin, cardboard, and old car parts. The largest, in Central Park, New York, housed hundreds. Others appeared in Seattle, Washington D.C., St Louis, and most major cities. Bitter nicknames spread: newspapers as blankets were ‘Hoover blankets,’ empty pockets turned out were ‘Hoover flags,’ and broken cars pulled by horses were ‘Hoover wagons.’

President Hoover’s Response

Herbert Hoover was President when the Crash struck. He kept insisting recovery was near, famously declaring “Prosperity is just around the corner.” To millions of starving Americans, this showed how out of touch he was.

  • Hoover believed in ‘rugged individualism’ — the idea that Americans should help themselves, not rely on government handouts. He feared direct relief would make people dependent.
  • He encouraged voluntary action — asking businesses not to cut wages and urging charities to help the poor. This was nowhere near enough.
  • In 1931, he invested $400 million in government building projects, including the Hoover Dam, to try to create jobs. This helped, but it was too small for the scale of the crisis.
  • In 1932, he set up the Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC), making $1,500 million available in loans to banks and businesses. Critics called this “feeding the sparrows by feeding the horse” — it helped the rich while ordinary people starved.
  • He raised tariffs with the Hawley-Smoot Tariff (1930), which actually worsened the Depression by cutting international trade as other countries retaliated.

The Bonus Army, 1932

In summer 1932, about 20,000 World War One veterans and their families marched on Washington D.C. They demanded early payment of a war service bonus not due until 1945. These desperate, unemployed veterans set up camp near the Capitol and called themselves the Bonus Expeditionary Force (or Bonus Army).

Key Fact — The Bonus Army Incident

The Senate rejected the bonus bill, but most veterans refused to leave. Hoover ordered the US Army, led by General Douglas MacArthur, to clear the camp. Soldiers used tear gas, bayonets, and tanks to drive out the veterans and their families, burning their shelters. Two veterans were killed and over 1,000 injured, including women and children. An 11-month-old baby died in the attack. The sight of the Army attacking its own war heroes horrified the nation and destroyed Hoover’s reputation.

The Dust Bowl

While the Depression devastated cities, a separate catastrophe struck rural America. The Dust Bowl was an ecological disaster affecting the Great Plains — Oklahoma, Texas, Kansas, Colorado, and New Mexico — throughout the 1930s.

Causes of the Dust Bowl

  • Over-farming: During WWI and the 1920s, farmers ploughed up vast grasslands to plant wheat and cotton, stripping the soil’s protective covering.
  • Drought: A severe drought hit from 1931, lasting most of the decade. The exposed topsoil dried out completely.
  • Dust storms: Huge windstorms picked up loose topsoil, creating ‘black blizzards’ that travelled hundreds of miles. On ‘Black Sunday’ (14 April 1935), dust storms turned day into night.

Impact on Farmers

The Dust Bowl destroyed farming across a vast area. Crops failed, livestock died, and the land became useless. About 2.5 million people left the Dust Bowl states. Many headed to California, where they were called ‘Okies.’ They found only low-paid seasonal work and faced hostility from locals.

Key Fact — The Scale of the Dust Bowl

At its peak, the Dust Bowl affected 100 million acres. Dust storms reached as far as New York and Washington D.C., dropping Great Plains soil on politicians’ desks. The disaster showed how environmental mismanagement could worsen an economic crisis.

Impact on Different Groups

African Americans: Already the poorest group, they were hit hardest. ‘Last hired, first fired’ — Black unemployment reached 50% in many cities, double the national average. In the South, Black sharecroppers were evicted as farm income collapsed. Racial violence continued with no government help for Black communities.

Hispanic Americans: Many Mexican Americans were forced out through ‘repatriation.’ An estimated 500,000–1 million people of Mexican descent (including US citizens) were deported or pressured to leave between 1929 and 1936.

Women: Working women were accused of ‘stealing’ men’s jobs. Some states banned married women from government jobs. However, some women found work in service roles that men refused.

Children: Many were pulled out of school to work or beg. In some mining towns, 90% of children were malnourished. Thousands of teenagers became ‘hobos,’ riding freight trains looking for work.

Farmers: Falling prices, bank foreclosures, and the Dust Bowl devastated farming communities. Thousands lost farms that had been in their families for generations.

The 1932 Election

By 1932, Americans had had enough of Hoover. The Democratic candidate, Franklin D. Roosevelt (FDR), promised a “New Deal” for the American people. He pledged the “Three Rs” — Relief for the poor, Recovery for the economy, and Reform to prevent another depression.

Roosevelt won in a landslide, carrying 42 out of 48 states. Americans had rejected Hoover’s rugged individualism and voted for a president who promised the government would act. The stage was set for a dramatic change of direction.

Exam Tip

Questions often ask about the impact on different groups. Discuss urban workers, farmers, African Americans, and women separately with specific evidence. Hoover’s failures — “Prosperity is just around the corner,” the Bonus Army, and his late, inadequate RFC — explain why Americans voted for Roosevelt’s New Deal in 1932.

Conclusion

The Great Depression was a catastrophe. By 1933, 13 million were unemployed, 5,000 banks had failed, and millions had lost homes and savings — including innocent savers whose banks had gambled their money away. Hoovervilles filled the cities, dust storms swept the Plains, and Hoover’s ‘rugged individualism’ left him unable to help. The Bonus Army disaster destroyed his reputation. In 1932, Roosevelt won 42 out of 48 states, promising a New Deal of Relief, Recovery, and Reform.

Key Takeaways

  • Mass suffering: 13 million unemployed by 1933 (25%), no welfare system, and innocent savers wiped out by reckless banks.
  • Hoover failed: His “rugged individualism,” the Bonus Army attack, and the late RFC ($1,500 million) were not enough. His $400 million building projects barely scratched the surface.
  • The Dust Bowl: An ecological disaster displacing 2.5 million people, compounding the economic crisis.
