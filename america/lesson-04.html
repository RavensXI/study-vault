A Divided Society

Introduction: A Nation of Divisions

The ‘Roaring Twenties’ hid deep divisions. While some enjoyed new wealth and freedoms, millions faced racism, prejudice, and poverty. Around 60% of American families lived below the poverty line — even during the boom. Tensions between old and new, native-born and immigrant, white and Black, rich and poor ran through every part of society.

This lesson covers economic inequality, the causes of racial tension, the experiences of immigrants, the revival of the Ku Klux Klan, and the Red Scare — including the case of Sacco and Vanzetti.

Economic Inequality Beneath the Boom

Not everyone shared in 1920s prosperity. The boom was largely an urban, industrial success story. Millions in rural America and in older industries were left behind.

Farmers in Crisis

American farmers had expanded production during World War One to feed Europe. After the war, demand dropped and prices collapsed. Around 6 million farmers were forced off their land during the 1920s. Mechanisation — especially combine harvesters and tractors — replaced manual labour, cutting the number of jobs available in farming.

Declining Industries

Workers in older industries also suffered. Coal miners faced low wages and dangerous conditions as oil and electricity replaced coal. Textile workers in New England saw jobs move south, where wages were lower. These workers had no share in the consumer boom happening in cities.

The Rural–Urban Divide

A deep cultural divide separated rural and urban America. Cities were centres of jazz, cinema, new fashions, and liberal attitudes. Rural America — especially in the South and Midwest — was more traditionalist, religious, and conservative. Many rural Americans saw city life as immoral and un-American. This clash fuelled support for Prohibition, hostility to immigration, and the revival of the KKK.

Key Fact — Poverty in the Boom

Around 60% of American families earned less than $2,000 per year — the minimum needed for a decent standard of living. About 6 million farmers lost their land. The boom mainly benefited white, urban, middle-class Americans, not the majority of the population.

The Causes of Racial Tension

Racial tension in 1920s America had deep roots. The country had a long history of discrimination against non-white groups, and the 1920s saw these tensions grow worse.

The Great Migration

During and after World War One, hundreds of thousands of African Americans moved from the rural South to northern cities. This movement was called the Great Migration. They were escaping sharecropping poverty, lynching violence, and the humiliation of Jim Crow laws.

By 1920, there were around 11 million Black Americans living in the USA. In cities like Chicago, Detroit, and New York, African Americans hoped for better factory jobs. They still faced serious discrimination — given the lowest-paid and most dangerous jobs, forced into overcrowded ghettos, and resented by white workers who saw them as competition.

Key Fact — The Great Migration

Between 1916 and 1930, about 1.5 million African Americans left the South for northern cities. They still faced prejudice. In 1919, race riots broke out in over 25 cities during the ‘Red Summer.’ The worst was in Chicago, where 38 people were killed.

Jim Crow and Segregation

In the Southern states, African Americans lived under Jim Crow laws — legal racial segregation. These laws kept Black and white people apart in schools, restaurants, buses, parks, and hospitals. Black people were denied the vote through literacy tests, poll taxes, and the ‘grandfather clause’ (you could only vote if your grandfather had voted — impossible for descendants of enslaved people).

Segregation was backed up by violence. Lynching — the murder of Black people by white mobs — was common. Between 1882 and 1968, nearly 5,000 lynchings were recorded, mostly of African Americans in the South. The federal government repeatedly failed to pass anti-lynching laws.

Immigration and Its Impact

The USA had long been a nation of immigrants. By the 1920s, attitudes had turned sharply against newcomers — especially those from Southern and Eastern Europe.

Who Were the Immigrants?

Between 1850 and 1914, around 40 million people emigrated to the USA. The earliest settlers — the ‘old immigrants’ — came mainly from Britain, Germany, Holland, and Scandinavia. Their descendants held the best jobs, the most wealth, and most political power. They were the WASPs (White Anglo-Saxon Protestants). America was described as a ‘melting pot’ — the idea being that all races would mix together and become one nation.

From the late nineteenth century, ‘new immigrants’ arrived from Italy, Poland, Russia, Greece, and the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Many were Catholic or Jewish, spoke little English, and had very different cultures from the WASPs. A smaller number of Hispanics and Asians also arrived. These new immigrants settled in cities, living in ethnic neighbourhoods and working in factories, mines, and construction.

Why Did Attitudes Harden?

Hostility towards immigrants grew for several reasons:

  • Competition for jobs: Native-born Americans resented immigrants who worked for lower wages.
  • Cultural fears: Immigrants brought different languages, religions, and customs. Many WASPs feared America’s identity was being diluted.
  • The Red Scare: After the Russian Revolution of 1917, many feared immigrants from Eastern Europe were bringing communist and anarchist ideas.
  • Racism: Pseudo-scientific ideas about racial superiority were widespread. Many Americans saw Southern and Eastern Europeans as racially inferior.

Immigration Restriction

Growing hostility led to new laws that slashed immigration:

1917

Immigration Act

Introduced a literacy test — immigrants had to prove they could read and write. This targeted poorer immigrants from Southern and Eastern Europe.

1921

Emergency Quota Act

Set a maximum of 375,000 immigrants per year. Each country was limited to 3% of the number from that country already in the USA (based on the 1910 census). This was the first numerical cap on immigration.

1924

National Origins Act (Johnson-Reed Act)

Tightened quotas to 2% of each nationality, now based on the 1890 census — before the surge of Southern and Eastern European immigration. This deliberately favoured Northern and Western Europeans and blocked Italians, Poles, Russians, and Greeks.

1929

Immigration Act

Total immigration was capped at just 150,000 per year. Immigration from Asia was banned entirely. This completed a decade of increasingly restrictive controls.

The Ku Klux Klan

The Ku Klux Klan (KKK) was a white supremacist group that first appeared after the Civil War in the 1860s. It was revived in 1915 and grew rapidly during the 1920s.

The Revival of the Klan

The KKK was re-founded in 1915 by William J. Simmons at Stone Mountain, Georgia, inspired by the hit film The Birth of a Nation (1915). The new Klan kept the old symbols — white robes, hoods, burning crosses — but expanded its targets to include Catholics, Jews, immigrants, and anyone seen as ‘un-American.’

Growth and Membership

Under Hiram Wesley Evans, who became Imperial Wizard in 1922, the Klan grew fast. It used professional recruiters called ‘Kleagles’ and charged $10 to join (a large sum then), with the recruiter keeping $4. Its message of white, Protestant, native-born supremacy appealed to those who felt threatened by change.

Key Fact — KKK Membership

At its peak, the Klan had 4–5 million members. It was not just a Southern movement — states like Indiana, Ohio, Oregon, and Oklahoma had huge memberships. In Indiana, about 30% of all white men were members. Klan-backed candidates won governorships and seats in Congress.

Klan Methods and Violence

The KKK used intimidation, beatings, tarring and feathering, arson, and murder. It boycotted Black-owned and Jewish-owned businesses. In 1925, 40,000 Klansmen marched down Pennsylvania Avenue in Washington, D.C. The Klan claimed to defend traditional American values and the Protestant faith.

The Klan collapsed in the late 1920s, mainly because of the D.C. Stephenson scandal. Stephenson, the Grand Dragon of the Indiana Klan, was convicted in 1925 of the kidnap, rape, and murder of Madge Oberholtzer. This exposed the Klan’s hypocrisy.

From prison, Stephenson named politicians who had taken Klan bribes. Several were jailed. Membership fell from millions to about 30,000 by 1930. The Klan did not vanish entirely — it resurfaced during the Civil Rights movement of the 1950s–60s.

The Red Scare

After World War One and the Russian Revolution of 1917, fear swept across America. Many were terrified that communists and anarchists were plotting to overthrow the government. This panic became the Red Scare.

Why Were Americans Afraid?

Key events fuelled the panic:

  • The Russian Revolution (1917) overthrew the Russian government and set up a communist state. Many feared the same in America.
  • In 1919, a wave of strikes hit the USA, including a general strike in Seattle and a police strike in Boston. Many Americans blamed communists.
  • In April and June 1919, anarchists sent bombs to politicians and businessmen, including Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer. One bomb damaged his home.

The Palmer Raids

Attorney General Palmer launched raids against suspected radicals. Between November 1919 and January 1920, his agents arrested over 6,000 people. Many were held without charge and denied lawyers. Around 500 foreign-born suspects were deported, including anarchist Emma Goldman.

The raids were popular at first, but support faded when Palmer clearly exaggerated the threat. His prediction of a communist revolution on 1 May 1920 never happened, and he was ridiculed. The Red Scare calmed down, but suspicion of immigrants and radicals lasted throughout the decade.

The Sacco and Vanzetti Case

The most famous example of how the Red Scare and anti-immigrant prejudice combined was the case of Nicola Sacco and Bartolomeo Vanzetti.

The Crime

In April 1920, paymaster Fred Parmenter and an armed guard were shot dead during a robbery at a shoe factory in South Braintree, Massachusetts. About $15,000 was stolen. Before he died, Parmenter described his attackers as “slim foreigners with olive skins.” Three weeks later, Sacco (a shoe-maker) and Vanzetti (a fish-seller) were arrested. Both were Italian immigrants and anarchists who had dodged the military draft.

The Trial

Their 1921 trial was deeply controversial. The evidence was weak. The judge, Webster Thayer, was openly hostile — he reportedly called them “those anarchist bastards” outside court. Both were found guilty of murder and sentenced to death.

Many believed they were convicted for being Italian, immigrant, and anarchist — not because of the evidence. The case became a worldwide cause, with protests across Europe and South America.

Over six years, their lawyers launched appeals. A convicted criminal named Celestino Madeiros confessed to the robbery and said Sacco and Vanzetti were innocent. All appeals were rejected — several by the same Judge Thayer.

A special committee reviewed the case in 1927 but upheld the verdict. On 23 August 1927, Sacco and Vanzetti were executed by electric chair. Their deaths sparked worldwide protests. In 1977, the Governor of Massachusetts declared the trial unfair.

The Significance of the Case

The case exposed deep prejudice against immigrants and radicals. It showed how the Red Scare and anti-immigrant feeling could corrupt the justice system. It became a symbol of the intolerance beneath the surface of the ‘Roaring Twenties.’

Exam Tip

Questions often ask you to explain causes of tension or evaluate how far America was ‘divided.’ Cover multiple groups (African Americans, immigrants, radicals, the poor) and link causes together. The Red Scare fuelled anti-immigrant prejudice, which shaped the Sacco and Vanzetti trial. The rural–urban divide fuelled Klan support. Always use specific evidence — names, dates, statistics, and laws.

Conclusion

The 1920s were deeply divided. Around 60% of families lived below the poverty line, 6 million farmers lost their land, and rural America clashed culturally with the cities. African Americans faced racism and violence North and South. Immigrants were shut out by quota laws. The KKK had millions of members. Sacco and Vanzetti’s execution showed how fear and prejudice could corrupt justice. The ‘Roaring Twenties’ was no golden age for most Americans.

Key Takeaways

  • Economic division: 60% of families lived below the poverty line. 6 million farmers were forced off their land by falling prices and mechanisation.
  • Racial tension and the KKK: The Great Migration, Jim Crow, and the KKK (4–5 million members) showed that racism shaped every part of American life.
  • Immigration restriction: The 1921 and 1924 quota laws cut immigration from 800,000 to 150,000 a year, deliberately targeting Southern and Eastern Europeans.
