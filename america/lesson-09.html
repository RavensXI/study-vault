Post-War Prosperity & Popular Culture

0:00 / 0:00

The Post-War Economic Boom

After World War Two ended in 1945, the United States entered a period of huge economic growth. Unlike the fragile boom of the 1920s, this one was sustained and widespread. The USA emerged as the world’s dominant economic and military superpower. Many Americans felt they were living the American Dream — the idea that through hard work, anyone could achieve prosperity and success.

Why Did the Economy Boom?

  • Wartime production: Factories switched from making tanks and aircraft to cars, fridges, and televisions. They were modern, efficient, and ready to meet demand.
  • Pent-up demand: Consumer goods had been rationed during the war. Americans had saved around $140 billion and were eager to spend.
  • Government spending: Cold War defence spending kept factories busy. The Korean War (1951) was another boost — military spending accounted for around 10% of GDP in the 1950s.
  • New technology: Wartime innovations were adapted for peacetime. Televisions, synthetic materials, jet travel, and plastics created new industries.
  • Population growth: The ‘baby boom’ (see below) created huge demand for housing, schools, and consumer goods.

Truman’s ‘Fair Deal’

After Roosevelt’s death in 1945, President Harry Truman promised Americans a ‘Fair Deal.’ He said: ‘Every segment of our population has a right to expect from his government a fair deal.’ Truman’s policies continued the New Deal approach — using government spending to support jobs, housing, and welfare. President Eisenhower (1953–1961) continued many of these programmes too. The idea that government should help ordinary people was now firmly established.

The GI Bill

The Servicemen’s Readjustment Act of 1944 (the GI Bill) was one of the most important laws in American history. It gave returning veterans:

  • Education: The government paid tuition and living costs. By 1947, veterans were 49% of all college admissions. Nearly 8 million veterans used this benefit.
  • Home loans: Low-interest, zero-deposit mortgages helped millions buy homes, driving suburban growth.
  • Unemployment support: $20 per week for up to a year while veterans found work.
Key Fact — The GI Bill’s Impact

The GI Bill opened higher education to millions of working-class Americans, creating a larger, more skilled workforce. Before WWII, college was mainly for the wealthy. However, Black veterans often struggled to access benefits — many colleges (especially in the South) refused to admit them, and banks sometimes refused mortgages to Black applicants in white areas.

The Baby Boom

When soldiers returned home, the birth rate soared. Between 1946 and 1964, about 76 million babies were born — the ‘baby boomers.’ The peak year was 1957, with 4.3 million births.

The baby boom created huge demand for housing, schools, children’s products, and food. It fuelled suburban growth as families needed bigger homes. In the 1960s and 1970s, the baby boomers themselves became a powerful cultural and political force.

Suburbia and Levittown

One of the most visible changes was the rapid growth of suburbs — residential communities on the outskirts of cities. They offered affordable homes, gardens, good schools, and a quieter life away from crowded inner cities.

Levittown

The most famous suburb was Levittown, built by William Levitt on Long Island, New York, from 1947. Levitt used assembly-line techniques — each identical house could be built in just 16 minutes per stage. Houses started at $7,990 (about $100,000 today). Levittown eventually held 17,000 homes and 82,000 people.

Key Fact — Suburban Growth

By 1960, one-third of all Americans lived in suburbs. Suburban population grew by 47% in the 1950s. However, the suburbs were overwhelmingly white. Levittown’s contracts explicitly banned sales to African Americans. This practice, known as ‘redlining,’ trapped Black families in overcrowded inner-city areas with declining services.

Suburban Women’s Isolation

Suburban life was not perfect for everyone. Many women who had worked during the war were now expected to be full-time housewives. Isolated in their suburban homes, unable to work, some women felt bored, lonely, and depressed. This sense of frustration would later inspire Betty Friedan’s feminist movement in the 1960s.

Consumer Culture

Post-war America saw a consumer explosion that dwarfed even the 1920s:

Television

Television was the defining technology of the era. In 1945, fewer than 10,000 homes had a TV. By 1960, 46 million did — about 90% of all homes. TV transformed entertainment, news, advertising, and politics. Adverts beamed into living rooms drove consumer spending.

Car Culture

The car became central to American life. By the mid-1950s, there were over 60 million cars. The Interstate Highway Act of 1956, signed by President Eisenhower, built 41,000 miles of interstate highways — the largest public works project in American history. Highways made suburban living practical and spawned new businesses: drive-in cinemas, drive-in restaurants (including the first McDonald’s in 1955), shopping malls, and motels.

Consumer Goods and Credit

Advertising, hire purchase (buying on credit), and rising incomes meant Americans bought more than ever. Hire purchase increased by 800% between 1945 and 1957. By the late 1950s, the average family owned a car, television, washing machine, and refrigerator. America consumed one-third of the world’s goods despite having only 6% of the world’s population.

Rock and Roll

Rock and roll was the biggest cultural change of the 1950s. It grew out of African American rhythm and blues music and became hugely popular with white teenagers.

Elvis Presley — the ‘King of Rock and Roll’ — became a sensation with hits like Hound Dog and Jailhouse Rock. Other stars included Chuck Berry, who helped define the rock and roll guitar sound, and Buddy Holly. Their music thrilled young people and horrified many adults.

Rock and roll created a ‘generation gap’ between teenagers and their parents. Adults criticised the music for encouraging rebellion, sexual freedom, and disrespect for authority. For the first time, teenagers emerged as a distinct group with their own money, music, fashion, and identity. Businesses quickly targeted this new market. The teenager became a powerful consumer.

The Limits of Post-War Prosperity

African Americans: Black Americans still faced segregation, especially in the South. They were excluded from many suburbs, faced job discrimination, and lived in poverty at much higher rates. In 1950, average Black family income was 54% of white family income.

Inner-city poverty: As white families moved to suburbs (‘white flight’), inner cities lost tax revenue, jobs, and services. The remaining population — mainly Black and Hispanic — faced declining schools, rising crime, and crumbling infrastructure.

Rural poverty: In areas like Appalachia and the Deep South, millions lived in severe poverty without proper healthcare, education, or housing.

Conformity and discontent: Suburban life was criticised for its conformity and materialism. Writers like Jack Kerouac (the Beat Generation) questioned whether the ‘American Dream’ was truly fulfilling or just shallow consumerism.

Exam Tip

Show the examiner you understand the contrast between the image and reality of post-war America. Many Americans enjoyed huge prosperity, but the benefits were not shared equally. Suburbs were largely white-only, African Americans faced ongoing discrimination, and inner cities declined. Strong answers link the boom to the civil rights movement — prosperity made inequality even more visible and unacceptable. Mention rock and roll and the generation gap to show awareness of cultural change.

Conclusion

Post-war America was transformed by an economic boom, suburban expansion, and a consumer revolution. The GI Bill created an educated, home-owning middle class. Truman’s Fair Deal continued government support for ordinary Americans. Television, cars, and rock and roll defined a new way of life. However, prosperity was not shared by all — African Americans, inner-city residents, and rural communities were largely excluded. The gap between affluent white suburbia and continued racial inequality would fuel the upheavals of the 1960s.

Key Takeaways

  • Economic superpower: GNP doubled between 1945 and 1960. Cold War and Korean War defence spending kept the economy booming. Hire purchase rose 800%.
  • Suburban revolution: One-third of Americans lived in suburbs by 1960. Levittown symbolised affordable living, but minorities were excluded and some suburban women felt isolated.
  • Cultural change: Rock and roll created a generation gap. Teenagers emerged as a distinct consumer group. 90% of homes had TV by 1960.
