Roosevelt’s New Deal

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

FDR’s Election in 1932

Franklin Delano Roosevelt (known as FDR) won the 1932 presidential election in a landslide, defeating the unpopular Herbert Hoover. He won 42 of 48 states and 57% of the popular vote. Americans were desperate for change. Roosevelt promised a ‘New Deal’ — a bold programme of government action to tackle the Depression.

Key Fact — Roosevelt’s Promise

In his acceptance speech, Roosevelt declared: ‘I pledge you, I pledge myself, to a new deal for the American people.’ At his inauguration on 4 March 1933, he said: ‘The only thing we have to fear is fear itself.’ He projected confidence and energy — a sharp contrast with Hoover’s perceived inaction.

Roosevelt’s Approach

FDR’s approach was very different from Hoover’s. He believed the federal government had a responsibility to help ordinary Americans in crisis. He surrounded himself with the ‘Brain Trust’ — a team of expert advisors from universities and business — to design his policies. His New Deal had three main aims, known as the ‘Three Rs’:

A key idea behind the New Deal was ‘priming the pump’. If the government spent money to create jobs, those workers would spend their wages in shops and businesses, which would then hire more workers. This would create a cycle of rising prosperity. In total, the New Deal cost $50.2 billion — the biggest government spending programme in world history at the time.

The First Hundred Days (March–June 1933)

Roosevelt acted fast. In his first 100 days, he pushed 15 major pieces of legislation through Congress. He also used ‘fireside chats’ — radio broadcasts to the nation — to explain his policies in simple, reassuring language. Around 60 million Americans listened.

The Emergency Banking Act (March 1933)

Roosevelt’s first act was to declare a ‘bank holiday’ — closing all banks for four days. Government inspectors checked every bank’s accounts. Only sound banks were allowed to reopen. This restored public confidence, and within weeks Americans were depositing money back into banks instead of hiding it at home.

The Alphabet Agencies

The New Deal created many new government agencies to tackle different parts of the Depression. These became known as the ‘alphabet agencies’ because of their abbreviations:

CCC — Civilian Conservation Corps (1933): Gave work to young unemployed men aged 18–25 in conservation projects — planting trees, building roads, and preventing soil erosion. Workers received $30 per month ($25 sent home to their families). By 1942, around 3 million young men had worked in the CCC. It planted over 3 billion trees. Around 200,000 African Americans served in the CCC, but women were banned entirely.

AAA — Agricultural Adjustment Administration (1933): Aimed to raise farm prices by paying farmers to produce less. Reducing supply pushed crop prices up. Farm income doubled between 1933 and 1939. However, critics argued it was wrong to destroy food when millions were hungry. It also hurt sharecroppers and tenant farmers who were evicted when landowners took land out of production.

TVA — Tennessee Valley Authority (1933): A huge government project to develop the Tennessee Valley, one of the poorest regions in America. The TVA built 21 dams to control flooding, generate cheap hydroelectric power, and improve navigation. It brought electricity to rural areas for the first time and transformed the region’s economy. The TVA is often seen as the most successful New Deal programme.

NRA — National Recovery Administration (1933): Set minimum wages, maximum working hours, and banned child labour. Businesses that signed up displayed the ‘Blue Eagle’ symbol. However, the Supreme Court declared the NRA unconstitutional in 1935 because it gave the federal government too much power over businesses.

FERA — Federal Emergency Relief Administration (1933): Gave $500 million in grants to states for direct relief — food, clothing, and shelter. This was the first time the federal government had given money directly to help the poor.

CWA — Civil Works Administration (1933): Run by Harry Hopkins, the CWA quickly created 4 million short-term jobs for unskilled workers during the harsh winter of 1933–34 — building roads, schools, and airports. It was replaced by the WPA in 1935.

PWA — Public Works Administration (1933): Run by Harold Ickes, the PWA focused on large-scale, skilled construction projects. It spent over $6 billion building 34,000 projects, including the Triborough Bridge in New York. The PWA used skilled workers; the CWA and WPA used mostly unskilled labour. This was a key distinction.

WPA — Works Progress Administration (1935): The largest New Deal agency. Run by Harry Hopkins, the WPA employed around 8.5 million people between 1935 and 1943. It built 650,000 miles of roads, 125,000 public buildings, and 8,000 parks. It also employed artists, writers, actors, and musicians. Critics mocked some WPA jobs as ‘boondoggles’ — pointless make-work like sweeping leaves, scaring pigeons from buildings, or putting on plays. The word came from old cowboy slang for a useless task.

The Second New Deal (1935–1937)

In 1935, Roosevelt launched a second wave of New Deal laws, focusing more on long-term reform and social welfare:

The Social Security Act (1935)

This was arguably the most important and lasting New Deal reform. It created:

  • A national pension system for Americans over 65
  • Unemployment insurance — for the first time, workers who lost their jobs received temporary financial support
  • Financial help for the disabled and for mothers with dependent children

The Social Security Act created America’s first welfare state. It established the principle that the federal government had a duty to care for its citizens — a revolutionary idea in the United States.

The Wagner Act (1935)

Also known as the National Labor Relations Act, this gave workers the legal right to join trade unions and bargain collectively with employers. It set up the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) to protect workers’ rights. Union membership grew from 3.7 million in 1933 to 9 million by 1938.

Opposition to the New Deal

Despite its popularity, the New Deal faced strong opposition from several directions:

Opposition from the Right (Republicans and Big Business)

  • Republicans and business leaders accused Roosevelt of spending too much and creating dangerous federal power.
  • They called the New Deal ‘socialism’ or even ‘communism,’ saying it interfered with free enterprise and individual liberty.
  • The American Liberty League, funded by wealthy businessmen, campaigned against the New Deal.
  • Critics also attacked ‘boondoggles’ — claiming the government was paying people to do pointless jobs.

Opposition from the Left

  • Huey Long, the populist Senator from Louisiana, argued the New Deal did not go far enough. He proposed a ‘Share Our Wealth’ programme that would cap personal fortunes at $3 million and redistribute the rest to give every American family a minimum income of $5,000 per year, a free education, and a car. Long attracted millions of followers before he was assassinated in 1935.
  • Father Charles Coughlin, a Catholic priest with a radio audience of 30–40 million, initially supported FDR but turned against him. He called the New Deal too friendly to bankers and not doing enough for ordinary people.
  • Dr Francis Townsend campaigned for a pension of $200 per month for all Americans over 60, funded by a national sales tax. His plan attracted 5 million supporters.

The Supreme Court

The Supreme Court was a major threat to the New Deal. In 1935, it declared the NRA unconstitutional, and in 1936 it struck down the AAA. Conservative judges argued that New Deal agencies gave the federal government powers that belonged to individual states. In 1937, Roosevelt tried to ‘pack’ the Court by adding up to six extra, sympathetic judges. This ‘court-packing’ plan was hugely controversial and was rejected by Congress.

Key Fact — The Court-Packing Crisis

Roosevelt’s attempt to add extra judges in 1937 was his biggest political mistake. Even his supporters saw it as an attempt to undermine the courts’ independence. The plan failed, but the Supreme Court then began ruling more favourably on New Deal laws — a shift called ‘the switch in time that saved nine.’

Despite all this opposition, Roosevelt won the 1936 election in a landslide, carrying all but 2 states. He joked: ‘Everyone is against the New Deal — except the voters!’ This showed that ordinary Americans strongly supported his programmes, even if the rich and powerful did not.

Successes and Limitations of the New Deal

Jobs created: Millions were put back to work through the CCC, PWA, and WPA. Unemployment fell from 14 million (25%) in 1933 to 7 million (14%) by 1937.

Infrastructure: The New Deal built roads, bridges, dams, schools, hospitals, and airports that served America for decades. The TVA transformed an entire region.

Banking reform: The Emergency Banking Act and the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) restored confidence in banks and protected savers.

Social Security: The Social Security Act (1935) created a lasting safety net — pensions, unemployment insurance, and disability support — that still exists today.

Workers’ rights: The Wagner Act strengthened trade unions and gave workers more bargaining power.

Hope and confidence: The New Deal restored hope. Roosevelt’s fireside chats and energetic leadership gave Americans confidence that their government cared.

Popular support: Roosevelt’s 1936 re-election (all but 2 states) proved the New Deal had wide public backing.

Did not end the Depression: Unemployment fell to 7 million by 1937, but when Roosevelt cut spending that year, the economy slipped back into recession (the ‘Roosevelt Recession’). Unemployment jumped back to 10 million. It was World War Two, not the New Deal, that finally ended the Depression.

African Americans: The New Deal did little for Black Americans. CCC camps were segregated. The AAA often helped white landowners at the expense of Black sharecroppers. Roosevelt refused to support anti-lynching legislation, fearing he would lose white Southern Democrats. Around 200,000 African Americans did benefit from the CCC, but discrimination was widespread.

Women: Most New Deal jobs were designed for men. Women were banned from the CCC entirely. Only around 8,000 women were enrolled in CCC-equivalent programmes, compared to 3 million men. Some NRA codes set lower wages for women than for men.

Huge government debt: The New Deal cost $50.2 billion in total — the biggest government scheme in world history. Critics said this was unsustainable.

Did not fix the economic system: American capitalism stayed the same. Wealth inequality persisted, and the root causes of the Depression were not fully addressed.

States’ Rights vs Federal Government

The New Deal permanently changed the relationship between the federal government and the American people. Before Roosevelt, most Americans expected state governments — not Washington — to handle welfare, jobs, and economic problems. The New Deal shifted power to the federal government on a scale never seen before. This was a revolutionary change. From this point on, Americans looked to the president and Congress to solve national crises. The debate over states’ rights versus federal power remains one of the biggest issues in American politics today.

Exam Tip

A common exam question is: ‘How successful was the New Deal?’ Cover both successes and limitations with specific evidence. Name agencies (CCC, TVA, WPA, AAA) and their achievements. Use the term ‘priming the pump’ to show you understand the economic thinking. A top answer will note the New Deal gave hope and created jobs, but did not end the Depression — and that its benefits were unevenly distributed, especially for African Americans and women.

Conclusion

Roosevelt’s New Deal was a turning point in American government. For the first time, the federal government took direct responsibility for citizens’ welfare — creating jobs, building infrastructure, and setting up a social safety net. It gave millions of Americans hope during the worst economic crisis in history. However, it did not end the Depression, and its benefits were uneven. The New Deal’s lasting legacy was a permanent shift of power from the states to the federal government — and the principle that government must protect its people from economic disaster.

Key Takeaways

  • FDR’s election (1932): Won 42 of 48 states. Promised a ‘New Deal’ based on Relief, Recovery, and Reform. His ‘Brain Trust’ of expert advisors designed the policies.
  • Alphabet agencies: CCC (3 million employed), TVA (21 dams), WPA (8.5 million employed). Total cost: $50.2 billion. Unemployment fell from 14 million to 7 million by 1937.
  • Limitations: Did not end the Depression (WWII did). African Americans and women benefited less. The ‘Roosevelt Recession’ (1937) saw unemployment jump back to 10 million after spending cuts.
