Kennedy, Johnson & the Great Society

America and the ‘Great Society’

In the early 1960s, the United States was the richest country in the world — yet millions of Americans still lived in poverty. Presidents John F. Kennedy (1961–1963) and Lyndon B. Johnson (1963–1969) both believed the federal government should tackle poverty, improve education, and expand healthcare. Their programmes — Kennedy’s New Frontier and Johnson’s Great Society — were the biggest expansion of government social policy since Roosevelt’s New Deal in the 1930s.

Key Fact — The Scale of Poverty in 1960s America

When Kennedy took office in January 1961, around 40 million Americans — roughly 22% of the population — lived below the poverty line. Poverty hit African Americans, the elderly, and rural communities in Appalachia and the Deep South hardest. Michael Harrington’s book The Other America (1962) shocked the nation by exposing the “invisible poor” hidden behind suburban prosperity.

Kennedy’s New Frontier (1961–1963)

John F. Kennedy won the 1960 election by a razor-thin margin against Richard Nixon. At the Democratic convention, he spoke of a “New Frontier” — promising bold action on poverty, education, healthcare, and civil rights. He was young, charismatic, and inspired hope for a more equal America.

What Did Kennedy Propose?

Kennedy’s New Frontier included proposals for:

  • Healthcare: Federal health insurance for the elderly (which would later become Medicare)
  • Education: Federal funding for schools, teacher training, and university scholarships
  • Poverty: A higher minimum wage and investment in depressed areas
  • Housing: Slum clearance and urban renewal
  • Civil rights: Laws to end racial discrimination

What Did Kennedy Actually Achieve?

Kennedy faced serious opposition in Congress. Many Southern Democrats were deeply conservative and opposed social reform — especially anything linked to civil rights. They teamed up with Republicans to block much of Kennedy’s programme.

Despite this, Kennedy did achieve some important reforms:

  • The minimum wage was raised from $1.00 to $1.25 per hour in 1961
  • The Area Redevelopment Act (1961) gave loans and grants to economically depressed areas
  • The Manpower Development and Training Act (1962) funded retraining for workers who had lost jobs to new technology
  • The Housing Act (1961) provided $4.9 billion for urban renewal — though housing loans mostly helped existing homeowners, not the poorest families
  • Social Security benefits were extended and the minimum payment was increased
  • Kennedy created the Peace Corps (1961), sending American volunteers to developing countries to help with education, healthcare, and farming
  • Unemployment fell from 4.5 million to 3.5 million during Kennedy’s time in office
  • Kennedy appointed African Americans to federal courts, including Thurgood Marshall to the Court of Appeals

However, Kennedy’s major proposals on Medicare, federal aid to education, and civil rights were all blocked by Congress before his assassination on 22 November 1963. Many historians argue that the New Frontier promised far more than it delivered.

Johnson’s Great Society (1964–1968)

When Lyndon B. Johnson became president after Kennedy’s assassination, he was determined to push through Kennedy’s unfinished reforms — and go far beyond them. Johnson was a master of congressional politics. He had served as Senate Majority Leader for years and knew how to twist arms, make deals, and get laws passed in a way Kennedy never could.

In May 1964, Johnson outlined his vision at the University of Michigan. He declared that America could build a “Great Society” — a nation free from poverty and racial injustice. He then won the 1964 election in a landslide against Republican Barry Goldwater, carrying 61% of the popular vote. This gave him a huge mandate and large Democratic majorities in both houses of Congress.

The War on Poverty

Even before the 1964 election, Johnson declared an “unconditional War on Poverty” in his State of the Union address in January 1964. The centrepiece was the Economic Opportunity Act, signed on 20 August 1964. It created the Office of Economic Opportunity (OEO) to coordinate anti-poverty programmes across the federal government.

Key Fact — Key War on Poverty Programmes
  • Head Start (1965): Pre-school education programme for children from low-income families, providing early learning, nutrition, and healthcare. By 1968, over 2 million children had been enrolled
  • Job Corps (1964): Residential training centres offering education and vocational skills to disadvantaged young people aged 16–24. By 1968, over 100,000 young people had enrolled
  • Community Action Program (CAP): Funded local anti-poverty organisations with “maximum feasible participation” of the poor themselves in decision-making
  • Volunteers in Service to America (VISTA): A domestic version of the Peace Corps, sending volunteers to work in impoverished communities
  • Food Stamps Act (1964): Expanded the food stamp programme to help low-income families afford food

Medicare and Medicaid (1965)

One of the Great Society’s most lasting achievements was Medicare and Medicaid, signed into law on 30 July 1965.

  • Medicare gave federal health insurance to all Americans aged 65 and over, regardless of income. It covered hospital stays and, through an optional plan, doctor visits. This was the reform Kennedy had failed to pass
  • Medicaid gave health insurance to Americans on very low incomes, funded jointly by federal and state governments

Before Medicare, around half of all Americans over 65 had no health insurance. By 1966, 19 million elderly Americans had enrolled. These programmes remain the foundation of America’s public healthcare system today.

Education

Johnson, a former schoolteacher, saw education as the key to breaking the cycle of poverty. His major reforms included:

  • The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 — the first major federal funding for schools. It provided over $1 billion in its first year, targeted at low-income areas
  • The Higher Education Act (1965) created federal scholarships and low-interest loans for university students from poorer families
  • The Head Start programme (described above) gave pre-school education to disadvantaged children

Federal spending on education rose from $2.3 billion in 1963 to $12.3 billion by 1970 — a huge increase that transformed schools in the poorest communities.

Housing: The Housing and Urban Development Act (1965) funded low-income housing. Robert Weaver became the first African American in a president’s cabinet as head of the new Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). The Model Cities Act (1966) targeted the most run-down urban areas. The Fair Housing Act (1968) banned racial discrimination in housing — making it illegal to refuse to sell or rent to someone because of their race.

Immigration: The Immigration and Nationality Act (1965) scrapped the discriminatory national origins quota system from the 1920s. It replaced it with a system based on skills and family ties, greatly increasing immigration from Asia, Latin America, and Africa.

Consumer protection: New laws improved car safety, required clearer labelling on food and medicines, and protected consumers from dangerous products.

Environment: The Wilderness Act (1964) protected 9 million acres of land. The Clean Air Act (1963) and Water Quality Act (1965) set federal standards to tackle pollution.

Civil rights: The Civil Rights Act (1964) banned racial discrimination in employment and public places. The Voting Rights Act (1965) outlawed discriminatory voting practices. These are covered in earlier lessons.

Successes of the Great Society

Johnson’s Great Society achieved real results:

  • The poverty rate fell from 22% in 1960 to 13% by 1968 — the biggest drop in poverty in American history
  • The number living below the poverty line fell from 40 million to 25 million
  • Medicare and Medicaid gave millions of elderly and poor Americans healthcare for the first time
  • Head Start gave early education to millions of disadvantaged children and still runs today
  • Federal education spending transformed schools in the poorest areas
  • African American poverty fell from 55% in 1960 to 32% by 1968
  • Infant mortality dropped and life expectancy rose, especially among the poorest

Limitations and Criticisms

Despite its achievements, the Great Society faced criticism from both left and right:

Criticisms from the Right (Conservatives)

  • The programmes were too expensive and created welfare dependency instead of self-reliance
  • The federal government was growing too large, interfering in areas best left to individuals and states
  • Some programmes, like the Community Action Program, were seen as funding radical activist groups
  • The programmes did not tackle the root causes of poverty — poor education, family breakdown, and lack of jobs

Criticisms from the Left (Liberals and Radicals)

  • The programmes did not go far enough — poverty remained widespread and the rich–poor gap was still huge
  • Funding was inadequate: many programmes were underfunded from the start, then cut further as Vietnam spending rose
  • The programmes failed to address structural racism built into American society
  • African Americans still faced discrimination in housing, employment, and education despite the new laws

The Impact of the Vietnam War

The biggest factor that undermined the Great Society was the Vietnam War. As American involvement grew from 1965, the cost soared — from $6 billion in 1966 to $26 billion by 1968. Johnson had to choose between funding his domestic programmes and funding the war. The war won. Great Society programmes were cut back, and the “War on Poverty” was overshadowed by Vietnam. As Martin Luther King Jr. said in 1967, the Great Society had been “shot down on the battlefields of Vietnam.”

Kennedy set the agenda and raised awareness of poverty, but achieved little in practice. He faced a hostile Congress and was in office for only 1,036 days. His greatest contribution was inspiring the belief that government should act on poverty and inequality.

Johnson was far more effective at getting laws through Congress. Between 1964 and 1966, he passed over 200 pieces of legislation — more than any president since FDR. His political skill, public sympathy after Kennedy’s assassination, and his landslide election win gave him the power to push through reforms Kennedy could not.

However, Johnson’s legacy was overshadowed by Vietnam. By 1968, the war had destroyed his presidency, split the Democratic Party, and diverted resources from reform. Johnson chose not to seek re-election in March 1968.

Exam Tip

Questions often ask “how far” Kennedy and Johnson succeeded in reducing poverty. You need balance: acknowledge the achievements (poverty falling from 22% to 13%, Medicare, Head Start) but also explain the limits (25 million still in poverty, Vietnam diverting funds, conservative opposition, structural racism). Use specific statistics and named laws — the examiner rewards precise evidence. Compare Kennedy and Johnson: Kennedy set the vision but achieved little; Johnson passed the laws but was undone by Vietnam.

Conclusion

Kennedy’s New Frontier and Johnson’s Great Society were the boldest attempt to tackle poverty since the New Deal. Johnson nearly halved the poverty rate, gave millions healthcare through Medicare and Medicaid, and transformed education through Head Start. Kennedy contributed the Peace Corps, reduced unemployment, and appointed African Americans to federal courts. Yet the Great Society fell short. Conservative opposition, structural racism, and above all the Vietnam War meant the “War on Poverty” was never fully won. The Fair Housing Act (1968) was a late landmark, but millions of Americans still lived in poverty.

Key Takeaways

  • Kennedy’s New Frontier set the agenda but was mostly blocked by Congress. Johnson’s political skill let him pass over 200 laws, including Medicare, Medicaid, and the Economic Opportunity Act.
  • Real progress was made: poverty fell from 22% to 13% (1960–1968) and programmes like Head Start and Job Corps helped millions.
  • The Vietnam War diverted billions from domestic reform, destroyed Johnson’s presidency, and left the “War on Poverty” incomplete.
