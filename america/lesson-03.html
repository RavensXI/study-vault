Prohibition & Organised Crime

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

What Was Prohibition?

Prohibition was the ban on making, selling, and transporting alcoholic drinks in the United States. The 18th Amendment to the Constitution was passed in January 1919 and took effect on 17 January 1920. The Volstead Act (October 1919) set out the rules, making it illegal to produce, sell, or move any drink with more than 0.5% alcohol.

Key Fact — The Legal Framework

The 18th Amendment (1919) made Prohibition part of the Constitution. The Volstead Act (1919) set the enforcement rules. Together, they banned the manufacture, sale, and transport of ‘intoxicating liquors’ — but it was never illegal to drink alcohol. People could consume it; they just could not legally get it.

Why Was Prohibition Introduced?

The campaign for Prohibition had been growing for decades:

The Temperance Movement

Groups like the Anti-Saloon League (founded 1893) and the Women’s Christian Temperance Union (WCTU) had campaigned against alcohol for years. They argued it destroyed families, caused domestic violence, and led to poverty. These groups were well-funded and politically powerful.

Religious and Moral Arguments

Many Protestant Christians, especially in rural areas and the South, saw alcohol as sinful. They called saloons ‘dens of vice’ and believed a ban would create a more moral, God-fearing society.

Industrial Efficiency

Industrialists like Henry Ford and John D. Rockefeller backed Prohibition. They believed sober workers would be more productive. Fewer accidents and less absenteeism meant higher profits.

Anti-German Sentiment

During World War One, many of America’s biggest breweries were owned by German-Americans (such as Anheuser-Busch and Pabst). Anti-German feeling during the war made it easier to target the brewing industry. Grain used for brewing could go to the war effort instead.

How Was Prohibition Enforced?

Prohibition agents were responsible for enforcement, but the task was almost impossible from the start:

  • Only about 1,500 agents policed the entire country — a vast territory with 18,700 miles of coastline and borders.
  • Agents were poorly paid (about $2,500 a year), making many easy to bribe.
  • In the first four years, 1 in 12 agents were sacked for corruption.
  • Many state and local police forces were unwilling or unable to enforce the law, especially in big cities.
Key Fact — Eliot Ness

Eliot Ness was a young Prohibition agent in Chicago. He led a team called ‘The Untouchables’ because they refused bribes. They raided Capone’s breweries, but Ness’s team was the exception — widespread corruption undermined enforcement everywhere.

Breaking the Law: Bootlegging and Speakeasies

Instead of stopping drinking, Prohibition drove the alcohol trade underground and created a huge illegal industry:

Bootlegging

Bootleggers illegally made, moved, or sold alcohol. It was smuggled from Canada and Mexico, or shipped in from the Caribbean (‘rum running’). Famous rum runners like William McCoy made fortunes — he smuggled an estimated $70 million worth of whiskey in just four years. Even Joseph Kennedy (father of the future President) reportedly made money from bootlegging. Others brewed ‘moonshine’ in bathtubs or hidden stills. There were never enough agents to close down all the stills. This home-made alcohol was often deadly — 98 people died from poisonous moonshine in 1920, rising to 760 deaths in 1926 as illegal production spread. In total, an estimated 10,000 people died from poisonous bootleg liquor during Prohibition.

Speakeasies

Speakeasies were illegal bars that sprang up across America. By 1925, there were an estimated 15,000 in Detroit alone. By 1929, New York had around 32,000 — more than double the number of legal bars before Prohibition. Nationwide, there were roughly 200,000 speakeasies by 1930. The name came from speaking quietly (‘easy’) about their location. Many hid behind secret doors, in basements, or disguised as shops. Some had concealed entrances and required a secret password to get in. The famous 21 Club in New York was a speakeasy for the rich and famous. Others simply had supplies of illegal alcohol delivered to their homes.

The Rise of Organised Crime

Prohibition created a huge opportunity for organised crime. Criminal gangs took control of the illegal alcohol trade, making vast fortunes and wielding huge power.

Al Capone

Alphonse ‘Al’ Capone was the most notorious gangster of the Prohibition era. From a poor immigrant background, Capone rose to control most of Chicago’s criminal underworld. His gang earned an estimated $100 million a year in total ($1.3 billion in today’s money) from bootlegging, gambling dens, brothels, and protection rackets. He had around 600 gangsters under his command, as well as lawyers and accountants to manage his empire.

Key Fact — Al Capone’s Power

Capone controlled much of Chicago through bribery and violence. He had judges, police, and politicians on his payroll — including the Chief of Police and the Mayor of Chicago — bribing officials with an estimated $75 million over his career. Capone appeared on the front cover of Time magazine in March 1930, opened nightclubs, and was cheered at horse races. Many poor Americans admired him as a ‘Robin Hood’ figure — he even opened soup kitchens to feed the homeless when the Depression began. Capone himself said: “I make my money by supplying a popular demand. If I break the law, my customers are as guilty as I am… I call myself a businessman.” Despite ordering many murders, Capone was finally convicted in 1931 — not for murder or bootlegging, but for tax evasion. He was sentenced to 11 years in prison.

The St Valentine’s Day Massacre

The most infamous act of gang violence was the St Valentine’s Day Massacre on 14 February 1929. Gunmen working for Capone dressed as police officers and entered a garage on Chicago’s North Clark Street. They told seven members of the rival Bugs Moran gang to line up against the wall, then shot them all in the back. Moran himself arrived late and missed the attack. When asked who was behind it, Moran said: “Only Capone kills like that!” One victim survived long enough to be questioned, but kept to the gangster code of silence — his only statement was “No-one.” The massacre shocked the nation. Capone was in Florida at the time and was never charged.

Gang Wars and Violence

Chicago was not the only city hit. The fight for control of bootlegging — known as the ‘Beer Wars’ — caused hundreds of murders across American cities. In Chicago alone, there were over 500 gang-related murders between 1927 and 1930. Corruption was rampant — in many cities, police and politicians were on the gangsters’ payroll.

Why Did Prohibition Fail?

By the late 1920s, it was clear Prohibition was not working:

Impossible to enforce: The USA was too large. With thousands of miles of borders and only 1,500 agents, there was no way to stop illegal alcohol.

Widespread law-breaking: Millions of Americans kept drinking. Many saw Prohibition as an unfair limit on personal freedom. Even some politicians and judges drank illegally.

Corruption: Poorly paid agents, police, judges, and politicians were easily bribed. In some cities, the criminal underworld and the authorities were almost the same thing.

Organised crime: Prohibition handed the drinks trade to violent criminals who made huge profits. The gangs grew more powerful and more dangerous.

Lost tax revenue: The government lost the tax it used to collect from legal alcohol, while enforcement costs drained public funds.

The Great Depression: After the 1929 Crash, ending Prohibition became an economic argument too — legal alcohol would create jobs and generate tax revenue the government badly needed.

The End of Prohibition

In the 1932 election, Franklin D. Roosevelt promised to end Prohibition. After his landslide win, the 21st Amendment was ratified on 5 December 1933, repealing the 18th Amendment and ending Prohibition after nearly 14 years. It remains the only time a Constitutional Amendment has been repealed.

Key Fact — The 21st Amendment

The 21st Amendment (1933) repealed Prohibition. Roosevelt reportedly said: ‘I think this would be a good time for a beer.’ Ending Prohibition created jobs, brought in tax revenue, and cut off a major income source for organised crime — though gangs simply moved into other rackets like drugs and gambling.

Exam Tip

Questions often ask why Prohibition was introduced or why it failed. Give multiple reasons with specific evidence. For top marks, show how the reasons link — impossible enforcement led to mass law-breaking, which created the chance for organised crime, which corrupted the very officials meant to enforce the law.

Conclusion

Prohibition was widely regarded as a failure. Introduced with genuine moral aims, it proved impossible to enforce, fuelled the rise of organised crime, corrupted law enforcement, and was ignored by millions. Rather than creating a sober society, it showed the limits of using the law to change deep-rooted social behaviour.

Key Takeaways

  • Good intentions, disastrous results: Moral, religious, and economic arguments backed Prohibition — but its consequences (organised crime, corruption, mass law-breaking) were far worse than the problems it aimed to fix.
  • Al Capone and organised crime: The illegal alcohol trade made gangsters like Capone hugely wealthy, with empires that corrupted police, judges, and politicians.
  • The 21st Amendment: Prohibition was repealed in 1933 — the only time a Constitutional Amendment has been undone. Its failure showed laws only work when the public supports them.
