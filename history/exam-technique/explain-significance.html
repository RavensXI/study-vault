8 marks

“Explain the significance of…”

Also asked as “Explain what was important about…” — Used in: Health & the People • Elizabethan England

What the Examiner Wants

“Significance” means WHY something mattered. The examiner wants you to explain what impact or importance something had — not just describe what happened. Think about: what changed because of it? Who was affected? What were the short-term and long-term consequences?

Level Descriptors (Simplified)

Level Marks What You Need
Level 4 7–8 Complex explanation of significance, showing multiple ways something mattered with specific supporting evidence
Level 3 5–6 Explains significance with relevant knowledge but doesn’t fully develop all points
Level 2 3–4 Describes what happened but doesn’t clearly explain WHY it was significant
Level 1 1–2 General statements with limited relevant knowledge
— 0 Nothing relevant

Step-by-Step Formula

  1. 1
    Identify what you’re explaining — What person, event, or development does the question name? Write it down.
  2. 2
    Think of 2 reasons it was significant — Each reason is a separate paragraph. Think: What changed? Who was affected? What were the consequences (short-term AND long-term)?
  3. 3
    Write paragraph 1 — State your first reason for significance. Support it with specific factual evidence (dates, names, numbers). Explain the impact clearly.
  4. 4
    Write paragraph 2 — State a different reason for significance. Again, support with evidence and explain the impact. If possible, link the two reasons together.

Timing

Plan
2 min Para 1
5 min Para 2
5 min

Two well-developed paragraphs are better than three rushed ones. Spend your time on evidence and explanation, not trying to cram in extra points.

Paragraph 1 (first reason for significance)

“One reason [topic] was significant is that…”

“For example, [specific evidence with dates/names]…”

“This was important because it meant that…”

Paragraph 2 (second reason for significance)

“[Topic] was also significant because…”

“Evidence for this includes [specific facts]…”

“As a result, [explain the wider impact or long-term consequence]…”

Explain the significance of Pasteur’s Germ Theory (1861). [8 marks]

Significance + Evidence

Pasteur’s Germ Theory was significant because it proved for the first time that microorganisms caused disease, overturning the miasma theory that had dominated medicine for centuries. In 1861, Pasteur used his swan-neck flask experiment to demonstrate that germs in the air caused decay, not ‘spontaneous generation.’ This was a turning point because it gave doctors a scientific basis for understanding illness. Before this, treatments were based on balancing humours or avoiding bad smells, which were ineffective.

Wider Impact + Long-Term

Pasteur’s discovery was also significant because it led directly to practical breakthroughs that saved millions of lives. Robert Koch built on Germ Theory to identify specific bacteria causing diseases like tuberculosis (1882) and cholera (1883), which allowed targeted treatments and public health measures. In surgery, Joseph Lister used Pasteur’s ideas to develop antiseptic techniques using carbolic acid in 1867, dramatically reducing death rates from infection after operations. Without Germ Theory, modern medicine — including vaccinations, antibiotics, and hygiene standards — would not exist.

Explain the significance of the Religious Settlement of 1559. [8 marks]

Level 2 Answer

Describes what happened, not why it mattered

The Religious Settlement was when Elizabeth made England Protestant again. She became Supreme Governor of the Church of England. The Act of Supremacy was passed and then the Act of Uniformity. Priests had to use the Book of Common Prayer. This happened in 1559 at the start of Elizabeth’s reign.

Level 4 Answer

Explains significance with context

The Religious Settlement of 1559 was significant because it established a ‘Middle Way’ that aimed to unite a dangerously divided country. By making herself Supreme Governor rather than Supreme Head, Elizabeth avoided directly challenging Catholics who believed only the Pope could lead the Church. The deliberately vague wording of the 39 Articles meant that both moderate Catholics and Protestants could attend the same services without feeling they were betraying their beliefs. This prevented the kind of religious civil wars that tore apart France and the Netherlands during the same period.

A full Level 4 answer would add a second reason for significance (e.g. its long-term impact on Puritan and Catholic challenges) — see the model answer above.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Describing what happened instead of explaining significance — “Pasteur did an experiment with a flask” is description. You need to say WHY it mattered: “This proved germs caused disease, which transformed medicine.”
  • ❌
    Only giving one reason — Two developed reasons is the minimum for Level 4. One reason, however detailed, caps you at Level 3.
  • ❌
    Forgetting to include evidence — General claims like “it was really important” score poorly. You need specific facts: dates, names, statistics, examples.
  • ❌
    Confusing significance with causes — The question asks why something MATTERED, not why it happened. Focus on impact and consequences, not background.
