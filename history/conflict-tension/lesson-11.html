Remilitarisation of the Rhineland

Overlay:
0:00 / 0:00

Hitler’s Boldest Gamble

On 7 March 1936, Hitler ordered German troops to march into the Rhineland. This was his boldest move yet — a direct violation of both the Treaty of Versailles and the Locarno Treaties of 1925. Hitler timed it carefully — the League was distracted by the Abyssinian Crisis and Italy had invaded Ethiopia, leaving the Allies divided.

What Was the Rhineland?

The Rhineland was Germany’s industrial heartland, on the western border with France and Belgium. Under the Treaty of Versailles, it was a demilitarised zone — no German troops or fortifications allowed. It acted as a buffer protecting France from a future German attack.

Key Fact — Why the Rhineland Mattered

Germany had freely accepted the Rhineland’s demilitarised status in the Locarno Treaties (1925). Hitler could not use his usual argument that he was overturning an unfair Diktat. This was a violation of an agreement Germany signed willingly.

The Gamble: 7 March 1936

Hitler sent about 22,000 troops into the Rhineland. The German army was still weak and no match for France. His own generals were against the move — they thought it was too risky. Hitler used an excuse: he claimed France’s recent alliance with the Soviet Union was a threat to Germany, so Germany had a right to defend its own border.

Key Fact — Hitler’s Own Admission

Hitler later admitted: “If the French had marched in, we would have had to withdraw with our tails between our legs.” He had given his generals standing orders to retreat at the first sign of French military action.

Why Did Nobody Act?

Despite the clear treaty violation, neither France, Britain, nor the League stopped Hitler. Each had its own reasons for doing nothing.

France was in the best position to act but was paralysed. The country was in a political crisis, and no politician wanted to start a military confrontation. French intelligence also overestimated the size of the German force.

France refused to act without British support. When Britain said no, France felt it could not respond alone — even though it had more than enough troops to push the Germans out.

Many in Britain felt Germany had the right to station troops in its own territory. Lord Lothian, a leading British politician, called it “no more than the Germans walking into their own backyard.” This reflected widespread sympathy for German grievances about the Treaty.

The British public had no appetite for war over what seemed like a distant border dispute. The government was not prepared to commit forces over what it saw as an inevitable change to an outdated treaty.

The League was still dealing with the Abyssinian Crisis. It condemned Hitler’s action but was powerless to do anything. The Rhineland confirmed what Abyssinia had already shown — the League could not stop determined aggressors.

Consequences of the Rhineland Remilitarisation

The failure to act shifted the balance of power in Europe firmly in Hitler’s favour.

Key Fact — Four Critical Consequences
  • Hitler’s prestige soared — He had proved his generals wrong. His position at home became unchallengeable and the German public celebrated.
  • The Treaty of Versailles was effectively dead — The Locarno Treaties were also proven worthless. If freely-agreed borders could be broken without consequence, no treaty had meaning.
  • Strategic disaster for France — German troops could now sit on the French border. Germany began building the Siegfried Line of fortifications, making any future French attack far harder.
  • Hitler and Mussolini drew closer — Italy’s isolation after Abyssinia pushed Mussolini towards Hitler. By late 1936, they signed the Rome-Berlin Axis — the two most aggressive leaders in Europe were now allies.
Exam Tip

Call the Rhineland a crucial turning point. It was the last real chance to stop Hitler without a full-scale war. Had France acted, Germany’s weak army would have retreated — Hitler said so himself. Instead, inaction gave him confidence and made every later crisis harder to handle.

Conclusion

The Rhineland was a calculated risk that paid off for Hitler. It destroyed the Treaty’s remaining authority, proved Locarno worthless, and convinced Hitler the Allies would never stand up to him. The failure to respond when the Allies still had military superiority made it far harder to contain Hitler later.

Key Takeaways

  • A bluff the Allies failed to call: Hitler’s generals were against it and had orders to retreat if France acted. This was not an unstoppable advance — it was a gamble.
  • The last cheap chance to stop Hitler: France could have pushed Germany back easily, but political crisis, overestimation of German strength, and British reluctance stopped them.
  • Consequences reshaped Europe: The Siegfried Line closed France’s window for action, the Rome-Berlin Axis united two fascist dictators, and Hitler’s confidence grew with every unchallenged move.
