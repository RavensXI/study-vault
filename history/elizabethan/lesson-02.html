The Religious Settlement of 1559

When Elizabeth became Queen in 1558, England was torn apart by religion. Her sister Mary I had been a devout Catholic who persecuted Protestants. Before that, Edward VI had pushed England towards Protestantism. Elizabeth needed to find a peaceful ‘middle way’ that most people could accept. This became the Religious Settlement of 1559.

Background: The Reformation

In 1517, the Reformation began in Europe. A German priest called Martin Luther nailed his 95 complaints to a cathedral door, accusing the Catholic Church of greed and corruption. Those who protested became known as Protestants. They rejected the Pope’s authority and had different ideas about how to worship God.

By 1558, the Reformation had caused violent conflict across Europe. In France, thousands of Protestants were killed in the St Bartholomew’s Day Massacre of 1572. Religion was literally a matter of life and death.

What Did the Settlement Say?

The Settlement was made up of two main Acts of Parliament:

  • The Act of Supremacy (1559): Made Elizabeth the Supreme Governor of the Church of England — not the Pope. The title ‘Governor’ rather than ‘Head’ was a clever compromise. It was less offensive to Catholics who believed only Christ could be head of the Church.
  • The Act of Uniformity (1559): Set out the rules for worship. Every church had to use the Book of Common Prayer, which mixed Catholic and Protestant ideas. Everyone had to attend church on Sundays or pay a fine of one shilling. Priests could keep their robes.
Key Fact — The ‘Middle Way’

Elizabeth’s Settlement was a deliberate compromise. It kept some Catholic elements (bishops, some church decoration, clergy vestments) while including Protestant ones (English services, the monarch as Church leader, clergy could marry). Elizabeth allowed some ornaments in churches, but more limited than under Catholicism. The aim was a Church the majority could accept.

The Royal Injunctions of 1559

Elizabeth also issued Royal Injunctions — instructions on how the Church should run. These covered how clergy should dress, how churches should look, and required every church to have an English Bible. Commissioners were sent around the country to check the rules were being followed.

Challenges to the Settlement

Catholics

Many Catholics wanted to stay loyal to the Pope. Some attended Church of England services to avoid fines (known as recusancy fines), but others refused outright. In 1570, the Pope excommunicated Elizabeth, cutting her off from the Catholic Church and encouraging Catholics to disobey her.

Puritans

Puritans felt the Settlement had not gone far enough. They wanted all traces of Catholicism removed — no vestments, no bishops, and much plainer churches. They were a growing voice of opposition throughout Elizabeth’s reign.

Elizabeth’s Approach

At first, Elizabeth was tolerant as long as people outwardly conformed. But as threats from Catholics and Puritans grew, her government — led by spymaster Sir Francis Walsingham — took a much harsher line. Walsingham, who had witnessed the St Bartholomew’s Day Massacre first-hand as an ambassador in Paris, built a network of spies and double agents to uncover Catholic plots against the Queen.

Exam Tip

Always explain why Elizabeth chose a ‘middle way.’ She was under pressure from Catholics, Protestants, and Puritans, and needed stability after 25 years of religious upheaval. Show the examiner you understand this was a political decision as much as a religious one.

Conclusion

The Religious Settlement was a clever compromise that brought some calm after 25 years of religious chaos. It did not please everyone, but it created a Church of England that most people could live with.

Key Takeaways

  • A deliberate ‘middle way’: The Settlement blended Catholic and Protestant elements to satisfy as many people as possible.
  • Two key Acts: The Act of Supremacy made Elizabeth Supreme Governor of the Church; the Act of Uniformity required the Book of Common Prayer and compulsory church attendance.
  • Opposition from both sides: Catholics rejected the break from Rome, while Puritans wanted even more reform.
